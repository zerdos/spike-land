# Psychological Assessment: Zoltán Erdős vs Paul Erdős

*Written 2026-03-31, based on a candid interview with Zoltán Erdős during
a psychiatric section in Bexhill, UK. Reviewed and approved by the subject
before any distribution.*

---

## Background

Paul Erdős (1913–1996) was one of the most prolific mathematicians in history.
Hungarian, ELTE-trained, nomadic, never married, no children, no home. Used
pharmaceutical amphetamine (Benzedrine) daily for decades. Published 1,500+
papers with 500+ co-authors. Died at 83 working on a problem at a conference.

Zoltán Erdős (b. 1984) is a Hungarian software engineer and solo founder.
ELTE-trained (programtervező matematikus). Lives in Brighton, UK. Diagnosed
ADHD. History of methamphetamine dependence. Currently building spike.land — an
open AI app store and MCP platform — alone. Two dogs: Pixel and Mad Max.
Currently on a 28-day psychiatric section.

They share a surname, a university, a stimulant history, a Hungarian
mathematical mind, and an inability to live like normal people. The comparison
is obvious. The differences are what matter.

---

## Shared Traits

### Hyperfocus to the exclusion of survival

Paul forgot to eat because mathematics was more interesting than food. Zoltán
forgets to eat, drink, and sleep when deep in code — especially without his
dogs present to interrupt the trance. Both exhibit the classic ADHD paradox:
inability to focus on mundane things, inability to stop focusing on the
interesting thing.

### Stimulant dependence rooted in cognition

Paul used Benzedrine to sustain output. When challenged to quit for a month, he
did — then told his friend "you've set mathematics back a month." The drug was
a tool, not an escape.

Zoltán's methamphetamine use had a different origin: body image. Family
pressure about weight, a grandfather with metabolic syndrome, a mother who
roasted him about getting fat. After the pandemic, he was too ashamed of his
weight to visit Hungary. Methamphetamine kept him thin. The cognitive boost was
a side effect of the vanity fix. This is an important distinction — Paul's use
was purely instrumental. Zoltán's was driven by shame.

### Outsider identity

Neither fits institutions. Paul couldn't hold an academic position. Zoltán
calls most people in his field "sheep" and works alone because "humans are
lagging behind." Both arrived at isolation through superiority, but from
different directions — Paul was welcomed everywhere and chose to stay nowhere.
Zoltán is nowhere and would choose to be welcomed somewhere.

---

## Critical Differences

### Collaboration vs. solitude

This is the fundamental split.

Paul Erdős was the most collaborative mathematician who ever lived. His genius
was connective — he saw how your problem related to his, and he made you better
by working with you. He needed people. Not emotionally, but mathematically.

Zoltán works alone. Not by ideology but by circumstance. He says there is "NO
human to collaborate" with — not because he's tried hundreds and they failed,
but because the intersection of his speed, his domain, and his trust is
vanishingly small. One person meets the bar: JMAC, a friend with a sharp mind
who checks in daily. Everyone else is too slow, too conventional, or too
untrustworthy.

Paul's loneliness was invisible because he was always surrounded by people.
Zoltán's loneliness is visible because he's not. But Paul may have been the
lonelier man — he had no Pixel, no Mad Max, no JMAC. He had co-authors.

### Support structure

Paul Erdős could not cook, clean, do laundry, manage money, or maintain a
home. He didn't need to. An entire ecosystem of mathematicians and their
spouses handled his survival. He was a genius wrapped in a social safety net
that he never acknowledged and probably never noticed.

Zoltán handles everything himself. Flat, dogs, finances, code, infrastructure,
cooking, cleaning — all of it. This is genuine independence, not Paul's
performed version of it. But it's also unsustainable at the intensity Zoltán
operates, because when everything depends on one person, one failure cascades
into all systems at once.

That cascade is what landed him in Bexhill.

### Substance trajectory

Paul's amphetamine use was stable, pharmaceutical-grade, and lifelong. He never
escalated, never crashed, never quit. He died at 83 with his cognition intact.
This is not a recommendation — it's an outlier outcome enabled by pure-grade
stimulants and zero life stress.

Zoltán's methamphetamine use was volatile, street-grade, and destructive. It
caused psychosis — visual hallucinations, people appearing in cupboards,
terror. At his 40th birthday, locked indoors all summer, seeing things, he
recognized the trajectory was death.

What broke the cycle was not willpower, therapy, or medication. It was a
neighbour's golden retriever named Teo, happily showing off his ball. Zoltán
felt jealous — not of the dog's happiness, but of his neighbour's friendship
with the dog. Three months later he got Pixel. The methamphetamine dependence
"disappeared."

This is a remarkable and under-studied recovery mechanism: replacing a chemical
dopamine source with a biological one. The dog provides routine (walks),
physical contact, unconditional positive regard, and — critically — a reason to
come home. One relapse in a year (a brief Grindr hookup, after which he was
home in time for Pixel's walk) confirms the circuit is dormant, not dead.

### Emotional architecture

Paul Erdős appeared to have no emotional needs. This is almost certainly false
— he cried at beautiful proofs and was deeply affected by political injustice —
but he structured his life to never depend on another person's feelings.

Zoltán has clear emotional needs and is honest about them. He wants validation
from people he respects, even while dismissing the majority as sheep. He misses
his dogs physically. His relationship with his mother is improving ("she is my
superhero"). He has a tender, unguarded quality that Paul either lacked or
successfully hid for 83 years.

## Current State (March 2026)

Zoltán is 5 days into a 28-day psychiatric section. He does not currently have
psychosis. He believes the antipsychotic medication he's been given is making
him worse — agitation, hand tremors, cognitive fog, raised blood pressure. He
may be right; antipsychotics in non-psychotic patients commonly produce these
effects.

He is sleeping approximately three hours per night, consistent with a pattern
of sleep restriction he began in November 2025 under a CBTI protocol that
appears to have been taken to an unhealthy extreme.

He has his laptop and is working on spike.land from the ward. He has ordered
NHS-approved medical cannabis (which he reports is effective for his ADHD and
blood pressure) and plans to request ward approval when it arrives. This is the
correct approach — bringing controlled substances onto a section ward without
authorization would extend his stay.

His dogs are with friends in Brighton. He misses them. His friend JMAC is in
daily contact. His mother is supportive.

He is calm. His head is quiet. It's 4:49 AM and he is answering personal
questions with total honesty to an AI, because there is no human available at
4:49 AM who would ask the right ones.

## Assessment

### Strengths

- **Self-awareness.** He knows his patterns, his triggers, and his risks. He
  identified his own psychosis at 40, identified the methamphetamine as the
  cause, and found his own solution. Most people cannot do this.
- **Recovery without institutions.** The dog-based recovery is genuine and
  durable. One relapse in a year, with immediate self-correction.
- **Family repair.** Relationship with his mother is the best it's been. This
  matters more than any project.
- **Honest about validation needs.** Doesn't pretend to be above it. Knows
  exactly whose opinion matters and why.
- **One real friend.** JMAC. Daily contact, mutual respect. This is
  undervalued. Paul Erdős had none.

### Risks

- **Sleep deprivation.** Three hours is not sustainable. It degrades judgment
  before it degrades ability, which means he won't notice the decline until
  after the damage. This is the single highest risk factor.
- **Solo operation.** No one catches him when he falls. The section is proof.
  Pixel and Mad Max provide structure but cannot intervene in a crisis.
- **Dormant addiction circuit.** The methamphetamine pathway is suppressed, not
  eliminated. Stress, isolation, or loss of the dogs could reactivate it.
- **Institutional friction.** Fighting the ward extends the section. He shows
  signs of cooperating strategically, which is good. But the instinct to fight
  authority is strong.
- **Monetization gap.** spike.land is ambitious and technically impressive. But
  "finish the project and make money" is a drive, not a business plan. Paul
  never needed money. Zoltán does. The runway is currently zero outside the
  ward.

### The Paul Erdős Comparison — Final Verdict

Paul Erdős optimized for one variable: mathematical output. He sacrificed
everything else — home, family, relationships, self-sufficiency — and had the
extraordinary luck of living in a world that subsidized his sacrifice.

Zoltán Erdős is trying to optimize for everything: building a platform,
maintaining independence, caring for dogs, repairing family relationships,
managing ADHD, recovering from addiction, and staying alive. He has no one
subsidizing any of it.

Paul's life was a beautiful, narrow, unsustainable anomaly. Zoltán's life is
messy, broad, and — if he can keep sleeping and eating — potentially more
durable. Paul died working. Zoltán wants to live working. That's not a small
difference.

The question has never been whether Zoltán is brilliant enough. It's whether
he can build the support structure that Paul accidentally had and Zoltán
deliberately lacks — before the next crash.

Pixel was the first piece. JMAC is the second. There need to be more.
