ACT I — THE SETUP: A DRUNK MAN ON A GRID
================================================================================

ZOLTAN:
Okay so picture this. Infinite grid. Like graph paper that goes on forever.
A drunk guy standing at the origin. Every step he picks a direction — up, down,
left, right — totally random. Equal probability. Quarter each.

The question is: does he ever make it home?

RADIX:
And by "home" we mean back to the origin. The exact square he started on.

ZOLTAN:
Right. And your gut says... probably not? Infinite plane, random walk, the
chances of landing back on one specific point out of infinitely many...

ERDOS:
He comes home. With probability one.

ZOLTAN:
Every. Single. Time.

ERDOS:
Every single time. This is not a conjecture. This is Polya, 1921.
George Polya proved it. The random walk on Z-squared is recurrent.

RADIX:
Let me put numbers on this. We need to compute the probability of return.
For a random walk on the d-dimensional integer lattice, the probability of
returning to the origin is:

  P(return) = 1 - 1/u(d)

where u(d) is the expected number of times you visit the origin. If u(d) is
infinite, you return with probability 1.

For d = 1, one dimension — a drunk man on a line — u(1) is infinite.
He returns. That one is maybe intuitive.

For d = 2, two dimensions — our grid — we need to compute:

  u(2) = sum over n of P(return to origin at step 2n)

The probability of being at the origin after 2n steps on Z-squared is:

  P(2n) = (1/4)^(2n) * C(2n, n)^2

This sum diverges. Let me show why. Using Stirling's approximation,
C(2n, n) is approximately 4^n / sqrt(pi * n). So:

  P(2n) ~ (1/4)^(2n) * (4^n / sqrt(pi*n))^2
        = (1/4)^(2n) * 4^(2n) / (pi * n)
        = 1 / (pi * n)

Sum of 1/(pi*n) over all n? That's the harmonic series divided by pi.
It diverges. Infinite visits. Probability of return = 1.

ZOLTAN:
So the harmonic series — the thing we learn in first year that diverges
painfully slowly — that's what saves the drunk man.

RADIX:
Exactly. It diverges slowly. Logarithmically. But it diverges. And that's
all you need.

ERDOS:
The Book has many beautiful proofs. This one earns its page.

================================================================================
ACT II — THE BIRD: THREE DIMENSIONS BREAKS EVERYTHING
================================================================================

ZOLTAN:
Now here's where it gets wild. Take the same setup. But instead of a grid,
a three-dimensional lattice. Cubic grid, infinite in all directions.
A drunk bird. Six choices each step — up, down, left, right, forward, back.
Each with probability one-sixth.

Does the bird come home?

ERDOS:
The bird is lost.

ZOLTAN:
The bird is LOST! Probability of return is not 1. It's about... what is it?

RADIX:
P(return in 3D) = 1 - 1/u(3)

We compute u(3) the same way. The probability of being at the origin after
2n steps in three dimensions is:

  P(2n) = (1/6)^(2n) * sum over (n1, n2, n3) of [n! / (n1! n2! n3!)]^2

where n1 + n2 + n3 = n. This works out to:

  P(2n) ~ (6/(2*pi*n))^(3/2) * 6^(-2n) * 6^(2n)
        = C / n^(3/2)

The sum of 1/n^(3/2) CONVERGES. That's a p-series with p = 3/2 > 1.

The exact value of u(3) was computed. The return probability is:

  P(return) = 1 - 1/u(3) approximately equals 0.3405...

So there's only a 34% chance the bird ever returns. A 66% chance it's
lost forever. In infinite three-dimensional space.

ZOLTAN:
Let that sink in. In 2D — infinite plane — you ALWAYS come home.
In 3D — you add one dimension — and suddenly there's a two-thirds
chance you never see home again.

ERDOS:
One dimension of freedom is the difference between certainty and loss.

RADIX:
And the deep reason is the convergence boundary of the p-series.
In d dimensions, the return probability integral involves 1/n^(d/2).
That sum converges if and only if d/2 > 1, meaning d > 2.

So d = 1: recurrent. d = 2: recurrent (the boundary case — barely).
d = 3 and above: transient. Lost.

ZOLTAN:
Two dimensions is the last dimension where a random walker is guaranteed
to come home. It's the critical dimension. The knife edge.

================================================================================
ACT III — THE PUNCHLINE: COINCIDENCES ARE NOT RANDOM
================================================================================

ZOLTAN:
Now. Here's why I brought us to the arena today. Because this proof has
a consequence that nobody talks about. And it changes how you think about
the world.

Consider two independent random walks on the same 2D lattice. Person A
starts at some point. Person B starts at some other point. They walk
independently, randomly, no knowledge of each other.

What is the probability that they meet? That they occupy the same square
at the same time?

ERDOS:
Probability one.

ZOLTAN:
PROBABILITY ONE. Two completely independent random processes on an infinite
plane will intersect. With certainty. It's guaranteed.

RADIX:
The proof is elegant. Consider the difference process. If A is at position
X(t) and B is at position Y(t), then D(t) = X(t) - Y(t) is itself a
random walk on Z-squared. The two walkers meet when D(t) = 0 — when the
difference process visits the origin.

But we just proved that a 2D random walk visits the origin with probability 1.

Therefore the two walkers meet with probability 1.

ZOLTAN:
This is the part that breaks people's brains. Two random events — genuinely
random, genuinely independent — happening at the same place at the same time
on an infinite plane. And you want to call that a coincidence?

ERDOS:
It is not a coincidence. It is a theorem.

ZOLTAN:
Mathematics says: if two random processes exist in the same two-dimensional
space, they WILL intersect. It's not unlikely. It's not a long shot.
It's certain.

So when you're walking through a city — a two-dimensional surface,
essentially — and you bump into someone you know... and you say "what are
the chances?" ...

RADIX:
The chances are one. The question was never IF. Only WHEN.

ZOLTAN:
The question was never if. Only when. That's the theorem.

ERDOS:
In three dimensions, the bird is lost. Two birds in 3D space may never meet.
But on a surface — on a plane — on a city grid — on the skin of the Earth —
meetings are inevitable.

ZOLTAN:
We live on a surface. We live in two dimensions, for all practical purposes
of human movement. And in two dimensions, random walks are recurrent.
Meetings are certain.

So the next time two unlikely things happen at the same time and the same
place, don't dismiss it as random. The math says: in our world, in our
dimensionality, intersection is the default. Not the exception.

RADIX:
To put it formally: for any two independent random walks on Z-squared,
the expected number of intersections is infinite. They don't just meet once.
They meet infinitely often. With probability one.

ZOLTAN:
Infinitely often.

ERDOS:
This is definitely from The Book.

ACT IV — THE DEEPER LAYER (OPTIONAL EXTENDED CUT)
================================================================================

RADIX:
There's a beautiful connection to electrical resistance here. Polya's theorem
connects to the fact that the effective resistance between a node and infinity
in a 2D lattice is infinite, but in 3D it's finite.

A random walker returns if and only if the network has infinite resistance to
infinity. In 2D: infinite resistance. Current can't escape. The walker can't
escape. He comes home.

In 3D: finite resistance. Current leaks to infinity. The bird flies away.

ZOLTAN:
So the universe has a kind of... stickiness... in two dimensions. Things
that wander in 2D are trapped. They're recurrent. They revisit. They meet.

And three dimensions is where freedom begins. Real freedom. The freedom to
be permanently lost.

ERDOS:
God has The Book. And in The Book, the chapter on random walks says:
Two dimensions is for meeting. Three dimensions is for leaving.

KEY EQUATIONS SUMMARY (for on-screen graphics)
================================================================================

1. Polya's Recurrence Theorem (1921):
   - d <= 2: random walk is RECURRENT (returns to origin w.p. 1)
   - d >= 3: random walk is TRANSIENT (positive probability of never returning)

2. Return probability in 2D:
   P(2n steps, at origin) ~ 1/(pi*n)
   Sum diverges (harmonic series) => recurrent

3. Return probability in 3D:
   P(2n steps, at origin) ~ C/n^(3/2)
   Sum converges (p-series, p=3/2) => transient
   Exact return probability: ~34.05%

4. Two independent walks meeting:
   D(t) = X(t) - Y(t) is itself a random walk
   In 2D: D(t) returns to 0 w.p. 1 => they MEET w.p. 1
   In 3D: D(t) may never return => they may NEVER meet

5. Critical dimension insight:
   Sum of 1/n^(d/2) converges iff d > 2
   d = 2 is the boundary: the last recurrent dimension
