Hungary 2030: Three Scenarios for a Country Running Out of Road

Zoltan Erdos
April 2026


Preface: Why This Is Painful to Write

I left Hungary. I am writing this from Brighton, where I build software
and walk my dogs along the seafront and do not have to think about
Hungarian politics unless I choose to. I am choosing to now because
someone asked, and because looking away is also a choice, and because
four years is long enough for things to get either meaningfully better
or irreversibly worse, and I no longer trust my instinct for which.

What follows is not prediction. It is scenario analysis -- three
plausible futures for Hungary by 2030, built on what we know as of
spring 2026. I have tried to be honest rather than hopeful. Hope is
easy from abroad.


1. Where We Stand: Spring 2026

Viktor Orban has been Prime Minister since 2010. Fidesz holds a
constitutional supermajority for the fourth consecutive term. The 2026
spring elections -- the most contested in a decade -- produced a result
that landed somewhere between the opposition's desperate hopes and the
regime's confident projections.

The key dynamics entering the 2026 election cycle:

Magyar Peter and TISZA. The most significant opposition figure to emerge
since 2010. A former Fidesz insider who broke with the regime in 2024,
Magyar built TISZA (Tisztelet es Szabadsag -- Respect and Freedom) into
a genuine electoral force within eighteen months. His European Parliament
result in June 2024 -- nearly 30% on a standing start -- was the first
credible challenge to Fidesz's dominance in a decade. Whether TISZA
could translate European election momentum into a national result was
the central question of 2025-2026.

The economy. Hungary's GDP growth has been anaemic. Inflation, which
peaked above 25% in early 2023, came down but left permanent damage to
household purchasing power. The forint remains structurally weak. EU
cohesion funds -- tens of billions of euros -- remained partially frozen
over rule-of-law conditionality. The brain drain continues: an estimated
600,000-800,000 Hungarians live abroad, disproportionately young,
educated, and productive. The demographic arithmetic is brutal: Hungary
is losing the people it needs most to sustain the economy it has.

The media landscape. Fidesz controls or influences approximately 80% of
Hungarian media. The Central European Press and Media Foundation (KESMA)
-- a Fidesz-aligned media conglomerate declared a matter of "national
strategic importance" to exempt it from competition scrutiny -- dominates
regional media. Independent outlets survive but reach a self-selecting
audience. The information asymmetry between urban and rural Hungary is
not a bug; it is the architecture.

The EU relationship. Hungary under Orban has been the EU's most
persistent internal disrupter: vetoing Ukraine aid, blocking sanctions,
courting Moscow and Beijing, holding the 2024 Council presidency while
freelancing a "peace mission" to Kyiv and Moscow that infuriated every
other member state. The EU's leverage -- frozen funds, rule-of-law
mechanisms, potential voting-rights suspension under Article 7 --
remains powerful in theory and hesitant in practice.

The constitutional lock. Fidesz rewrote the constitution (Fundamental
Law) in 2011 and has amended it repeatedly since. The electoral system
is gerrymandered. The Constitutional Court is packed. The prosecution
service is controlled. The state audit office targets opposition
parties. Media regulation favours regime-aligned outlets. These are not
features of a normal democracy experiencing a right-wing government.
They are features of a hybrid regime that has restructured the state to
make power transfer extraordinarily difficult even if the electorate
turns.

This is the machine any future has to pass through.


2. Scenario A: Managed Stagnation (Most Likely)

Probability estimate: 50%

In this scenario, the 2026 elections produce a result close to the
structural expectation: Fidesz loses vote share but retains a
parliamentary majority, possibly losing the supermajority. TISZA and
allied opposition parties make significant gains but fall short of
government. The opposition declares moral victory. Fidesz declares
actual victory. Nothing fundamental changes.

By 2030 under this scenario:

Politics. Orban, now 67, remains Prime Minister. Succession speculation
intensifies but produces no resolution. Fidesz's internal structure --
a patronage network organised around personal loyalty to Orban rather
than institutional party governance -- makes succession inherently
destabilising. The party cannot easily replace its central node without
the network fragmenting. Orban's strategy is to make this everyone
else's problem by not retiring.

The opposition consolidates around TISZA as the primary vehicle but
remains structurally disadvantaged by the electoral system, media
landscape, and campaign finance asymmetry. Opposition support stabilises
at 35-42% of the national vote -- enough to sustain hope, not enough to
win power under the current rules. The democratic opposition faces the
classic authoritarian-competitive trap: participating in elections
legitimises a system designed to produce predetermined outcomes, but
boycotting elections abandons the only remaining institutional pathway
to power.

Economy. GDP growth remains below the EU average. The EU partially
releases frozen funds under a face-saving compromise (Hungary makes
cosmetic rule-of-law concessions; the Commission declares progress;
everyone knows it is theatre). The funds flow primarily through
Fidesz-connected contractors and municipal governments, reinforcing the
patronage network. The brain drain does not reverse. Wages in healthcare,
education, and public administration remain uncompetitive with Western
Europe. The demographic crisis deepens: Hungary's population, already
below 9.6 million, continues to decline.

Society. The culture war -- anti-LGBTQ legislation, "gender ideology"
panic, anti-immigration rhetoric, "sovereignty protection" -- continues
to serve its primary political function: keeping the base mobilised and
the opposition divided between liberals who prioritise rights and
pragmatists who want to talk about the economy. Hungarian society
becomes incrementally more atomised: low trust, low civic participation,
high emigration intent among the young.

EU relationship. Hungary remains inside the EU but operates as a
permanent spoiler, extracting concessions by threatening vetoes on
matters requiring unanimity. The EU's institutional inability to expel
or effectively discipline a member state becomes a chronic governance
problem. Hungary is not the only beneficiary of this weakness (Poland's
trajectory, Italy's Meloni government, the rise of hard-right parties
across the continent all exploit the same structural gap), but it is the
most systematic.

This scenario is depressing not because it is catastrophic but because
it is sustainable. The system is not in crisis. It is in equilibrium.
The equilibrium is simply unjust, undemocratic, and economically
suboptimal. Managed stagnation can continue for a long time. Ask any
Hungarian who lived through the Kadar era.


3. Scenario B: Democratic Breakthrough (Possible but Fragile)

Probability estimate: 25%

In this scenario, the 2026 elections produce a narrower-than-expected
Fidesz result -- perhaps a simple majority of 2-5 seats, or an outright
opposition victory through an electoral alliance. Alternatively, a
post-election crisis (economic shock, corruption scandal reaching
Orban's inner circle, a foreign policy humiliation) fragments Fidesz
sufficiently to force early elections or a coalition realignment.

By 2030 under this scenario:

Politics. A non-Fidesz government takes office, either after 2026 or in
a subsequent early election. The immediate challenge is governing within
institutions that Fidesz spent 16 years reshaping. The Constitutional
Court, prosecution service, state audit office, media authority, and
central bank are all staffed with Fidesz loyalists whose terms extend
well beyond any single electoral cycle. A new government faces the same
structural problem Poland's Tusk government faced after 2023: winning
the election is the easy part; unwinding institutional capture is the
decade-long part.

The new government is a coalition of convenience -- TISZA, remnants of
the traditional opposition (DK, Momentum, or their successors), and
possibly defectors from Fidesz. It is ideologically incoherent, united
only by opposition to Orban. Internal tensions between liberal
internationalists and national-conservative reformers surface within
months. Governing is harder than opposing. The coalition's approval
rating drops rapidly as it inherits an economy distorted by 16 years of
crony capitalism, a public administration hollowed out by political
loyalty requirements, and a population exhausted by politics.

Economy. EU funds are fully released, providing a short-term stimulus.
But the structural problems -- low productivity, dependence on
automotive manufacturing (increasingly exposed to Chinese EV
competition), underinvestment in education and healthcare, demographic
decline -- require reforms that take longer than one electoral cycle to
produce results. The government must choose between popular short-term
spending and unpopular structural reform. History suggests it will
choose spending.

Society. A democratic breakthrough produces a brief euphoria followed
by disappointment as the depth of institutional damage becomes visible.
The Hungarian diaspora watches with cautious hope. Some return. Most do
not -- they have built lives elsewhere, and "the country is changing"
is a less compelling reason to uproot than "the country has changed."
The cultural divide between urban liberals and rural conservatives does
not dissolve with a change of government; it may sharpen as the losing
side feels newly threatened.

Risk. Fidesz in opposition is a formidable force. It retains media
dominance, municipal power bases, and the institutional loyalists
embedded throughout the state. It campaigns on the theme that the new
government is destroying Hungary's sovereignty, caving to Brussels, and
betraying national values. If the new government stumbles -- and it
will -- Fidesz returns in the next election with a narrative of
restoration. The democratic breakthrough becomes an interregnum rather
than a transition.

This scenario is not impossible. The Polish precedent shows that
competitive authoritarian regimes can lose elections. But Poland's
opposition had stronger institutional foundations, a less gerrymandered
electoral system, and the galvanising effect of the abortion ban. Hungary
would need a comparable catalyst and a more disciplined opposition
coalition than it has yet demonstrated.


4. Scenario C: Authoritarian Consolidation (Unlikely but Not Impossible)

Probability estimate: 15%

In this scenario, Fidesz uses its supermajority to further entrench its
position: additional constitutional amendments, further media
consolidation, restrictions on civil society funding, expanded
surveillance under "sovereignty protection" legislation, and possible
withdrawal from or effective nullification of EU judicial oversight. The
2026 election result is comfortable enough to embolden further
institutional capture.

By 2030 under this scenario:

Politics. Hungary moves from competitive authoritarianism (elections
happen but are structurally unfair) toward hegemonic authoritarianism
(elections happen but outcomes are effectively predetermined). The
distinction matters: in competitive authoritarian regimes, the
opposition can still win; in hegemonic regimes, it cannot. The key
indicators would be: prosecution of opposition figures on politically
motivated charges, restrictions on independent media funding, mandatory
"transparency" requirements for civil society that function as
harassment, and electoral law changes that further disadvantage the
opposition.

Orban's model has always been Kadar's "goulash communism" updated for
the 21st century: material comfort in exchange for political quiescence,
national pride as a substitute for democratic participation, and a
controlled public sphere that makes alternatives invisible. The
question is whether this model is sustainable in a digitally connected,
EU-member state where the population can leave freely.

Economy. The authoritarian consolidation scenario paradoxically requires
economic performance. If living standards decline, the regime loses its
implicit social contract (stability and modest prosperity in exchange
for democratic concessions). The risk is that corruption, which is
structural rather than incidental in the Fidesz system, degrades
economic performance to the point where the contract breaks. But
corruption also distributes rents to a large enough coalition of
beneficiaries that many actors have a material interest in the regime's
survival. The system is corrupt and stable, until it isn't.

Brain drain. This scenario accelerates emigration. By 2030, the
Hungarian diaspora in Western Europe could exceed one million -- a
staggering figure for a country of under ten million. The demographic
consequences compound: fewer young workers, higher dependency ratios,
lower tax revenue, declining public services, more emigration. This is
the doom loop that no amount of pro-natalist policy (baby bonds,
mortgage subsidies, IVF funding) can reverse, because the push factors
are not primarily economic. They are about whether young Hungarians see
a future worth staying for.

EU. The EU's tolerance for internal democratic backsliding has limits
that have not yet been reached. An Article 7 procedure, which could
suspend Hungary's voting rights, requires unanimity among all other
member states -- a threshold that has never been met. But financial
conditionality is already being applied, and further authoritarian moves
could trigger harsher measures: suspension from EU programmes,
exclusion from Schengen, or informal marginalisation in Council
decision-making. The ultimate question -- whether Hungary could be
effectively expelled or choose to leave -- is one that neither side has
an interest in forcing, but that authoritarian consolidation brings
closer to the table.

I give this scenario only 15% because it requires Fidesz to take risks
that managed stagnation does not require. The regime's genius has been
to achieve authoritarian outcomes without authoritarian aesthetics: it
looks democratic enough to avoid the costs of overt repression. Moving
beyond competitive authoritarianism would sacrifice that deniability for
marginal additional control. Orban is many things, but he is not stupid.


5. The Variables That Matter Most

Across all three scenarios, four variables carry disproportionate weight.

Orban's health and succession. The system is built around one person.
There is no institutional mechanism for succession. There is no
designated heir. There is no Fidesz without Orban, only a collection of
patronage networks that Orban holds together. Any scenario involving
Orban's departure -- health, retirement, scandal, fatigue -- triggers a
succession crisis that could produce outcomes none of the three
scenarios anticipate.

The EU's willingness to enforce. The EU has the tools to discipline
Hungary. It has not consistently used them. If the EU hardens its
approach -- genuinely conditioning all structural funds on democratic
standards, using the rule-of-law conditionality regulation aggressively,
building coalitions to isolate Hungary in the Council -- the cost of
managed stagnation rises. If the EU continues to compromise, managed
stagnation remains cheap.

Economic shocks. The Hungarian economy is unusually exposed to
automotive manufacturing (German supply chains), energy dependence
(historically on Russian gas, now diversifying but slowly), and
demographic decline. A European recession, an energy crisis, or a
collapse in automotive demand (driven by Chinese EV competition) could
destabilise the social contract that sustains the regime. Economic
crises do not automatically produce democratic outcomes -- they can just
as easily produce authoritarian deepening -- but they force change.

The opposition's institutional capacity. Magyar Peter proved in 2024
that a single charismatic figure can galvanise opposition support. But
charismatic leadership is not institutional capacity. TISZA in 2026
faces the same challenge every Hungarian opposition party has faced
since 2010: building durable organisations -- with local branches,
trained candidates, fundraising infrastructure, media capacity, and
policy development -- under conditions deliberately designed to prevent
exactly that. The question is whether TISZA is a party or a person. If
it is a person, its half-life is one electoral cycle.


6. What the Diaspora Can and Cannot Do

There are approximately 600,000-800,000 Hungarians living abroad.
Diaspora voting rights exist but are structurally disadvantaged: postal
voting is available to ethnic Hungarians in neighbouring countries
(Fidesz voters, predominantly) but citizens living in the EU must vote
at embassies in person. The system is designed to enfranchise the
diaspora that votes Fidesz and disenfranchise the diaspora that does
not.

Beyond voting, the diaspora's influence is ambiguous. Remittances
support family members. Professional networks create opportunities.
Cultural ties persist. But the act of leaving is also an act of exit
in Hirschman's sense: choosing exit over voice reduces the domestic
pressure for change. Every young Hungarian who moves to London, Berlin,
or Brighton is one fewer voter, one fewer taxpayer, one fewer voice in
domestic politics. The regime benefits from this safety valve. If the
dissatisfied cannot leave, they must organise. If they can leave, many
do -- and the ones who remain are disproportionately those who benefit
from, or are resigned to, the status quo.

I am part of this equation. I left. I build things in Brighton. I write
essays about Hungarian politics from a comfortable distance. This is
not a neutral position. It is a choice that makes me simultaneously
more free to speak honestly and less entitled to prescribe solutions
for people who stayed.


7. What I Actually Think

The honest answer is that I expect managed stagnation. Not because it
is the best outcome, but because it is the most structurally stable.
The regime is not collapsing. The opposition is not strong enough to
win under the current rules. The EU is not willing to force the issue.
The economy is bad enough to produce emigration but not bad enough to
produce revolt. The cultural infrastructure of democratic citizenship
-- independent media, civic organisations, institutional trust, a
shared commitment to procedural fairness -- has been systematically
degraded over 16 years. Rebuilding it takes longer than destroying it
took.

Hungary in 2030 will most likely look like Hungary in 2026, only more
so: a little poorer relative to its neighbours, a little smaller in
population, a little more captured institutionally, a little more
isolated within the EU, a little more resigned. The people who could
change this -- the young, the educated, the energetic -- will
disproportionately be living somewhere else.

This is not despair. Despair would be saying it cannot change. It can.
Poland changed. Slovakia wobbles. Romania surprises. The Central
European story is not one-directional. But the window for change in
Hungary is narrower than in 2010, and narrowing.

The thing I keep coming back to is something Istvan Bibo wrote in 1948,
in "The Misery of the Small States of Eastern Europe": that the
fundamental political pathology of the region is the fear of the
community's extinction -- a fear that makes democratic self-governance
feel like an unaffordable luxury, and strongman rule feel like
protection. Orban understands this fear better than his opponents do.
He does not need to be loved. He needs to be seen as necessary. And in
a country that has been invaded, partitioned, occupied, and betrayed by
every major power in its neighbourhood over the past three centuries,
the argument that Hungary needs a strong hand at the helm resonates at
a level that liberal universalism cannot easily reach.

The opposition's task is not to defeat Orban. It is to offer a
different answer to the same fear. One that says: we can be safe and
free. We can be Hungarian and European. We can be proud and democratic.
We can be small and sovereign without being captured by a single party
that has confused the nation with itself.

That is a harder story to tell than "Brussels wants to destroy us."
But it is the only story that leads somewhere worth going.


Note on Sources

This is a scenario analysis, not an academic paper. It draws on publicly
available reporting, EU institutional documents, Hungarian election data,
and the author's own experience as a Hungarian citizen living abroad.
Specific claims about media ownership, electoral system design, and EU
fund conditionality are well-documented in Freedom House reports,
Transparency International Hungary publications, the European
Commission's annual Rule of Law Reports, and the academic work of
Balint Magyar (The Post-Communist Mafia State, 2016) and Gabor Scheiring
(The Retreat of Liberal Democracy, 2020).
