Community Energy Projects in the United Kingdom:
A Systems-Thinking Analysis of Energy Justice in Brighton

Zoltan Erdos
April 2026

Abstract

This essay examines the role of community energy projects in advancing
energy justice across the United Kingdom, with particular focus on
Brighton and Hove as a site of emerging practice. Drawing on the
three-tenet framework of energy justice (Jenkins et al., 2016), Donella
Meadows' leverage points theory (1999), and Frank Geels' multi-level
perspective on socio-technical transitions (2002), the essay argues that
community-led energy initiatives operate at uniquely high leverage
points within the UK energy system -- challenging incumbent paradigms,
redistributing benefits to marginalised communities, and creating
procedural pathways for democratic participation. A case study of Radix
Brighton, a hyperlocal circular-economy platform connecting food waste
collection to biogas energy production, illustrates how systems thinking
can integrate waste policy, distributed energy generation, and community
engagement into a single intervention. The essay situates these
developments within the policy landscape of 2025-2026, including the
Great British Energy Act 2025, the Local Power Plan, and the Simpler
Recycling mandate of 31 March 2026.


1. Introduction

The United Kingdom's energy system stands at a critical juncture. On one
hand, the country has committed to achieving net zero greenhouse gas
emissions by 2050 and, more ambitiously, to decarbonising its
electricity grid by 2030 through the Clean Power Mission. On the other
hand, 2.73 million English households remain in fuel poverty under the
Low Income Low Energy Efficiency (LILEE) measure, with an aggregate fuel
poverty gap of GBP 1.11 billion per year (DESNZ, 2025). The energy
transition, if left to incumbent market actors alone, risks reproducing
and deepening existing inequalities -- a phenomenon the emerging
scholarship of energy justice seeks to identify, critique, and redress.

Community energy projects represent a distinctive intervention in this
landscape. The UK hosts approximately 614 community energy organisations
generating 575 GWh of electricity annually from 411 MW of
community-owned renewable capacity -- enough to power 213,000 households
(Community Energy England, 2025). Yet these figures, while significant,
understate the transformative potential of community energy, which
operates not merely as a marginal supplement to the national grid but as
a fundamentally different model of energy governance: democratic,
distributed, and oriented toward local benefit.

This essay employs a systems-thinking approach to analyse how community
energy projects advance energy justice in the UK, with Brighton and Hove
as a primary site of investigation. Section 2 establishes the
theoretical framework. Section 3 surveys the UK community energy
landscape and policy context. Section 4 examines Brighton's distinctive
position. Section 5 presents Radix Brighton as a case study in
systems-level design. Section 6 discusses implications and limitations.


2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 Energy Justice: The Three Tenets

The foundational framework for energy justice was established by Jenkins,
McCauley, Heffron, Stephan, and Rehner (2016) as "a new crosscutting
social science research agenda which seeks to apply justice principles to
energy policy, energy production and systems, energy consumption, energy
activism, energy security and climate change" (p. 175). The framework
identifies three core tenets:

Distributional justice concerns the fair allocation of both the burdens
(pollution, health impacts, infrastructure proximity, cost) and benefits
(affordable energy, revenue, employment) of the energy system across
society. In the UK, distributional injustice is starkly visible: 55% of
social housing with Energy Performance Certificate ratings of D to G is
fuel poor, while under the broader 10% income threshold measure, 36.3%
of households -- 8.99 million -- spend more than a tenth of their income
on energy (DESNZ, 2025). Scotland's figures are starker still: 34% of
households are fuel poor, rising to 44% in remote rural areas (Scottish
House Condition Survey, 2023).

Recognition justice requires that the diverse identities, needs, and
vulnerabilities of different social groups be acknowledged and respected
in energy policy. This tenet demands more than formal inclusion; it
requires substantive acknowledgment of difference and historical
disadvantage. In the UK context, recognition justice implicates ethnic
minority communities disproportionately affected by fuel poverty,
tenants in social housing with no control over their building's energy
efficiency, disabled people with higher energy needs, and rural
households facing both higher costs and lower infrastructure investment.

Procedural justice addresses fairness in decision-making: who
participates in energy governance, whose voice carries weight, and
whether processes are transparent, inclusive, and accountable. The UK
energy system has historically been characterised by profound procedural
injustice, with planning decisions for energy infrastructure routinely
excluding affected communities and national energy policy designed
without meaningful public consultation.

Sovacool and Dworkin (2015) extend these three tenets to encompass
availability, affordability, due process, transparency, sustainability,
intergenerational equity, and responsibility, connecting energy justice
to broader human rights frameworks. Heffron, McCauley, and de Rubens
(2018) further propose a whole-systems approach covering the full energy
life-cycle and introduce the Energy Justice Metric as a quantitative
tool. Critically, Heffron and McCauley advocate adding restorative
justice as a fourth tenet -- the obligation to remedy past injustices
rather than merely prevent future ones.

2.2 Systems Thinking: Leverage Points and Socio-Technical Transitions

Donella Meadows' hierarchy of leverage points (1999; 2008) provides a
framework for understanding where interventions in complex systems are
most effective. Her twelve-point scale ranges from low-leverage
interventions (adjusting numerical parameters such as subsidies or tax
rates) to high-leverage interventions (changing the goals, mindsets, and
paradigms that organise the system). Meadows cautions that policymakers
overwhelmingly target low-leverage points -- adjusting numbers within
existing structures -- while neglecting the deeper structural, cultural,
and paradigmatic changes that produce lasting transformation.

For the energy system, this hierarchy is illuminating. Carbon pricing
(a numerical parameter, leverage point 12) has dominated policy debate
for decades, yet has not prevented continued fossil fuel expansion.
Feed-in tariffs (adjusting reinforcing feedback loops, leverage point 7)
were more effective at stimulating renewable deployment but still
operated within the existing utility-dominated paradigm. Community energy
projects, by contrast, operate at leverage points 2 through 6: they
challenge the paradigm that energy production is the exclusive domain of
private utilities (point 2), shift the goals of local energy systems
from profit maximisation to community benefit (point 3), enable
self-organisation among citizens as energy producers (point 4), change
the rules of who can own and benefit from energy infrastructure (point
5), and create new information flows that make energy data visible and
locally meaningful (point 6).

Frank Geels' multi-level perspective (MLP) on socio-technical
transitions (2002; 2014; 2016) provides a complementary analytical lens.
The MLP describes transitions as occurring across three levels: the
niche (protected spaces for radical innovations), the regime (the
dominant socio-technical system of incumbent utilities, regulatory
frameworks, and fossil fuel infrastructure), and the landscape
(exogenous macro trends such as the climate crisis, geopolitical energy
insecurity, and decarbonisation commitments). Community energy
organisations function as niche actors, demonstrating that distributed,
citizen-owned renewable energy is technically and financially viable.
Their role in MLP terms is to develop and stabilise innovations that
can, under favourable landscape pressures, destabilise the fossil fuel
regime.

Geels' comparative analysis of the German and UK low-carbon electricity
transitions (Geels et al., 2016) is particularly instructive. Germany
followed a substitution pathway in which new entrants -- including
thousands of energy cooperatives -- drove the deployment of small-scale
renewables. The UK, by contrast, followed a transformation pathway in
which incumbents deployed large-scale renewables under government
direction. This structural bias toward incumbents in the UK explains the
comparative underdevelopment of British community energy and the
significance of the policy shifts now underway.


3. The UK Community Energy Landscape

3.1 Scale and Growth

The community energy sector in the UK has reached meaningful scale. The
State of the Sector 2025 report documents 614 active organisations with
411 MW of community-owned renewable capacity, generating 575 GWh
annually (Community Energy England, 2025). The sector added 22 MW of new
capacity and created 88 full-time equivalent jobs in 2024, with
organisational turnover rising 58% year-on-year. These figures represent
growth despite the withdrawal of the Feed-in Tariff in 2019, which
removed the primary financial mechanism that had supported community
energy development.

Three exemplary organisations illustrate the sector's diversity and
impact:

Brighton Energy Co-operative (BEC), founded in 2010, has raised over
GBP 5 million in community investment, installed 4,844 kWp of solar
capacity, generated nearly 22,000 MWh of clean electricity, and avoided
over 4,500 tonnes of CO2 emissions. Operating as a Community Benefit
Society, BEC runs 13 Solar Schools in its environmental education
programme and has deployed the South of England's first community-owned
solar-connected electric vehicle charger.

Repowering London operates across nine London boroughs, has delivered
over 1.5 MWp of rooftop solar with 650 member investors, and ringfences
GBP 200,000 for local community projects. Repowering explicitly targets
underrepresented communities in low-income London boroughs, diversifying
cooperative membership and addressing recognition justice directly.

Bristol Energy Cooperative is responsible for 33% of solar energy in
Bristol, operating a 9 MW portfolio powering 3,000 homes. The adjacent
Lawrence Weston wind turbine -- a 4.2 MW, GBP 4 million community
project -- returns GBP 100,000 per year to one of Bristol's most
deprived neighbourhoods, demonstrating the direct link between community
energy and distributional justice.

3.2 Policy Context: 2025-2026

The policy environment for community energy has shifted substantially in
the past eighteen months. The Great British Energy Act 2025, which
received Royal Assent on 15 May 2025, establishes Great British Energy
(GBE) as a publicly owned clean energy company with an explicit mandate
to support local and community energy growth. Following sustained
lobbying by Community Energy England, the Act was amended to include
community energy explicitly in GBE's statutory remit.

In February 2026, GBE published the Local Power Plan, the most
significant policy instrument for community energy in a decade. The Plan
commits up to GBP 1 billion in investment, targets the support of over
1,000 community energy projects by 2030, and identifies approximately
1.9 GW of projects already in the development pipeline. Specific
near-term milestones include the launch of first grant schemes in spring
2026, shared ownership template documents, and a GBE Capital Toolkit
portfolio by autumn 2026. The Plan specifically targets underserved
areas and communities with historically low uptake.

Alongside these energy-specific policies, the Simpler Recycling
mandate -- which took effect on 31 March 2026 -- creates new
intersections between waste policy and energy production. By requiring
all English local authorities to implement weekly separate food waste
collections, the mandate channels millions of additional tonnes of
organic waste toward anaerobic digestion facilities, producing biogas
for local electricity generation. The UK biogas sector already processes
36 million tonnes of organic waste annually, generating 21 TWh of
biogas, supporting 4,800 jobs, and delivering a 1% reduction in
national greenhouse gas emissions (ADBA/Tolvik, 2025). Food waste
anaerobic digestion capacity is projected to power the equivalent of
163,000 homes by 2027.

3.3 Barriers and Regime Resistance

Despite favourable policy signals, significant barriers persist. The
withdrawal of the Feed-in Tariff in 2019 without adequate replacement
removed the sector's primary revenue mechanism. The Local Electricity
Bill, which would allow small-scale generators to sell directly to local
consumers without disproportionate licensing costs, has not yet passed
into law, although a related Community Energy (Review) Bill was
introduced in the 2024-26 parliamentary session. Planning restrictions
on onshore wind, only partially lifted in 2024, continue to constrain
development. Access to commercial-scale finance remains difficult for
smaller community organisations.

In systems terms, these barriers represent what Geels (2014) calls
regime resistance: incumbent actors using political and regulatory power
to slow niche innovations. The UK's historical transformation pathway --
privileging large incumbents deploying utility-scale renewables under
government direction -- has structurally disadvantaged community-scale
actors. The Local Power Plan explicitly acknowledges this imbalance and
seeks to redress it, but the translation of policy intent into
regulatory reality remains incomplete.


4. Brighton and Hove: A Site of Emerging Practice

Brighton and Hove occupies a distinctive position in the UK's community
energy and sustainability landscape. Several converging factors make the
city an unusually fertile site for energy justice innovation.

4.1 Political and Cultural Context

Brighton and Hove elected the UK's first Green-led council in 2011,
establishing sustainability not as an upsell but as a baseline
expectation of local governance. The council declared a Climate and
Biodiversity Emergency in December 2018 and adopted a 2030 Carbon
Neutral Programme following the city's first Climate Assembly in 2021.
The Tyndall Centre for Climate Change has calculated that the city must
reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 12.7% annually from 2020 to remain
within its carbon budget. Over GBP 7.1 million of sustainability and
carbon reduction funds have been allocated to this programme.

This political context creates what systems theorists would recognise as
a favourable regime environment: local government is aligned with, rather
than resistant to, niche energy innovations. The Green council's
orientation toward community participation and environmental governance
reduces the procedural barriers that community energy projects face
elsewhere.

4.2 Community Energy Ecosystem

Brighton hosts multiple community energy organisations operating in
complementary domains. Brighton Energy Co-operative (BEC) focuses on
renewable generation, primarily rooftop solar, with over GBP 5 million
raised and 4,844 kWp installed. Brighton and Hove Energy Services
Co-operative (BHESCo) focuses on energy efficiency retrofit and
financing mechanisms for fuel-poor households. The coexistence of
generation-focused and efficiency-focused cooperatives within a single
city demonstrates the systemic nature of community energy: addressing
energy justice requires interventions at multiple points in the energy
value chain simultaneously.

4.3 Demographics and Density

Brighton's compact urban form creates efficient collection routes and
high community engagement density -- a critical factor for any
initiative that relies on neighbourhood-level participation. The city's
34,000-plus university students (University of Sussex and University of
Brighton) represent a cohort of early adopters who are socially
motivated and digitally native. The tourism economy, with 11 million
annual visitors, means that visible sustainability initiatives carry
brand value for local businesses, creating economic incentives for
participation.

4.4 Food Waste Infrastructure

Brighton and Hove City Council received GBP 3,122,659.37 in Defra
grants to support the Simpler Recycling rollout and was among the
councils ready for the 31 March 2026 deadline, already collecting weekly
food waste. This positions the city ahead of the one-in-four councils
that missed the deadline, and ahead of the more than one-third of
English councils that had not yet begun food waste collection even on a
transitional basis. The existing infrastructure eliminates the cold-start
problem that impedes new circular economy initiatives elsewhere.


5. Case Study: Radix Brighton

5.1 Overview

Radix Brighton is a hyperlocal circular-economy platform designed to sit
on top of Brighton's existing food waste collection infrastructure,
adding a digital layer of tracking, community engagement, and local
economic value. The platform connects the new regulatory requirement for
food waste separation to the broader goals of distributed energy
production, carbon reduction, and community economic development. As a
case study, Radix illustrates how systems thinking can integrate
multiple policy streams -- waste regulation, energy transition, and
community development -- into a single coherent intervention.

5.2 The Systems Architecture

Radix's design reflects a deliberate application of systems-thinking
principles. The platform's core loop operates as a reinforcing feedback
mechanism (Meadows' leverage point 7): residents log food waste,
receiving immediate visibility into the energy equivalent of their
contribution ("your peelings powered a fridge for 18 hours," using
WRAP-verified conversion factors where one kitchen caddy of food waste
equals approximately 18 hours of fridge power via biogas). This
information flow (leverage point 6) transforms an invisible civic act --
putting food scraps in a caddy -- into a visible, quantified
contribution to the neighbourhood's energy production.

The neighbourhood leaderboard introduces a balancing feedback loop
(leverage point 8): as participation rises in one area, neighbouring
wards are motivated to match or exceed it, creating a competitive
dynamic that stabilises at high participation rates rather than decaying
as novelty fades. The local rewards system -- recycling credits
redeemable at Brighton's independent businesses -- closes the economic
loop, ensuring that value generated by household waste diversion
circulates within the local economy rather than being extracted by
distant utility companies.

At a deeper level, Radix operates at leverage points 3 and 4: it
changes the goals of the local waste-energy system from mere compliance
(meeting the Simpler Recycling mandate) to community empowerment and
local economic development; and it enables self-organisation, as
neighbourhoods form organic competitive and collaborative relationships
around waste reduction without top-down coordination.

5.3 The Five Product Pillars

Radix is structured around five product pillars, each addressing a
distinct dimension of the systems challenge:

First, the waste tracker allows residents to scan and log weekly caddy
contents and see the energy-equivalent output. This addresses the
information asymmetry that characterises most waste systems: residents
currently have no visibility into what happens to their food waste or
what value it generates.

Second, the impact dashboard provides neighbourhood-level data on biogas
production, landfill diversion, and CO2 reduction. By aggregating
individual contributions into collective impact metrics, the dashboard
makes the systems-level effects of household behaviour visible and
tangible.

Third, the community leaderboard enables streets and wards to compete on
diversion rates. This gamification layer operates as a social
reinforcing feedback loop, leveraging Brighton's strong neighbourhood
identities and civic pride.

Fourth, the local rewards system enables recycling credits to be
redeemed at Brighton's independent businesses -- cafes, shops, and
market stalls. This creates a direct economic link between waste
diversion and local commerce, aligning the interests of households,
businesses, and the council.

Fifth, route intelligence uses participation data to optimise collection
routes, offered to the council as a software-as-a-service product. This
operational efficiency layer generates revenue while reducing the
council's costs and carbon emissions from collection vehicles.

5.4 Revenue Model and Sustainability

Radix projects revenue of GBP 100,000 in Year 1, rising to GBP 280,000
in Year 2 and GBP 520,000 in Year 3, drawn from four streams:
business-to-government SaaS (council dashboard and route optimisation),
business-to-business rewards network (local business listing fees at
GBP 25 per month), sponsored challenges (brand-funded community
recycling drives), and data licensing (anonymised waste composition
insights to WRAP and Defra). The unit economics target 15,000 households
on the platform by Year 2, with 6,000 active weekly loggers (40%
retention) and 300 local businesses in the rewards network, at a
customer acquisition cost of GBP 2 per household.

The Year 1 cost structure of GBP 33,000 is notable for its leanness: by
building on an existing platform (spike.land's MCP infrastructure for
authentication, payments, and analytics), Radix avoids the engineering
costs that typically dominate early-stage technology ventures. The
primary cost is human: a part-time Brighton-based community manager at
GBP 18,000 per year, reflecting the platform's recognition that
community energy is fundamentally a coordination problem, not a
technology problem.

5.5 Energy Justice Analysis

Analysed through the three-tenet framework of energy justice, Radix
addresses each dimension:

Distributional justice: By channelling the economic value of food waste
diversion toward local independent businesses and providing quantified
energy-equivalent feedback to households, Radix ensures that the
benefits of the circular economy are distributed locally rather than
captured by centralised waste processing corporations. The target of
GBP 150,000 in revenue flowing to Brighton independents via rewards by
Year 2 represents a direct redistribution of value from the waste stream
to the local economy. Furthermore, by optimising collection routes,
Radix reduces the operational costs borne by the council -- costs
ultimately funded by council tax, which falls disproportionately on
lower-income households.

Recognition justice: The platform's neighbourhood-level design
recognises that Brighton's communities are not homogeneous. The pilot
phase targets three wards -- Hanover, Kemptown, and Preston Park --
selected for their high civic engagement and dense community networks.
However, the systems design anticipates expansion to all wards,
including those with lower engagement and higher deprivation, where the
information flows and economic incentives may prove most impactful. The
gamification structure is designed to be culturally accessible, avoiding
the assumption that all residents are motivated by the same factors.

Procedural justice: Radix is structured as a platform that operates
with council cooperation but is not controlled by the council, creating
a governance space in which residents have agency as participants rather
than merely as service recipients. The community leaderboard and reward
redemption mechanisms give residents visible, quantified evidence of
their collective impact, providing the informational basis for
meaningful participation in local waste and energy policy discussions.

5.6 Multi-Level Perspective Analysis

In Geels' MLP terms, Radix operates at the niche level, exploiting a
landscape-level disruption (the Simpler Recycling mandate) to introduce
an innovation (community-level waste-energy tracking with local economic
rewards) that challenges the prevailing regime (centralised, invisible,
non-participatory waste processing). The platform's go-to-market
strategy explicitly leverages the regime disruption: 79 councils missed
the Simpler Recycling deadline, creating an addressable market for
Radix's white-label offering in Year 3. This strategy follows the MLP
logic of niche diffusion: demonstrate viability in a favourable local
context (Brighton), then replicate through the windows of opportunity
created by landscape pressure on the regime.

5.7 Impact Projections

Using WRAP-verified conversion factors, Radix projects that by Year 2,
the platform will contribute to 3,000 additional tonnes of food waste
diverted from landfill, saving approximately 1,500 tonnes of CO2
equivalent. Each tonne of diverted food waste avoids approximately 0.5
tonnes of CO2 equivalent, and each tonne yields approximately 150 cubic
metres of biogas. Scaled to the city level, Brighton and Hove already
diverts approximately 15,000 tonnes of food waste from landfill annually;
Radix's target represents a 20% increase in effective diversion through
improved participation and reduced contamination.


6. Discussion

6.1 Community Energy as High-Leverage Intervention

The analysis presented in this essay suggests that community energy
projects -- and their adjacent circular-economy innovations such as
Radix -- operate at uniquely high leverage points within the UK energy
system. Unlike carbon pricing or even feed-in tariffs, which adjust
parameters within the existing system structure, community energy
challenges the foundational paradigm of centralised, corporate-owned
energy production. It does so not through protest or political advocacy
(though these play supporting roles) but through the practical
demonstration that another model is viable: democratic, distributed,
community-benefiting.

This is consistent with Meadows' (1999) observation that the most
powerful leverage points are also the most counterintuitive: changing
paradigms is more effective than adjusting parameters, yet paradigm
change rarely features in policy analysis. The Great British Energy Act
2025 and the Local Power Plan represent, in Meadows' terms, an attempt
to change the rules of the system (leverage point 5) to support the
paradigm shift (leverage point 2) that community energy organisations
have been demonstrating at the niche level for over a decade.

6.2 The Brighton Model: Conditions for Replicability

Brighton's distinctive conditions -- a Green political culture, compact
urban form, established community energy ecosystem, existing food waste
infrastructure, and a large university population -- raise questions
about the replicability of the model described here. Not all UK cities
share these advantages. However, the Simpler Recycling mandate creates
a universal regulatory baseline: every English local authority must now
collect food waste weekly, and 79 councils that missed the deadline face
particular urgency. The infrastructure gap that Brighton has already
closed will need to be closed everywhere, creating opportunities for
platforms like Radix to enter at the point of maximum organisational
need.

Moreover, the conditions that make Brighton distinctive are not unique;
they are concentrated. Other UK cities with strong civic identities,
dense urban cores, and active community sectors -- Bristol, Oxford,
Cambridge, Norwich, Exeter -- share many of Brighton's characteristics.
The scaling strategy is not to replicate Brighton's specific conditions
but to identify and serve communities where analogous conditions exist.

6.3 Limitations

This analysis has several limitations. The energy justice framework,
while analytically powerful, can underspecify the material conditions
required for justice to be realised: democratic governance structures do
not automatically produce just outcomes if underlying power imbalances
persist. The systems-thinking approach, similarly, can overestimate the
coherence of interventions that, in practice, face friction, delay, and
unintended consequences. Radix Brighton, as of this writing, remains in
its pre-launch phase; the projections cited are modelled rather than
observed. Longitudinal empirical research will be necessary to assess
whether the platform's systems-level design translates into measurable
advances in distributional, recognition, and procedural justice.

Furthermore, the food waste-to-energy pathway, while promising, is not
a substitute for the larger structural transformation required in UK
energy infrastructure. Biogas from anaerobic digestion of food waste
contributes meaningfully to distributed energy production, but at a
scale that remains modest relative to the UK's total energy demand. The
value of platforms like Radix lies less in their direct energy
contribution than in their capacity to shift paradigms: to demonstrate
that waste is a resource, that energy can be locally produced and
locally beneficial, and that citizens can be participants rather than
mere consumers in the energy system.


7. Conclusion

Community energy projects in the United Kingdom represent a high-leverage
intervention in a system characterised by deep distributional,
recognition, and procedural injustices. The theoretical frameworks of
energy justice (Jenkins et al., 2016), leverage points (Meadows, 1999),
and socio-technical transitions (Geels, 2002) converge on a consistent
analysis: lasting transformation requires not merely the adjustment of
system parameters but the challenging of paradigms, the alteration of
rules, and the creation of new information flows and self-organising
structures.

Brighton and Hove, with its Green political culture, established
community energy ecosystem, compact urban form, and early adoption of
food waste infrastructure, provides a laboratory for testing these
propositions. The Radix Brighton case study demonstrates how
systems-thinking design can integrate waste regulation, distributed
energy production, community engagement, and local economic development
into a single platform, addressing all three tenets of energy justice
simultaneously. The convergence of the Great British Energy Act 2025,
the Local Power Plan, and the Simpler Recycling mandate creates a
historically unusual alignment of landscape pressures, regime
disruptions, and niche opportunities.

The task ahead is translation: from niche demonstration to regime-level
change, from policy intent to regulatory reality, from Brighton's
favourable conditions to the diverse and often more challenging contexts
of other UK communities. Community energy cannot, alone, deliver the
just energy transition the United Kingdom requires. But it can -- and
demonstrably does -- offer a model of what that transition might look
like when justice, rather than mere decarbonisation, is the organising
principle.
