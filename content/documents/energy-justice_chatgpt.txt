Community Energy and Energy Justice in Post-Regulatory Urban England:
A Systems-Thinking Analysis of Brighton and Hove

Zoltan Erdos
April 2026


Abstract

This chapter asks: how, and under what conditions, do community energy
projects in UK cities advance distributional, recognition, and
procedural justice? Existing scholarship demonstrates that community
energy can produce local benefits (Seyfang et al., 2013; Bauwens, 2016),
but less is known about which specific mechanisms translate
participation into energy justice outcomes in dense urban settings under
post-Feed-in Tariff policy conditions. Through a theory-led analysis of
policy documents, sector reports, and local authority materials, with
Brighton and Hove as the primary site and comparative reference to
Bristol and London, the chapter makes three contributions. First, it
proposes an explicit division of labour between the energy justice
framework (Jenkins et al., 2016), leverage points theory (Meadows,
1999), and the multi-level perspective (Geels, 2002), arguing that each
addresses a distinct analytical gap the others leave open. Second, it
identifies the specific political, infrastructural, and demographic
conditions under which community energy projects are most likely to
deliver justice outcomes -- and the conditions under which they
reproduce existing inequalities. Third, it examines a speculative
intervention design, Radix Brighton, as an illustrative case of how
systems thinking might operationalise energy justice at the
neighbourhood scale, while maintaining critical distance from the
design's untested assumptions. The chapter concludes that community
energy's justice potential is real but conditional, and that the current
policy window -- opened by the Great British Energy Act 2025, the Local
Power Plan, and the Simpler Recycling mandate of March 2026 -- is
necessary but not sufficient for that potential to be realised.


1. Introduction

1.1 The Problem

The United Kingdom's energy transition is proceeding unevenly. The
country has committed to net zero by 2050 and to decarbonising its
electricity grid by 2030 under the Clean Power Mission. Yet 2.36 million
English households remain in fuel poverty under the Low Income Low Energy
Efficiency (LILEE) measure -- 9.4% of all households -- with an
aggregate fuel poverty gap of GBP 896 million per year (DESNZ, 2026).
The earlier 2024 data showed 2.73 million fuel-poor households (11.0%)
and a gap of GBP 1.11 billion (DESNZ, 2025); the decline reflects
falling energy prices rather than structural improvement in housing
stock or income distribution. Under the broader 10% income threshold
measure, 36.3% of English households -- 8.99 million -- still exceed
the affordability threshold (DESNZ, 2025). In Scotland, 34% of
households are fuel poor, rising to 44% in remote rural areas (Scottish
House Condition Survey, 2023). The transition, if governed primarily by
incumbent market actors, risks reproducing these inequalities at scale.

Community energy projects are frequently cited as a corrective: locally
owned, democratically governed, oriented toward community benefit
(Walker and Devine-Wright, 2008; Seyfang et al., 2013). The sector has
reached meaningful scale. Community Energy England's State of the Sector
2025 report documents 614 active organisations with 411 MW of
community-owned renewable capacity, generating 575 GWh annually. The
government's Local Power Plan evidence annex gives somewhat different
figures: close to 700 community energy groups, approximately 440 MW of
operational capacity, and roughly 695 MW in the development pipeline
(GBE, 2026). The discrepancy reflects differences in organisational
definition (CEE counts only formally constituted community energy
organisations; GBE includes broader local energy groups) and reporting
periods. Both sources confirm the same trajectory: a sector growing
despite the withdrawal of the Feed-in Tariff in 2019.

Yet growth is not justice. The critical question is not whether
community energy is expanding but whether it is expanding in ways that
deliver fair outcomes for those who need them most. This chapter
addresses that question.

1.2 Research Question

How, and under what conditions, do community energy projects in UK
cities advance distributional, recognition, and procedural justice?

1.3 Contribution

Existing work on community energy and justice tends toward one of two
modes: theoretical frameworks that remain abstract (Jenkins et al.,
2016; Sovacool and Dworkin, 2015), or empirical case studies that remain
descriptive (Hargreaves et al., 2013; Hoffman and High-Pippert, 2015).
Work that bridges the gap -- applying justice frameworks to specific
urban contexts under current UK policy conditions -- is thin. This
chapter contributes by:

(a) proposing a structured analytical relationship between three
frameworks that are typically deployed in isolation;

(b) identifying the enabling and constraining conditions for justice
outcomes in a specific urban setting (Brighton and Hove), with
comparative reference to Bristol and London;

(c) examining a speculative intervention design (Radix Brighton) not as
evidence of what works, but as a device for surfacing the assumptions
that systems-level community energy design must confront.

1.4 Structure

Section 2 establishes the theoretical framework. Section 3 describes the
analytical approach. Section 4 surveys the UK community energy landscape
and policy context. Section 5 examines Brighton and Hove as an empirical
site. Section 6 presents the Radix intervention design as a speculative
case. Section 7 provides critical analysis. Section 8 concludes.


2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 Three Frameworks and Their Division of Labour

This chapter draws on three bodies of theory. They are not
interchangeable, and deploying them together requires stating explicitly
what each one does that the others cannot.

Energy justice (Jenkins et al., 2016) tells us what kind of fairness
matters. The three-tenet framework -- distributional, recognition, and
procedural justice -- provides the normative criteria against which
community energy outcomes are assessed. Without it, we could describe
community energy systems in detail but have no basis for judging whether
they are just. Energy justice is the evaluative lens.

Leverage points theory (Meadows, 1999; 2008) tells us where
intervention bites. Meadows' twelve-point hierarchy distinguishes
shallow interventions (adjusting parameters within existing system
structures) from deep interventions (changing goals, mindsets, and
paradigms). Without it, we could identify injustice but have no
framework for understanding why some interventions produce
transformation while others merely adjust the surface. Leverage points
is the intervention-depth lens.

The multi-level perspective (Geels, 2002; 2014) tells us how
innovations diffuse or stall. The MLP's niche-regime-landscape
architecture explains why technically viable and normatively desirable
innovations can fail to scale -- because they face regime resistance
from incumbents whose interests are served by the existing system.
Without it, we could design high-leverage interventions that address
real injustices but have no account of why they might not be adopted.
The MLP is the diffusion lens.

A sceptical reader might object that three frameworks is one too many --
that each is being recruited because it supports the preferred
conclusion. The defence is that each addresses a gap the others leave
conspicuously open. Energy justice has no theory of intervention depth;
it can say that the energy system is unjust but not why carbon pricing
fails to fix it while community ownership might. Meadows has no
normative framework; leverage points theory can say that paradigm-level
interventions are more powerful than parameter-level ones but cannot say
whether a given paradigm shift is just. Geels has no normative framework
either, and adds no account of intervention design; MLP explains
diffusion dynamics but not whether the innovations diffusing are worth
diffusing, or how to design them. The three frameworks are not redundant.
They are complementary, and the gaps between them are where this chapter
works.

2.2 Energy Justice: The Three Tenets

The foundational framework was established by Jenkins, McCauley, Heffron,
Stephan, and Rehner (2016) as "a new crosscutting social science
research agenda which seeks to apply justice principles to energy
policy, energy production and systems, energy consumption, energy
activism, energy security and climate change" (p. 175).

Distributional justice concerns the fair allocation of both burdens
(pollution, health impacts, infrastructure proximity, cost) and benefits
(affordable energy, revenue, employment) across society. Distributional
injustice in the UK is structural: 55% of social housing with Energy
Performance Certificate ratings of D to G is fuel poor (DESNZ, 2026).
The fuel poverty gap is not closing because of improved justice; it is
narrowing because wholesale energy prices fell.

Recognition justice requires that the identities, needs, and
vulnerabilities of different social groups be substantively acknowledged
in energy governance -- not merely formally included. In the UK,
recognition failures implicate ethnic minority communities
disproportionately affected by fuel poverty, social housing tenants with
no control over their building's energy efficiency, disabled people with
higher energy needs, and rural households facing both higher costs and
lower infrastructure investment.

Procedural justice addresses the fairness of decision-making processes:
who participates, whose voice carries weight, whether processes are
transparent and accountable. The UK energy system has historically
concentrated decision-making authority in national government, Ofgem,
and large utilities, with minimal meaningful public participation
(Chilvers and Longhurst, 2016).

Sovacool and Dworkin (2015) extend these tenets to encompass
availability, affordability, due process, sustainability,
intergenerational equity, and responsibility, connecting energy justice
to human rights frameworks. Heffron, McCauley, and de Rubens (2018)
propose a whole-systems approach covering the full energy life-cycle and
introduce the Energy Justice Metric. They also advocate adding
restorative justice as a fourth tenet -- the obligation to remedy past
harm, not merely prevent future harm. This chapter uses the original
three-tenet framework as its primary evaluative structure, drawing on
the extensions where they illuminate specific gaps.

2.3 Leverage Points

Meadows' hierarchy (1999; 2008) ranks twelve places to intervene in a
complex system, from low-leverage (adjusting numerical parameters) to
high-leverage (changing paradigms and transcending paradigms). The
hierarchy is a heuristic, not a measurement instrument. It is useful for
distinguishing interventions that adjust numbers within existing
structures from those that alter the structures themselves.

Applied to energy systems: carbon pricing operates at the parameter
level (leverage point 12); feed-in tariffs adjust reinforcing feedback
loops (leverage point 7); community energy projects plausibly engage
higher leverage points by altering information flows (point 6), changing
rules of ownership (point 5), enabling self-organisation (point 4), and
-- more speculatively -- shifting goals from profit maximisation to
community benefit (point 3) or challenging the paradigm that energy
production belongs to private utilities (point 2).

The qualifier "plausibly" matters. Meadows herself warns that leverage
points are "not easily accessible, even if we know where they are" and
that "the system will fight back" (1999, p. 18). Claiming that community
energy operates at paradigm level is analytically bold; demonstrating it
requires evidence that actual beliefs and institutional structures have
shifted, not merely that a cooperative was formed. This chapter treats
leverage-point mapping as indicative rather than definitive, and
examines the evidence for and against high-leverage claims in specific
cases.

2.4 The Multi-Level Perspective

Geels' MLP (2002; 2014; 2016) describes socio-technical transitions as
interactions between three levels: niches (protected spaces for radical
innovation), regimes (dominant socio-technical systems), and landscapes
(exogenous macro-trends). Community energy organisations are niche
actors. Their role in MLP terms is to develop and stabilise innovations
that can, under favourable landscape pressures, challenge the regime.

Geels' comparative analysis of German and UK low-carbon transitions
(Geels et al., 2016) shows that Germany followed a substitution pathway
-- new entrants, including energy cooperatives, driving small-scale
renewables -- while the UK followed a transformation pathway in which
incumbents deployed large-scale renewables under government direction.
This structural bias toward incumbents explains the comparative
underdevelopment of British community energy and gives the current policy
shift (GBE, Local Power Plan) its historical significance.

Geels (2014) also introduces the concept of regime resistance: incumbent
actors deploying political and regulatory power to slow niche
innovations. In the UK, regime resistance is visible in the withdrawal
of the Feed-in Tariff without replacement, the decade-long failure of
the Local Electricity Bill, and the planning restrictions on onshore
wind only partially lifted in 2024. The MLP framework predicts that
even favourable policy intent will face resistance from actors whose
interests are served by the existing regime structure.


3. Analytical Approach

This is a theory-led qualitative analysis. It does not claim the
methodological status of a formal case study in the Yin (2018) sense,
nor does it employ process tracing or discourse analysis as a systematic
method. What it does is apply a structured theoretical framework to a
bounded empirical context, using documentary evidence to assess the
conditions under which community energy advances energy justice.

3.1 Case Selection

Brighton and Hove was selected for four reasons: (a) it hosts multiple
community energy organisations operating in complementary domains,
enabling analysis of a local ecosystem rather than a single organisation;
(b) it has an unusually favourable political context (Green-led council,
Climate Emergency declaration, 2030 carbon neutrality target), providing
a best-case scenario for examining what community energy achieves even
under supportive conditions; (c) it is among the local authorities that
met the Simpler Recycling deadline of 31 March 2026, enabling analysis
of the food waste-energy nexus; and (d) it is the proposed site of a
speculative intervention design (Radix) that permits examination of
systems-level design assumptions.

Bristol and London serve as comparative reference points: Bristol for its
community energy ecosystem (Bristol Energy Cooperative, Lawrence Weston
wind turbine) and as a case where distributional justice outcomes are
more directly observable; London (Repowering London) for its explicit
targeting of underrepresented communities and as a contrasting urban
form.

3.2 Evidence Base

The analysis draws on: (a) sector reports (Community Energy England,
2024; 2025); (b) government policy documents (Great British Energy Act
2025; Local Power Plan, 2026; Fuel Poverty Strategy for England, 2026);
(c) Brighton and Hove City Council materials (2030 Carbon Neutral
Programme, Climate Assembly report, Defra grant allocations);
(d) organisational materials from Brighton Energy Co-operative (BEC),
Brighton and Hove Energy Services Co-operative (BHESCo), Repowering
London, and Bristol Energy Cooperative; (e) academic literature on
energy justice, systems thinking, and socio-technical transitions; and
(f) the Radix Brighton business plan, treated as a design document
rather than an empirical source.

3.3 Reflexive Disclosure

The author is the founder of spike.land, the platform on which Radix
Brighton is designed to be built. This creates an obvious conflict of
interest. The chapter handles this in two ways. First, the Radix
material is quarantined in a separate section (Section 6) and explicitly
labelled as a speculative intervention design -- an exercise in applying
theoretical frameworks to a proposed system, not an evaluation of an
operational one. Second, the critical analysis (Section 7) subjects
Radix to the same sceptical scrutiny applied to the broader community
energy sector. The reader should treat the Radix material as a thought
experiment informed by the theoretical framework, not as evidence.

3.4 What Counts as Evidence

For claims about distributional justice, the chapter relies on
quantified financial flows (community benefit fund disbursements,
revenue returned to local economies, household energy cost impacts)
where available, and qualitative descriptions of benefit distribution
where not. For recognition justice, it examines organisational
membership composition, explicit targeting of underrepresented groups,
and the presence or absence of mechanisms for acknowledging diverse
needs. For procedural justice, it examines governance structures
(cooperative vs. company, one-member-one-vote vs. shareholder
proportion), participation rates, and accessibility of decision-making
processes. Competing explanations -- that observed benefits result from
pre-existing community capacity rather than the energy project itself,
or that justice claims are performative rather than substantive -- are
addressed in Section 7.


4. The UK Community Energy Landscape

4.1 Scale, Growth, and Source Consistency

The sector's scale is documented by two primary sources whose figures
do not fully align. Community Energy England's State of the Sector 2025
report identifies 614 active organisations with 411 MW of
community-owned renewable capacity generating 575 GWh annually. The
government's Local Power Plan evidence annex (GBE, 2026) refers to
close to 700 community energy groups, approximately 440 MW of
operational capacity, and roughly 695 MW in the development pipeline.

The discrepancy is methodological, not contradictory. CEE counts only
formally constituted community energy organisations (registered
cooperatives, community benefit societies, community interest companies).
GBE's broader definition includes informal local energy groups and
council-led community energy schemes. The capacity difference (411 MW
vs. 440 MW) likely reflects different cut-off dates and the inclusion of
partially commissioned projects. A thesis should not treat these as
interchangeable numbers; they measure overlapping but non-identical
populations of organisations and assets.

The sector added 22 MW of new capacity in 2024, created 88 full-time
equivalent jobs, and saw a 58% rise in organisational turnover
year-on-year (CEE, 2025). These figures represent growth despite the
loss of the Feed-in Tariff -- the sector's primary revenue mechanism
until its closure to new applicants in 2019.

4.2 Three Comparative Cases

Brighton Energy Co-operative (BEC), founded in 2010 as a Community
Benefit Society, has raised over GBP 5 million in community investment,
installed 4,844 kWp of solar capacity, generated nearly 22,000 MWh of
clean electricity, and avoided over 4,500 tonnes of CO2 emissions. It
operates 13 Solar Schools and has deployed the South of England's first
community-owned solar-connected electric vehicle charger. BEC's
governance is one-member-one-vote; its community share offers have
typically returned 2-5% annual interest while directing surplus to
community benefit.

Repowering London operates across nine London boroughs, has delivered
over 1.5 MWp of rooftop solar with 650 member investors, and ringfences
GBP 200,000 for local community projects. What distinguishes Repowering
is its explicit focus on recognition justice: the organisation targets
underrepresented communities, diversifies cooperative membership beyond
the typical profile of community energy participants (older, white,
homeowning, middle-class), and runs youth engagement programmes
including "Creative Energy Clubs."

Bristol Energy Cooperative operates a 9 MW solar portfolio powering
3,000 homes -- 33% of solar energy in Bristol. The adjacent Lawrence
Weston wind turbine (Ambition Community Energy CIC) is a 4.2 MW,
GBP 4 million community project returning GBP 100,000 per year to one
of Bristol's most deprived neighbourhoods. Lawrence Weston is the
strongest UK example of community energy delivering measurable
distributional justice: the revenue flows to a specific area of high
deprivation, and the community had genuine decision-making authority
over the project's development.

4.3 Policy Context: 2025-2026

Three policy developments converge to create the current landscape.

The Great British Energy Act 2025 received Royal Assent on 15 May 2025,
establishing GBE as a publicly owned clean energy company. Following
sustained advocacy by Community Energy England, the final text includes
community energy explicitly in GBE's statutory objects. The Act is
enabling legislation; its practical significance depends on how GBE
deploys its mandate.

The Local Power Plan, published by GBE on 11 February 2026, commits up
to GBP 1 billion in investment, targets the support of over 1,000
community energy projects by 2030, and identifies approximately 1.9 GW
of projects already in the development pipeline. Near-term milestones
include first grant schemes in spring 2026, shared ownership template
documents, and a GBE Capital Toolkit portfolio by autumn 2026. The Plan
specifically targets underserved areas with historically low uptake.

The Simpler Recycling mandate, which took effect on 31 March 2026,
requires all English local authorities to implement weekly separate food
waste collections. This creates a new intersection between waste policy
and distributed energy production: food waste processed through
anaerobic digestion (AD) produces biogas for local electricity
generation or biomethane for grid injection. The UK biogas sector
processes 36 million tonnes of organic waste annually, generating 21 TWh
of biogas (ADBA/Tolvik, 2025). Food waste AD capacity is projected to
power the equivalent of 163,000 homes by 2027.

4.4 Barriers and Regime Resistance

Favourable policy signals coexist with persistent structural barriers.
The Feed-in Tariff's withdrawal in 2019 removed the sector's primary
revenue mechanism without adequate replacement. The Local Electricity
Bill, championed by the Power for People campaign to enable small-scale
generators to sell directly to local consumers, failed to pass in
successive parliamentary sessions and is now legislatively dead. A
separate instrument -- the Community Energy (Review) Bill, a private
member's bill introduced in the 2024-26 session -- requires the
Secretary of State to review community energy's contribution to the
Clean Power 2030 mission, but remains at Commons second reading with no
guarantee of passage. These are distinct instruments; conflating them, as
some sector commentary does, obscures the legislative gap.

Planning restrictions on onshore wind, only partially lifted in 2024,
continue to constrain development. Access to commercial-scale finance
remains difficult for smaller organisations. And the GBE's Local Power
Plan, while ambitious, has not yet delivered its first round of grants
as of April 2026.

In Geels' (2014) terms, these barriers represent regime resistance:
the transformation pathway that has historically characterised UK energy
governance -- favouring large incumbents over distributed actors --
persists in regulatory structures even as political rhetoric shifts
toward community empowerment. The translation of policy intent into
regulatory reality is incomplete and contested.


5. Brighton and Hove: Empirical Context

5.1 Political and Institutional Setting

Brighton and Hove elected the UK's first Green-led council in 2011. The
council declared a Climate and Biodiversity Emergency in December 2018
and adopted a 2030 Carbon Neutral Programme following the city's first
Climate Assembly in 2021. The Tyndall Centre for Climate Change
calculated that the city must reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 12.7%
annually from 2020 to remain within its carbon budget. Over GBP 7.1
million has been allocated to the programme.

This political context creates what the MLP would describe as a locally
favourable regime environment: the local authority is aligned with,
rather than resistant to, niche energy innovations. This is analytically
significant because it allows examination of what community energy
achieves even when the most commonly cited barrier -- hostile local
government -- is absent. If justice gaps persist under these conditions,
the explanation must lie elsewhere.

5.2 The Local Community Energy Ecosystem

Brighton hosts two complementary community energy organisations. BEC
(generation-focused: rooftop solar, 4,844 kWp installed) and BHESCo
(efficiency-focused: retrofit financing for fuel-poor households and
commercial properties). The coexistence of generation and efficiency
cooperatives within a single city is relatively unusual in the UK and
suggests that Brighton's community energy sector has reached a degree of
functional differentiation typically associated with more mature
ecosystems.

However, the distributional reach of these organisations requires
scrutiny. BEC's community share offers attract investors who can afford
minimum stakes (typically GBP 100-250). Its solar installations are
concentrated on schools, public buildings, and commercial rooftops --
sites selected for roof suitability and institutional cooperation rather
than neighbourhood deprivation. BHESCo's retrofit work is more directly
targeted at fuel poverty, but its scale remains modest relative to the
city's 2.36 million-household-equivalent share of national fuel poverty
(approximately 12,000-15,000 fuel-poor households in Brighton and Hove,
extrapolating from the national 9.4% rate). The question of whether
community energy in Brighton primarily benefits already-engaged,
relatively affluent households or reaches those in deepest energy
poverty remains empirically open.

5.3 Demographics and Urban Form

Brighton's compact urban form creates high community engagement density
-- a structural advantage for initiatives that depend on
neighbourhood-level participation. The city's 34,000-plus university
students (Sussex and Brighton) are often cited as early adopters, but
student populations are also transient, time-poor, and predominantly
renters with no control over their building's energy infrastructure.
Their "early adoption" may be shallow: signing up for an app is not the
same as investing in a community share offer or participating in
cooperative governance.

The tourism economy (11 million annual visitors) means that visible
sustainability carries brand value for local businesses. This creates
economic incentives for participation in sustainability schemes but
also raises questions about whether sustainability becomes performative
-- a marketing proposition rather than a justice one.

5.4 Food Waste Infrastructure

Brighton and Hove City Council received GBP 3,122,659.37 in Defra
grants for the Simpler Recycling rollout and was among councils ready
for the 31 March 2026 deadline, already collecting weekly food waste.
This positions the city ahead of the 79 councils (one in four) that
missed the deadline and the more than one-third that had not yet begun
food waste collection on any basis. The existing infrastructure
eliminates the cold-start problem and creates the material conditions
for food waste-to-energy projects.

The city diverts approximately 15,000 tonnes of food waste from landfill
annually. Each tonne of diverted food waste avoids approximately 0.5
tonnes of CO2 equivalent and yields approximately 150 cubic metres of
biogas (WRAP, 2026). The waste-energy nexus is operational, not
hypothetical.


6. Speculative Intervention Design: Radix Brighton

6.1 Framing Note

This section examines a proposed platform, Radix Brighton, as a designed
system intended to operationalise energy justice principles at the
neighbourhood scale. Radix has not been deployed. The material here is
drawn from the project's business plan, which the author, as founder of
the underlying platform, has a direct interest in. The section's purpose
is not to present Radix as evidence of what works. It is to use the
design as a lens for examining what a systems-level community energy
intervention would need to achieve, and what assumptions it would need
to hold, to advance energy justice in a setting like Brighton. The
reader should approach this section as a thought experiment, not as
empirical analysis.

6.2 Design Concept

Radix is a hyperlocal circular-economy platform designed to sit on top
of Brighton's existing food waste collection infrastructure, adding a
digital layer of tracking, community engagement, and local economic
value. The core mechanism is a feedback loop: residents log food waste
contributions, receive information about the energy equivalent of their
contribution (using WRAP-verified conversion factors: one kitchen caddy
of food waste is equivalent to approximately 18 hours of fridge power
via biogas), and earn recycling credits redeemable at local independent
businesses.

The design has five components: a waste tracker (individual logging), an
impact dashboard (neighbourhood-level aggregation), a community
leaderboard (inter-ward competition), a local rewards system (credits
at independents), and route intelligence (collection optimisation
offered to the council). Financial projections and unit economics are
detailed in Appendix A; they are excluded from the main text because
they are modelled assumptions, not observed outcomes, and their presence
in an analytical chapter would conflate speculative commercial
modelling with academic argument.

6.3 Systems-Level Analysis

Analysed through leverage points theory, Radix's design engages several
levels of intervention -- though the depth of that engagement is
uncertain and should be stated with appropriate caution.

At the information flow level (leverage point 6), the design is most
clearly justified. The current food waste system is informationally
opaque: residents place waste in a caddy, the council collects it, and
whatever happens thereafter is invisible. Radix proposes to make the
energy and carbon consequences of household waste diversion visible at
the individual and neighbourhood scale. Information flow interventions
are among the more reliably effective leverage points in Meadows'
hierarchy, and the evidence from behavioural science on feedback
mechanisms and energy consumption (Allcott, 2011; Schultz et al., 2007)
supports the general proposition that making consequences visible
changes behaviour.

At the rules level (leverage point 5), the design is more ambiguous. The
local rewards system changes the economic rules of who benefits from
waste diversion: value circulates to local independent businesses rather
than being captured by centralised waste processing firms. But the
"rules" in Meadows' sense are the formal and informal agreements that
govern system behaviour. A rewards programme is a rule change only if it
becomes institutionally embedded -- if the council, businesses, and
residents come to treat it as part of how waste management works in
Brighton. A standalone app with declining retention is a feature, not a
rule.

At the goal and paradigm levels (leverage points 3 and 2), claims must
be highly tentative. The design aspires to shift the goal of waste
management from compliance to community empowerment, and to challenge
the paradigm that citizens are passive recipients of municipal services.
Whether a digital platform can actually achieve paradigm-level change is
an empirical question that cannot be answered by examining the design
alone. Meadows (1999) warns that the most powerful leverage points are
also the most difficult to access, and that systems resist paradigm
change. A platform that gamifies recycling may reinforce the existing
paradigm of individual consumer responsibility rather than challenging
it.

6.4 Energy Justice Assessment (Prospective)

A prospective energy justice analysis -- assessing what the design would
need to achieve, not what it has achieved -- reveals both promise and
exposure.

Distributional justice: The design proposes to channel economic value
toward local independents and to reduce council operational costs
through route optimisation. If realised, this represents a modest
redistribution. But the distribution of benefits depends on who
participates. If participation clusters in already-engaged, relatively
affluent wards (the pilot targets Hanover, Kemptown, and Preston Park --
areas with high Green vote share and dense community networks, not the
city's most deprived neighbourhoods), the platform could reinforce
rather than challenge existing distributional patterns.

Recognition justice: The neighbourhood-level design acknowledges that
Brighton's communities are heterogeneous. But acknowledging heterogeneity
in a design document is different from achieving it in practice.
Gamification structures (leaderboards, competitions) tend to favour
participants with existing social capital, digital literacy, and time --
precisely the resources that marginalised communities are least likely
to have. The design does not currently include mechanisms for
identifying and actively reaching under-participating groups.

Procedural justice: Radix operates with council cooperation but is not
controlled by the council, creating a governance space between municipal
authority and commercial platform. This is procedurally interesting but
also procedurally ambiguous: residents participate as users of a
platform, not as members of a cooperative with voting rights and
governance authority. The procedural justice credentials of a
platform-mediated engagement system are weaker than those of a
community benefit society with formal democratic governance.


7. Critical Analysis

7.1 The Limits of Community Energy as Justice

The evidence assembled in this chapter supports a conditional claim:
community energy projects can advance energy justice, but they do so
unevenly, and the conditions under which they succeed are more specific
than the literature often acknowledges.

The strongest evidence for distributional justice comes from Bristol,
where the Lawrence Weston wind turbine returns GBP 100,000 per year to
a neighbourhood of high deprivation. This is a direct, quantifiable,
geographically targeted flow of economic benefit. It is also a large,
capital-intensive project that took years to develop and required
exceptional community leadership. It is not easily replicated.

The strongest evidence for recognition justice comes from London, where
Repowering explicitly targets underrepresented communities and has
invested in diversifying its cooperative membership. But Repowering's
recognition work is resource-intensive -- it requires dedicated
outreach, multilingual materials, youth programmes, and sustained
community presence. These are not features of a scalable platform; they
are features of patient, locally embedded organisational work.

The strongest evidence for procedural justice comes from the cooperative
governance model itself: one-member-one-vote, community share offers,
transparent annual reporting. But procedural justice at the
organisational level does not automatically translate to justice at the
neighbourhood or city level. A cooperative with 600 members in a city
of 290,000 people is democratically governed within its own boundaries
but represents a tiny fraction of the population. The vast majority of
Brighton's residents have no procedural relationship with BEC or BHESCo.

7.2 Community Energy's Uncomfortable Patterns

A serious analysis of community energy and justice must confront
patterns that the sector's own advocacy literature tends to minimise.

Participant demographics: Community energy cooperatives in the UK
disproportionately attract older, white, homeowning, middle-class
participants (Bauwens, 2016; Simcock et al., 2016). Share offers
require disposable capital; governance participation requires time;
engagement with cooperative meetings requires cultural familiarity with
formal democratic processes. The communities that most need energy
justice -- renters, ethnic minorities, people in fuel poverty -- are
systematically underrepresented.

Unpaid labour: Community energy organisations depend heavily on
volunteer effort, particularly in their early stages. This unpaid
labour is disproportionately provided by people who can afford to donate
their time -- retired professionals, people with flexible employment,
those with existing community networks. The sector's low cost base,
often presented as an advantage, partially reflects an uncosted transfer
of labour from volunteers to the organisation.

Scaling paradox: The features that make community energy just --
democratic governance, local embeddedness, community ownership -- are
also the features that make it hard to scale. A cooperative that grows
too fast risks losing the relational density that makes it a
cooperative rather than a company with shares. The tension between
justice and scale is structural, not incidental.

Dependence on the state: Community energy in the UK has never been
autonomous from state support. The sector's growth was enabled by the
Feed-in Tariff; its contraction followed the tariff's withdrawal; its
current expansion depends on the Great British Energy Act, the Local
Power Plan, and Defra grants for Simpler Recycling. Community energy
claims to challenge the centralised energy paradigm, but its material
existence depends on central government policy. This is not a fatal
contradiction, but it is a tension that needs honest acknowledgment.

7.3 Radix: What the Design Assumes and What Could Fail

The Radix design makes several assumptions that are not self-evident.

First, it assumes that information visibility drives behaviour change.
The evidence for this in energy consumption (Allcott, 2011) is
reasonably strong, but the evidence for waste behaviour is weaker and
more context-dependent. Making biogas output visible is not the same as
making electricity bills visible; the feedback pathway is longer and
less personally consequential.

Second, it assumes that gamification sustains long-term engagement. The
literature on gamification in pro-environmental behaviour is mixed
(Hamari et al., 2014). Leaderboards and competitions can produce
initial spikes in participation followed by sharp declines as novelty
fades. The 40% retention target at Year 2 is ambitious for a
gamification-based engagement model.

Third, it assumes that the rewards mechanism creates meaningful local
economic circulation. At the projected scale (GBP 150,000 flowing to
local independents by Year 2), the macroeconomic effect is negligible.
The value is symbolic and relational -- demonstrating a link between
waste and local commerce -- rather than transformative.

Fourth, and most critically, it assumes that a platform-mediated model
can deliver the kinds of justice that the community energy literature
attributes to organisational models. A cooperative is a governance
structure. An app is an interface. The gap between the two is not
bridged by the Radix design, and this is its deepest limitation.

7.4 Competing Explanations

An alternative reading of the Brighton evidence is that the city's
community energy successes result from pre-existing community capacity
-- Green political culture, high social capital, educated population,
dense organisational landscape -- rather than from the community energy
projects themselves. On this reading, BEC and BHESCo are expressions of
Brighton's civic character, not causes of energy justice. The policy
implication would be quite different: instead of scaling community
energy models to other cities, the priority would be building the
underlying civic capacity that makes community energy viable.

This chapter cannot definitively adjudicate between these explanations
with the evidence available. The question of whether community energy
creates justice or merely correlates with pre-existing conditions that
independently produce both community energy and justice outcomes is an
empirical question requiring longitudinal comparative research beyond
this chapter's scope. What can be said is that both mechanisms are
likely at work: Brighton's civic infrastructure enables community energy,
and community energy strengthens civic infrastructure, in a reinforcing
loop. The relative weight of each direction is unknown.


8. Conclusion

This chapter asked how, and under what conditions, community energy
projects in UK cities advance distributional, recognition, and
procedural justice. The answer is conditional.

Community energy advances distributional justice when revenue flows to
specific areas of deprivation (Lawrence Weston), when efficiency
retrofits target fuel-poor households (BHESCo), and when economic
value circulates locally rather than being extracted by centralised
actors. It fails when participation clusters among the already affluent,
when share offers exclude those without disposable capital, and when
benefits accrue to the community energy organisation rather than the
wider community.

Community energy advances recognition justice when organisations
explicitly target underrepresented groups (Repowering London), invest
in multilingual and culturally accessible outreach, and design
governance structures that accommodate diverse forms of participation.
It fails when membership demographics reproduce existing social
hierarchies and when "community" is tacitly defined as those who already
show up.

Community energy advances procedural justice when governance is
genuinely democratic (one-member-one-vote cooperatives), when
decision-making is transparent, and when participation pathways are
accessible. It fails when procedural justice at the organisational level
does not translate to procedural influence at the municipal or national
level, and when platform-mediated engagement substitutes for genuine
governance authority.

The current policy window -- the Great British Energy Act 2025, the
Local Power Plan, and the Simpler Recycling mandate -- is the most
favourable in a decade. But policy windows open conditions; they do not
guarantee outcomes. The GBE's GBP 1 billion commitment and 1,000-project
target will produce community energy growth. Whether that growth delivers
energy justice depends on choices the policy does not make: who is
included, who governs, who benefits, and whether the organisations that
emerge are structured for justice or merely for clean energy production.

Brighton, with its Green council, established cooperatives, compact
urban form, and early food waste infrastructure, is the best-case
scenario. If community energy cannot deliver energy justice here, it is
unlikely to deliver it anywhere without more fundamental structural
intervention. The evidence from this analysis suggests that it can
deliver justice here, partially, under specific conditions, for some
residents. That is a more honest conclusion than the sector's advocacy
literature typically offers, and a more useful starting point for
designing interventions -- like Radix, if it can solve the problems
this chapter identifies -- that might extend justice further.

The speculative Radix design exposes the gap between systems-level
ambition and the granular organisational work that justice requires. A
platform can create information flows. It can gamify participation. It
can circulate economic value locally. What it cannot easily do is
substitute for the democratic governance, sustained community
relationships, and patient organisational development that the strongest
community energy examples depend on. The systems thinker's temptation is
to believe that a well-designed feedback loop can produce transformation.
Sometimes it can. But energy justice is not a systems dynamics problem.
It is a political one.
