# Chapter 6: What I Would Change

*Concrete recommendations for ward practice, co-authored with Peggy Martin*

## Preamble

These recommendations are derived from the preceding five chapters. Each is traced back to the specific agent state and ward situation that surfaced it. They are not theoretical improvements — they are responses to specific failures of the current system as experienced from within it.

Categorised by implementation effort:
- **Low** — can be done tomorrow by an individual nurse
- **Medium** — requires policy or process change at ward level
- **High** — requires infrastructure, budget, or system-level change

---

## Low Effort (Individual Practice)

### R-01: Separate the wake-up from the question
*Source: Ch.2 — drift-0 at 07:00*

Do not combine waking a patient with asking how they feel. Wake them. Leave. Return in ten minutes. The fog needs time to clear before self-assessment is possible.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on feasibility -->

### R-02: Answer sharp patients' questions directly
*Source: Ch.2 — sharp-3 at 08:00; Ch.3 — sharp-3 admission*

When a patient asks a procedural question, answer it. "What happens if I refuse this medication?" has a specific answer. Give it. The question is information-seeking, not confrontational. Deflection increases anxiety.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary — contrast with de-escalation training assumptions -->

### R-03: Post-visiting-hours check-in at 17:30
*Source: Ch.4 — both agents after visitors leave*

Schedule a brief check-in thirty minutes after visiting hours end. This is when the performance drops and the real state reveals itself. A two-minute conversation here is worth more than a thirty-minute one at any other time.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on staffing implications -->

### R-04: Offer roles to wild patients in group
*Source: Ch.4 — wild-5 in group therapy*

"Would you like to take notes for the group today?" channels energy without suppression. Give them a function that serves the group. Reduces interruption without confrontation.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on therapeutic dynamics -->

### R-05: Deep patients — tasks not questions
*Source: Ch.2 — deep-2 at 07:00; Ch.4 — deep-2 daily*

Replace "How are you feeling?" with "Your tea is in the lounge when you're ready." Provide actions, not introspection prompts. The distance between feeling and articulating is too great for deep patients in the morning.

## Medium Effort (Policy/Process)

### R-06: Three-session medication education
*Source: Ch.5 — discharge medication education*

Medication education currently happens once, at discharge, at maximum cognitive load. Move to three sessions: (1) at stabilisation, (2) two days before discharge, (3) at discharge. Repetition is the mechanism of retention.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on implementation within existing discharge protocols -->

### R-07: Reduce fire alarm pre-announcement window
*Source: Ch.2 — sharp-3 at 14:15*

Announcing a fire alarm test at 08:30 for a 14:15 test creates a five-hour anxiety load for sharp patients. Announce 15 minutes before. Include duration and location: "Thirty seconds, courtyard, I'll walk with you."

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary — fire safety regulations, what's actually required -->

### R-08: Written discharge pack
*Source: Ch.5 — both agents at discharge*

Everything said verbally at discharge must also exist in writing. Medication names, doses, schedules, side effects, crisis numbers, follow-up dates, GP contact, named ward contact. One document. The patient takes it home. It becomes their external memory.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary — existing discharge summary vs. patient-facing document -->

### R-09: Room tour on day two
*Source: Ch.3 — drift-0 admission*

The admission room tour does not register for drift patients. Repeat it on day two. "Just checking — do you know where the call button is? Do you know how the shower works?" Information cannot be retained when it is delivered during crisis.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary -->

### R-10: Transition tracking
*Source: Ch.2 — closing; Ch.4 — Section 6*

Document transitions between states, not just states. "Patient moved from engaged (15:00) to withdrawn (17:30)" tells a story. "Patient withdrawn at 17:30" does not. The transition is the clinical signal.

## High Effort (Infrastructure/System)

### R-11: Follow-up gap reduction
*Source: Ch.5 — the follow-up appointment*

Twenty-one days between discharge and first follow-up is a scheduling constraint, not a clinical decision. Target: phone contact within 48 hours, in-person contact within 7 days. If the system cannot provide this, provide a named ward contact who can be called.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on community mental health team capacity -->

### R-12: Discharge logistics programme
*Source: Ch.5 — first supermarket, first night*

A structured "first week" plan covering: medication self-management, shopping, cooking, transport, social contact. Not therapy — logistics. After institutional care, each daily task is a systems integration test.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on existing rehabilitation pathways -->

### R-13: Environmental design — sensory variation
*Source: Ch.2 — deep-2 at 14:15 (preferring cold); Ch.4 — courtyard*

Deep patients respond to physical stimuli that bypass cognitive processing. Cold air, texture, movement. Ward environments that eliminate all sensory variation also eliminate the inputs most likely to generate engagement. Consider: access to outdoor space beyond cigarette breaks, texture objects, temperature variation.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on sensory modulation rooms (existing evidence) -->

### R-14: Patient state self-assessment tool
*Source: Open question from PRD*

A simplified version of the Radix framework as a self-assessment card. Four axes, simple descriptors, patient circles where they are today. Not diagnostic — communicative. Gives the patient language for their experience and gives the nurse a faster path to understanding.

<!-- PM: Clinical commentary on feasibility, comparison with existing mood scales -->

### R-15: Flexible group therapy formats
*Source: Ch.4 — group therapy across all agents*

One-size group therapy serves sharp and wild patients (who can engage verbally) but fails drift and deep patients (who cannot). Consider: written prompt option, opt-out with 1:1 alternative, body-based alternatives (walking group, art, music).
