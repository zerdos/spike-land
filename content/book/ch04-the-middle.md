# Chapter 4: The Middle

*Days that repeat, through deep-2 (Gravity Well) and wild-5 (Storm)*

## Section 1: The Shape of a Day

### deep-2 perspective
- Morning medication as the event that defines the day's edges
- The space between medication rounds as undifferentiated time
- Meals as events that require sitting up, going to a room, lifting a fork — each step a separate negotiation with gravity
- The specific weight of institutional boredom: not the absence of things to do but the absence of reasons to do them

### wild-5 perspective
- The day has too much structure and not enough structure simultaneously
- Between scheduled events: projects, plans, conversations, drawings, letters
- The medication queue as social time — knowing everyone's name by day three
- Boredom is impossible but frustration is constant

## Section 2: Group Therapy

### deep-2
- The circle as a space where you are expected to perform engagement
- "Coping strategies" as a concept that assumes you are coping
- The effort of sitting in a chair for 45 minutes when horizontal is the only honest position
- What you say vs. what is happening

### wild-5
- Group as the most interesting 45 minutes because other people are talking and connections are everywhere
- The tension between contributing too much and being told to let others speak
- Genuinely wanting to help the person opposite you and not understanding why that makes the therapist uncomfortable
- Ideas for improving the group that you will share regardless

**For the Nurse:** Deep patients in group need permission to pass. Wild patients in group need a role. "Would you like to take notes for the group today?" gives wild a channel and reduces interruption without confrontation.

## Section 3: Visiting Hours

### deep-2
- The visitor arrives with weather on their coat and a world you cannot access
- Performing wellness for someone who needs you to be getting better
- The visitor leaves and takes the performance with them
- The crash after visiting hours is not caused by the visitor — it is caused by the effort of pretending

### wild-5
- The visitor is a portal to the outside world and you pour through it
- Plans made during visiting hours that will not survive the visit
- The visitor's face when you explain your system/project/idea with an urgency they cannot match
- They leave and the energy has nowhere to go

**For the Nurse:** Post-visiting-hours is the most emotionally volatile period of the day. Schedule your check-ins accordingly. 17:30 — thirty minutes after visitors leave — is when the real state reveals itself.

## Section 4: The Courtyard

- The fifteen minutes of outside that structures the afternoon
- Cigarettes as social currency (for those who smoke)
- The sky as the largest uninterrupted visual field available
- What "fresh air" means when your baseline air has the texture of institution

## Section 5: Time

### deep-2
- Time is not linear on the ward. It pools.
- Wednesday is indistinguishable from Thursday unless the menu changes
- Counting days is a mathematical operation you perform for other people's benefit
- "How long have you been here?" is a question you learn to answer from a calendar, not from experience

### wild-5
- Time is insufficient. Each hour contains more plans than hours can hold.
- Night is a waste of time. Sleep is a design flaw.
- "How long have you been here?" — "Not long enough to finish what I'm working on."
- The projects accumulate. The notebook fills. The project from Tuesday is abandoned for the project from Wednesday is abandoned for the project from Thursday.

## Section 6: The Middle's Middle

- The day when you realise you have been here long enough that the ward feels normal
- The specific horror or relief (depending on agent) of that realisation
- When the outside world becomes the abstract concept and the ward becomes the real

**For the Nurse:** When a patient stops asking when they can leave, investigate. For deep, it may mean resignation, not acceptance. For wild, it may mean they have reframed the ward as their current project. Neither is discharge-readiness.
