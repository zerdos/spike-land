# Chapter 3: Admission

*The first 72 hours through drift-0 (The Fog of Arrival) and sharp-3 (Interrogator)*

## Section 1: Arrival

### drift-0 perspective
- The ambulance or car or taxi. The door that locks behind you.
- Intake as a sequence of sounds directed at you: name, date of birth, next of kin, allergies, medication history
- The gap between being asked and being able to answer
- Belongings in a clear plastic bag. Why clear? Because transparency is policy even when the patient is opaque.

### sharp-3 perspective
- Mapping the building from the car park. Counting doors, noting locks, reading signs.
- Intake as an information asymmetry: they have a form, you have questions, only the form gets completed
- Noticing what they write. Noticing what they don't write. Noticing that they notice you noticing.

**For the Nurse:** The intake form is designed for the system, not the patient. Both drift and sharp patients experience intake as something that happens *to* them. One coping mechanism is absence (drift), the other is control-seeking (sharp). Neither is pathological. Both are rational responses to radical loss of agency.

## Section 2: The Room

- The bed, the window, the call button, the bathroom
- What patients inventory first (sharp) vs. what patients fail to notice (drift)
- The locked window. The observation window in the door.
- Night one: the lights that never fully go off

**For the Nurse:** The room tour matters more than you think. Walk the patient through everything, including things that seem obvious. "This is the call button. If you press it, someone will come within five minutes. Here is how the shower works. The window doesn't open but I can ask for a fan." Information converts sharp-anxiety into sharp-knowledge. For drift patients, the room tour may not register — do it again on day two.

## Section 3: First Medication Round

- The conversation about medication that happens at maximum cognitive load
- Consent when you cannot fully process what you are consenting to
- The first dose: what it feels like when something chemical changes the parameters

## Section 4: First Night

- Sounds: other patients, doors, the nurse's shoes at 02:00
- The bed that is not your bed
- Sleep as a concept that requires safety, and safety as a concept that requires time

## Section 5: Day Two — The Rules Emerge

- When the meal is, where the lounge is, who the staff are
- The unwritten rules: which chair is whose, who to avoid, who to approach
- The patient hierarchy that nobody acknowledges

## Section 6: Day Three — The Question

- The moment the patient asks themselves: how long?
- The answer they get vs. the answer they need
- The recalibration of time from "days" to "sections of day"

**For the Nurse:** "We'll see how you go" is the most anxiety-producing sentence in psychiatric care. It means nothing to drift and everything to sharp. Offer structure even if the timeline is uncertain: "We'll review on Friday. Between now and then, here is what will happen each day."
