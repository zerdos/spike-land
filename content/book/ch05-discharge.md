# Chapter 5: Discharge and After

*The gap between the ward and the world, through sharp-7 (Overload) and drift-4 (Re-entry)*

## Section 1: The Discharge Conversation

### sharp-7 perspective
- Maximum information arriving at maximum speed
- Medication changes, GP letters, crisis numbers, follow-up dates, housing, benefits, prescriptions
- Each item a node in a network you are trying to build while the network is being delivered
- Asking for written copies of everything and receiving some of them

### drift-4 perspective
- The fog is thinning but clarity brings anxiety
- Discharge as a concept that was abstract until today
- Nodding at information you will not retain
- Signing forms that represent agreement with plans you cannot fully parse

**For the Nurse:** Discharge education is currently delivered at the worst possible time — when the patient is simultaneously processing the logistics of leaving, the anxiety of re-entry, and the medication adjustments that preceded the decision. Separate the administrative discharge from the educational discharge. Paperwork today. Medication education tomorrow morning when they're rested. If you can't separate them: write everything down. Everything.

## Section 2: Medication Education

- The name of each medication, the dose, the schedule, the side effects, the interactions, what to do if you miss a dose, what to do if you take two, what to do if you feel worse
- This is a twelve-minute conversation that contains six months of daily decisions
- The difference between understanding and remembering
- The pharmacy bag as a physical object that contains your future stability

**For the Nurse:** Medication education should happen on at least three separate occasions before discharge, not once. Repetition is not redundancy — it is the mechanism by which information transfers from "I was told" to "I know."

## Section 3: Walking Out

### sharp-7
- The door opens outward. This is the first time it has opened outward for you.
- The car park / bus stop / whoever is collecting you
- The volume of the world: traffic, wind, distance, people walking without wristbands
- The first ten minutes of freedom as sensory bombardment

### drift-4
- Walking through a door that does not lock behind you
- The world is large and unstructured
- Nobody will bring you medication at 08:00. Nobody will check on you at 02:00.
- This is freedom. It does not feel like freedom. It feels like falling.

## Section 4: The First Supermarket

<!-- This section is about the specific, concrete experience of re-entering a normal environment -->

- The fluorescent lights that are the same as the ward but mean something different
- Choice as a concept you have not exercised in weeks
- Forty types of bread when the ward had one
- The self-checkout that expects you to function at normal speed
- The parking ticket machine that has changed since you went in

**For the Nurse:** Consider a "reorientation plan" as part of discharge. Not therapy. Logistics. "Your first week will include: going to a shop, managing your own medication schedule, cooking a meal, navigating public transport." These are not trivial. After institutional care, each is a systems integration test for a mind that has been running in controlled conditions.

## Section 5: The First Night

- The bed that is your bed but does not feel like your bed
- Nobody checking. Nobody present.
- The medication you take yourself at the time the ward would have given it, because the structure persists even without the institution
- Sleep as a test of whether the ward worked

## Section 6: The Follow-Up Appointment

- Three weeks away
- Twenty-one days between the ward and the next professional contact
- What happens in those twenty-one days when you are drift-4 or sharp-7
- The phone number on the discharge sheet that you are encouraged to call "if you need to" — the ambiguity of "need"

**For the Nurse:** The follow-up gap is the most dangerous structural flaw in psychiatric care. Twenty-one days is not a clinical decision — it is a scheduling constraint. If you cannot change the system, change what you give the patient: daily structure template, medication tracker, permission to call the ward directly, a named person they can ask for. Connection survives discharge. Anonymity does not.

## Closing

Discharge is not the end of care. It is the transfer of care from a system designed to provide it to a person who is still learning whether they can.

The agents do not stop at the ward door. Drift-4 goes home with you. Sharp-7 goes shopping with you. The framework applies wherever the patient is.

The question is whether the patient has been given enough to recognise their own states and respond to them. That is the goal of this book.
