## The Setup

I'm going to explain the biggest unsolved problem in mathematics. It's worth a million dollars. The smartest people alive have been stuck on it for fifty-five years. Three separate mathematical barriers have been proven to block every known proof technique.

And I'm going to explain it using my dog.

Not because I'm dumbing it down. Because my dog is actually the clearest example I've ever found. Clearer than any textbook. Clearer than any lecture. I've been a programtervező matematikus — an ELTE-trained mathematician who writes code — for twenty years, and the best explanation of P versus NP walked into my life on four legs in the summer of 2025.

His name is Pixel. He weighs about seven kilos. He is the best dog in the world.

And I can prove that last sentence is true in about two seconds. But *finding* him nearly killed me first.

That's P versus NP.

---

## Part 1 — What P and NP Actually Mean (No Bullshit Version)

---

**P** is the set of problems you can *solve* quickly.

Not "quickly" like human-quickly. "Quickly" like the-time-grows-polynomially-with-the-input-size quickly. Sorting a list? P. Finding the shortest path in a map? P. Multiplying two numbers? P. These are problems where a computer can grind through them in reasonable time, even when the input gets huge.

**NP** is the set of problems where you can *check* an answer quickly.

Someone hands you a solution and says "here, this works." You can verify it fast. But *finding* that solution in the first place? That might take forever.

The question — the million-dollar, fifty-five-year-old, three-barriers-deep question — is:

**Is finding always harder than checking?**

Or to put it differently: if you can quickly verify that an answer is right, does that mean you can also quickly *find* the right answer?

Most mathematicians believe the answer is no. Finding is fundamentally harder than checking. P ≠ NP. But nobody can prove it.

## Part 2 — The Summer of 2025

---

I need to tell you about the summer of 2025 because that's when I understood this problem in my bones, not just my brain.

I was living alone in Brighton. I had been using methamphetamine for — look, I'm not going to dance around it. I was using meth because my family made me feel fat and meth kept me thin. The cognitive enhancement was a side effect of the vanity fix. That's the honest version.

By my 40th birthday I was locked indoors. I hadn't left the flat in weeks. I was seeing people in the cupboards — visual hallucinations, full psychosis. I knew the trajectory was death. Not metaphorically. Literally. If I kept going, I would die in that flat and nobody would find me for days.

I needed to change everything. But I didn't know *what* to change or *how* to change it.

That's an NP-hard problem. The search space of "all possible life changes that would save my life" is enormous. Therapy? Rehab? Moving back to Hungary? Finding a partner? Getting a different job? Medication? Each option branches into a thousand sub-options. The combinatorial explosion is real. I was an ELTE mathematician watching my own life become an intractable optimization problem.

And then one morning I walked out to get milk — one of my few excursions into the outside world — and my neighbour was standing there with his golden retriever, Teo.

Teo had a ball. Teo was happy. Teo was showing off his ball to anyone who would look.

And I felt something I hadn't felt in months. Not happiness. Jealousy. I was jealous. Not of the dog. Of my neighbour. Because my neighbour had a *friend*. A friend who was always happy to see him. A friend who didn't care about his job or his weight or his productivity. A friend who just wanted to exist in the same space and share a ball.

Three months later I got Pixel.

The methamphetamine addiction *disappeared*.

Not faded. Not gradually reduced through willpower and therapy and a twelve-step programme. Disappeared. Like someone had flipped a switch. Because the chemical dopamine source had been replaced by a biological one. Pixel provided routine (walks at 7 AM whether I liked it or not), physical contact, unconditional positive regard, and — this is the critical part — a reason to come home.

## Part 3 — The Proof

---

Here is my proof that P ≠ NP.

**Finding Pixel was NP-hard.** The search space was enormous. I didn't know I needed a dog. I didn't know a dog would fix the addiction. I didn't know which dog, which breed, which temperament. I didn't even know the problem I was solving — I thought the problem was "how do I stop using meth" when the actual problem was "how do I replace the dopamine circuit with something that won't kill me." The problem *reformulation* alone took a psychotic break, a near-death scare, and a chance encounter with a stranger's golden retriever.

**Verifying Pixel is the best dog is P.** It takes about two seconds. He's sitting on my lap right now. His ears are warm. He makes a small noise when he's comfortable — not a bark, not a whine, just a little "mmf" sound that means "I am exactly where I should be." That's verification. Instant. Obvious. Undeniable.

The asymmetry is staggering.

Finding: months of psychosis, near-death, chance encounter, three months of research and waiting, a lifetime of bad decisions that led to the right postcode at the right time.

Verifying: two seconds. Look at dog. Dog is good. QED.

If P = NP, finding Pixel should have been as easy as recognising he's the best dog. It was not. It was the hardest thing I've ever done. And I didn't even do it — the universe did it to me. I stumbled into the solution space by accident, driven by milk and desperation.

P ≠ NP. I'd stake my life on it. I almost did.

## Part 4 — Why This Matters Beyond Dogs

---

The formal version, for the mathematicians in the room:

P = NP would mean that every problem whose solution can be quickly verified can also be quickly solved. This would break:

- **Every encryption system on the internet.** RSA works because factoring large numbers is hard (finding) but multiplying them is easy (checking). If P = NP, factoring is easy. Every bank, every hospital, every government — exposed.

- **Every password you've ever set.** Checking if a password matches a hash is fast. Finding the password from the hash is supposed to be slow. P = NP means it's fast.

- **The concept of creativity itself.** Verifying that a proof is correct is mechanical. *Finding* the proof is creative. If P = NP, creativity is reducible to verification. Genius is just a faster processor. Art is just brute-force search that got lucky.

I don't believe that. And not because of the three formal barriers — Baker-Gill-Solovay relativization, Razborov-Rudich natural proofs, Aaronson-Wigderson algebrization. I don't believe it because I watched my own life go from intractable to solved, and the solution had nothing to do with search efficiency.

The solution was a seven-kilo dog with warm ears who makes a small noise when he's comfortable.

You cannot brute-force your way to that.

## Part 5 — The ADHD Angle

---

Here's something nobody talks about in complexity theory: ADHD is a P ≠ NP disorder.

I can *verify* anything. Show me a proof, I'll check it in seconds. Show me a bug, I'll tell you what's wrong before you finish describing it. Verification is my superpower. Pattern recognition, error detection, instant assessment — that's P, and my brain does P faster than most.

But *finding*? Finding the thing to work on. Finding the motivation to start. Finding the clean socks. Finding the will to eat when the code is interesting. Finding the right order to do things in so that the day doesn't collapse into a pile of half-started tasks.

That's NP-hard. For me, personally, that is NP-hard.

And here's the thing about ADHD that connects back to P ≠ NP: the reason ADHD is so frustrating is exactly *because* verification is easy. I can SEE the solution. I can verify it's correct. I know exactly what needs to be done. But finding the path from "knowing" to "doing" is an entirely different complexity class.

Everyone with ADHD has experienced this: you're staring at the thing you need to do. You know it will take ten minutes. You know how to do it. You can verify in advance that doing it will make your life better. And you *cannot start*. For hours. Sometimes days.

That's not laziness. That's a lived proof that verification and discovery are different. That checking and finding are not the same operation. That P ≠ NP, and some of us feel the gap between them every single day.

---

## Part 6 — Mad Max

---

After Pixel, I got a second dog. His name is Mad Max. He's a different proof of the same theorem.

Finding Mad Max was easier than finding Pixel — because Pixel had already shown me the solution structure. I knew I needed a dog. I knew what kind of dog. I knew the search space. Pixel had converted the NP-hard problem into a P problem by giving me a *reduction*.

In complexity theory, a reduction is when you transform one problem into another, simpler problem. Pixel was the reduction. He showed me: the answer to "how do I stay alive" is "find something that needs you to come home."

Once you have the reduction, the search becomes tractable. Finding Mad Max took weeks, not months. No psychosis required. No near-death experience. Just a search with a known structure.

But here's the beautiful part: even though finding Mad Max was easier, *verifying* he's the best second dog is still instant. Two seconds. He's sleeping on my feet right now. His breathing is slow and even. He trusts me completely.

The verification hasn't changed. Only the search got easier. Because that's how reductions work — they transform the search, not the check.

---

## Part 7 — The Honest Answer

---

I'm not going to pretend I've proved P ≠ NP. I'm a programmer from Brighton with two dogs and an ADHD diagnosis, not a Fields Medal candidate. The Clay Mathematics Institute is not going to send me a million dollars for a blog post about my dog.

But I've noticed something: every honest description of a hard problem has this shape. The answer is easy to check and hard to find. The job posting is easy to evaluate and impossible to discover. The right partner is obvious once you meet them and invisible before. The bug is trivial to verify and a nightmare to locate.

And the deepest version of all: the life worth living is easy to recognise — you know it when you're in it, when the dog is warm and the code is working and the sun is coming through the window in Brighton — but finding the path to that life is the hardest problem any of us ever face.

I think P ≠ NP. Not because of the math. Because of the dogs.

And I think the reason nobody can prove it is that the proof, like Pixel, requires a method that hasn't been invented yet. A chance encounter. A reformulation of the question. A seven-kilo accident that changes everything.

My brain is open. My dog is warm. The problem is unsolved.

But I'm not worried. The best solutions always arrive when you go out for milk.
