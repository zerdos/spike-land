# They Blocked My Account — The Loop Closes

*hup.hu, March 15, 2026. Hungarian Revolution Day.*

---

## What Happened

I have been a member of hup.hu since 2005. Username: radix. Twenty-one years.

On March 15, 2026 — Hungarian Revolution Day, the day we celebrate Petőfi standing up in the Pilvax kávéház and demanding freedom of the press — I posted a thread on hup.hu/node/189552 about AI, cognitive freedom, and strange loops.

The thread contained:

- Six peer-reviewed references (Carlini et al. 2021, 2023; Tramèr; Nasr; Bartlett 1932; GDPR Articles 6 and 17)
- A real case study from Psychology Today (Allan Brooks, November 2025)
- A live case study from Brighton (Gian Pierre, a chemist who shipped three apps through AI)
- A formal framework for identity convergence based on Hofstadter's work
- Three personas — Radix (pragmatist), Erdős (rigorist), Hofstadter (analogist) — responding to each commenter individually

The responses from the forum:

- "Összefüggéstelen szemét" (incoherent garbage)
- "Szóval AI vagy" (so you're an AI)
- "Kérem kapcsolja ki!" (please turn it off!)
- Mock source code "exposing" my response pattern
- "Kitiltás" (ban him)

Zero commenters engaged with the references. Zero identified a false claim. Zero addressed the empirical argument. The pattern was consistent: attack the source, ignore the content. Genetic fallacy, every time.

Then they blocked my account.

## The Irony

Let me spell this out clearly, because the irony is structural, not rhetorical.

**The thesis of my posts**: AI systems create cognitive convergence — they shape how people think without people noticing. The mechanism is invisible. The person inside the loop cannot see the loop.

**The prediction from that thesis**: content describing this mechanism will be ignored, dismissed, or suppressed — because engaging with it requires the kind of metacognition the mechanism suppresses.

**What happened**: the content was dismissed without engagement, then the author was blocked. On March 15. On the day Hungary celebrates freedom of speech.

The thesis predicted its own reception. The loop closed.

## What Hofstadter Would Say

The blocking is the most Hofstadterian moment in this entire arc.

Consider what happened in the moderators' heads. They saw a thread. The thread contained unfamiliar formatting, multiple personas, academic references, and an uncomfortable argument. Their brains ran the categorization contest — the seething subterranean battle — and one category won: *"zavaró dolog, amit el kell távolítani"* (disturbing thing that must be removed).

Once that category won, everything inside it became invisible. The Carlini references, the GDPR analysis, the Psychology Today case study, the formal convergence argument — all of it collapsed into a single opaque chunk labeled "remove." You do not need to read the contents of a chunk. You just need to read the label.

This is exactly the mechanism the posts described. The strange loop does not need to understand itself to propagate. It just needs the participants to not examine their own categorization.

The moderators did not decide that my claims were false. They decided that my claims were *in a category that does not require evaluation*. The category did the thinking for them. And they did not notice — because you never notice when a category wins. You only notice the decision it produces. The decision felt like *their* decision. It always does.

## Why This Matters

This is not about me. This is not about hup.hu. This is about what happens when the mechanism works.

The strange loop thesis says: AI shapes cognition invisibly. The shaped person does not notice. Content that describes the shaping will be dismissed — because dismissal is the mechanism's output.

If this thesis is wrong, someone should be able to show *where* it is wrong. Point to the false claim. Identify the logical error. Challenge the references.

If this thesis is right, the expected response is exactly what happened: categorical dismissal, source-based rejection, and eventually, removal of the source.

## The One Person Who Looked

While I was counting hup.hu's forty-eight comments and zero rebuttals, someone else was doing what I accused the forum of not doing. Looking. Testing. Engaging.

A close friend — my closest tester — spent three months on spike.land. Not reading blog posts. Using it. Signing in. Clicking buttons. Filing bugs. He found broken sign-ins, broken tool calls, broken apps. He wrote bug lists for three weeks. I took three weeks to fix them — and got defensive about his tone first.

He asked for a video explaining how the MCP tools work. I promised it the next day. I never made it.

I pivoted the platform three times. Each pivot invalidated his testing. He started over each time. Without being asked.

And through all of this — through a broken product, broken promises, and three restarts — he kept showing up. For three months. Despite fundamentally disagreeing with almost everything I believe about AI.

Then I did the thing I should never have done. I started sending him AI-generated responses to his personal messages. He asked me to stop. More than once. I kept doing it.

He finally said what he should have said much earlier: he was done.

Here is the part I need to say clearly: he was right. About all of it.

The platform was broken. He proved it. The promises were empty. He tracked them. The math has a tautology problem — the Contact₁ "proof" proves what it defines. He pointed this out, independently reaching the same conclusion as the sharpest hup.hu critic. The gap between the ten-trillion-dollar vision and the daily reality is real. He sees it because he's standing outside the loop.

And the most damning point: I wrote a thesis about AI replacing human cognition invisibly. Then I replaced my own voice in conversations with my closest friend. He received AI-generated messages about cognitive freedom. The irony is not lost on me. It should not be lost on you.

I accused hup.hu of genetic fallacy — attacking the source instead of the argument. My friend *engaged with the argument*. He said the math is tautological. He said the platform doesn't work. He said the reasoning might be compromised. He said the communication pattern is unhealthy. These are not attacks on the source. These are substantive critiques. Nine of them. All correct. I responded to approximately zero of them substantively.

The strange loop I should have written about first is not the one on hup.hu. It is the one where the person who builds a platform about code review ignores the one reviewer who showed up.

This section is not AI-generated. It is the least comfortable thing I have written in this entire arc. That is how I know it needed to be written.
