## Most A/B Testing Asks the Wrong Question

Most teams use experiments to settle small arguments. Should the button be blue? Should the headline be shorter? Does pricing card A beat pricing card B?

These are fine questions. But they are not the most interesting ones.

The more valuable question is: **Which variant is less broken?**

Once you start asking that, A/B testing transforms. It stops being a growth-only tool — the kind of thing marketing teams use to squeeze out conversion points — and starts becoming a quality system. A feedback loop that catches instability before users feel it.

Think of it like a thermostat. A thermostat does not just tell you the temperature. It keeps the system within bounds. That is what we want experiments to do: not just measure, but regulate.

---

## Two Systems, One Loop

spike.land has two related pieces here, and the interesting part is how they connect.

The first is the **app-store-facing workflow**: deployments, variants, visitor assignment, impression tracking, error tracking, and winner declaration. This is the visible machinery.

The second is the **general experiments engine**: hash-based assignment, event tracking, Bayesian evaluation, and anomaly monitoring. This is the invisible machinery.

Together, they form a single loop:

1. Ship multiple versions of something
2. Watch what users actually hit
3. Measure errors alongside outcomes
4. Promote the better version only when the data justifies it

The key word is "only." Most systems are too eager to declare winners. Ours is deliberately slow about it.

---

## Where AI Belongs (and Where It Does Not)

The useful role for AI here is not "decide the winner." That would be like letting the person who generated the hypothesis also grade the experiment. The roles need separation.

AI is strong at:

- generating a cleaner variant
- proposing a fallback path when something breaks
- clustering error patterns into likely bug themes
- explaining why a losing variant probably failed

In systems thinking terms, AI is good at the generative side — the part that increases variety. But when it comes to shipping, the platform uses explicit rules. The selection side stays deterministic.

This separation matters. It is the difference between a system that learns and a system that hallucinates progress.

---

## The Shipping Rules

The experiment engine is not subtle about its thresholds. Before a winner can graduate automatically, the experiment must satisfy all four conditions:

- At least **48 hours** of runtime
- At least **500 impressions** per variant
- A winning probability above **95%**
- At least **10% lift** over control (unless control itself wins)

Why these specific numbers? Because they represent the minimum evidence where a wrong decision becomes unlikely enough to accept. Under these thresholds, you are guessing. Above them, you are deciding.

We let AI help us explore. We do not let it hallucinate certainty.

---

## Bayesian Instead of Wishful

I like Bayesian evaluation here because it matches the real question you are trying to answer in production:

**How likely is it that this variant is actually better?**

That is a fundamentally different question from "did the metric go up?" A metric going up is a story. A posterior probability is an argument.

The engine samples the posterior for each variant, compares them repeatedly, and measures which one wins most often. That gives us a probability, not just a green checkmark on a dashboard.

It is the difference between a weather forecast that says "sunny" and one that says "82% chance of sun." The second one lets you make a real decision.

---

## Bug Detection, Not Just Lift Detection

This is where the growth-metric framing falls apart. Lift is a narrative. Error rate is a fact.

The quality angle shows up in two places.

First, **error-heavy variants reveal themselves quickly**. If one version produces meaningfully worse runtime behavior, you do not need a long product debate. The data is already telling you something. A variant that crashes 3% of the time is not "losing the conversion test" — it is broken.

Second, **anomaly monitoring catches operational failures**. If an active variant suddenly drops to zero impressions, that is not a copywriting problem. That is a routing or rollout problem. Something in the system is no longer connected.

These are bug signals, not growth signals. And they are the more important ones to catch early, because a bug that ships is far more expensive than a headline that underperforms.

---

## What This Means for an Open App Store

An open app store only works if quality can scale without human gatekeepers reviewing every submission.

The more developers publish, the more chances there are for unstable behavior to reach users. The answer cannot be "manually inspect everything forever." That approach does not scale — it just creates a bottleneck that looks responsible.

So the platform needs a self-regulating loop:

`generate -> test -> observe -> promote -> learn`

AI handles generation and diagnosis. The experimentation system handles measurement and promotion. Together they form a reinforcing loop: better variants get promoted, broken ones get caught, and the system's overall quality rises over time without someone manually tending it.

That is the real point of AI-powered A/B bug detection on spike.land. It is not a slogan. It is the platform learning how to ship safer apps at store scale — a quality system that gets smarter the more it runs.
