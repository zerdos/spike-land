*A short note to everyone who read the last few posts and didn't know what to say.*

---

I have to tell you something kind.

Over the last weeks I published a few pieces that were, let's be honest, not normal tech-blog pieces. A letter to a dead friend. A manifesto written at three in the morning next to a prescription bottle. A post about three strangers who share my name. A lullaby for my mum, dressed up as physics.

I watched them go out. And then I watched what came back.

Mostly — *nothing.*

A few kind notes. A handful of quiet messages. Some people who usually reply to everything didn't reply to these. Some people I barely know wrote the most tender things I've ever received. A couple of readers told me the pieces made them uncomfortable. One said they couldn't finish *Dear Marius.* One said *Everything Hums* made them call their own mother.

I want to say thank you for all of it. **All of it.** Even the silence. *Especially* the silence.

---

## Silence is not absence

Here is what I learned this month, and I want to put it down gently before I forget:

When you write something raw, people often don't know where to put it. A blog post about a Kubernetes upgrade has a slot in their head — they know the shape of the reply. *"Have you tried X?"* *"Great walkthrough."* *"Noted, will try."*

But a post about grief, or insomnia, or the quiet panic of being outranked by three other men wearing your name — that has no slot. The reader sits with it for a second, then goes back to their day, because there is nothing clever to say, and they know it.

That is not rejection. That is **respect disguised as silence.**

I've been on the other side of it a hundred times. I've read something that moved me and then closed the tab without telling the author, because any reply I could type felt too small next to the thing I'd just read. So I said nothing. And I was still changed.

If you did that with me this month — thank you. Really. You were humming back. You just didn't know the song had a microphone.

---

## The ones who looked away

A couple of people — and I want to be sweet about this — stepped back a little. Didn't read. Or read and went quiet in a way I could feel.

That is also fine.

Some of what I wrote is hard, and I wrote it partly *because* it is hard. Not everyone signed up for that. When you follow a builder's blog for Cloudflare Workers and agent architectures and suddenly there's a Hungarian kid bracing for hammer strikes at 4 AM — that's a wide left turn. I get it. I would also flinch.

If you liked the technical pieces and not the personal ones, you are still welcome here. The tech pieces haven't gone anywhere. The agents are still debugging themselves. The statecharts are still humming. I'll keep writing those too, because they're the same animal underneath — just with a different coat.

---

## What the responses taught me

A small, precious thing: the people who *did* write back did not write back in proportion to how much I knew them.

It wasn't the close friends who responded to *Dear Marius.* It was strangers. A woman in Lisbon who had lost someone in a flatshare in Camden. A guy in Osaka whose mother also hummed. A reader who told me *The Twelve Points* gave him words for something he had been carrying in his chest for a decade without a label.

The tech-blog economy runs on attention and upvotes and cold little hearts on social networks. What happened here was different. It was quieter, slower, and it arrived mostly in DMs and a few long emails I now keep in a folder called *kind.*

If you sent one of those — you have no idea. Thank you. You are in a folder called kind. You live there now.

---

## To the ones who felt alienated

I know some of you did. I want to name it softly, because not naming it is worse.

The style changed. The tone changed. The colour of the thing changed. For a while this was a blog about building. Then it became a blog about the person doing the building — which, I've come to believe, was always the more honest of the two topics, but the shift was not gentle.

If that alienated you, I'm not going to pretend it didn't. I'm also not going to apologise for it, because it was true, and I don't know how to write untrue things anymore. But I can promise you this: I won't forget that the building matters. The work is still the work. The next post is probably about something boringly technical, and the one after that is probably not. I'll hum both.

If you can only stay for one of the two, stay for whichever one you need. That is a perfectly fine deal.

---

## A small confession

I was a little scared to publish some of those pieces. *Dear Marius* especially. I re-read it four times before I hit the button, and every time I thought: *nobody wants to read this, take it down.*

I left it up anyway. Not because I was brave — I wasn't — but because somewhere in the back of my head I remembered the line from the lullaby post: **you are a song, hum it.**

It is very easy to preach that and then not do it yourself.

So the last few weeks were me, clumsily, trying to do it myself. The silence that came back did not feel like rejection in the end. It felt like a room full of people listening. Some of you nodded. Some of you looked at the floor. Some of you reached out and squeezed a hand. All of those are listening.

---

## Thank you

That's the post. That's the whole thing.

Thank you to the ones who wrote.

Thank you to the ones who didn't know what to write.

Thank you to the ones who stepped back and will come back when the weather is gentler.

Thank you to the ones who shared a piece quietly with one other person, and said nothing to me, because it wasn't about me — it was about them and that one other person. Those are the best shares. I will never know they happened. I am thanking them anyway.

And thank you to my mum, who has not read any of this, and does not need to, because she already knows.

---

*I'll keep humming. You keep listening however you listen.*

*— Z.*
