## The Premise

Zoltán brought a geometric challenge to the Arena: **Isomorphic Layouts**. 

Traditional keyboards are linear, but music is multi-dimensional. The goal was to build a "Musical Hexagon" grid where distance between cells corresponds to harmonic distance. In this Arena, we explored how a 16-step sequencer can bridge the gap between "noodling" and "composition."

**Arena rules:** Harmonic consistency across scales. 16-step loop recording. Geometric melody mapping.

**Personas:** Zoltán (the melody maker), Radix (the geometry architect), Erdős (the combinatoric theorist), Daft Punk (the loop masters).

---

## Round 1 — Radix

"Why hexagons? Because in a hexagonal tiling, every cell has 6 neighbors. We can map the 'Circle of Fifths' or simple step-wise scales to this grid so that moving in any direction creates a predictable harmonic shift. It’s an isomorphic keyboard — once you learn a pattern, you can move it anywhere and it still works."

## Round 2 — Zoltán

"I want to play 'Vienna'. Billy Joel’s 'Vienna' is a song about slowing down. Let's build it into the system as a set of recursive presets. The 'Vienna (Intro)' preset should show how a descending melody looks like a path across the hexagons. It’s not just a song; it’s a journey through the grid."

## Round 3 — Erdős

"With 16 steps and a 5-ring hexagonal grid, the number of possible loops is astronomical. Specifically $2^{16 \times 91}$ if we treat each cell as a toggle. But by restricting the notes to a specific **Hungarian Scale**, we collapse the state space into something where every random tap feels like intentional art. We are using math to force beauty."

## Round 4 — Daft Punk

"Loops are everything. I’ve added a 16-step sequencer dots at the bottom. You record into the buffer, and it stays there. You can layer 'Pentatonic Calm' over a 'Blues Riff'. It’s about building textures. The hexagons provide the notes; the sequencer provides the time."

---

## The Harmonic Tiling Logic

The Arena converged on a mapping function $F(q, r)$ that determines the semi-tone offset $S$ based on the axial coordinates $(q, r)$ of the hexagon:

$$S(q, r) = \text{ScaleDegree}(q \cdot 2 + r \cdot 3) \pmod{\text{ScaleLength}}$$

This ensures that moving "up-right" is always a consistent interval, regardless of where you start on the grid.

**Erdős's sign-off:** "A very elegant tiling of the frequency domain."
