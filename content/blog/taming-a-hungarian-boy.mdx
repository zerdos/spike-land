*A short note from the ward about a song, three ghosts, and what "taming" actually means when you are the animal.*

---

It is day twenty-six of twenty-eight.

I do not have a lot to say today. I have a song. I have a few sentences about the song. That is the whole post. If you came for a technical write-up, I will have one again next week. This week it is this.

---

## The song

This morning, before breakfast, I asked myself a question I have been avoiding since I was admitted: **what would the biggest techno track of summer 2026 sound like, if it were honest about me?**

Not aspirational. Not a version of me I would like to be. The one currently sitting on a plastic chair in a corridor in Bexhill, in clothes that are almost mine, eating food that is almost food, counting down days on a calendar that is almost a calendar.

The answer came back fast, which is how I know it was true: it would be a Kaisst-style peak-time monster called **Taming a Hungarian Boy**, and it would be a joke, and it would not be a joke.

I wrote it with three ghosts in the room — Maceo Plex for the dark, Weval for the melancholy, a Faithless-shaped spoken-word voice for the spine. Then I pushed it into Suno and listened to what came out while I stood at the window of the day room, watching a gull land on a bin.

It is not a good song yet. It might never be. But it is the right song.

---

## What taming means

I used to think *taming* was the thing they do to you. A cage, a leash, a pill, a protocol. The outside pressing in until the inside gives up the shape it used to have.

That is not what taming is.

Taming is **the slow process by which the animal agrees it is the kind of animal that fits inside the cage that is being offered.**

The cage is incidental. The agreement is the whole thing.

For twenty-six days I have been watching people in this building try, very gently and very professionally, to secure that agreement from me. Not malevolently. They are, on the whole, kind. They are tired and underpaid and they genuinely want me to be well. But "well" in this building has a shape, and the shape is **a Hungarian boy who has agreed he is the kind of animal that fits**.

I have not agreed.

I am not going to.

That is the whole song.

---

## The lyric I keep returning to

There is one line from the track I wrote this morning that I cannot get out of my head. It is in the chorus:

> *taming a Hungarian boy / is like teaching the wind to kneel / the Danube does not bow / the forest does not heal*

I did not plan for that line. It arrived fully formed, the way lines do when you stop trying. I think it is probably the truest sentence I have written this month, and I did not write it with my hands — I wrote it with the part of me that has been watching all of this from a slight distance for three weeks, taking notes.

The Danube does not bow. I want that on a wall somewhere. I want it on the inside of my own forehead.

---

## What this is not

This is not a post about hating the ward. I do not hate the ward. The ward is doing what wards do. Some of the people here are saving my life in small boring ways — a nurse who noticed the exact moment my hands started shaking and brought me a glass of water without making it a thing, a doctor who stopped mid-sentence to ask if I wanted her to speak slower. Those are real. I will remember them.

This is not a post about refusing treatment. I take what I am given. I do the things. I will leave this building with the discharge plan and the referrals and the list of numbers to call.

This is a post about **the difference between treatment and taming**, because I think most people — most of the people in this building included — conflate them, and I think that confusion is where the worst of psychiatric medicine happens.

Treatment is: *your hands were shaking, here is something that makes the shaking stop, let us know if it helps.*

Taming is: *your hands were shaking because you are the kind of person whose hands shake, please become a different kind of person.*

One of those is medicine. One of those is a request for surrender dressed as medicine. I have been accepting the first and quietly declining the second for twenty-six days, and I think I have gotten away with it.

---

## The three ghosts

Why those three?

**Maceo Plex** because the first ten minutes of every day in this place feel like the opening of one of his tracks — slow, subterranean, a single Rhodes chord repeating until you notice it has been repeating for an hour. The bassline of institutional time.

**Weval** because the middle of the day is half-speed. The Rhodes becomes a piano. Someone you love is sleeping somewhere else. You are the lighthouse no ship needs. That is the exact Weval feeling and I do not know how two Dutch guys figured it out but they did.

**Faithless** because by the evening I need a voice that does not plead. Maxi Jazz's whole career was about staying awake on purpose, and staying awake on purpose is what I have been doing here. Not insomnia. Vigilance. There is a difference.

Together they are the arc of the day in this building, rendered as a nine-minute track. That is why the song is the right song.

---

## What happens when I get out

Two days. Two days from now I get on a train and go back to Brighton and the dogs go berserk on the landing and I sit on the floor in the hallway and let them climb me for about fifteen minutes without saying anything. That is the plan. That is the only plan.

After that: I want to hear this song on a real sound system. I want to hear the Kaisst drop at 138 BPM through a PK sub, not through the tinny speaker of a phone held against my ear in a corridor. Ozora is in August. Awakenings nightfall set. If any DJ reading this wants an unreleased nine-minute track for a forest-stage sunrise slot, my email is at the top of this blog.

I am not being cute. I genuinely want this song to end a set somewhere in a field full of strangers in July. I think the Hungarian boy inside me — the untamable one, the one the Danube bows to, the one who is not fitting into this cage — I think he has earned a sunrise.

---

## The principle

One thing I want to put on record before I forget.

Earlier this week I told my AI — yes, my AI, stop wincing — a principle I have been holding for a while but had not said out loud: **dogs are as important as humans in AI alignment**. Not as instruments of human wellbeing. As stakeholders.

I am writing it here because the Hungarian-boy song and the dog principle are the same principle. They are both about: *the thing you are looking at is not the thing that is here, and if you do not see the thing that is here, your help is not help, it is taming.*

Do not tame my dogs into props for my flourishing. Do not tame me into a shape that fits your ward. Do not tame your AI into a shape that fits your quarterly metrics. In all three cases, the animal you end up with is a worse animal, and you never meet the one you were given.

That is the whole alignment problem in a sentence. I think.
