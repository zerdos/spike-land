# Twelve Things Nobody Is Predicting About AI

---

Two of us wrote lists. Independently. Different continents, different jobs, different starting assumptions. Three of the predictions overlapped almost word for word. The rest were complementary. We sat with that for a while. Then we combined them, cut the weak ones, and kept twelve.

The rule was simple: if it shows up in a search result for "top 10 AI predictions," it's out. No mass unemployment. No Skynet. No "AI will revolutionize healthcare." Those aren't wrong. They're just boring. Everyone's already pricing them in.

These are the ones nobody's pricing in.

---

## 1. The Motivation Crisis

Not mass unemployment but mass purposelessness. The psychological reward loop of getting good at something collapses when AI does it better from day one. You don't lose your job. You lose the reason you got up early to get good at it.

We see a generation-wide aimlessness that doesn't map neatly onto depression. It's more like a quiet withdrawal from effort. The kid doesn't stop coding. He just stops caring whether his code is good, because the bar moved somewhere he can't reach. The mental health system has no diagnostic code for this. It's not sadness. It's the evaporation of a reason to try hard.

This is the big one. Everything else downstream.

## 2. Cooking Survives Everything

Every other creative act gets augmented, automated, or drowned in AI output. Cooking survives. Not because AI can't generate recipes or optimise nutrition or even control a robot arm. But because cooking is the only creative act where the output is literally consumed in the same room as the creator, by the same people, within minutes.

You can't email a smell. You can't stream the texture of bread you just tore apart with your hands. The feedback loop between maker and audience is so tight, so physical, so immediate that there's no seam for AI to slip into.

Cooking becomes what live music was after Spotify. The thing you do to prove you were in the room.

## 3. Taste Becomes the Apex Skill

When execution is cheap, knowing what's worth executing becomes the bottleneck. "Taste" overtakes technical skill as the primary economic differentiator. The ability to look at forty options and say *that one, not the other thirty-nine* is suddenly the hardest, most valuable thing a person can do.

This inverts most of our education and hiring systems, which are built to reward execution. We train people to do things. We don't train people to know which things are worth doing. The person who can't code but knows exactly what should be built becomes more valuable than the person who can build anything but doesn't know what matters.

Nobody has figured out how to teach this. Possibly nobody can.

## 4. Physical Fitness Becomes Cognitive Infrastructure

When the bottleneck for knowledge work shifts from "how much do you know" to "how clearly can you think in a 20-minute window between AI outputs," your cardiovascular system becomes your most important professional tool. Not in the vague "exercise is good for you" way. In the "your VO2 max correlates with your performance review" way.

The person who can sustain sharp judgment across a 12-hour day of evaluating AI outputs, making taste calls, catching subtle errors that the model won't flag, that person is an athlete. The gym becomes the new continuing education. Companies subsidize running shoes the way they subsidize laptops.

This is the most testable prediction on this list. Check back in five years.

## 5. A Religious Resurgence

When AI handles the rational, productive, and analytical dimensions of life, people lean harder into the transcendent. Not a return to 1950s churchgoing. Something weirder. New syncretic movements. AI-informed mysticism. Communities organised around meaning-making rather than production. The "secular by default" era turns out to have been a blip, a historical anomaly enabled by the fact that rationality and productivity were still hard enough to keep people busy.

When those stop being hard, the questions underneath them surface. Why am I here? What is this for? The questions don't have rational answers. That's the point.

## 6. Forgetting Becomes an Offense

When your AI can recall every conversation you've had with someone, every promise, every birthday, every preference they mentioned once in passing three years ago, "I forgot" stops being a minor social lapse. It becomes a choice.

"I forgot your birthday" used to mean "my brain is imperfect." Now it means "I chose not to have my system remind me of your birthday." The social meaning of memory inverts. Forgetting was human. Now it's negligent. People get genuinely angry at being forgotten in ways that would have seemed unhinged a decade ago, because now there's no excuse.

The institutional version of this is worse. Hospitals forget triage protocols. Governments forget regulatory logic. Companies forget why they made decisions. Not because of a catastrophe. Because the knowledge was offloaded to AI systems, and when those systems get swapped or updated or quietly deprecated, nobody remembers how things were actually done. The muscle atrophied. The knowledge didn't get lost. It just evaporated, like water from a stock that nobody was watching.

## 7. Proof-of-Human as Social Currency

A new kind of loneliness emerges. Not from isolation but from the uncanny abundance of AI interaction. You talk to AI all day. It's helpful. It's patient. It remembers your preferences. And you feel nothing. Not bad. Just nothing.

People develop a hunger for verifiably human contact. Restaurants where humans visibly cook. Schools that market their lack of AI. "Handmade" becomes a prestige label not for goods but for experiences and relationships. The premium isn't on quality. It's on provenance.

The first time someone pays double for a worse product because a human made it, the economics of the next twenty years become visible.

## 8. The Viable City-State

AI collapses the minimum viable scale of governance. You no longer need millions of citizens to sustain a functional civil service, military intelligence apparatus, or diplomatic corps. A city of 300,000 with good AI infrastructure can run governance at a level that previously required a mid-sized nation.

Small countries, wealthy cities, and even large private entities become genuinely sovereign-capable. Singapore already operates this way. Dubai is trying. The interesting cases will be the ones nobody expects: a Baltic city, an African tech hub, a wealthy European municipality that quietly starts running its own foreign policy because it can.

The Westphalian nation-state doesn't die. It just gets a lot more competition from below.

## 9. Dialects Get Stronger

AI standardizes written language globally. Everyone's emails and documents converge toward the same clean, fluent, accent-less prose. The model's voice becomes the default voice. You can't tell if a paragraph was written in Lagos or Lisbon or Louisville.

In response, spoken dialects become fierce identity markers. People deliberately cultivate regional speech as proof of origin. The way you talk in Debrecen vs. Budapest. Cockney. Appalachian vowels. Catalan. The less your writing distinguishes you, the more your mouth does.

Written language becomes a utility, like plumbing. Spoken language becomes a flag. The sociolinguists will have a field day. The language preservation people will be confused, because the languages aren't dying. They're splitting: the written form converges, the spoken form diverges. Same language, two trajectories.

## 10. Price Signals Break Down

AI-driven production makes so many goods and services nearly free that price loses its function as an information carrier. A price is supposed to tell you something about scarcity, demand, and the cost of alternatives. When the cost of producing a thing drops to near zero, the price tells you nothing.

Markets still exist but become increasingly poor at coordinating complex activity. We muddle into post-market coordination mechanisms not out of ideology, not because someone read Marx and got inspired, but because the old signals stop working in sector after sector as marginal cost hits zero.

Nobody plans this. Nobody names it. It just happens, the way the gold standard ended: not with a declaration but with a series of pragmatic exceptions that eventually became the rule.

## 11. The Oral Culture Returns

Writing as a daily skill quietly atrophies. Not literacy itself. People can still read. But the habit and ability to compose structured text declines sharply. Communication shifts to voice and video, with AI handling transcription, summarisation, and documentation.

This happens for a reason deeper than laziness: text becomes untrustworthy (could be AI), photos become untrustworthy (generated), video becomes untrustworthy (deepfaked). What remains reliable? Being in the same room as someone, hearing them speak, watching their hands.

Live presence becomes the gold standard of authenticity. Pre-literate cultures valued oral testimony and physical witness because they didn't have reliable recording. We return to valuing them because we have too much recording and no way to tell which of it is real.

Writing becomes a specialist craft, like calligraphy. Most people go months without composing a paragraph. The podcast isn't a trend. It's the early form of the dominant medium.

## 12. Biological Enhancement Stops Being Optional

AI-designed gene therapies and cognitive modifications arrive not as luxury upgrades but as a competitive response. The framing shifts from "would you like to be enhanced?" to "can you still function without it?"

Unmodified humans become economically marginal in enough domains that modification stops feeling like a choice and starts feeling like an obligation. Like glasses. Like vaccines. Like literacy. Something you technically don't have to do, but the world is built assuming you did.

The ethics debate happens after the adoption curve, not before. It always does. We argued about the morality of IVF for decades while millions of children were being born through it. Enhancement follows the same pattern: by the time the philosophers reach consensus, the question is already settled by ten million early adopters who couldn't afford to wait.

---

## A note on overlap

Two people wrote these lists independently. Three predictions appeared on both: taste as apex skill, the return of oral culture, and the slow atrophy of knowledge and effort. We didn't coordinate. We don't even live in the same country.

That's either a coincidence or a signal. Systems thinking says: when two observers in different positions see the same structure, the structure is probably real. The overlap isn't where we agreed. It's where the system is loudest.

The things only one of us saw are more interesting. That's where the peripheral vision lives. Pay attention to the ones that surprised you. Those are the ones you haven't priced in yet.
