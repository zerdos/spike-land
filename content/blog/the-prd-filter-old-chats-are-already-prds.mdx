<Tldr>

- Most long AI chats are not really conversation. They are broken PRDs spread across too many turns.
- The right move is not to replay the whole transcript. It is to extract the smallest truthful execution artifact.
- A PRD filter turns messy chat into task, constraints, acceptance criteria, context, and priority.
- That changes how much one developer can ship.

</Tldr>

Most people think the main problem with AI chat is context window size.

That is not the real problem.

The real problem is representation.

Most systems keep the conversation in transcript form long after the useful part of the conversation has already finished.

They preserve everything:

- false starts
- stale tool results
- repeated explanations
- outdated assumptions
- detours that no longer matter

Then they call that “context.”

It is not context.

It is a badly managed working set.

## The PRD Filter

Here is the claim:

> most long chats are already PRDs. They are just trapped inside transcript form.

By turn 20, the user has usually already said the important parts:

- what they want
- what must not break
- what success looks like
- what environment they are in
- what constraints they care about
- what edge cases are making the task awkward

That is already enough for real work.

Not a corporate PRD full of roadmap theater.

A real one.

Small.
Operational.
Executable.

The mistake is leaving it scattered across old messages.

The right system extracts the useful shape:

- `intent`
- `task`
- `constraints`
- `acceptance`
- `context`
- `priority`

That is enough to build, debug, review, deploy, or analyze.

Once you have that shape, old chats stop being baggage.

They become source material.

## Why old chats should become PRDs

Transcript-first systems keep asking the model to reread the archaeology of the task.

That is expensive and wasteful.

The model does not need to relive the conversation.

It needs the final truth that emerged from the conversation.

If a user spent forty messages clarifying a feature, the answer is not:

> send all forty messages back forever

The answer is:

> extract the requirement and carry that forward instead

This is the real job of the PRD filter.

It is not summarization.

It is not note taking.

It is not chat cleanup.

It is a representation upgrade.

You are converting conversational entropy into an execution artifact.

That changes the economics of building software.

## What the filter actually keeps

A good PRD filter is ruthless.

It does not care about the emotional history of the conversation.

It cares about the minimum truthful state required to continue.

That usually means:

- the core task in one sentence
- a handful of hard constraints
- acceptance criteria tight enough to test
- relevant technical or business context
- an honest priority level

Everything else is optional.

This is why old chats are valuable.

They contain negotiation.
They contain corrections.
They contain hidden constraints the user forgot to say at the start.

The transcript is noisy.

The requirement hidden inside it is not.

## Why this matters in practice

Once you see this clearly, the workflow changes.

The real work is:

1. extract the requirement
2. formalize the capability
3. enforce the constraints
4. verify the output

The code is downstream of that.

Once the requirement is sharp, implementation gets much lighter.

That is why the PRD filter matters.

It is not a UI trick.
It is not a prompt trick.
It is not magic.

It is a system for keeping the working set honest.

## Why this matters for solo developers

If you are working alone, transcript sludge is expensive.

You do not need more chat history.

You need cleaner state.

A PRD filter reduces the amount of nonsense both the model and the human have to carry.

It turns “what were we doing?” into a compact artifact you can trust.

That is one of the big reasons a solo developer can now move faster than teams that still organize around meetings, handoffs, and transcript soup.

## The strategic implication

The future of AI software is not bigger transcript buffers.

It is better compression boundaries.

The best systems will not be the ones that remember the most.

They will be the ones that preserve the cleanest artifact of what matters now.

Old chats are not dead weight.

They are latent PRDs.

If your system cannot turn them into executable requirements, it is wasting the most valuable thing the conversation produced.

Not because models are too weak.

Because the artifact they are asked to carry forward is too messy.

That is fixable.

And once you fix it, one developer can ship a lot more than people expect.
