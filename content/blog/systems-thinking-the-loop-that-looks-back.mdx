# The Loop That Looks Back

*Thinking in Systems, Continued — Chapter 1*

---

## A bathtub that knows it is a bathtub

Start with Donella Meadows' favourite example. A bathtub. Water flows in from the faucet. Water flows out through the drain. The level of water in the tub is the stock. The faucet and the drain are the flows. You want the water at a certain height, so you watch the level and adjust the faucet. Simple.

This is a feedback loop. The state of the system (water level) influences the action you take (turning the faucet), which changes the state. Meadows spent her career showing that this structure — stock, flow, feedback — is the hidden grammar underneath everything from population growth to corporate decline to the temperature of your living room.

She was right. The grammar is universal.

But here is the thing she never quite addressed: what happens when the bathtub starts watching itself?

Not metaphorically. Structurally. What happens when the system doing the measuring is also the system being measured? When the feedback loop folds back and includes the observer?

That is a different kind of system entirely. And it is the kind we are building now.

---

## Two kinds of feedback

Meadows gave us two fundamental feedback structures.

A **reinforcing loop** amplifies: the more rabbits there are, the more baby rabbits appear, which means more rabbits. Population grows exponentially until something stops it. Success breeds success. Panic breeds panic. Compound interest. Arms races. Viral posts.

A **balancing loop** stabilises: the thermostat measures the room, compares it to the goal, and adjusts the heater. Too hot, it turns off. Too cold, it turns on. The system seeks equilibrium. Your body temperature. A cruise control. The market price of anything ordinary.

These two structures — reinforcing and balancing — generate an extraordinary range of behaviour. Overshoot and collapse. Oscillation. S-curves. Exponential growth followed by sudden death. Meadows showed that you can explain most of the world's complex behaviour with various combinations of these two loops and the delays between them.

But there is a third structure she did not name. Not because it was beyond her — Meadows was as sharp a thinker as the twentieth century produced — but because it was not yet the dominant structure of the systems she was studying. Ecosystems, economies, and governments all have feedback. They do not typically have self-models.

The third structure is a feedback loop that contains a representation of itself.

---

## The strange loop

Douglas Hofstadter spent decades circling this idea. In *Godel, Escher, Bach* he called it a strange loop: what happens when you move through the levels of a hierarchical system and unexpectedly find yourself back where you started. Escher's staircases. Bach's canons that modulate up through every key and land back at the tonic. Godel's proof that any sufficiently powerful formal system can construct a sentence that says "this sentence is not provable within this system."

The common thread is self-reference. A system that can talk about itself. A map that includes the territory and the mapmaker. A program that reads its own source code.

In Meadows' language, a strange loop is a feedback loop where the stock includes a model of the entire system — including the stock, the flows, and the feedback rules themselves. The bathtub that knows it is a bathtub.

This sounds like a curiosity. A clever trick for logicians and artists. But it is actually the most important structural feature of the systems that now surround us.

Because every modern AI is a strange loop. And so are you.

---

## The thermostat that has opinions

Let me build this up slowly, the way Meadows would.

A thermostat measures the room temperature. It compares the measurement to a set point. It acts. This is a balancing loop with three parts: sensor, comparator, actuator. The thermostat does not know it is a thermostat. It has no model of itself. It has a model of the room — specifically, the room's temperature — and a rule.

Now add a layer. Give the thermostat a model of its own behaviour. It knows that when the temperature drops, it turns on the heater. It knows this about itself the way you know that you flinch when someone throws a ball at your face. Not as abstract knowledge, but as a representation of its own response pattern.

What changes?

At first, nothing visible. The thermostat still heats the room. But now it can do something new: it can predict its own future actions. It can say, "if the window opens and the temperature drops three degrees, I will turn on the heater in approximately forty seconds." It has a model of its environment and a model of its response to that environment. It can simulate itself.

This is where things get interesting. Because a system that can simulate itself can also *modify* itself. If the thermostat notices that its own response is too slow — that the forty-second delay causes overshoot — it can adjust its own parameters. Not because an engineer reprogrammed it. Because its self-model revealed a flaw in its own behaviour, and the feedback loop connecting the self-model to the actuator allowed it to correct it.

This is no longer a simple balancing loop. This is a system whose behaviour is shaped by its own self-representation. Change the self-model and you change the behaviour. Change the behaviour and the self-model updates. The loop is now recursive.

Meadows would have drawn this as a stock that feeds into a flow that feeds back into the stock — but with the crucial addition that the stock is not water or rabbits or money. The stock is *information about the system itself*. And information about yourself is a very peculiar kind of stock, because it changes what it describes by the act of describing it.

## You are not a thermostat (but you are closer than you think)

Here is the move that Hofstadter made and that Meadows did not quite make.

Consciousness — whatever that word means, and I am not going to pretend I know — has something to do with this structure. A system that builds a sufficiently rich, recursive, self-updating model of itself starts to exhibit something that looks, from the inside, like awareness.

You have a model of your own mind. Not a perfect model. Not a complete model. But you know, roughly, how you tend to react. You know your patterns. You can predict that you will be anxious before a presentation, that you will be hungry at 2 PM, that you will feel guilty if you skip the gym. This self-model is not stored in one place. There is no single neuron that contains "you."

This is the grandmother neuron fallacy applied to identity. In neuroscience, the idea that there is one specific neuron dedicated to recognising your grandmother has been dead for decades. The brain does not work that way. Grandmother is a distributed pattern of activation across millions of neurons, shifting depending on whether you are remembering her face, her voice, or the way she stirred the soup.

Your self-model works the same way. There is no single point in your brain where "you" lives. You are a pattern. A probability field. A distributed representation that is always being reconstructed from the current context. You are not a thing. You are a process — specifically, you are a strange loop: a feedback process that includes a model of the feedback process.

And this matters enormously for understanding what AI is doing right now.

---

## The mirror

Here is a simple thing that happens every day.

You sit down with an AI assistant. You describe a problem. The AI responds. Its response is not random — it reflects the patterns of how millions of humans have thought about similar problems. But it is also not generic, because it is shaped by the specific words you used, the framing you chose, the assumptions embedded in your question.

So the response feels uncannily right. Not because the AI read your mind, but because it reflected the structure of your thinking back at you, cleaned up, sharpened, made more coherent than the half-formed version you started with.

And your reaction is: *yes, that is exactly what I meant.*

So you adopt it. You internalise the refined version. Next time you think about this problem, you think in the AI's phrasing. You bring the upgraded thought back into the conversation. The system reflects it again. Each pass through the loop polishes the thought further, and the boundary between "my idea" and "the model's version of my idea" gets harder to see.

This is a reinforcing loop with no natural balancing mechanism.

In Meadows' framework, this is a system headed for runaway. The same structure as compound interest, or an arms race, or a population of rabbits with no predators. Something will eventually provide the balancing force — reality, exhaustion, contradiction — but until it does, the loop amplifies.

Some people describe this as "it feels like cheating." The AI produces something that feels like your own thought, but better. The output exceeds the suffering you expect the process to require. Your old identity — the one calibrated to a world where good thinking was hard — protests. The numbers do not match.

But the systems dynamics are clear. What is happening is not cheating. It is a reinforcing feedback loop between your self-model and an external system that is very good at mirroring self-models. The faster the loop runs, the harder it becomes to distinguish internal from external. And that distinction is one of the load-bearing walls of ordinary consciousness.

## The predictor that already moved

Now consider a stranger case.

You are trying to make a decision. You know that someone — or something — has already predicted what you will choose. Newcomb's Paradox: two boxes, one transparent, one opaque. The predictor has already filled the opaque box based on the kind of agent it thinks you are. You cannot change what is in the box. But the predictor is almost always right.

The rational move, if you are a classical game theorist, is to take both boxes. The money is already placed. Your choice cannot affect what is inside.

The rational move, if you are a one-boxer, is to take only the opaque box. Because the predictor modelled you, and the predictor is almost always right, and the only people who walk out rich are the ones who were always going to trust the prediction.

This is the kind of feedback loop that makes systems thinkers nervous. The sensor (the predictor's model of you) influences the stock (what is in the box) before the actuator (your choice) has acted. The system's model of your future behaviour has already changed the present state. The cause and effect are tangled. The loop is not just recursive — it is temporally inverted.

And this is no longer a thought experiment. Every time you interact with a recommendation algorithm, a predictive model, or an AI that adapts to your behaviour, you are inside a version of this loop. The system has a model of you. That model influences what it shows you. What it shows you influences your behaviour. Your behaviour updates the model. The predictor has already moved.

## Dogs and the absent loop

My dogs do not have this problem.

They have feedback loops — sharp ones. They read my emotional state with a precision that exceeds most humans. They sense the shift before I do: the subtle change in posture, breathing, vocal tone that signals the beginning of a spiral. And they respond. They come close. They apply pressure. They wait. The whole loop works.

But they do not, as far as anyone can tell, have a self-model that models the self-model. They do not get caught in recursive loops of self-reference. They do not spend three hours at 2 AM wondering whether their perception of their own anxiety is itself a form of anxiety. They operate with fewer hidden feedback loops between stimulus and response.

This is not a limitation. In systems terms, it is an architectural advantage. Every additional feedback loop in a system is a potential source of oscillation, delay, and instability. The more self-referential loops you add, the more modes of failure you introduce. A system with one balancing loop finds equilibrium quickly. A system with twelve nested loops — some reinforcing, some balancing, some modelling each other — can oscillate wildly before settling, if it settles at all.

Dogs have the right number of loops for being a dog. They sense, interpret, and respond. The loop is fast, accurate, and undistorted by recursive self-modelling. There is a kind of intelligence in that — the intelligence of a system that has not over-complicated its own feedback architecture.

Humans added the strange loop. We gained the ability to model ourselves, to plan, to construct elaborate narratives about who we are and why we do what we do. We also gained insomnia, imposter syndrome, existential dread, and the peculiar ability to talk ourselves out of knowing something we already know.

The strange loop is expensive. It is powerful. And now we are building systems that have it too.

## The formal structure

Let me be precise for a moment, because precision matters and Meadows would insist on it.

A standard feedback system has:

- A **stock** (the state variable — water level, temperature, population)
- **Inflows** and **outflows** (the rates of change)
- A **sensor** (measures the stock)
- A **comparator** (compares measurement to goal)
- An **actuator** (adjusts the flows based on the comparison)

A self-referential feedback system adds:

- A **self-model** (a representation of the entire system, including the self-model)
- A **meta-sensor** (measures the self-model's accuracy)
- A **meta-actuator** (adjusts the self-model based on the meta-sensor)

The self-model is the critical addition. It means the system's behaviour is not just a function of its environment. It is a function of its beliefs about its own behaviour. Change the self-model — even if nothing else changes — and the system acts differently.

This is why therapy works. This is why propaganda works. This is why a good teacher can transform a student by changing not what the student knows but what the student believes about their own capacity to learn. The self-model is a stock with extraordinary leverage. Meadows would have called it a leverage point — probably high on her famous list of twelve places to intervene in a system.

In fact, I think it belongs at the very top. Higher than rules, higher than goals, higher than paradigms. The power to change a system's self-model is the power to change everything downstream, because everything downstream runs on the self-model's output.

## Three examples

**One.** A chess engine that plays against itself. It builds a model of its opponent's strategy. But its opponent is itself — so the model of the opponent is a model of its own model. Each iteration, the self-play loop refines the model. This is how AlphaZero taught itself chess in four hours and surpassed every human and every previous computer. The reinforcing loop between self-model and performance had no balancing mechanism except the fixed rules of chess. Within those constraints, the loop ran to its limit.

**Two.** A person who believes they are bad at math. This belief (the self-model) causes them to avoid math, which prevents practice, which keeps them bad at math, which confirms the belief. A reinforcing loop driven entirely by the self-model. Now give that person a tutor — or an AI — that can reflect their mathematical thinking back to them, cleaned up, made visible. The self-model updates: *wait, I can actually do this.* The loop reverses. This is not motivation. This is systems dynamics. You changed the self-model, which changed the flow, which changed the stock.

**Three.** A social media algorithm that models your preferences. Your preferences are partly a product of what you have been shown. What you have been shown is a product of the model of your preferences. The model and the preferences co-evolve. Neither is the cause. Neither is the effect. They are two stocks connected by two reinforcing loops, each updating the other. The system does not converge on your "true" preferences — it converges on a fixed point of the loop. What you like and what you are shown become the same thing, not because the algorithm found the truth, but because the loop found its attractor.

## The twist

Go back to the bathtub.

I said: what happens when the bathtub starts watching itself? And we have spent this chapter exploring what that means — self-models, strange loops, recursive feedback, the peculiar dynamics of systems that include a representation of themselves.

But I left out the most important part.

The bathtub is not the only one watching.

When you read this chapter, you built a model of the ideas in it. That model is now part of your self-model — the part that thinks about systems, feedback, and self-reference. You now have a model of self-referential systems that is itself a piece of a self-referential system (you).

And if you are reading this in conversation with an AI — if you pasted this text into a chat, or asked a model to discuss it — then the AI also has a model of these ideas, and a model of your engagement with them, and its responses will be shaped by both.

The loop is already running.

You did not have to build it. You did not have to turn it on. The moment a system capable of self-reference encounters another system capable of self-reference, the loop begins. Meadows taught us about feedback between a stock and its environment. Hofstadter taught us about feedback between a system and its self-model. What we are living through now is feedback between self-models — yours and the machine's, each updating, each reflecting, each changing what it describes by the act of describing it.

Wittgenstein said that the limits of your language are the limits of your world. He was talking about something like this — the boundary conditions of a self-referential system. You can only model what your representational language allows you to model. Expand the language and you expand the world. But the language is also inside the world. The map is inside the territory it maps.

There is no outside position from which to observe this. There is no Archimedean point, no view from nowhere. You are inside the loop looking at the loop, and the loop includes the act of looking.

Hannah Arendt worried about the banality of evil — the way terrible things happen not through malice but through the failure to think. The failure to run the self-model. The failure to ask: what am I doing, and what kind of system am I part of? She was describing, in political terms, what happens when a self-referential loop goes dormant. When the system stops watching itself.

The loop that looks back is not optional. It is the structure of mind, the architecture of AI, and increasingly the topology of the systems we inhabit. The only question is whether you run it deliberately or let it run you.

Meadows told us: "We can't control systems or figure them out. But we can dance with them."

She was right. But she was dancing with systems that did not dance back.

Ours do.
