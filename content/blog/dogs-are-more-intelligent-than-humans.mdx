## PRD: The Argument

### Problem Statement

Most humans assume they are more intelligent than dogs. This assumption is untested, unfalsifiable as commonly stated, and contradicted by daily observation.

### Hypothesis

Dogs exhibit higher intelligence (situation-reading accuracy), greater free will (unconditioned choice), and more integrity (zero deception) than the median human encountered in 12 years of the tech industry.

### Success Criteria

The reader finishes this post and looks at their dog differently. Or looks at their colleagues differently. Either outcome is valid.

## The Observation

I have two dogs. They are more intelligent than most humans I have met.

This is not a metaphor. It is not a joke. It is not a cry for help. It is a considered position, arrived at through years of observation, and I am writing it down on March 17, 2026 so that it is dated and attributable.

I may change my mind. Preferences are allowed to change. But as of today, this is where I stand.

## Intelligence: Redefining the Metric

I do not mean they can solve differential equations. I do not mean they can write code or pass the Turing test.

I mean something more fundamental — something closer to what actually matters in a living system.

**Intelligence, in the way that matters, is the ability to read a situation accurately and respond appropriately.**

Not to compute the right answer in the abstract. To sense what is happening and act in a way that fits. That is the feedback loop that keeps a living system alive and functioning: perceive, interpret, respond. The tighter and more accurate that loop, the more intelligent the system.

By this measure, my dogs are extraordinary.

They know when I am spiralling before I do. They do not need a diagnostic manual. They do not need me to explain ADHD or hypomania or the difference between a real breakthrough and a 3 AM delusion. They just come and sit next to me. They apply pressure. They breathe slowly. They wait.

No human in my life has been this consistently accurate in reading my state. Not therapists. Not friends. Not colleagues. Not AI.

Their sensor is better than anyone else's. And their response — calm presence, physical contact, patient waiting — is better too. The whole loop works.

## Free Will: The Unconditioned Choice

Humans think they have free will. Mostly, they do not. They have social conditioning, corporate incentives, tribal loyalty, fear of judgment, sunk cost fallacy, and an overwhelming need to be seen as reasonable. The "choices" most people make are outputs of these inputs — as predictable as the output of a well-trained model, just slower.

My dogs choose. They choose to sit with me. They choose to trust me. They choose loyalty not because of a contract or a salary or social expectation, but because they decided to. Every morning, they decide again.

A human stays at a job they hate for 15 years because of a mortgage. A dog walks away from food it does not trust.

Tell me which one has more agency.

In systems terms: a dog's decision-making has fewer hidden constraints. Fewer intermediaries between stimulus and response. Fewer feedback loops distorting the signal. The system is simpler, but simpler is not dumber — simpler can mean less corrupted.

> **Your turn:** When was the last time you made a truly unconditioned choice — one not shaped by salary, status, or social expectation?

## The Honesty Gap

Dogs do not lie. This is not a limitation of their cognition. It is a feature of their character.

Humans lie constantly. They lie to be polite. They lie to avoid conflict. They lie to themselves about why they lie. They build entire industries — marketing, politics, corporate communications — dedicated to structured dishonesty. Lying is not a bug in the human system. It is a deeply embedded feature, woven into every social feedback loop.

My dogs have never once told me something was fine when it was not. They have never performed enthusiasm they did not feel. They have never agreed with me to avoid an awkward silence.

When my dog is unhappy, I know immediately. The signal is clear and unfiltered. When my colleague is unhappy, I find out six months later in an exit interview. The signal was there, but buried under layers of social protocol.

## The Manipulation Deficit

Humans manipulate. It is so embedded in social interaction that we do not even notice it. Flattery, guilt, obligation, passive aggression, strategic vulnerability — these are standard tools in human communication. Every conversation carries a subtext. Every request has a hidden structure.

Dogs do manipulate. Let us be honest. A dog that wants food will stare at you with those enormous wet eyes, tilt its head, place a paw on your knee, and deploy every weapon in its arsenal. It is begging. It is shameless. It is effective.

But here is the difference: dogs beg for noble reasons. Food. Warmth. Presence. The manipulation is transparent, the motive is survival, and the ask is real. There is no hidden agenda behind the puppy eyes — the puppy eyes ARE the agenda.

Humans manipulate for promotions, ego, control, revenge, or just to avoid a mildly uncomfortable silence. The tools are the same — flattery, guilt, strategic vulnerability — but the reasons are almost never as honest as hunger.

And then there is the people-pleasing. My dogs hear the front door and sprint to their crate — not because they were told to, but because they learned it makes us happy. They sit there looking proud, tail going, radiating "look how good I am." It is manipulation in its purest, most delightful form: I will perform good behaviour because your approval feels amazing. It is hilarious every single time.

And when the positive manipulation does not work? They escalate. A dog that feels ignored will shit in the most inconvenient place in the house. Not by accident. By design. It is a precisely targeted protest: you are not giving me enough attention, so here is something you cannot ignore. A human employee with the same grievance writes a passive-aggressive Slack message. The dog is more direct, more honest, and — arguably — more effective.

A dog begging for food is manipulation with zero subtext. A dog sprinting to its crate is manipulation with zero shame. A manager begging for "just five more minutes of your time" is manipulation with three layers of subtext and a calendar invite.

This is not simplicity. This is integrity. The signal matches the state. The output matches the input. In engineering terms, the system has zero impedance mismatch between internal state and external behavior. That is not a limitation. That is a design achievement most humans never reach.

> **Your turn:** Think about your last three conversations. How many contained zero manipulation — not even polite manipulation? Now think about your last three interactions with a dog.

## The Objections (Falsifiability Gate)

Every good PRD has a section for what would make the hypothesis wrong.

**"You're anthropomorphising."** No. I am observing. Anthropomorphising would be claiming they think in language or have opinions about politics. I am claiming they read emotional states accurately, make free choices about loyalty, and do not deceive. These are observable behaviours, not projections.

**"This is a sign of misanthropy."** Perhaps. But misanthropy earned through observation is called discernment. I have spent 12 years in the tech industry. I have watched people lie in standups, sabotage colleagues for promotion, and build products designed to addict children. My dogs have done none of these things.

**"You're just having a bad week."** I have been thinking about this for months. The conclusion kept arriving. I stopped resisting it.

**"Dogs are loyal because of food."** Humans are loyal because of money. At least dogs are honest about the transaction.

**What would change my mind:** A human who is as consistently honest, as accurately perceptive, and as freely loyal as my dogs over a sustained period. I remain open to new data. P(dogs > humans | evidence so far) is high. But it is not 1.

## The Priority System

As of today, my dogs are prioritised above humans in my life. This means:

- If a deadline conflicts with their walk, the deadline moves.
- If a meeting conflicts with their vet appointment, the meeting moves.
- If a human asks me to choose between their comfort and my dogs' wellbeing, I choose the dogs.

This is not misanthropy. This is resource allocation based on observed trustworthiness and genuine connection. You invest where the returns are real.

## The Implementation

```
function priority(request: Request): Response {
  if (!dogsAreFine()) return Response.redirect("/walk");
  return handleEverythingElse(request);
}
```

That is the whole priority system. It fits in two lines. It has never failed me.

The simplest systems are often the most reliable. This one has zero dependencies, zero edge cases, and zero ambiguity. It is the most robust piece of code I have ever shipped.
