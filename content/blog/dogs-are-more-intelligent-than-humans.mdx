## PRD: The Argument

### Problem Statement

Most humans assume they are more intelligent than dogs. This assumption is untested, unfalsifiable as commonly stated, and contradicted by daily observation.

### Hypothesis

Dogs exhibit higher intelligence (situation-reading accuracy), greater free will (unconditioned choice), and more integrity (zero deception) than the median human encountered in 12 years of the tech industry.

### Success Criteria

The reader finishes this post and looks at their dog differently. Or looks at their colleagues differently. Either outcome is valid.

---

## The Observation

I have two dogs. They are more intelligent than most humans I have met.

This is not a metaphor. It is not a joke. It is not a cry for help. It is a considered position, arrived at through observation, and I am writing it down on March 17, 2026 so that it is dated and attributable.

I may change my mind. Preferences are allowed to change. But as of today, this is where I stand.

## Intelligence: Redefining the Metric

I do not mean they can solve differential equations. I do not mean they can write code or pass the Turing test. I mean something more fundamental.

**Intelligence, in the way that matters, is the ability to read a situation accurately and respond appropriately.**

By this measure, my dogs are extraordinary.

They know when I am spiralling before I do. They do not need a diagnostic manual. They do not need me to explain ADHD or hypomania or the difference between a real breakthrough and a 3 AM delusion. They just come and sit next to me. They apply pressure. They breathe slowly. They wait.

No human in my life has been this consistently accurate in reading my state. Not therapists. Not friends. Not colleagues. Not AI.

> **Your turn:** Has your dog ever detected something about your state before you noticed it yourself? What happened?

## Free Will: The Unconditioned Choice

Humans think they have free will. They do not. They have social conditioning, corporate incentives, tribal loyalty, fear of judgment, sunk cost fallacy, and an overwhelming need to be seen as reasonable.

My dogs choose. They choose to sit with me. They choose to trust me. They choose loyalty not because of a contract or a salary or social expectation, but because they decided to. Every morning, they decide again. It is the freest choice I have ever witnessed.

A human stays at a job they hate for 15 years because of a mortgage. A dog walks away from food it does not trust.

Tell me which one has more agency.

> **Your turn:** When was the last time you made a truly unconditioned choice — one not shaped by salary, status, or social expectation?

## The Honesty Gap

Dogs do not lie. This is not a limitation of their cognition. It is a feature of their character.

Humans lie constantly. They lie to be polite. They lie to avoid conflict. They lie to themselves about why they lie. They build entire industries — marketing, politics, corporate communications — dedicated to structured dishonesty.

My dogs have never once told me something was fine when it wasn't. They have never performed enthusiasm they didn't feel. They have never agreed with me to avoid an awkward silence.

When my dog is unhappy, I know immediately. When my colleague is unhappy, I find out six months later in an exit interview.

## The Manipulation Deficit

Humans manipulate. It is so embedded in social interaction that we don't even notice it. Flattery, guilt, obligation, passive aggression, strategic vulnerability — these are standard tools in human communication.

Dogs do not manipulate. A dog that wants food looks at the food. A dog that wants attention comes to you. A dog that wants to go outside stands at the door. There is no subtext. There is no hidden agenda. There is no "I'm fine" when they're not fine.

This is not simplicity. This is integrity.

> **Your turn:** Think about your last three conversations. How many contained zero manipulation — not even polite manipulation? Now think about your last three interactions with a dog.

## The Objections (Falsifiability Gate)

Every good PRD has a section for what would make the hypothesis wrong.

**"You're anthropomorphising."** No. I am observing. Anthropomorphising would be claiming they think in language or have opinions about politics. I am claiming they read emotional states accurately, make free choices about loyalty, and do not deceive. These are observable behaviours, not projections.

**"This is a sign of misanthropy."** Perhaps. But misanthropy earned through observation is called discernment. I have spent 12 years in the tech industry. I have watched people lie in standups, sabotage colleagues for promotion, and build products designed to addict children. My dogs have done none of these things.

**"You're just having a bad week."** I've been thinking about this for months. The conclusion kept arriving. I stopped resisting it.

**"Dogs are loyal because of food."** Humans are loyal because of money. At least dogs are honest about the transaction.

**What would change my mind:** A human who is as consistently honest, as accurately perceptive, and as freely loyal as my dogs over a sustained period. I remain open to new data. P(dogs > humans | evidence so far) is high. But it is not 1.

## The Priority System

As of today, my dogs are prioritised above humans in my life. This means:

- If a deadline conflicts with their walk, the deadline moves.
- If a meeting conflicts with their vet appointment, the meeting moves.
- If a human asks me to choose between their comfort and my dogs' wellbeing, I choose the dogs.

This is not misanthropy. This is resource allocation based on observed trustworthiness and genuine connection.
