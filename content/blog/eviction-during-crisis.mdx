## A Note Before We Begin

This article is not an attack on anyone. It is not written in anger. It is written by someone who genuinely wants to understand the system he finds himself in — and who believes that understanding, shared openly, can make that system kinder for everyone.

If you are involved in this situation and you are reading this: I would rather talk than fight. I would rather find a solution than prove a point. But I also believe that what happens to me is not just about me, and that writing honestly about it might help someone else one day.

## The Situation

I am writing this from a psychiatric ward in East Sussex. I have been here since late March 2026. A mental health crisis brought me here. I agreed to stay for treatment. I am cooperating — taking medication, attending groups, doing the work that recovery requires.

While I am here, a process has begun regarding my tenancy at Apartment 70, 42 Mighell Street, Brighton. The place my dogs know. The place my books are. The place I was living when the ambulance came.

I am not going to assume the worst about anyone's motives. But I do want to ask a question — sincerely, as a mathematician who analyses systems for a living:

**Why now?**

## Understanding the Timing

There may be perfectly reasonable explanations. Lease cycles, administrative timelines, decisions made before anyone knew I was in hospital. Systems have their own momentum, and the people operating them do not always have visibility into the lives of the people they affect.

But timing matters. And the timing of this — while I am hospitalised, unable to visit the property, unable to meet in person, limited in my ability to respond to correspondence — creates a situation that is, at minimum, very difficult for the person on the receiving end.

I want to believe this is a coincidence. I am writing this article in case it is not — and in case it helps the next person in a similar position.

## What the Law Says (For Anyone Who Needs It)

I have been learning about housing law from a psychiatric ward. This is not how anyone should have to learn about housing law. But here is what I have found, in case it helps you:

**1. A notice is not an eviction.** You do not have to leave your home until a court issues a possession order and a bailiff enforces it. A letter is the start of a process, not the end of one.

**2. Your circumstances matter.** Under the **Pre-Action Protocol for Possession Claims**, landlords must take reasonable steps before issuing proceedings. This includes considering the tenant's health conditions and personal circumstances. Being in a psychiatric ward is a circumstance.

**3. The Equality Act 2010 applies.** Mental illness is a disability under the Act when it has a substantial and long-term effect on day-to-day activities. Section 15 protects against unfavourable treatment arising from disability. Courts take this seriously.

**4. Hospitalisation is not abandonment.** Being temporarily away from your home for medical treatment does not mean you have surrendered your tenancy.

**5. Help exists.** Shelter (0808 800 4444), Citizens Advice, and local law centres provide free housing advice. In Brighton, the Brighton Housing Trust is a resource.

## The Deeper Question

I want to ask something that is uncomfortable but important. Not as an accusation — as a genuine inquiry into how systems work.

Would the timing be the same if I were different?

If the resident of Apartment 70 were a retired professional with deep local connections, a family solicitor, and a name that required no spelling out — would the process move at the same pace? Would anyone pause and think: *perhaps we should wait until they are out of hospital?*

I am Hungarian. I am a one-person startup founder. I have ADHD. I am currently in a psychiatric facility. I have no family in this country.

I am not saying anyone involved has discriminatory intent. I believe most people are doing their jobs as best they can. But systems do not need intent to produce unequal outcomes. They just need to be designed without imagining that someone like me might be on the receiving end.

The question is not whether anyone meant to be unfair. The question is whether the outcome would be the same regardless of who was in Apartment 70. If the answer is yes — if Charles from Winchester and Zoltán from Budapest would receive the same letter at the same time with the same options — then the system is fair, and I simply need to navigate it. I can do that.

If the answer is *maybe not* — then the system has room to improve. And naming that, calmly and specifically, is how improvement begins.

## What I Would Like

I would like to return to my flat when I am well enough to leave hospital.

I would like to have a conversation about the tenancy — as adults, with goodwill, when I am in a position to participate meaningfully.

I would like whoever is involved in this decision to know that I am not a problem to be managed. I am a person in a temporary crisis, doing everything the clinical team asks of me, with every intention of returning to a stable and productive life in Brighton.

If there are arrears, I want to discuss a plan. If there are concerns, I want to hear them. If there is a timeline, I want to understand it.

I am not asking for special treatment. I am asking for the same consideration that any resident would receive — the benefit of the doubt, the space to recover, and a conversation before a conclusion.

## For Anyone Else in This Situation

If you are reading this from a hospital bed, or from a sofa where you are trying to recover, and you have received a letter about your housing that you do not fully understand — here is what I want you to know:

You have rights. They are real and they are enforceable.

You are not powerless. The fact that you are unwell does not mean you are without options.

Ask for help. Call Shelter. Call Citizens Advice. Tell your clinical team — they can connect you with a social worker or housing advocate.

Do not ignore the letter. But do not panic either. A letter is the beginning of a conversation. Make sure your side of that conversation is heard.

## A Final Thought

I analyse systems. It is what I do — in code, in mathematics, in life. The housing system in England is not broken. It functions precisely as designed. But design is a choice, and choices can be revised.

I am not angry. I am observant. And I believe that careful observation, shared honestly, is more powerful than anger.

I would like to go home. I think that is a reasonable thing to want.
