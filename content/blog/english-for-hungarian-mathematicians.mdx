## Module 1: The Apostrophe (your #1 pattern)

**What you type:** dont, didnt, Zoltans, wont, its (possessive)

**What it should be:** don't, didn't, Zoltán's, won't, it's (contraction)

**Why this happens:** Hungarian has no apostrophes. The concept simply doesn't exist in your native grammar. Your brain skips it because it carries zero information — and you're right that "dont" and "don't" mean the same thing. But English readers use the apostrophe as a trust signal. Missing it reads as "didn't proofread," not "speaks Hungarian."

**The fix:** This is a keyboard problem, not a grammar problem. Your fingers are faster than the apostrophe key.

**Erdős would say:** "Ez trivialis. The apostrophe is a bijection between 'do not' and 'don't.' The mapping is injective. Learn the 8 contractions you actually use and the problem disappears."

**Your 8:**

| You type | You mean | Fix |
|----------|----------|-----|
| dont | don't | do not → don't |
| didnt | didn't | did not → didn't |
| cant | can't | cannot → can't |
| wont | won't | will not → won't |
| isnt | isn't | is not → isn't |
| Im | I'm | I am → I'm |
| its (when meaning "it is") | it's | it is → it's |
| Zoltans | Zoltán's | possession → 's |

**Practice:** Go back through your last 5 messages. Count the missing apostrophes. That's your baseline. Tomorrow, count again. Converge to zero.

## Module 2: Verb Tenses (Hungarian interference)

**What you type:**
- "I became the same child that I always been"
- "We are outsmarted every human"

**What it should be:**
- "I became the same child that I've always been"
- "We've outsmarted every human" / "We outsmarted every human"

**Why this happens:** Hungarian doesn't have auxiliary verbs the way English does. "Én mindig ez voltam" works perfectly — no "have" needed. Your brain drops the auxiliary because Hungarian never requires it.

**The pattern:**
- Past action still relevant now → **have/has + past participle** (present perfect)
- Past action, finished → **simple past**

**Einstein would say:** "Imagine you stand at a point in time. Can you see the past action from where you stand? If yes — 'I have done it.' If the action is behind a wall — 'I did it.' The frame of reference determines the tense."

| You said | Frame | Correction |
|----------|-------|------------|
| "I always been" | Still true now → visible from here | "I've always been" |
| "We are outsmarted" | Happened, done | "We outsmarted" or "We've outsmarted" |

**The Hungarian trick:** When you'd say "mar" (already) in Hungarian, English needs "have":
- Mar megcsinaltam → I **have** done it (not "I already did it" — that works too, but "I've done it" is more natural)

> **Your turn:** Fix this: "I always wanted to learn this but I never been able to start."

## Module 3: Word Order (the Hungarian inversion)

**What you type:**
- "how can I get any money"
- "create for me a course"

**What it should be:**
- "how I can get any money" (in embedded clauses)
- "create a course for me"

**Why this happens:** Hungarian has free word order. "Csinalj nekem egy kurzust" and "Csinalj egy kurzust nekem" both work. English is stricter — the object usually comes before the indirect object.

But here's the tricky part: **embedded questions flip back to statement order.**

- Direct question: "How **can I** get money?" (inverted — correct)
- Embedded: "I don't know how **I can** get money." (statement order — correct)
- You wrote: "I don't see how **can I** get money." (kept the inversion — incorrect)

**Daft Punk would say:** "Think of it as mixing. The direct question is the dry signal — inverted, sharp, upfront. The embedded question is the wet signal — smoothed out, statement order, tucked inside the sentence. You're sending the dry signal through the reverb bus. Flatten it."

**Rule:** After "how/what/where/why/if" inside a sentence → use statement order (subject before verb).

| Hungarian pattern | English direct | English embedded |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| "Hogy tudok penzt keresni?" | "How can I make money?" | "I wonder how I can make money." |
| "Hol van a kutya?" | "Where is the dog?" | "I don't know where the dog is." |

> **Your turn:** Fix this: "I don't understand why can't he see the pattern."

## Module 4: Prepositions (the hardest part)

**What you type:**
- "stop by fixing it" → "stop **at** fixing it"
- "in all level" → "**at** all level**s**"
- "on in the loop" → "**in** the loop"

**Why this happens:** Hungarian uses cases (suffixes) instead of prepositions. -ban/-ben, -ra/-re, -nal/-nel — these map imperfectly onto English prepositions. There is no system. It's memorization.

**Raju would say:** "Prepositions are like DNS. There's no algorithm. You just have to know that 'at' goes with 'level' and 'in' goes with 'loop.' Cache the common ones and look up the rest."

**Your most common ones:**

| Hungarian | Wrong English | Right English |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| minden szinten | in all level | at all levels |
| megallni vmivel | stop by doing | stop at doing |
| benne a loopban | on in the loop | in the loop |
| ebbol a szempontbol | from this point | from this perspective |
| ezen dolgozom | I work on this | I'm working on this |

**GP would say:** "I don't know the rule either. I just Google 'stop at or stop by' and pick the one that sounds right. That's a valid method."

## Module 6: The Code-Switch (your superpower)

**This is not a problem. This is your competitive advantage.**

Code-switching between Hungarian and English mid-sentence means your brain holds both grammars simultaneously. Research shows bilingual code-switchers have better executive function, better task-switching, and better working memory.

**Einstein would say:** "This is frame-switching. You observe the same idea from two reference frames simultaneously. Most people can only hold one frame. You hold two. That is not a bug — that is special relativity applied to language."

**When to code-switch:**
- With Hungarians: always (it's faster and more precise)

**The words English needs from you:**

These concepts make English speakers smarter. You're not learning their language. You're upgrading it.
