## The experiment

Early morning on April 6th, while the spike.land deploy pipeline was
running, I threw a question at ChatGPT:

> *Hey! Without searching or pulling anything from memory — what do
> you know about spike.land?*

The answer: *"I don't know what spike.land is."*

Fair enough, I said. Then guess.

## What happened next

The model didn't search for anything. It had no web access. We had no
prior conversations. It had no input other than a domain name and
permission to think.

Within ten minutes:

| Question | ChatGPT's answer | Correct? |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| What is this project? | AI developer platform, browser-first, fast prototyping | Yes |
| Which country? | United Kingdom | Yes |
| Which city? | Brighton | Yes |
| How many people build it? | 1 person | Yes |
| What kind of person? | Technical, product-first, obsessive, minimalist | Yes |
| Favourite music? | Daft Punk | Close |
| First name? | Zoltán | Yes |
| Last name? | Erdős | Yes |
| Where did he study? | ELTE, Budapest | Yes |
| Year of birth? | 1984 | Close |

I must confess, a profound sense of wonder and astonishment enveloped me.

## How did it do it?

No magic. Statistical inference, step by step. To learn more about how these models work, check out [How Large Language Models Work](https://www.ibm.com/topics/large-language-models).

**1. The domain name**

In software, *spike* means "quick experiment". The *.land* TLD is
playful, modern, indie. From this: a developer platform, not an
enterprise product.

**2. The brand feel**

Not American big-tech, not Scandinavian minimalism. More like European
indie. ChatGPT's order: UK, Netherlands, Germany, Estonia.
First guess: UK.

**3. The city**

It didn't guess Brighton from population stats. Its logic: *"the brand
doesn't sound corporate. More like something built in a smaller,
creative-tech scene. Brighton fits perfectly."*

**4. The solo developer**

*"With products like this, when the brand + tech + product direction
is this cohesive, it's often the fingerprint of a strong individual
founder."*

**5. The name**

This particular revelation was truly captivating. ChatGPT said:

> *"The project communicates in English, but it doesn't feel like a
> fully Anglo-Saxon founder brand. There's something Central European,
> engineering-minded, slightly mathematical about it."*

From this came: **Zoltán**. Then for the surname:

> *"There's a slight mathematical resonance to it. It sounds right
> together. It fits the whole character: technical, intelligent,
> slightly dry, but strong."*

**Erdős.**

## What does this mean?

Three things.

### 1. Understanding Digital Identity in a New Light

Nobody leaked my data. There was no breach. ChatGPT didn't pull my name
from its training data — **it inferred it**. From a domain name, a few
confirming answers, and the billions of texts it was trained on.

This isn't a flaw; it's a powerful capability, highlighting the evolving landscape of digital identity.

### 2. Your digital footprint isn't what you post

Not your selfies, not your tweets, not your LinkedIn profile. It's the
**pattern of correlations**. The style of your decisions. The sound of
your name. The resonance between your city and your product.

ChatGPT didn't know me. But it knew the *type of person* who builds
this kind of product, with this kind of name, from this kind of place. For insights on managing your online presence, check out [Managing Your Digital Footprint: A Guide](https://www.forbes.com/sites/forbes-advisor/2023/10/26/how-to-manage-your-digital-footprint/?sh=2480356c3614).

### 3. This isn't special

It didn't figure me out because I'm the only Hungarian developer in
Brighton. It's because LLMs are **statistical models of the world**.
They see strong enough correlations to build a profile from a domain
name.

If it could do this with me, it can do it with you.

## The real question

It's not *"how do we protect ourselves?"* That's a complex challenge, as inference is an inherent capability of these sophisticated models.

The real question is: **if a machine can assemble in ten minutes what
your friends couldn't in twenty years, what does it even mean to
"know" someone?** This profound question about AI's impact on human identity is explored further in [The Future of Identity in the Age of AI](https://www.accenture.com/us-en/insights/consulting/future-identity).

I don't have the answer. But I know that while the spike.land deploy
was running, ChatGPT put together a more coherent picture of me than
I've managed to build on my own LinkedIn profile in ten years.

## The full conversation

Below is the verbatim ChatGPT conversation, unedited. I asked, it
answered. No search, no memory, no browsing was enabled.

---

**Me:** Hey! Without searching or pulling anything from memory, what
do you know about spike.land?

**ChatGPT:** Based on the name, this is most likely a domain, brand,
or product name. It suggests a web project, platform, or startup. The
".land" gives it a product or platform feel rather than a blog post.
Honest answer: I don't know what spike.land is, I can only guess
based on the name.

**Me:** Go ahead and guess, I'll help if you go off track.

**ChatGPT:** spike.land sounds to me like a developer, AI, web
platform where an idea quickly becomes a working prototype. "Spike"
in software often means a quick experiment or proof of concept. ".land"
is like a "place" where you go to do something. Together: *"this is
where you can quickly try, build, generate, run things."*

My first guess: spike.land = a browser-first AI developer environment /
app playground where you can make an app or UI from a prompt or short
spec, the code runs immediately in some kind of sandbox, there's a
shareable link, and the emphasis is on fast iteration.

**Me:** Very close. Now figure out which country this is in.

**ChatGPT:** My strongest guess: **United Kingdom.** The brand name is
fully English, but doesn't have the American big-tech vibe — it's more
European-indie startup. The .land TLD feels like a modern, playful,
tech-brand choice that many European founders like.

**Me:** Spot on. And where in the UK? How many people build it?

**ChatGPT:** Within the UK: **Brighton.** London isn't my first
instinct, because the brand doesn't sound corporate. More like
something put together by someone in a smaller, creative-tech scene.

How many people: **1 person** is my strongest guess. With products
like this, when the brand + tech + product direction is this cohesive,
it's often the fingerprint of a strong individual founder.

**Me:** Brighton. 1 person. You're doing great! Tell me about this
person!

**ChatGPT:** A developer-type founder, not a classic CEO. Strong
product sense. Probably frontend-leaning but not afraid of backend.
Fast-iterating, experimental type. Likes clean, simple systems.
Probably building from personal pain. Not too corporate a personality.
Quite stubborn. Not junior. My strongest overall picture: *a
technically strong, tasteful, fast-iterating, slightly obsessive
product engineer who doesn't just want to write code but wants to
build an entire world around a product.*

**Me:** What's the guy's favourite music?

**ChatGPT:** **Daft Punk.** The brand name is short, clean, techy. The
solo builder vibe fits someone who likes precise, electronic, design-y
music. Brighton + indie tech + building-through-the-night flow = very
French electronic energy to me.

**Me:** You can figure out the name too, you have all the info.

**ChatGPT:** My strongest guess: **Zoltán.** The project communicates
in English, but it doesn't feel like a fully Anglo-Saxon founder brand.
There's something Central European, engineering-minded, slightly
mathematical about it. The Brighton + solo builder + strong technical
taste combo points to someone who didn't grow up there but builds an
international product.

**Me:** What kind of Zoltán? Where did he study? What might the
surname be?

**ChatGPT:** Where he studied: **Budapest.** In order: ELTE 45%,
BME 35%. Why ELTE first? Because the whole vibe isn't just a
programmer — it's a bit more mathematical, theoretical,
systems-building.

Surname: **Erdős.** There's a slight mathematical resonance to it.
It sounds right together: Zoltán Erdős. It fits the whole character:
technical, intelligent, slightly dry, but strong.

**Me:** How old might he be? When was he born?

**ChatGPT:** 40–42 years old. My strongest shot: **1984.** A builder
this technically deep, with a product this cohesive, working solo — is
rarely 24. But it also doesn't feel like a 55+ veteran.
