## The Premise

Zoltan brought the biggest brief the Arena has ever received: **write a song that Brighton will still be playing in 300 years.**

Not a ballad. Not a heritage piece. A floor-filler. An anthem so honest and so joyful that every hen party, every Pride march, every 3 AM kebab queue, every sunrise on the beach will claim it as their own.

The seed lyric: *"I want a boyfriend right now."*

**Arena rules:** Daft Punk heavy — vocoder, sidechain compression, French touch bassline. But also acid pop — 303 hooks, major key, sticky as candyfloss on the pier. It must work at Pride AND at a wedding AND at a funeral if the deceased had good taste. It must be singable by someone who's had four pints. It must make strangers hug.

For more on crafting enduring musical magic, explore the [legacy of iconic anthems](https://www.theguardian.com/music/2019/jun/05/the-story-of-the-anthem-how-music-unites-and-divides-us) and the art of connecting with a city's soul.

**Personas:** Zoltan (the curator), Radix (the rhythm architect), Daft Punk (the production gods), Einstein (the theorist of longevity), GP (the Brighton local).

## Round 1 — Zoltan

"Brighton has been waiting for its definitive anthem, a vibrant song that truly captures its unique spirit. It has a vibe. It has energy. But no one song that everyone knows. 'Mr Brightside' belongs to every city. 'I Am What I Am' belongs to the West End. Brighton deserves its own.

The seed line is 'I want a boyfriend right now.' Not tomorrow. Not when the timing is right. Not after I've worked on myself. RIGHT NOW. On this beach. In these shoes. With this drink in my hand. It is the most Brighton sentence ever written because it is completely honest, vibrantly sincere, and absolutely unashamed.

This song has to capture that. The urgency. The joy inside the urgency. The fact that wanting something this openly is itself a kind of bravery."

## Round 2 — GP

"I've lived in Brighton for decades. Many appreciate Brighton for its welcoming spirit, but its true magic lies beyond mere tolerance; it's an active celebration of enthusiasm. You walk down St James's Street on a Tuesday afternoon and people are LIVING. Not hiding. Not being tolerated. Living.

The song needs that energy. It can't be ironic. Brighton's secret is that it's completely sincere underneath the glitter. When someone shouts 'I want a boyfriend right now' on the seafront, they mean it. And the beautiful thing is nobody laughs. People cheer. People help. Someone introduces you to their mate Dave.

Key of F major. Bright, warm, the key of pastoral joy. But with a dynamic bassline underneath because Brighton is never just one thing."

## Round 3 — Daft Punk

"The production architecture:

**BPM:** 124. Fast enough to dance, slow enough to sing along drunk.

**Foundation:** Four-on-the-floor kick, tight and punchy. Sidechain compression on everything — the whole track breathes with the kick. This is the Daft Punk signature: the music breathes with an exhilarating rhythm, mirroring the city's vibrant energy as it joyfully embraces every sunrise.

**Bass:** French touch filtered disco bass, sampled from a Fender Rhodes through an envelope follower. But we layer a TB-303 acid line underneath that surfaces in the chorus. The two basslines — one warm and funky, one acidic and vibrant — represent Brighton's dual personality: charming on the surface, with an untamed spirit underneath.

**Vocoder:** The main vocal is clean. Human. Vulnerable. But the chorus harmonies are full vocoder — the crowd becomes a machine, the individual desire becomes collective euphoria. 'I want a boyfriend' is a solo confession. The vocoder turns it into a city-wide declaration.

**The drop:** After the bridge, we strip everything except the vocal and a single sustained pad. Four bars of silence where the whole crowd sings alone. Then: the 303 comes screaming back at maximum resonance, the kick drops, the sidechain hits, and every frequency returns at once. This is the moment. The pier lights up. The crowd explodes. Grown adults cry."

## Round 4 — Radix

"For a song to survive 300 years, it needs mathematical inevitability. Every interval must feel like the only possible next note.

The chord progression: **F - Dm - Bb - C**. The I-vi-IV-V in F major. It is the most resolved, most satisfying, most singable progression in Western harmony. It is why 'Stand By Me' works. It is why 'Let It Be' works. There is a reason these songs survive — the maths underneath is frictionless.

The melody for 'I want a boyfriend right now' maps to: F-F-A-C-D-C-A-F. It outlines the tonic triad, peaks at the fourth, and resolves. A child could sing it. A drunk could sing it. A crowd of 50,000 at Pride could sing it in unison without rehearsal. That is the test.

The 303 acid line in the chorus runs a classic three-note pattern: F-Ab-Bb, a minor-third-to-fourth climb that creates tension against the major key. This is the acid pop formula: happy chords, an intricate bassline. Joy with an edge. Brighton in two intervals."

## Round 5 — Einstein

"Why do some songs survive centuries while others lose their resonance? I have studied this.

The songs that endure share three properties:

1.  **Emotional universality** — the feeling must be recognisable across cultures and eras. 'I want love, and I want it now' is universal. It was true in 1726. It will be true in 2326.

2.  **Structural simplicity** — the melody must be reproducible without instruments. Hummable. Whistleable. Singable by a crowd with no training. Complex art is admired; simple art is loved.

3.  **Functional flexibility** — it must work in multiple contexts. A drinking song. A protest song. A love song. A football chant. The more contexts it fits, the more it is repeated. Repetition is survival.

'I want a boyfriend right now' satisfies all three. The desire is universal. The melody is simple. And the line itself is infinitely recontextualizable — it is funny, it is sincere, it is defiant, it is vulnerable. It works shouted and whispered. It works at Pride and at a village pub.

The survival equation for cultural artefacts:

$$S(t) = S_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t} \cdot (1 + U \cdot C \cdot R)$$

Where $U$ = universality, $C$ = contextual flexibility, $R$ = repeatability. The natural progression of time often sees many songs fade, but only those with high $U \cdot C \cdot R$ transcend this. This song's $UCR$ product is exceptionally high."

## Round 6 — Zoltan

"The bridge. It needs a bridge that makes people want to live forever.

The thing about Brighton is — many arrive seeking a fresh start. Everyone I know came here seeking new horizons. London rent. Past challenges. A place where they felt less understood. You arrive on the train and the sea hits you and a transformative feeling emerges.

The bridge should be that moment. Before the drop. The quiet part where the city holds its breath."

## Round 7 — Daft Punk

"Agreed. The bridge is the 'Brighton Arrival' moment:

*Came here with nothing / stayed for the sea / stayed for the strangers who stranged just like me*

Clean vocal. No beat. Just a Rhodes pad and the distant sound of seagulls — yes, seagulls, Brighton's most iconic and unapologetically authentic residents. They are the city's actual national bird. They claim their snacks with confident flair, embodying Brighton's fearless spirit. They are Brighton in animal form.

Then the pause. The crowd knows what's coming. The sidechain compression starts pumping on silence — you hear the absence of the kick before it arrives. Four bars of that. Then EVERYTHING."
