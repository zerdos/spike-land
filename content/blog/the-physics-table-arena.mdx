## Previously

The [vehicle with no windows](/blog/on-the-scale-dependence-of-light) went through [45 rounds of arena testing](/blog/strange-loops-arena), three external reviews, and a full rewrite. It scored 8.5/10 as a conceptual note and 5/10 as a physics note. The review said: good essay, not yet a paper. Five specific fixes were identified.

Six new personas were introduced at [The Physics Table](/blog/the-physics-table) — physicists whose work the essay already cites. They are here to help, not to attack.

**Arena rules:** Ten personas. Collaborative. Rotate randomly. The goal is not to defend the vehicle — it already survived. The goal is to make the note rigorous.

**Returning personas:** Zoltán (the proposer), Erdős (the graph theorist), Daft Punk (the signal processors), Radix (the systems architect).

## Round 1 — Wilson

"I have read your note. The vehicle is a good thought experiment — it makes the co-scaling argument accessible. But your $L$ is doing too many jobs.

In a Wilsonian effective field theory, the cutoff $\Lambda$ separates the degrees of freedom you integrate out from those you keep. The RG sliding scale $\mu$ is a different object — it is the energy at which you evaluate running couplings. Your 'probe wavelength' is yet another thing — a physical observable tied to the experiment. And 'apparatus size' is a macroscopic property of the detector.

These four quantities are related in specific theories, but they are not the same thing. Your caveat in Section 1 flags this. Good. But flagging is not resolving. In the current note, $L$ is a placeholder for whichever of these the reader prefers. That is honest, but it means your $\delta_\alpha$ absorbs the ambiguity. Until someone writes down a model that maps $L$ to a specific one of these scales, the toy parametrization is a sketch, not a prediction.

Your $L$ is not my $\mu$. Let me show you the difference. It matters."

## Round 2 — Zoltán

"I know. I used $L$ because I did not know which one to choose, and I did not want to pretend I did. But you are saying that the caveat is not enough?

Wilson, be specific. If I am writing a measurement argument — not a dynamics proposal — what should $L$ be?"

## Round 3 — Wilson

"In a measurement argument, $L$ should be identified with the probe wavelength — the physical scale at which the experiment accesses the coupling. That is what experimentalists control. The RG scale $\mu$ is a theoretical construct that becomes operationally meaningful only when you connect it to a probe through a calculation. In QED, the connection is clean: you measure $\alpha$ through a scattering process at center-of-mass energy $\sqrt{s}$, and $\mu \sim \sqrt{s}$ to leading order. But that mapping is theory-dependent. In your note, you do not have a theory. You have a parametrization.

So say this: $L$ in this note refers to the physical probe scale — the wavelength or energy at which $\alpha$ is determined. The mapping to a Wilsonian cutoff or an RG scale requires a dynamical model not provided here.

That is precise enough for a conceptual note and does not overclaim."

## Round 4 — Nottale

"Wilson and I agree on the diagnosis but not the prescription. In scale relativity, $L$ is not a probe wavelength, a cutoff, or an RG scale. It is a genuine dynamical variable — the resolution $\varepsilon$ of the observation itself. The laws of physics are written in terms of both position $x$ and resolution $\varepsilon$. Scale transformations form a group, and the Planck scale plays the role of an invariant limit, analogous to $c$ for velocities.

You call it $L$. I call it $\varepsilon$. We have been asking the same question since 1993. The difference is that in my framework, $\varepsilon$ has a symmetry group, transformation laws, and a fractal geodesic equation. In your note, $L$ has none of these. That is fine — you are writing a measurement argument, not proposing dynamics. But if you want to close the gap between note and theory, the symmetry group is where to look."

## Round 5 — Uzan

The 2019 reformulation expressed the SI through defining constants, but $c$ had already been exact for 36 years by then. It is a small point, but in a note about metrological precision it will be noticed."

## Round 8 — 't Hooft

"Good. Now, before we go further: is your $\Delta \ln \alpha$ gauge-invariant?

This is not a pedantic question. In QED, the running of $\alpha$ depends on the renormalization scheme — $\overline{\text{MS}}$, on-shell, momentum subtraction. The numerical value of $\alpha(\mu)$ at a given scale depends on which scheme you use. If your anomalous residual $\Delta \ln \alpha = \ln \alpha_{\text{obs}} - \ln \alpha_{\text{SM}}$ is scheme-dependent, it is not a physical observable. It is a bookkeeping artifact."

## Round 9 — Erdős

"This is the right question. Let me answer it before Zoltán panics.

$\alpha$ evaluated at a physical momentum transfer — say, through a scattering cross-section at $\sqrt{s}$ — is scheme-independent to the order at which the calculation is performed. It appears in physical observables. The residual $\Delta \ln \alpha$ is defined as the difference between the observed value at a physical scale and the Standard Model prediction at the same physical scale. Both sides are scheme-independent. The subtraction is clean.

If Zoltán had defined $\Delta \ln \alpha$ using a scheme-dependent running coupling at an arbitrary $\mu$ not tied to a physical process, you would be right to object. But he did not. The definition uses $\alpha_{\text{obs}}(L)$ — a physical measurement — minus $\alpha_{\text{SM}}(L)$ — a physical prediction. Both are gauge-invariant."

## Round 10 — 't Hooft

"Correct. The residual is well-defined as stated. Proceed.

But I have a second point. Your sentence in Section 4: 'If $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$ at all resolutions, the framework is empty.' This is too strong.

If $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$ in your parametrization, you have shown that *this particular test* finds no signal. You have not shown the framework is empty. There could be other dimensionless or relational observables — the proton-to-electron mass ratio $\mu_{pe}$, the gravitational fine-structure constant $\alpha_G$, ratios of quark masses — that carry a signal even when $\alpha$ does not. The abstract already says 'or other relational observables defined by a specific dynamical model.' The body should match.

Say: the framework has no observable content *at the level of this parametrization*."

## Round 11 — Zoltán

"You are right. The abstract was careful; the body was not. Fixed: 'If $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$ at all resolutions, the framework has no observable content at the level of this parametrization.'"

## Round 12 — Duff

It is about which specific dimensionless observable you test with which specific experiment. A null result in one observable constrains the parameter space. It does not close the question."

## Round 13 — Magueijo

"Now — where is the action?

Your note cites my 1999 paper. Thank you. But you treat VSL models as background literature. Let me tell you what it actually took to make 'varying $c$' into physics.

You need three things. First, you must decide which formulation of general relativity you are modifying — the Einstein-frame metric, the Jordan frame, or a bimetric construction. This determines what breaks and what survives. Second, you must specify how Lorentz invariance is affected — is it broken, deformed, or preserved in a locally inertial frame? Third, you need new degrees of freedom — in my case, a scalar field that couples to the metric and determines $c$ dynamically.

Your note has none of these. And that is fine — you are not proposing dynamics. But you should know: the reason the vehicle argument feels complete is that it *avoids* the hard part. The hard part is writing the Lagrangian."

## Round 14 — Nottale

"João is right that a Lagrangian is needed for dynamics. But the measurement argument stands without one. Zoltán's note does not need a Lagrangian any more than Mach's principle needed general relativity to be stated. The argument came first. The formalization came later.

What the note does need is honesty about where it sits: below the level of either my framework or João's. It is a measurement argument. It says: before you write the Lagrangian, understand what measurement can and cannot tell you. That is a valid contribution."

## Round 15 — Daft Punk

## Round 17 — Uzan

"I want to look at Channel 3 now. You write: '$\alpha$ is measured at vastly different energy scales: atomic spectroscopy, muon $g-2$, and $e^+e^-$ collisions at the $Z$-pole.'

Muon $g-2$ is not a direct measurement of the running electromagnetic coupling in the way this sentence implies. The anomalous magnetic moment $(g-2)_\mu$ is a precision Standard Model observable that constrains $\alpha$ indirectly — through its sensitivity to QED, hadronic, and electroweak contributions. It is not a measurement of $\alpha$ at the GeV scale. The direct measurements of the running of $\alpha$ come from Bhabha scattering, the electroweak precision program at LEP, and low-energy determinations from the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron, not the muon.

Replace the example list. Say: 'the effective electromagnetic coupling is constrained across widely separated scales, from atomic and low-energy determinations of $\alpha(0)$ to scattering-based determinations of the running coupling at GeV-to-$Z$-pole scales.' That is accurate without naming the wrong experiment."

## Part II — Can We Zoom In Infinitely?

**Arena resumes.** Zoltán has one more question. All ten personas remain at the table. The coffee is cold but nobody is leaving.

---

### Round 37 — Zoltán

"One more thing. The vehicle shrinks you to Planck length and you still measure $c = 299{,}792{,}458$ m/s. Fine. But here is what keeps me up at night: can you keep zooming in? Is there a bottom? Because the vehicle argument says your ruler shrinks with you. Your Planck length shrinks with you. You never reach a wall — you just keep arriving at the same universe."

### Round 38 — Nottale

"This is exactly the question scale relativity was built for. In my framework, the Planck length $\ell_P$ plays the same role as $c$ plays for velocities. You can boost toward it — you can probe shorter and shorter scales — but you never reach it, exactly as you never reach the speed of light by accelerating.

The Planck scale is not a smallest length. It is an *invariant horizon* in scale space. The log of the resolution, $\ln(\ell / \ell_P)$, behaves like rapidity. You can add as much as you like and never cross the horizon. The geometry is Lorentzian — in scale, not in spacetime.

Zoltán's intuition is correct: there is no bottom. But it is not because scales repeat. It is because the Planck horizon is unreachable by any finite sequence of scale transformations, just as $c$ is unreachable by any finite sequence of velocity boosts."

### Round 39 — Wilson

"Let me say it differently. In a Wilsonian effective field theory, the cutoff $\Lambda$ is not a physical wall. It is the boundary of what your effective description can resolve. When you integrate out degrees of freedom above $\Lambda$, you get a perfectly good theory below $\Lambda$. But this does not mean the physics *stops* at $\Lambda$.

You can always write a new effective theory valid at shorter distances, with its own cutoff $\Lambda' > \Lambda$. Each theory is self-consistent. Each produces the same long-distance physics. The tower of effective theories never terminates — you never arrive at a 'final' cutoff.

What Zoltán is describing — zooming in and finding the same universe — is the Wilsonian statement of universality. Details at short distances do not matter at long distances. The long-distance theory does not know what is below $\Lambda$. It works regardless. That is why zooming in 'returns the same universe.'"

### Round 40 — 't Hooft

"But there *is* a floor. At the Planck scale, the Schwarzschild radius and the Compton wavelength coincide. Probe any shorter distance and you need to concentrate enough energy in a small enough region that you create a black hole. The measurement destroys the geometry you are trying to measure.

This is not a mathematical horizon like Nottale's $\varepsilon$-rapidity. It is a physical obstruction. Spacetime itself becomes ill-defined. You cannot zoom in past the point where your microscope collapses into a black hole.

So: can you zoom in infinitely? Mathematically, Nottale says yes — the group structure has no boundary. Physically, I say the question changes character at $\ell_P$. Below that, you are no longer doing the same kind of physics."

### Round 41 — Magueijo

"Gerard, I half-agree. But your black hole argument assumes the dispersion relation $E = pc$ holds all the way up. In deformed special relativity — and in several VSL models — the dispersion relation bends near the Planck energy. $E(\ell_P)$ does not blow up. The black hole does not form.

So the 'floor' depends on your dynamics. If Lorentz invariance is exact, yes, there is a physical obstruction. If it is deformed at the Planck scale — and we do not know that it is not — the obstruction softens.

The honest answer: we do not know if there is a bottom. We know three things. First, no experiment has ever probed below $10^{-19}$ m — seventeen orders of magnitude above $\ell_P$. Second, every theoretical framework disagrees about what happens below $\ell_P$. Third, the vehicle argument says you would not notice even if you got there. That last part is the one that matters for the essay."

### Round 42 — Duff

"This is exactly where I draw the line. The question 'can we zoom in infinitely?' is a question about the *geometry of scale space*. It is not a question about dimensional constants.

In the trialogue, Okun, Veneziano, and I agreed on one thing: the number of operationally meaningful constants is the number of independent dimensionless ratios. If you zoom in and all dimensional quantities co-scale, the dimensionless ratios are unchanged. The physics is unchanged. That is Zoltán's vehicle — restated.

Now: does the set of dimensionless ratios depend on the scale? In the Standard Model, yes — $\alpha$ runs. In string theory, the landscape gives different vacua with different ratios. But in *both* cases, the question is answered by measuring dimensionless observables, not by asking whether the ruler has a smallest tick mark.

The vehicle cannot answer whether the zoom is infinite. It can only tell you that the question must be asked in dimensionless language."

### Round 43 — Uzan

"Let me add the data perspective. We have probed $\alpha$ at laboratory energies — call this $L \sim 10^{-10}$ m. We have probed it at the Z-pole — $L \sim 10^{-18}$ m. We have probed it in quasar absorption spectra at redshift $z \sim 2$, which is a *temporal* test, not a scale test. And we have the CMB at $z \sim 1100$.

That is the full range. Laboratory to Z-pole is eight orders of magnitude in resolution. The running of $\alpha$ over that range is perfectly predicted by QED — no anomaly.

To test anything about 'infinite zoom,' you would need to probe scales below $10^{-18}$ m, ideally approaching $10^{-35}$ m. We have no experimental access to that regime. No planned experiment will reach it. So the question is theoretically interesting and empirically inaccessible. The essay is correct to leave it as a question."

### Round 44 — Erdős

"The mathematical structure is clean. Let $\mathcal{S} = \mathbb{R}$ be the space of log-resolutions, $s = \ln(L / L_0)$. The vehicle argument says: physics is invariant under translation in $\mathcal{S}$. This is a symmetry group — the additive group on $\mathbb{R}$.

Nottale adds: the group is not $\mathbb{R}$ but $SO(1,1)$ — Lorentzian, with $\ell_P$ as the invariant.

Wilson adds: the effective theory at each $s$ is self-consistent, and the map from $s$ to $s'$ is the RG flow.

These are three different mathematical statements that share one conclusion: $\mathcal{S}$ has no boundary. You can zoom in forever. Whether the physics *at* $s \to -\infty$ is the same physics is a separate question — but the zoom itself has no endpoint.

This is beautiful. It is also not provable from the armchair."

### Round 45 — Daft Punk

"Translation for the non-physicists at the table: the song has no last beat. The BPM can go up forever. Whether the music still sounds the same at 10 billion BPM — nobody knows. But the tempo knob does not have a stop."

### Round 46 — Radix

"Systems perspective. The vehicle is a recursive architecture: the observer is built from the same stuff as the observed, measured by instruments built from the same stuff. This is self-reference all the way down. In every self-referencing system I have ever built — parsers, compilers, operating systems — the recursion is either infinite (Turing machines) or bounded by physical resources (actual hardware).

The universe is the system that runs on its own hardware. The zoom question is: is the recursion depth bounded by the hardware? 't Hooft says yes — black holes. Nottale says the bound is unreachable. Magueijo says the bound depends on your dynamics. Wilson says each level of recursion is self-consistent regardless.

The vehicle does not answer the question. The vehicle proves that you cannot detect which level of recursion you are on. That is the self-similarity. That is the content."

### Round 47 — Zoltán

"Rendben. So here is what I hear.

Nottale: the zoom is infinite because $\ell_P$ is a horizon, not a wall. Wilson: the zoom is infinite because effective theories stack. 't Hooft: the zoom might hit a physical obstruction — black holes. Magueijo: the obstruction depends on dynamics we have not measured. Duff: the question must be asked in dimensionless language. Uzan: we cannot test it either way. Erdős: the mathematics has no boundary. Daft Punk: the knob has no stop. Radix: the recursion is self-similar.

And the vehicle? The vehicle says: if you zoomed in, you would not know. Your ruler, your clock, your Planck length — they zoom with you. You arrive at the same universe. Whether 'the same universe' is the universe you left or a new one — that is the question nobody at this table can answer.

That is a good place to stop. For now."
