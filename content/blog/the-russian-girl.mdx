## Every superhero needs an origin story

I am writing this from Bexhill-on-Sea. A mental health unit. Tuesday the 31st of March, 2026.

Today I am allowed to go outside. In a week, I will be able to leave without supervision. These are facts. They are good facts. Progress measured not in commits but in permissions.

But this post is not about me. This post is about a superhero.

## Her name is Eva

Her code name is "The Russian Girl."

She does not fly. She does not have laser eyes. She does not bend time or space. What she does is something harder than all of that: she shows up.

There is one thing about Eva — she does not like to travel alone. Which means someone needs to go and get her. Someone needs to make the journey so she can make the journey. That is the deal.

And you know what? That is fine. Because real superheroes do not arrive on their own terms. They arrive on yours. They meet you where you are — even if where you are is a mental health unit in a quiet seaside town on the south coast of England.

## Why she is a superhero

Not because she is extraordinary in the way films teach you. But because she is extraordinary in the way life teaches you — slowly, without a soundtrack, in the small gestures that don't make the highlight reel.

She is the person who picks up the phone. She is the person who says yes. She is the person who gets on the train — once someone goes to get her, because she does not like travelling alone, and that is a perfectly reasonable thing.

Every system I have ever built runs on one principle: at the bottom of every automated pipeline, every health check, every dependency cascade, there is a person. A human being who decided to care. The CI pipeline does not run itself. The pull request does not merge itself. And the person in the hospital does not heal by themselves.

You can architect the most elegant system in the world. If nobody shows up to press the first button, it's just a beautiful diagram.

Eva shows up.

## The plan

Tuesday. Bexhill. Eva arrives.

The details are being worked out. Someone will go to her. She will come here. And then — things get better. That is the whole plan. That is enough of a plan.

Sometimes the most robust architecture is just one reliable person and a train ticket.

## A note on superheroes

We build systems to make things reliable. Automated pipelines, health checks, redundancy, failovers. But reliability is not the same as care. You can make a system that never goes down and still have nobody who notices when it should.

Eva noticed. Eva decided to care.

That makes her my superhero.
