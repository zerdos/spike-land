> **Abstract.** I propose a thought experiment — the vehicle with no windows — that exposes a gap between two symmetry claims: Einstein's postulate that $c$ is the same for all inertial observers (Lorentz invariance), and the unstated assumption that $c$ is the same at all operational resolution scales (scale invariance). I argue that no single-scale measurement of a dimensional constant can distinguish between true constancy and co-variant scaling, because the measuring apparatus is built from the same physics as the thing being measured. The only escape is through dimensionless observables. I define a falsifiable parameter $\delta_\alpha$ — the anomalous scaling exponent of the fine-structure constant — and survey current experimental bounds. This is not a new theory. It is a question that the existing data has not yet closed.
>
> **Keywords:** scale invariance, fine-structure constant, self-referential measurement, varying constants, dimensionless observables
>
> **Zoltan Erdos** — Brighton, UK, March 2026

## 1. The vehicle with no windows

Imagine a vehicle that can change your size — from the Planck length to the observable universe. It has no windows. You have only your instruments: a ruler, a clock, a laser, and a mirror.

You measure the speed of light. You get $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle shrinks you by a factor of $10^{35}$. Your ruler shrinks. Your clock shrinks. Your laser, your nervous system, your concept of "one meter" — all shrink with you. You measure again. $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle grows you to cosmological size. Same number.

You will *always* get this number. Not because the speed of light is constant at every scale — but because your measuring apparatus scales with the thing being measured. A ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

This is not a paradox. It is a constraint on what measurement can tell us: **a dimensional constant measured with instruments made of the same physics is self-referential.** To ask whether $c$ varies with scale, we must look at *dimensionless* combinations of constants — ratios that do not depend on our choice of units.

---

## 2. Two symmetries, not one

In 1905, Einstein postulated that the speed of light is the same for all *inertial observers*. That is Lorentz invariance — a symmetry under changes of velocity. He did not postulate that $c$ is the same at all *operational resolutions*. These are different claims. Lorentz invariance is about motion. Scale invariance would be about resolution. One has been tested to extraordinary precision. The other has barely been asked about — because our instruments always sit at one scale.

This gap was noticed decades ago. Nottale proposed *scale relativity* in 1993, postulating exactly this symmetry: the laws of physics should be invariant under changes of resolution scale, just as they are under Lorentz boosts [1]. Magueijo and Albrecht developed varying-speed-of-light cosmologies in 1999, with a concrete dynamical model [2]. Barrow explored the broader question of varying constants in 2002 [3]. I am not inventing a new idea. I am trying to state the old one as clearly as I can.

In quantum field theory, the electromagnetic coupling $\alpha$ runs with the energy at which you probe it — well-tested QED. The question I am asking is different: does $\alpha$ show any *additional* scale-dependence beyond standard running, tied to the operational resolution of the measurement itself?

The natural dimensionless observable is the fine-structure constant:

$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{\hbar c}$$

If $c$ varies with scale while $e$ and $\hbar$ vary differently, $\alpha$ will change. That change is measurable. That is the way out of the vehicle.

## 3. A toy model

Let $L$ be the operational resolution scale — the wavelength of the probe, or the size of the measuring apparatus. (I will be honest: what exactly $L$ *is* remains ambiguous. A real theory would need to pin this down. For now, it is a placeholder.) Assume:

$$c(L) = c_0 \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^{\delta}$$

where $L_P$ is the Planck length and $\delta \ll 1$ is a dimensionless scaling exponent. If $\delta = 0$, there is nothing here — $c$ is truly constant. If $\delta \neq 0$, then $c$ changes with scale, but no single-scale observer can detect it because their instruments co-scale.

Now suppose other constants ($e$, $\hbar$) co-vary with their own exponents. The fine-structure constant at scale $L$ becomes:

$$\alpha(L) \propto \frac{e(L)^2}{\hbar(L) \, c(L)}$$

If the exponents do not cancel perfectly:

$$\frac{\alpha(L_1)}{\alpha(L_2)} = \left(\frac{L_1}{L_2}\right)^{\delta_\alpha}$$

Here $\delta_\alpha$ is the combination of individual scaling exponents that survives in the dimensionless ratio. This is the one number that matters:

- **$\delta_\alpha = 0$**: The framework is empty. All constants co-vary perfectly. Varying $c$ is a choice of units. I am wrong in a boring way.
- **$\delta_\alpha \neq 0$**: The framework has physical content. It can be tested.

I should be honest about what this model is: two free parameters ($\delta$ and the identification of $L$), zero data points. It is a bet, not a result.

## 4. What the data say

The fine-structure constant has been measured across different scales and epochs. The most sensitive constraints:

**Laboratory tests.** Atomic clock comparisons bound $\dot{\alpha}/\alpha$ at $\sim 10^{-17}$ per year. These constrain *temporal* variation — how fast $\alpha$ drifts over time. The mapping from temporal drift to the kind of scale-dependence I am describing is an additional assumption, not a direct measurement.

**Astrophysical quasar spectra.** Early claims of a dipole variation at $\sim 10^{-5}$ (Webb et al.) have not held up in larger, more carefully calibrated samples. Current combined analyses find no statistically significant deviation, with some bounds at sub-ppm precision. These constrain $\alpha$ at high *redshift* — a different epoch and distance — but not necessarily at a different operational resolution scale in the sense of this toy model.

**Cosmic microwave background.** The recombination history constrains $\alpha$ at redshift $z \sim 1100$ to within $\sim 10^{-3}$ of its present value.

If $\delta_\alpha$ exists, it must be very small — consistent with zero given current data. But "not yet detected" is not the same as "ruled out." The bounds constrain. They do not eliminate.

---

## 5. What would make this physics?

Three things are missing before this thought experiment becomes a testable theory:

1. **A concrete definition of $L$.** In RG theory, the scale is the probe energy, and running is derived from QFT. Here, $L$ is ambiguous — apparatus size? probe wavelength? coarse-graining cutoff? Without pinning $L$ to a physical observable, $\delta$ and $L$ absorb each other.

2. **A mechanism.** The ansatz $c(L) \propto L^{\delta}$ is a placeholder. Why should $c$ scale? How should the other constants scale? Nottale offered a fractal spacetime geometry. Magueijo and Albrecht offered a dynamical scalar field. I am offering neither — only the question.

3. **A dimensionless falsifiable prediction.** Measure $\alpha$ at two widely separated scales with enough precision to detect deviation from standard QED running. This is already being done. So far, no extra scale-dependence has been found.

## 6. Why I wrote this

I am a programmer, not a physicist. I have two dogs. I live in Brighton. I have no business writing about the fine-structure constant.

But I had this image in my head — a vehicle that changes your size, with no windows — and I could not get rid of it. The image said something simple: *you cannot tell if c changes with scale because you change with it.* That felt like it should be either obviously wrong or obviously known. It turned out to be known — Nottale, Magueijo, Barrow, others got there decades before me. But the *image* — the sticky, human-scale version of the argument — I had not seen stated this way.

So I wrote it down. I put it through a math arena with three personas who tried to destroy it. They could not destroy the thought experiment — but they showed me, correctly, that a thought experiment is not a theory. For that I need $\delta_\alpha$. I do not have it. Nobody does — yet.

The vehicle has no windows. But it has a door. The door is marked $\delta_\alpha$.
