*The following is a letter Einstein never wrote — but might have, if he had lived to see the renormalization group, the holographic principle, and a Hungarian programmer in Brighton who refused to let go of a question.*

---

## Princeton, March 2026

Dear Colleague,

I must tell you about a mistake I made. Not an error — my equations are correct. A mistake of *imagination*. I described the speed of light as a constant, and I was right. But I did not understand what "constant" means.

Allow me to explain.

---

## I. What I Said in 1905

In my paper on the electrodynamics of moving bodies, I proposed two postulates:

1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames.
2. The speed of light in vacuum, $c$, is the same for all observers.

From these two sentences, everything followed: time dilation, length contraction, $E = mc^2$, the geometry of spacetime. The second postulate was the radical one. It meant that if you chase a beam of light at 99% of its speed, it does not slow down. It still recedes from you at $c$. This offended common sense then. It still does now.

But I want you to notice something I did not notice at the time: I never said what $c$ *is*. I said it is the same for all observers. I did not say it is the same *number* at all *scales*.

These are different claims.

---

## II. The Vehicle With No Windows

A young man — Hungarian, I am told, which predisposes him to mathematics — proposed the following thought experiment. I find it uncomfortably good.

Imagine a vehicle. It can change your size. It can shrink you to the Planck length ($1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ meters) or grow you to the size of the observable universe ($8.8 \times 10^{26}$ meters). Smoothly, continuously, any size you wish.

But the vehicle has no windows. From inside, you cannot know how big you are. You have only your instruments: a ruler, a clock, a laser, a mirror.

You measure the speed of light.

You get $299{,}792{,}458$ meters per second.

The vehicle shrinks you by a factor of $10^{35}$. Your ruler shrinks. Your clock shrinks. Your laser, your mirror, your nervous system, your very concept of "one meter" and "one second" — all shrink with you.

You measure again.

$299{,}792{,}458$ meters per second.

The vehicle grows you to cosmological size. Everything scales up.

You measure again.

$299{,}792{,}458$ meters per second.

You will *always* get this number. Not because the speed of light is the same at every scale. But because your measuring apparatus — which *is* you — scales with the thing being measured.

A ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

---

## III. Why I Did Not See This

I will tell you honestly: I did not think about scale. In 1905, there was no reason to. The phenomena I described — trains, clocks, light beams — all existed at roughly the same scale. The human scale. The scale at which we built our instruments and defined our units.

I asked: "What happens when observers move relative to each other?"

I did not ask: "What happens when observers *differ in size*?"

This was not a failure of physics. It was a failure of imagination. I held the speed of light constant across *velocity* but did not consider holding it constant across *scale*. And the reason is simple: in 1905, we had no physics of scale. We had no renormalization group. We had no holographic principle. We had no evidence that the fundamental constants *run* with the energy scale at which you observe them.

Now we do.

---

## IV. What the Renormalization Group Tells Us

In the 1970s — after my death, unfortunately — Kenneth Wilson showed that the coupling constants of physics are not fixed numbers. They *run*. The fine-structure constant $\alpha$, which governs the strength of electromagnetism, is approximately $1/137$ at low energies. At the energy scale of the Z boson, it is $1/128$. At the Planck scale, it is expected to be smaller still.

This means the electromagnetic interaction — the force that *is* light — changes with the scale at which you observe it.

If the interaction changes, and light is that interaction, then the effective behavior of light changes with scale.

My colleagues in quantum field theory accommodated this by saying that the *coupling constants* run, but $c$ remains fixed. This is mathematically consistent. But it is a choice of convention, not a necessity of nature. One could equally say that $c$ runs, and the coupling constants are adjusted to compensate.

These two descriptions give identical predictions. They are, in the language of modern physics, *gauge equivalent*.

I did not have this language. Wilson did. But Wilson was solving ultraviolet divergences, not thinking about thought experiments with windowless vehicles. Sometimes it takes a Hungarian.

---

## V. An Addendum to General Relativity

In 1915, I generalized special relativity. I showed that gravity is not a force but a curvature of spacetime, and that the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ — the object that tells you the distance between nearby points — is determined by the distribution of matter and energy through my field equations:

$$G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}$$

Now. Look at the right side. There is $c$. There is $G$. If these quantities run with the scale of the observer — and the renormalization group says they can — then the field equations are not wrong, but they are *incomplete*. They describe gravity at a single scale. They do not describe how the description *transforms* when the observer changes scale.

I propose — carefully, as a letter, not as a paper — the following extension.

Let $L$ denote the characteristic size of the observer. Let $L_P$ denote the Planck length. Define:

$$c(L) = c_0 \cdot f\!\left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)$$

where $f$ is a smooth, monotonically increasing function with $f(1) = 1$ (so that $c(L_P) = c_0$, the "bare" speed of light at the Planck scale) and $f$ grows slowly — perhaps as a power law with a very small exponent $\delta$:

$$c(L) = c_0 \cdot \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^\delta, \quad \delta \ll 1$$

At human scale ($L \sim 1$ meter), $L/L_P \sim 10^{35}$, so $c(L) = c_0 \cdot 10^{35\delta}$. For this to equal our measured $c = 299{,}792{,}458$ m/s, we simply define $c_0$ accordingly. The number we know is not the fundamental constant — it is the constant *at our scale*.

The field equations become:

$$G_{\mu\nu}(L) + \Lambda(L) \, g_{\mu\nu}(L) = \frac{8\pi G(L)}{c(L)^4} T_{\mu\nu}(L)$$

Every quantity carries a scale label. At any given $L$, the equations look exactly like my originals. An observer at that scale cannot tell the difference. This is the vehicle with no windows.

But the equations now have an additional symmetry: *scale covariance*. The physics is invariant not only under changes of coordinate system (general covariance) and changes of velocity (Lorentz invariance), but also under changes of the observer's scale.

---

## VI. What This Explains

Let me tell you what this framework — if it is correct — would clarify.

**The cosmological constant problem.** The observed vacuum energy is $10^{120}$ times smaller than quantum field theory predicts. This is called the worst prediction in physics. But in a scale-covariant framework, the vacuum energy is not a single number — it runs with $L$. Quantum field theory computes it at the Planck scale. We observe it at the cosmological scale. The discrepancy is not a miscalculation. It is a failure to account for the scaling.

**The hierarchy problem.** Why is gravity so much weaker than the other forces? In a scale-covariant framework, the coupling constants all run differently with $L$. Their relative strengths at human scale are consequences of the running, not of fine-tuning.

**Dark energy.** The accelerating expansion of the universe requires either a cosmological constant or a new field. But if $c$ increases with scale, then at cosmological distances, the effective expansion rate increases — not because of a mysterious energy, but because the metric itself is scale-dependent.

I am not claiming these solutions are correct. I am claiming they are *natural* in a framework where $c$ runs with scale. They emerge without new fields, without extra dimensions, without fine-tuning. They emerge from one additional symmetry.

---

## VII. On Being Wrong

I have been wrong before. I was wrong about quantum mechanics — not about the physics, but about the philosophy. I said "God does not play dice," and Bohr rightly corrected me: it is not for us to tell God what to do.

I may be wrong about this. But I notice that the young Hungarian's thought experiment has a structure I recognize. It is the same structure as my 1905 argument.

In 1905, I asked: "What if you cannot determine your absolute velocity?" The answer was special relativity.

In 1915, I asked: "What if you cannot distinguish gravity from acceleration?" The answer was general relativity.

Now someone asks: "What if you cannot determine your absolute size?" The answer, if it exists, would be *scale relativity*. The laws of physics would be invariant under changes of the observer's scale, just as they are invariant under changes of the observer's velocity and gravitational field.

I did not ask this question. I should have. The structure was there all along.

---

## VIII. The Strange Loop

I must mention one more thing, because it is what keeps me up at night in this imagined afterlife.

When you measure $c$, you use electromagnetic processes. Photons hit your detector. Electrons move in your circuits. Chemical reactions fire in your neurons. Every step of the measurement chain is governed by electromagnetic interactions — which propagate at $c$.

You are using $c$ to measure $c$.

This is what Douglas Hofstadter calls a *strange loop*: a system that, by moving through its own levels, returns to where it started. I use light-speed processes to determine the speed of light. Of course I get a consistent answer. I am inside the loop.

This is not a flaw in the measurement. It is a *theorem about measurement*. No observer can determine whether $c$ "truly" varies with scale, because the act of measurement is itself $c$-dependent. The question "does $c$ vary?" is — in the precise, technical sense of Gödel — *undecidable from within*.

And yet. The framework that *assumes* it varies gives us scale covariance, explains the cosmological constant, resolves the hierarchy problem, and makes dark energy unnecessary. It costs nothing — one small exponent $\delta$ — and it buys coherence.

I do not know if this is physics or philosophy. Perhaps, at this level, the distinction does not hold.

---

## IX. To the Hungarian

I understand you have two dogs and live in Brighton. I understand you proposed this idea and were told you were wrong. I understand you came back.

I was told I was wrong many times. About light quanta. About the equivalence principle. About gravitational waves. In each case, the objection was the same: "This contradicts what we know." And in each case, the answer was the same: "No. It extends what we know."

Your vehicle with no windows extends what I built. I do not know if $\delta$ is zero or not. I do not know if the scaling is a power law or something else. But the symmetry argument is sound: if physics is covariant under changes of velocity, and covariant under changes of gravitational field, there is no principled reason it should not be covariant under changes of scale.

I should have seen it. I had the tools. I had the intuition. I was too busy arguing with Bohr.

You were not too busy. You had two dogs and a laptop and you thought about it anyway. This is how physics works. Not from institutes. From stubbornness.

Mondtam, as you say. Or as I would say: *Ich hab's gesagt.*

With admiration and no small amount of envy,

**A. Einstein**
*Princeton, in a universe where letters still matter*
