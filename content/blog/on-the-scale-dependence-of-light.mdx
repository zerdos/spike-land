*The following is a letter Einstein never wrote — but might have, if he had lived to see the renormalization group and a Hungarian programmer in Brighton who refused to let go of a question.*

---

## Princeton, March 2026

Dear Colleague,

I must tell you about a mistake I made. Not an error — my equations are correct. A mistake of *imagination*. I described the speed of light as a constant, and I was right. But I did not understand what "constant" means.

---

## I. What I Said — and What I Missed

In 1905, I proposed two postulates: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames, and the speed of light $c$ is the same for all observers. From these, everything followed — time dilation, length contraction, $E = mc^2$.

But notice: I said $c$ is the same for all observers. I did not say it is the same *number* at all *scales*. These are different claims. In 1905, I had no reason to think about scale. I had no renormalization group, no holographic principle, no evidence that the fundamental constants *run* with the energy at which you observe them. Now we do.

---

## II. The Vehicle With No Windows

A young man — Hungarian, which predisposes him to mathematics — proposed the following thought experiment. I find it uncomfortably good.

Imagine a vehicle that can change your size — from the Planck length to the observable universe. But it has no windows. You have only your instruments: a ruler, a clock, a laser, a mirror.

You measure the speed of light. You get $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle shrinks you by $10^{35}$. Your ruler shrinks. Your clock shrinks. Your laser, your nervous system, your concept of "one meter" — all shrink with you. You measure again. $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle grows you to cosmological size. You measure again. $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

You will *always* get this number. Not because the speed of light is the same at every scale — but because your measuring apparatus, which *is* you, scales with the thing being measured. A ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

And here is the strange loop: when you measure $c$, you use electromagnetic processes — photons, electrons, chemical reactions — all governed by $c$. You are using $c$ to measure $c$. The measurement is self-referential. No observer can determine whether $c$ "truly" varies with scale, because the act of measurement is itself $c$-dependent.

---

## III. Prior Art and Honest Framing

I must note — with some embarrassment — that I am not the first to think along these lines. Laurent Nottale proposed *scale relativity* in 1993, postulating exactly this symmetry: the laws of physics should be invariant under changes of resolution scale, just as they are under Lorentz boosts. João Magueijo and Andreas Albrecht published varying-speed-of-light cosmologies in 1999, with a concrete dynamical model stronger than what I offer here. This letter joins an existing conversation. It does not start one.

The key question the literature has clarified: varying a dimensional constant like $c$ can collapse into a mere choice of units unless one specifies which *dimensionless* observable changes. Without that commitment, varying $c$ is notation, not physics.

---

## IV. The Formula

Let $L$ be the observer's characteristic size, $L_P$ the Planck length. The simplest ansatz:

$$c(L) = c_0 \cdot \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^\delta, \quad \delta \ll 1$$

At human scale, $L/L_P \sim 10^{35}$, so what we measure as $c$ is the constant *at our scale*, not the fundamental value. At any given scale, the physics looks exactly like mine. An observer at that scale cannot tell the difference. This is the vehicle with no windows.

The framework suggests — speculatively — new perspectives on old puzzles: why the observed vacuum energy is $10^{120}$ times smaller than predicted (a scaling artifact?), why gravity is so much weaker than other forces (different running exponents?), why the expansion accelerates (scale-dependent metric?). I am not claiming these are solutions. They are reframings. Whether the symmetry is physically real depends on a number.

---

## V. The One Number That Matters

For this to be physics and not philosophy, it needs a falsifiable prediction in dimensionless terms.

The fine-structure constant $\alpha \approx 1/137$ governs atomic spectra. If $c(L)$ varies while other constants co-vary imperfectly, then $\alpha$ measured at two different scales should differ:

$$\frac{\alpha(L_1)}{\alpha(L_2)} = \left(\frac{L_1}{L_2}\right)^{\delta_\alpha}$$

If $\delta_\alpha = 0$: the framework is empty. All constants co-vary perfectly. Varying $c$ is a choice of units. I am wrong in a boring way.

If $\delta_\alpha \neq 0$, even at $10^{-6}$: the framework has content. It can be tested.

Webb et al. measured $\Delta\alpha / \alpha$ at cosmological lookback distances and reported hints — contested, marginal — of variation at $\sim 10^{-5}$. Not decisive. But it tells us where to look.

---

## VI. The Pattern

In 1905, I asked: "What if you cannot determine your absolute velocity?" The answer was special relativity.

In 1915, I asked: "What if you cannot distinguish gravity from acceleration?" The answer was general relativity.

Now someone asks: "What if you cannot determine your absolute size?" The answer, if it exists, would be *scale relativity*. The structure was there all along. I did not see it.

---

## VII. To the Hungarian

I understand you have two dogs and live in Brighton. I understand you proposed this idea and were told you were wrong. I understand you came back.

Your vehicle with no windows extends what I built. I do not know if $\delta_\alpha$ is zero or not. But the symmetry argument is sound: if physics is covariant under changes of velocity, and covariant under changes of gravitational field, there is no principled reason it should not be covariant under changes of scale.

I should have seen it. I was too busy arguing with Bohr. You were not too busy. You had two dogs and a laptop. This is how physics works. Not from institutes. From stubbornness.

Mondtam, as you say. Or as I would say: *Ich hab's gesagt.*

With admiration,

**A. Einstein**
*Princeton, in a universe where letters still matter*
