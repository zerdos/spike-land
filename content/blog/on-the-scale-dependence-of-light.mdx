> **Abstract.** A standalone variation of a dimensional constant is operationally empty unless it induces measurable residuals in dimensionless observables or other relational observables defined by a specific dynamical model. I present a thought experiment — the "vehicle with no windows" — to clarify why. If measuring devices co-scale with the physics they probe, single-scale measurements cannot distinguish genuine variation from a change of units. The physically meaningful question is not whether $c$ alone varies, but whether dimensionless couplings — principally the fine-structure constant $\alpha$ — exhibit anomalous departures beyond the predictions of the Standard Model renormalization group. I define an anomalous residual $\Delta \ln \alpha$, distinguish three operationally distinct channels through which such a residual could appear (temporal drift, spatial/redshift variation, and probe-scale resolution dependence), and survey the tightest current bounds in each channel. All are consistent with zero anomaly.
>
> **Note.** This is a conceptual note — a measurement argument and literature survey, not a proposal for new dynamics. It contains no new theorem, no Lagrangian, and no original data analysis. It may be of interest as a pedagogical restatement of well-known constraints.
>
> **Keywords:** varying constants, fine-structure constant, self-referential measurement, anomalous residual, dimensionless observables, resolution dependence
>
> **Zoltan Erdos** — Brighton, UK, March 2026

## 1. The vehicle with no windows

Imagine a vehicle that can change your size — from the Planck length [1] to the observable universe. It has no windows. You have only your instruments: a ruler, a clock, a laser, and a mirror.

You measure the speed of light. You get $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s. (In the modern SI, this number is fixed by the definition of the metre [2] — it is not an experimentally floating quantity. That fact already encodes the operational point this section makes: metrology tests theory through comparisons of clocks, resonators, and transition frequencies — through relational and dimensionless quantities, not through the naked symbol $c$.)

Now the vehicle shrinks you by a factor of $10^{35}$. Your ruler shrinks. Your clock shrinks. Your laser, your nervous system, your concept of "one meter" — all shrink with you. You measure again. $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle grows you to cosmological size. You measure again. Same number.

You will always get this number. Not because the speed of light is necessarily constant at every scale — but because your measuring apparatus scales with the thing being measured. A ruler always measures itself as one ruler long. A fish cannot measure the water it swims in.

This is not a paradox. It is a constraint on what measurement can tell us: **a dimensional constant measured with instruments made of the same physics is self-referential.** To ask whether $c$ varies, we must look at *dimensionless* combinations of constants — ratios that do not depend on our choice of units. Ratios are the way out of the vehicle.

**A caveat on $L$.** Throughout this note I use $L$ to denote an "operational resolution scale." This is deliberately imprecise, and the imprecision is load-bearing. Probe wavelength, apparatus size, coarse-graining cutoff, and renormalization-group (RG) scale $\mu \sim 1/L$ are not interchangeable — they live in different theoretical frameworks and couple to different physics. Until $L$ is pinned to a specific physical observable in a specific dynamical model, the toy parametrization in Section 3 has a free variable on top of its free parameter. This ambiguity is a weakness, not a feature, and I flag it here rather than burying it in Section 3.

## 2. Two symmetries, and the existing literature

In 1905, Einstein postulated that the speed of light is the same for all *inertial observers*. That is Lorentz invariance [3] — a symmetry under changes of velocity. He did not postulate that $c$ is the same at all *operational resolutions*. These are different symmetry claims, and the difference matters.

Think of it this way: Einstein said the speed of light looks the same whether you're standing still or running alongside it. The distinct question is whether it looks the same whether you're an atom or a galaxy. One is about motion. The other is about scale. One has been tested to extraordinary precision. The other has been explored in a substantial literature on varying constants.

This question was formalized decades ago. Nottale proposed *scale relativity* in 1993, postulating that the laws of physics should be invariant under changes of resolution scale, just as they are under Lorentz boosts [5]. Albrecht and Magueijo developed varying-speed-of-light (VSL) cosmologies in 1999, with a concrete dynamical model and a Lagrangian [6]. Barrow explored the broader question of varying constants in 2002 [7]. Duff, Okun, and Veneziano argued in their 2002 *trialogue* that only variations of dimensionless constants are operationally meaningful — varying a dimensional constant like $c$ alone is ambiguous without specifying which dimensionless observable changes [8]. Uzan published comprehensive reviews of varying fundamental couplings [9], updated by Martins [10].

**Scope of the present argument.** The vehicle with no windows demonstrates that *universal co-scaling* — a scenario in which every physical quantity rescales identically — is unobservable from within. This is a narrow point. It does not imply that every varying-$c$ or scale-dependent scenario is empirically empty. The VSL literature includes models with explicit dynamics: Lorentz-breaking versions, bimetric constructions, and locally Lorentz-invariant formulations [6, 11]. Their observable content arises from *relative* changes between sectors (e.g., matter vs. gravity), from modifications to causal structure, or from shifts in dimensionless coefficients — not from the naked symbol $c$ changing in isolation. This note's argument applies only to the co-scaling case. Models that break the co-scaling assumption have their own phenomenology and are not addressed here.

## 3. The dimensionless observable

In quantum field theory (QFT) [12], the electromagnetic coupling $\alpha$ runs with the energy at which you probe it — this is well-tested quantum electrodynamics (QED) [13]. Like a conversation that changes tone depending on how close you stand: the interaction looks different at different energies. That is standard physics. The question is different from ordinary running: does $\alpha$ show any *anomalous* resolution dependence beyond what the Standard Model [14] predicts?

The natural dimensionless observable is the fine-structure constant [15]. In Gaussian CGS units:

$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{\hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}$$

(In SI, the denominator includes $4\pi\varepsilon_0$: $\alpha = e^2 / (4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar c)$. The numerical value is the same.)

If $c$ varies with resolution while $e$ and $\hbar$ vary differently, $\alpha$ will change. That change is measurable. That is the way out of the vehicle — the window that the vehicle does not have.

## 4. A toy parametrization — and its limits

The vehicle motivates a question. To make that question precise, we need to move from the dimensional constant $c$ — which cannot be tested in isolation — to a dimensionless observable that can.

One could write a toy ansatz like $c(L) = c_0 (L/L_{P,0})^\delta$, where $L_{P,0}$ is a fixed fiducial Planck length and $\delta \ll 1$. But this equation is a disposable heuristic — a sketch on a napkin, not an architectural drawing. Its only purpose is to motivate the move to a dimensionless quantity that *can* be tested.

That quantity is $\alpha$. The electromagnetic coupling already runs with energy in standard QED. The question is whether there is an *additional* departure — an anomalous residual beyond the Standard Model prediction. Define:

$$\Delta \ln \alpha(L) = \ln \alpha_{\text{obs}}(L) - \ln \alpha_{\text{SM}}(L)$$

This is the physically meaningful quantity. **If $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$ at all resolutions, the framework has no observable content at the level of this parametrization** — any variation of $c$ is just a change of units, exactly as Duff argues [8]. If $\Delta \ln \alpha \neq 0$ at some resolution, the framework has physical content.

In the simplest power-law parametrization:

$$\Delta \ln \alpha \approx \delta_\alpha \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{L_1}{L_2}\right)$$

where $\delta_\alpha$ is the anomalous scaling exponent — the part that is *not* accounted for by standard QED running.

This model has two free parameters ($\delta_\alpha$ and the identification of $L$) and zero data points. It is a bet, not a result.

## 5. Three channels, not one

The existing literature constrains $\alpha$-variation through three operationally distinct channels. They test different physics and should not be conflated — like measuring temperature, humidity, and wind speed. All three describe weather, but they are not the same measurement.

**Channel 1: Temporal drift.** Does $\alpha$ change with cosmic time? Atomic clock comparisons constrain the fractional drift of $\alpha$ at the $\sim 10^{-17}$ yr$^{-1}$ level [9, 16]. The NIST Hg$^+$/Al$^+$ result was $(-1.6 \pm 2.3) \times 10^{-17}$ yr$^{-1}$, consistent with zero [17]. These measure temporal variation at a single laboratory scale. The mapping from temporal drift to the kind of anomalous residual $\Delta \ln \alpha(L)$ proposed here requires an additional model-dependent assumption — that temporal evolution and resolution dependence are coupled through a common underlying field.

**Channel 2: Spatial and redshift variation.** Does $\alpha$ differ between here and distant astrophysical environments? High-precision ESPRESSO measurements found $\Delta\alpha/\alpha = 1.3 \pm 1.3_{\text{stat}} \pm 0.4_{\text{sys}}$ ppm for the well-studied system HE 0515-4414, and a weighted mean from better-calibrated measurements of $-0.5 \pm 0.5_{\text{stat}} \pm 0.4_{\text{sys}}$ ppm — both consistent with zero [18]. Earlier claims of a spatial dipole at the $\sim 10^{-5}$ level (Webb et al.) have not held up in larger, more carefully calibrated samples. A 2026 ALMA analysis of molecular absorption at high redshift reported $3\sigma$ limits of $|\Delta\alpha/\alpha| < 0.55$ ppm (PKS 1830$-$211) and $< 1.5$ ppm (B0218+357), described as deeper than previous single high-$z$ system constraints [19]. These test spatial/redshift variation, not resolution dependence per se — but they are the tightest constraints on $\alpha$-variation from astrophysical distances.

**Channel 3: Probe-scale (resolution) dependence.** Does $\alpha$ measured at one probe energy differ from $\alpha$ at another, beyond what the Standard Model predicts? This is the channel most directly relevant to $\Delta \ln \alpha(L)$. In practice, the effective electromagnetic coupling is constrained across widely separated scales, from atomic and low-energy determinations of $\alpha(0)$ to scattering-based determinations of the running coupling at GeV-to-$Z$-pole scales. At each scale the measured value agrees with the Standard Model's predicted running to within experimental uncertainty. No anomalous residual has been reported. However, framing these existing particle-physics measurements as a systematic test of $\Delta \ln \alpha(L)$ would require an explicit mapping between probe energy and the operational scale $L$ of this note — a mapping that does not yet exist.

**Cosmic microwave background (CMB).** Recombination-era constraints from the Planck satellite [20] are a hybrid: they constrain $\alpha$ at a single cosmological epoch ($z \approx 1100$), mixing temporal and environmental dependence. Hart and Chluba report $\alpha_{\text{EM}}/\alpha_{\text{EM},0} = 1.0005 \pm 0.0024$ from Planck 2018 CMB-only data (a generic single-field recombination-era constraint), and $1.0019 \pm 0.0022$ when baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) data is added [21]. These place percent-to-subpercent-level bounds on recombination-era departures.

**Summary.** All three channels are consistent with $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$. The classic review is Martins [10]; the most recent cosmographic update [22] likewise reports no statistically significant evidence for varying dimensionless couplings in any channel. The allowed parameter space is very small — but it is not zero. The universe has not said no. It has said: *not yet, not at this precision.*

## 6. What would make this physics?

Three things are missing before this thought experiment becomes a testable proposal:

1. **A concrete definition of $L$.** Is it an RG scale $\mu$? A geometric length? A detector bandwidth? Without pinning $L$ to a specific physical observable in a specific model, $\delta_\alpha$ and $L$ absorb each other and the parametrization says nothing. It is a variable with no address.

2. **A mechanism.** Why should $c$ depend on resolution? How should the other constants co-vary? Nottale offered a fractal spacetime geometry [5]. Magueijo and Albrecht offered a dynamical scalar field [6]. This note offers neither — only the question and a toy parametrization. A question without a mechanism is a promissory note.

3. **A testable anomalous residual.** Compare precision measurements of $\alpha$ across widely separated probe scales against Standard Model running, and search for a nonzero $\Delta \ln \alpha$ — clearly separating any departure from (a) temporal drift, (b) spatial/environmental variation, and (c) ordinary QED running. This is already being done. So far, no anomaly has been found.

## 7. Closing

I had an image in my head — a vehicle that changes your size, with no windows — and I could not get rid of it. It said something simple: *you cannot tell if $c$ changes with resolution because you change with it.* That turned out to be a known question — Nottale, Magueijo, Barrow, Duff, Uzan got there decades before me. The image was mine. The physics was theirs. I put it through a [math arena](/blog/strange-loops-arena) — 45 rounds, three personas, three external reviews — to find out where the image stopped and the physics started.

This note does not propose a new dynamical theory. Its contribution, if any, is operational: a measurement argument showing why a standalone variation of a dimensional constant is operationally empty unless it induces measurable residuals in dimensionless observables defined by a specific dynamical model. Current bounds leave little room for any such anomaly. But the question the vehicle asks is real, and the sentence to build on is this:

**If there is no nonzero residual in a dimensionless or otherwise relational observable, there is no physics here at the level of this parametrization.**

The vehicle has no windows. But it taught me to look for doors.
