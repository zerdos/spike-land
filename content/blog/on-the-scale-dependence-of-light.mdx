> **Abstract.** I present a thought experiment — the "vehicle with no windows" — to clarify why a putative resolution dependence of a dimensional constant such as $c$ is not, by itself, operationally observable. If measuring devices co-scale with the physics they probe, single-scale measurements cannot distinguish genuine variation from a change of units. The physically meaningful question is therefore not whether $c$ alone varies with operational resolution, but whether dimensionless observables — such as the fine-structure constant $\alpha$ — exhibit anomalous resolution dependence beyond standard renormalization-group running. Framed this way, the proposal reduces to a falsifiable search for residual departures in dimensionless couplings. I define an anomalous residual $\Delta \ln \alpha$ and survey current experimental bounds. Existing laboratory, astrophysical, and cosmological constraints are consistent with zero anomaly, so any such effect must be very small.
>
> **Keywords:** resolution dependence, fine-structure constant, self-referential measurement, varying constants, anomalous residual, dimensionless observables
>
> **Zoltan Erdos** — Brighton, UK, March 2026

## 1. The vehicle with no windows

Imagine a vehicle that can change your size — from the Planck length to the observable universe. It has no windows. You have only your instruments: a ruler, a clock, a laser, and a mirror.

You measure the speed of light. You get $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle shrinks you by a factor of $10^{35}$. Your ruler shrinks. Your clock shrinks. Your laser, your nervous system, your concept of "one meter" — all shrink with you. You measure again. $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s.

The vehicle grows you to cosmological size. You measure again. Same number.

You will always get this number. Not because the speed of light is constant at every scale — but because your measuring apparatus scales with the thing being measured. A ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

This is not a paradox. It is a constraint on what measurement can tell us: **a dimensional constant measured with instruments made of the same physics is self-referential.** To ask whether $c$ varies with operational resolution, we must look at *dimensionless* combinations of constants — ratios that do not depend on our choice of units.

## 2. Two symmetries, not one

In 1905, Einstein postulated that the speed of light is the same for all *inertial observers*. That is Lorentz invariance — a symmetry under changes of velocity. He did not postulate that $c$ is the same at all *operational resolutions*. These are different symmetry claims. Lorentz invariance is about motion. The distinct question here is whether changing operational resolution introduces any unit-independent effect beyond standard renormalization-group running. One claim has been tested to extraordinary precision. The other has been explored in a substantial literature on varying constants.

This question was formalized decades ago. Nottale proposed *scale relativity* in 1993, postulating that the laws of physics should be invariant under changes of resolution scale, just as they are under Lorentz boosts [1]. Magueijo and Albrecht developed varying-speed-of-light cosmologies in 1999, with a concrete dynamical model and a Lagrangian [2]. Barrow explored the broader question of varying constants in 2002 [3]. Duff, Okun, and Veneziano argued in their 2002 *trialogue* that only variations of dimensionless constants are operationally meaningful — varying a dimensional constant like $c$ alone is ambiguous without specifying which dimensionless observable changes [4]. Uzan published comprehensive reviews of varying fundamental couplings [5]. I am not asking an unasked question. I am restating a known question with what I hope is a better metaphor.

In quantum field theory, the electromagnetic coupling $\alpha$ runs with the energy at which you probe it — well-tested QED. The question is different from ordinary running: does $\alpha$ show any *anomalous* resolution dependence beyond what the Standard Model predicts?

The natural dimensionless observable is the fine-structure constant. In Gaussian CGS units:

$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{\hbar c} \approx \frac{1}{137.036}$$

(In SI, the denominator includes $4\pi\varepsilon_0$: $\alpha = e^2 / (4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar c)$. The numerical value is the same.)

If $c$ varies with resolution while $e$ and $\hbar$ vary differently, $\alpha$ will change. That change is measurable. That is the way out of the vehicle.

## 3. A toy model — and its limits

The vehicle motivates a question. To make that question precise, we need to move from the dimensional constant $c$ — which cannot be tested in isolation — to a dimensionless observable that can.

Let $L$ be an operational resolution scale. I will not pretend I know exactly what $L$ is. It could be the probe wavelength, the apparatus size, the coarse-graining cutoff, or an RG scale in some future theory (in momentum-space language, roughly $\mu \sim 1/L$). These are not interchangeable, and until someone pins $L$ to a specific physical observable, the framework has a free variable on top of its free parameter. I am stating this upfront because hiding it would be dishonest.

One could write a toy ansatz like $c(L) = c_0 (L/L_{P,0})^\delta$, where $L_{P,0}$ is a fixed fiducial Planck length and $\delta \ll 1$. But this equation is a disposable heuristic — it is not doing the real work. Its only purpose is to motivate the move to a dimensionless quantity that *can* be tested.

That quantity is $\alpha$. The electromagnetic coupling already runs with energy in standard QED. The question is whether there is an *additional* departure — an anomalous residual beyond the Standard Model prediction. Define:

$$\Delta \ln \alpha(L) = \ln \alpha_{\text{obs}}(L) - \ln \alpha_{\text{SM}}(L)$$

This is the physically meaningful quantity. If $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$ at all resolutions, the framework is empty — any variation of $c$ is just a choice of units, exactly as Duff argues [4]. If $\Delta \ln \alpha \neq 0$ at some resolution, the framework has physical content.

In the simplest power-law parametrization:

$$\Delta \ln \alpha \approx \delta_\alpha \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{L_1}{L_2}\right)$$

where $\delta_\alpha$ is the anomalous scaling exponent — the part that is *not* accounted for by standard QED running.

This model has two free parameters ($\delta$ and the identification of $L$) and zero data points. It is a bet, not a result.

---

## 4. What the data say

Short answer: current data is consistent with $\Delta \ln \alpha = 0$. The bounds are tight. Any anomalous effect must be very small.

**Laboratory tests.** Atomic clock comparisons constrain the fractional drift of $\alpha$ at the $\sim 10^{-17}$ yr$^{-1}$ level. The older NIST Hg$^+$/Al$^+$ result was $(-1.6 \pm 2.3) \times 10^{-17}$ yr$^{-1}$, consistent with zero [6]. These measure *temporal* variation, not resolution dependence directly. The mapping from temporal drift to the kind of anomalous residual described here is an additional assumption.

**Astrophysical quasar spectra.** High-precision ESPRESSO measurements found $\Delta\alpha/\alpha = 1.3 \pm 1.3_{\text{stat}} \pm 0.4_{\text{sys}}$ ppm for the well-studied system HE 0515-4414, and a weighted mean from better-calibrated measurements of $-0.5 \pm 0.5_{\text{stat}} \pm 0.4_{\text{sys}}$ ppm — both consistent with zero [7]. Earlier claims of a spatial dipole at the $\sim 10^{-5}$ level (Webb et al.) have not held up in larger, more carefully calibrated samples.

**Cosmic microwave background.** Planck-era recombination constraints are also consistent with no anomaly. Hart and Chluba report $\alpha_{\text{EM}}/\alpha_{\text{EM},0} = 1.0005 \pm 0.0024$ from Planck 2018 CMB-only data, and $1.0019 \pm 0.0022$ when BAO is added [8]. These place percent-to-subpercent-level bounds on recombination-era departures.

Current bounds leave little room for any beyond-Standard-Model residual. The allowed space is very small — but it is not zero.

---

## 5. What would make this physics?

Three things are missing before this thought experiment becomes testable:

1. **A concrete definition of $L$.** Is it an RG scale? A geometric length? A detector bandwidth? Without pinning $L$ to a physical observable, $\delta$ and $L$ absorb each other and the model says nothing.

2. **A mechanism.** Why should $c$ depend on resolution? How should the other constants vary? Nottale offered a fractal spacetime geometry. Magueijo and Albrecht offered a dynamical scalar field. This essay offers neither — only the question and a toy parametrization.

3. **A testable anomalous residual.** Compare precision measurements of $\alpha$ across widely separated probe scales against Standard Model running, and search for a nonzero residual $\Delta \ln \alpha$ — distinguishing any departure from temporal drift. This is already being done. So far, no anomaly has been found.

## 6. Closing

I am a programmer, not a physicist. I had an image in my head — a vehicle that changes your size, with no windows — and I could not get rid of it. It said something simple: *you cannot tell if $c$ changes with resolution because you change with it.* That turned out to be a known question — Nottale, Magueijo, Barrow, Duff, Uzan got there decades before me. The image was mine. The physics was theirs. I put it through a [math arena](/blog/strange-loops-arena) — 45 rounds, three personas, three external reviews — to find out where the image stopped and the physics started.

This note does not propose a new dynamical theory. Its contribution is operational: a measurement argument showing why a standalone resolution dependence of a dimensional constant is not observable, and a falsifiable way to restate the question in terms of anomalous residuals of dimensionless observables. Current bounds leave little room for any such anomaly. But the question the vehicle asks is real.
