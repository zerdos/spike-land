## The Letter

On 27 March 2026, while I am detained in a mental health ward in Bexhill-on-Sea under the Mental Health Act, a letter arrives at my flat in Brighton. I am not there to receive it. My dogs are not there. Nobody is there.

The letter is from Russell-Cooke LLP, a London law firm at 8 Bedford Row, WC1R 4BX. They act for M&G RPF Nominee 1 Limited and M&G RPF Nominee 2 Limited — the trustees who own the legal title to 70, 42 Mighell Street, Brighton, on behalf of M&G RPF Limited Partnership.

They want me out.

## What They Allege

The letter and enclosed Section 8 notice claim:

1. **On 19 March 2026**, I caused damage to the communal elevator's access control panel, rendering it inoperable
2. **Police confiscated "three bags of illegal drugs"** from my flat on the same date
3. **I engaged in "violent disturbances"** — specifically, they allege I assaulted my friend and my sister
4. **I gave keys to an unknown member of the public**
5. I am therefore in breach of Clauses 19.4, 19.6, and 46.1 of my tenancy agreement

They seek possession under **Ground 12** (tenancy breach) and **Ground 14** (anti-social behaviour / nuisance) of Schedule 2, Housing Act 1988.

## What They Don't Say

The letter does not mention:

- That I was experiencing a **mental health crisis** on 19 March 2026
- That the police were called **instead of an ambulance** when I needed help
- That the "violent disturbances" were the **crisis itself**, not criminal acts
- That I was subsequently **sectioned** under the Mental Health Act
- That I gave my keys to someone because I was **being taken away** and needed someone to look after my dogs, Pixel and Mad Max
- That no **criminal charges** have been mentioned anywhere in this letter
- Whether those "three bags" constituted personal use or anything else

The letter constructs a narrative of a violent, antisocial tenant. The reality is a person in mental health crisis whose interaction with the system — police instead of ambulance, arrest instead of care — made everything worse.

## The Law: What Grounds 12 and 14 Actually Mean

Here is the thing the letter hopes you won't notice:

**Both grounds are discretionary.**

In English housing law, the grounds for possession fall into two categories:

- **Grounds 1-8: Mandatory.** If the court finds the ground is made out, it *must* order possession. The judge has no choice.
- **Grounds 9-17: Discretionary.** The court *may* order possession, but only if it considers it **reasonable** to do so.

Ground 12 and Ground 14 are both in the 9-17 range. The court must consider all the circumstances of the case. Including:

- The tenant's mental health
- Whether the behaviour was connected to a mental health crisis
- Whether the tenant has taken steps to address the issues
- Whether a lesser order (suspended possession) would be appropriate
- The proportionality of eviction (Article 8 ECHR — right to respect for the home)

The Equality Act 2010 is also in play. Mental health conditions are a protected characteristic. A landlord who seeks to evict a tenant for behaviour arising from a mental health crisis, without considering reasonable adjustments, may face a discrimination defence.

## The ADR Question

The letter includes this sentence:

> "We confirm that, at this stage, our clients do not consider that any form of Alternative Dispute Resolution process is currently relevant."

This is noted. Courts expect landlords to have made reasonable efforts to resolve disputes before issuing proceedings. The Pre-Action Protocol for Possession Claims based on rent arrears (and by analogy, other possession claims) encourages engagement. Russell-Cooke's explicit refusal to consider ADR may not play well with a judge who asks: "What did you try before sending solicitors after a man in a mental health ward?"

## What Happens Next

The Section 8 notice is **not an eviction**. It is a notice of intention to seek a court order. The landlord must still:

1. Apply to the county court for a possession order
2. Serve the court papers on me
3. A hearing will be listed (typically 4-8 weeks after application)
4. I will have the opportunity to file a defence and attend the hearing
5. The judge will hear both sides and decide whether it is reasonable to make an order

Even if a possession order is made, it can be:
- **Suspended** — I stay, subject to conditions (e.g., no further incidents)
- **Postponed** — delayed to allow time to find alternative accommodation
- **Dismissed** — if the court finds it unreasonable
