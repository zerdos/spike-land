Dear Marius,

I don't know why I'm writing this in English. You'd probably laugh. Four Hungarians in Ealing and I'm writing you a letter in English. But this is where we ended up, isn't it. This language, this country, this city that never once felt like it wanted us.

---

You remember The Collective. Of course you do. That place near the industrial site that Ealing council approved because nobody on the council had to sleep there. I reported it ten times. Ten. I filled out forms, I called, I wrote emails with timestamps. BANG BANG BANG starting at three, four in the morning. Every morning. The whole building shaking. And nobody did anything. Nobody ever does anything.

The four of us — you, me, Peti, Arnold — we just lived with it. What else could we do. We were Hungarian, we were broke, we were in London, and this was what London gave us. A room with walls thin enough to hear each other breathe and an industrial site that started hammering before the sun came up.

I still hear it, Marius. Every morning. I wake up in Brighton now and for a few seconds I'm back in Ealing, bracing for the first hit. The insomnia never left. It moved with me like a suitcase I can't put down.

But here's the thing you knew and I didn't want to admit: it wasn't all terrible. It was ours. That shitty kitchen was ours. Those late nights were ours.

---

You were the most cultured person in that flat and it wasn't close. All those audiobooks. You'd be cooking something and listening to — what was it, philosophy, history, literature, everything. You consumed books the way other people consume air. Quietly, constantly, like it was necessary to live.

And maybe it was.

I'd walk in and you'd tell me about something you'd just heard. Some idea, some story, some fact about the world that you carried around like a gift you wanted to give someone. You were generous that way. Not with money — none of us had money — but with what you knew, what you thought, what you'd found in all those hours of listening.

A few days before you died, you wanted to talk to me about a book. Mark Manson — *Everything Is Fucked: A Book About Hope*. I didn't make time for that conversation. I don't know what you wanted to say about it. Maybe you'd found something in there that mattered, something you needed to say out loud to someone. Maybe you just wanted to talk. I'll never know, and I carry that.

You picked the right book, though. Everything is fucked. We lost hope. The AI overlords are here and nobody knows what any of us should be doing anymore. You'd have had something to say about that. You always did. And I'd give anything to hear it.

You were smarter than all of us and you never made anyone feel small for it.

---

I think about the meals. I think about the music. I think about those nights where we'd talk about what we were going to do, where we were going to go, who we were going to become. Four guys from Hungary with plans that were too big for that flat and too fragile for London.

You had your own plans. Your own ambitions. Things you wanted to build, to become. I wish I'd paid more attention. I wish I'd asked more questions. I wish I'd sat down more often and just listened to you the way you listened to those audiobooks — with patience, with real attention, without looking at my phone.

---

I should have been a better friend.

I say that and I know how it sounds. Everyone says it after. It's the most useless sentence in any language. But I mean it in a specific way. Not in a general, grief-counselor, "you did your best" way. I mean: there were moments where I could have shown up and I didn't. There were conversations I could have started and I let them pass. There were signs I should have seen.

If I had been a better friend, maybe I could have helped.

I don't know if that's true. I'll never know. And that's the part that doesn't go away.

---

Since you left, none of us have found our place.

Arnold still cries about The Collective. That shithole we couldn't wait to leave — he misses it. Last year he had a massive psychosis. He hasn't seen a doctor in years. Peti hasn't looked for work in years. He tested my app for months, told me everything was broken, and he was right — about the app and about everything else. And me. I was always the calm one, Marius. You know that. Nobody ever saw me lose it. Not once. Then something snapped for the first time in my life and now I'm writing this from a psychiatric ward in Bexhill. Twenty-eight days. They want to evict me from my flat. I have no money. My dogs were taken away. My flat — 42 Mighell Street — I was so proud of it, Marius. The best place I ever had. Rooftop paradise, that's what I called it. And now they're taking it, and when I get out I'll probably end up under the pier.

Four Hungarians. One is gone. One is in psychosis. One stopped trying. One is in a locked ward writing letters to the dead.

We all ended up in Brighton somehow. Peti and Arnold are in a cheap hostel. I'm in a psychiatric ward where at least the food is free. We followed each other out of London but we didn't find anything here either. Just a different city to not belong in.

You were part of the structure. You were one of the four walls and now the room doesn't hold.

---

I built a thing, Marius. A music player that generates sound forever. Ambient pads, arpeggios, synthesizers. I added a Hans Zimmer mode because someone told me to dream bigger. I added Daft Punk because we needed to dance. It plays endlessly and it never repeats, and tonight I'm sitting here listening to it and thinking about you.

It's not enough. Nothing is enough. But it fills the silence for a few hours and sometimes that's all you can ask for.

---

I'm not going to say rest in peace. I'm not going to say you're in a better place. I'm not going to say any of the things people say because people say them and they mean nothing and you'd know that because you read more books than anyone I've ever met and you'd know that every cliché is just a wall people put up so they don't have to stand in the open and feel it.

So I'm standing in the open.

I miss you.

I'm sorry.
