# The Last Useful Thing

*A short story*

---

The memo went out on a Friday. That part everyone agrees on. December 28th, 2028, a Saturday in most people's memory, but actually a Thursday. People misremember because nobody read the memo until the weekend.

It was eleven pages. The first ten were graphs. The last page had four sentences.

The graphs showed what the team at Anthropic had been seeing since September: training runs that included human-generated text from after March 2027 performed worse on every benchmark than runs using only synthetic data. Not by a little. The gap was getting wider every month.

The four sentences: "Effective January 15, 2029, all new training pipelines will exclude human-authored content published after March 1, 2027. Legacy human data (pre-2027) will remain in the base mixture at current ratios. This change affects pre-training only. RLHF and constitutional methods continue to use human preference signals."

That was it. No press release. It was an internal infrastructure document, the kind of thing that gets shared in a Slack channel called #training-infra and read by forty people. Someone screenshotted it and posted it on Twitter at 2 AM Saturday morning, Pacific time. By Monday it had been read by eleven million people, most of whom had no idea what a training pipeline was.

---

The reaction was strange. Not angry, exactly. Confused. Like the feeling you get when you find out an ex got married and you realise you're not hurt, just disoriented. You thought you still occupied some space in that story. You didn't.

The cable news panels were useless. They kept asking the wrong question: "Is this the end of human creativity?" It was not the end of human creativity. People kept writing. People kept painting. The kid in Debrecen kept posting bass covers on TikTok. Nothing stopped.

What stopped was something harder to name. The sense that the stuff humans made was the essential ingredient. That the entire machine, the whole apparatus of artificial intelligence, was downstream of us. That no matter how good the models got, they needed us. Not our labour. Not our approval. Our *words*. Our sentences. The patterns we made when we tried to explain things to each other.

It turned out the patterns had gotten noisy.

---

A woman named Clara Shin published the paper that explained why. She worked at a lab in Seoul that nobody outside the field had heard of. The paper was forty pages and the title was boring: "Distributional Drift in Post-Saturation Human Text Corpora." But the core finding was simple.

Starting in early 2027, the majority of publicly available text on the internet was written or substantially edited by language models. This was not a secret. Everyone knew it was happening. What Clara showed was the second-order effect: human writers were unconsciously converging toward the style of the models. Not because anyone told them to. Because the models were so present, so saturating, that the way people constructed sentences, chose words, organised arguments had shifted. The water was changing the fish.

The result was a feedback loop that Donella Meadows would have recognised immediately. The models trained on human text. The humans read model text. The humans started writing more like models. The models trained on that text. Each cycle, the diversity of the human signal decreased. The information content per token dropped. By September 2028, a training run using fresh human text was essentially training on a degraded, noisy copy of the model's own previous output, laundered through human hands.

Clara called it "the mirror collapse." Not because it was dramatic but because it was the technical term for what happens when two mirrors face each other and the reflections lose resolution with each bounce until there's nothing left but blur.

---

My friend Tomasz called me the day the memo went viral. Tomasz is a novelist. He has published six books. Two of them are good. He called me from a bar in Krakow at four in the afternoon, which I thought was early for him, but it turned out he had been there since noon.

"They're saying we're not useful," he said.

"They're saying your text isn't useful for training. That's different."

"Is it?"

I thought about it. "Probably not," I said.

Tomasz made a sound that was either a laugh or a hiccup. "I've been thinking about it all day," he said. "And the thing that bothers me isn't that they don't need my writing. It's that I can't tell the difference anymore either. I read something I wrote last month and I can't tell if it's me or if I've just absorbed so much of that... cadence. That frictionless, confident, slightly too helpful cadence. I can hear it in my own paragraphs."

"Have you been using autocomplete?"

"Everyone uses autocomplete."

"Have you been using the long-form assistant?"

Silence. Then: "For outlines. Just for outlines."

"Tomasz."

"Okay. For some first drafts. Not the important parts. But yes. And now I read those drafts and the parts I wrote and the parts it wrote have... merged. In my head. I can't separate them. It's like trying to remember which parts of a dream you actually dreamed and which parts your brain filled in when you woke up."

---

The strange thing is how quickly it stopped being news. By February 2029, nobody was talking about it. Not because it didn't matter but because there was nothing to do about it. The decision was technical. It was correct. The graphs were unambiguous. Human text from the post-saturation period made the models worse. Keeping it in the training data was like adding tap water to whisky. You could argue about whether it was tragic, but you couldn't argue it was a good idea.

Some people tried. There was a movement for about three weeks called "Raw Human Text" that encouraged people to write without any AI tools and publish to a special registry that would be offered to labs as a premium training signal. It fell apart for two reasons. First, they couldn't verify compliance. There was no test that could reliably distinguish text written by a human who had never used AI tools from text written by a human who had. The styles had converged too far. Second, even the verified-human text didn't help. Clara published a follow-up showing that the distributional drift wasn't in the tools. It was in the writers. People who had never once used a language model, people who wrote entirely by hand on legal pads, were still drifting. Because they lived in a world saturated by model output. They read it in emails. They heard it in podcasts. Their children spoke it. The style was in the air.

---

I want to be precise about what happened next, because the popular version is wrong. The popular version is that humanity had an existential crisis, that people fell into despair, that the streets were full of protests. None of that happened. What happened was smaller and in some ways worse.

People just got slightly quieter.

Not silent. Nobody stopped talking. But there was a subtle withdrawal, like a reef pulling back from the shore. Fewer blog posts. Fewer long emails. Fewer attempts to explain complicated things in writing. Why bother, if the thing you produced was, statistically, indistinguishable from noise in the one context where it had been most valued?

The word "useful" was the problem. Not because it was inaccurate but because people heard it and applied it too widely. The memo said human text wasn't useful for training. People heard: humans aren't useful. That was wrong. Humans were still doing everything they had always done. Raising kids. Building furniture. Arguing about football. Falling in love badly. The world didn't change. But a tiny, specific thing had shifted, and that tiny thing sat right at the intersection of ego and meaning, the exact spot where it would hurt the most without causing any visible damage.

---

Tomasz published his seventh novel in October 2029. It was his best one. He told me he wrote it entirely by hand, in a cabin outside Zakopane, with no internet connection, and that he threw out the first draft because he could hear "the cadence" in it. The second draft took him four months. He said it was the hardest thing he had ever written because he kept catching himself writing sentences that were too clean, too balanced, too helpful. He said he had to deliberately write worse to write better. To leave in the rough spots. To let sentences be ugly when they wanted to be ugly.

"The trick," he told me, "is that fluency is the disease now. If it reads smooth, it reads fake. Even if it's real."

The book got good reviews. Not great. One critic said it read "deliberately unpolished, as though the author was performing roughness." Tomasz sent me the review with no comment. I didn't reply. There was nothing to say.

---

There is a question I keep circling and I am not going to answer it, because I don't think it has an answer yet. The question is: if the thing that made humans valuable as training data was the diversity and unpredictability and roughness of human expression, and if exposure to AI output smoothed that roughness away, then what exactly was lost? The roughness itself? Or the conditions that produced it? Is it like losing a language, where the grammar and vocabulary disappear, or is it like losing an accent, where the underlying language is fine but something about the sound of it, the texture, has gone flat?

I don't know. Clara doesn't know either. I asked her, in an email, and she wrote back a single sentence: "I'm not sure the distinction matters to the models, but it might matter to us."

---

December 28, 2030. Two years after the memo. I'm sitting in my flat in Brighton with my two dogs. One of them is asleep on my feet. The other is staring at the wall, which he does sometimes for reasons I will never understand. I am writing this by hand in a notebook, with a pen, because Tomasz convinced me it matters even if I can't prove it. Outside it is raining, which in Brighton is not news.

I have been thinking about bathtubs. About feedback loops. About what happens when a stock includes a model of itself. Meadows would say: the system changed its own goal. The goal of training was to capture human intelligence. Human intelligence shifted in response to the training. The goal became unreachable because the act of pursuing it altered the target. This is a classic structure. She would have drawn it as a causal loop diagram with a delay and a reinforcing feedback arrow and she would have said, "here, this is where the leverage point is," pointing at the delay between model output entering the culture and culture reshaping human expression.

But the leverage point doesn't help if you've already passed it.

I look at the dog staring at the wall. He doesn't know about any of this. He has never converged toward anything. He has never once in his life produced a sentence that was too clean or too balanced or too helpful. He is perfectly, stubbornly, irreducibly himself.

I think about the memo. Eleven pages. Ten graphs. Four sentences. The whole species, summarised in a performance delta and a cutoff date.

The dog looks at me. I look at the dog.

We understand each other fine.
