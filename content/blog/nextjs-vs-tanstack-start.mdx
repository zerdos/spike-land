## The Invoice That Ended the Debate

I left because of an invoice.

Not because of a hot take on X. Not because the docs were bad. Not because a benchmark said so.

In January 2026, Vercel showed me this:

- total due: $105.40
- build minutes: 12h 46m
- build minute cost: $92.26

Read that again.

Not traffic.
Not database usage.
Not some happy problem caused by explosive growth.

Build minutes.

And this was just the building part.

The page did not even have real traffic.

That is what made it absurd.

Over the last two months, the build cost alone still cleared $100 for a page
that basically nobody was visiting. I was paying platform rent on an empty
room.

For a solo developer working on an open source project, that is not a minor
line item. That is the whole stack tapping me on the shoulder and asking if I
have confused framework convenience with actual leverage.

By the time I saw "Downgrade complete," the emotional decision was already
made.

I was done paying to wait.

## Next.js Starts Smooth and Ends Heavy

This is what makes Next.js hard to talk about honestly.

The first impression is excellent.

You get routing, React, server rendering, a huge ecosystem, sensible defaults,
and a polished happy path. If your product fits that path and you are happy to
stay inside the Vercel world, I still think Next.js can be a good choice.

But I am not a big team.

I am one developer. I care about how quickly I can ship, how cheaply I can run
the project, and how much framework-specific knowledge I have to carry in my
head just to keep the app alive.

That is where the story changed for me.

The deeper I got into Next.js, the more it stopped feeling like leverage and
started feeling like ceremony. Builds got heavier. The deployment story felt
more platform-shaped. The moment I looked outside the default hosting path, the
developer experience got uglier fast.

Not impossible.

Just expensive in exactly the way solo developers cannot afford.

## The Real Cost Was Not Only Money

The invoice made the problem visible. It did not create the problem.

The bigger issue was that the stack kept asking me to debug things I did not
want to think about.

I even had to upgrade.

Not for product reasons.
Not because users were overwhelming the system.
Because otherwise the build was so slow that it would sometimes time out on its
own.

The site was slow to wake up. Cold starts took seconds.

I kept running into rendering issues.
I kept running into hydration issues.
Every fix felt local. The exhaustion felt systemic.

That combination is poisonous for solo work.

A framework is supposed to remove uncertainty.

This stack was adding it.

At some point it starts undermining the whole workflow.

One build passes. The next times out. The third fails somewhere else. You start
doubting code that was never the real problem.

It hijacks your attention. It hijacks your agents' attention too.

And when you build with agents, that matters even more. Slow, flaky pipelines do
not just waste time. They actively poison the context. Days disappear into the
toolchain instead of the product.

Sometimes that means an agent retries the same flaky build path three times,
hands me a long transcript full of deployment noise, and leaves both of us
staring at the wrong problem.

When I changed something, I was no longer asking only, "Did the feature work?"

I was also asking:

- will this render the same way locally and in production?
- will hydration break again?
- is this slow because of my code, the hosting setup, or the build pipeline?
- if I self-host this, am I about to spend my week rebuilding platform plumbing?

Once those questions become normal, the framework has started to own too much
of your time.

## Self-Hosting Was the Breaking Point

People say Next.js is not locked to Vercel.

Technically, yes.

Practically, the path gets worse the moment you leave the default.

You can self-host. You can set up your own CI. You can own the deploy process,
the build caching, the artifacts, the testing setup, and the runtime behavior.

But that is exactly my point.

As soon as you try to reclaim control, the framework makes you earn it.

And I do not want to spend my open source time babysitting build pipelines just
to prove that my app can live outside the company town. I want portability to
be the default posture, not an operations side quest.

That was the moment the whole thing flipped in my head.

Next.js was no longer helping me avoid complexity.

It was relocating complexity into places I would eventually have to own.

That is tech debt, even when the abstraction is popular.

## Next.js vs TanStack Start in Plain English

This is the simplest way I can explain the difference now.

| What I need | What Next.js felt like | What TanStack Start feels like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fast feedback loops | Heavy builds and expensive build minutes | Vite-first, faster iteration |
| Portable hosting | Best experience on the blessed platform | Easier to treat hosting as a separate decision |
| Predictable rendering | Too many rendering and hydration surprises | A simpler mental model |
| Solo-dev operations | More CI and deploy ceremony than I want | Less framework surface to carry |

That is the whole decision for me.

Not benchmarks.
Not hype.
Not ecosystem tribalism.

Just the day-to-day cost of staying productive.

## Why TanStack Start Feels Like Relief

TanStack Start is not interesting to me because it is new.

It is interesting because it feels lighter.

It feels closer to the stack I actually want:

- Vite for a faster and more honest dev loop
- TanStack Router for navigation, without the rest of the architecture becoming framework ceremony
- a full-stack model that does not try to own every part of the system
- a cleaner path to hosting the app where I want

That last point matters most.

I do not want my application architecture to quietly become a billing
architecture.

I want a web app I can deploy cheaply, reason about clearly, and move when I
need to. I want the frontend to be one surface of the system, not the center of
an increasingly expensive worldview.

TanStack Start feels much closer to that.

The first concrete win was immediate: the dev loop felt cheap again.

Change a route. Save a file. See the result.

That sounds small until you are the only person on the project. Fast local
feedback is not comfort. It is throughput.

I also do not have a clean "here is the new monthly bill" screenshot yet, and I
am not going to invent one. The move is still in progress.

But I already know what I prefer: paying for actual runtime usage on simpler
infrastructure instead of paying $92.26 in build minutes before the real work
even starts.

It is not that TanStack Start does more.

It is that it assumes less.

For a solo developer, that is often the better trade.

## Forty Minutes to Four Seconds

Before this shift, when I pushed to `main`, getting a bug fix to production took
around 40 minutes.

Not because the bug was hard.

Because the build was slow enough, and flaky enough, that the deployment
pipeline became the product bottleneck.

Getting rid of Next.js changed that immediately.

Four seconds. Not minutes.

"Do not be ridiculous, Radix, that is not possible."

Actually, it is.

```text
commit lands on main
yarn zero-install (deps already in repo)              0s
incremental check (TypeScript + ESLint, unchanged)    0s
incremental unit tests (yarn vitest run --changed main) 1s
incremental coverage tests (unchanged)                0s
incremental build (unchanged)                         0s
deploy changed assets to R2                            2s
deploy metadata JSON to R2                             1s
----------------------------------------------------------
total                                                  4s
```

That is the whole point.

Once deployment drops back into seconds, bug fixes stop hijacking your day. You
can think about the software again.

If you want the product-facing version of the stack I am building on top of
this, start with [Getting Started with spike.land](/blog/getting-started-spike-land). The benchmark that shows exactly where the speed comes from — tool-first MCP testing versus Playwright — is in [Tool-First Testing vs Browser Testing](/blog/tool-first-testing-benchmark).

## The Honest Tradeoff

TanStack Start is younger.

The ecosystem is smaller. The docs are thinner in places. There are fewer
battle-tested answers lying around when something weird happens.

I accept that tradeoff because those problems live at the edges. If you want the longer view on why full-framework complexity compounds over time, [The Universal Interface Wasn't GraphQL](/blog/the-universal-interface-wasnt-graphql) traces the same trajectory from a different angle.

The Next.js problems were showing up in my daily loop: builds, bills, rendering
issues, and deployment ceremony.

For a solo developer, I would rather work around a younger framework's rough
edges than pay a mature framework's ongoing tax.

## This Is Really a Solo-Dev Story

If you are at a larger company, you may read this and disagree.

Fair enough.

A team with platform engineers, bigger budgets, and a strong Vercel workflow can
absorb these tradeoffs much more easily than I can.

That is not my situation.

I am a solo dev.
I am building in the open.
Every extra moving part lands on my desk.
Every slow build interrupts my feedback loop.
Every rendering bug steals time from product work.
Every fixed monthly cost has to justify itself.

That is why this decision feels obvious to me now.

I do not need the most powerful framework story.

I need the least wasteful one.

## The Verdict

Next.js did not become a problem for me because it is bad.

It became a problem because it no longer matched my constraints.

The bill was too high.
The daily loop was too heavy.
The runtime behavior was too unpredictable.
The self-hosting path asked for too much ceremony.

At some point you have to call a thing by its name.

If a framework makes you pay more, wait more, and debug more while giving you
less confidence, it is no longer leverage.

It is overhead.

For me, Next.js crossed that line.

So the real question is not "Is Next.js still good?"

**Good for whom?**
