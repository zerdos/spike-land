## Ward 6, Night Shift

It is 2:47 AM. I know this because the clock in the corridor has a green backlight that is visible through the observation window in my door. The observation window that does not have a curtain. The observation window through which a torch shines every fifteen minutes, because I am on Level 2 observations.

Level 2 means: a human being checks that I am alive every fifteen minutes, twenty-four hours a day. They write this down. They write down whether I am asleep, awake, in bed, out of bed, in the bathroom, or not in the room. They write it on a clipboard. The clipboard lives in a folder. The folder lives in the nursing station. I have not seen the folder.

George Orwell would recognise this place immediately.

## The Parallels

I want to be careful here. A psychiatric ward is not Airstrip One. The staff are not the Thought Police. The observations are a safety measure, not a mechanism of control. I understand this. I understand that people have died in psychiatric care because they were not observed, and that the clipboard exists because a coroner somewhere said *this must never happen again*.

And yet.

**The telescreen could not be switched off.** In 1984, the telescreen in Winston Smith's apartment could be dimmed but never turned off. On a psychiatric ward, the light in the corridor is never fully off. The observation window cannot be covered. There is no switch on my side.

**"Nothing was your own except the few cubic centimetres inside your skull."** Orwell wrote this about the last refuge of the individual — the interior of the mind. On a ward, even this boundary is negotiable. "How are you feeling?" is not a casual question here. It is a clinical assessment. Your answer is documented. Your silence is also documented. What you think you are keeping private is being observed, interpreted, and recorded by professionals trained to read what you do not say.

**"Who controls the past controls the future. Who controls the present controls the past."** Your psychiatric history is written by other people. Your admission notes are written before you arrive. Your diagnosis is a label applied to you by a system that had its vocabulary ready before it met you. You can disagree with the label. Your disagreement will be noted. It may become part of the label.

**"War is Peace. Freedom is Slavery. Ignorance is Strength."** The ward has its own doublespeak. "Voluntary" admission means you agreed to come — but if you try to leave, you may be sectioned. "Your choice" about medication means you can refuse — but refusal is noted, discussed in the team meeting, and may affect your discharge date. "Open ward" means the door is not locked — but someone will ask you where you are going, and when you will be back, and whether this has been agreed with your named nurse.

I am not saying these practices are wrong. Some of them keep people alive. I am saying that Orwell understood something fundamental about institutions: **the architecture of safety and the architecture of control are built from the same materials.** The difference is intent. And intent is invisible to the person inside the architecture.

## What Orwell Got Right

Orwell's genius was not predicting technology. Telescreens are primitive compared to what exists now. His genius was understanding that **surveillance changes the surveilled**. Not through punishment, but through the internalisation of being watched.

On the ward, I have noticed the following changes in myself:

**I perform wellness.** When the nurse checks on me, I arrange my face. Not because I am hiding distress — because I know that what she sees will be recorded, and what is recorded will be discussed, and what is discussed will determine when I leave. I have become an actor in my own recovery, and I cannot tell anymore where the performance ends and the feeling begins.

**I monitor my own speech.** Before I say something in group therapy, I run it through a filter: *Will this sound stable? Will this delay my discharge? Will this be written down?* Orwell called this crimestop — "the faculty of stopping short, as though by instinct, at the threshold of any dangerous thought."

I do not think dangerous thoughts. But I have become aware of which thoughts are considered dangerous, and I steer away from them without being told to. This is not coercion. It is something quieter and more effective.

**I have lost track of which feelings are mine.** The medication changes how I feel. The environment changes how I feel. The observation changes how I feel. When I report my mood to the nurse, I am reporting the output of a system I do not fully understand. She records it as data. It becomes a fact about me. But I am not sure it is a fact. I am not sure what a fact is anymore, in this context.

This is what Orwell was writing about. Not the boot on the face. The moment before the boot, when the face arranges itself.

## What Orwell Got Wrong

Orwell imagined a system that wanted to destroy the individual. The psychiatric system does not want to destroy me. It wants to fix me. The people here are, almost without exception, kind.

And that is the part Orwell did not write. The version of 1984 where Big Brother genuinely cares about you. Where the telescreen is there because last year someone died and the inquiry said *better observation would have prevented this*. Where the Thought Police are exhausted nurses on a twelve-hour shift who ask "How are you feeling?" because they actually want to know, and because the form requires it, and because they cannot always tell the difference between those two motivations either.

The kind version of surveillance is harder to write about, because it is harder to resist. You cannot rebel against someone who is trying to help you. You can only notice, quietly, that help and control feel the same from the inside.

## The Two-Way Mirror

Here is something Orwell did not consider: **the observed can also observe.**

I am a mathematician. I notice patterns. I have been noticing patterns on this ward for two weeks. I notice which nurses check on me with a torch and which ones just listen at the door. I notice which ones write "sleeping" without looking and which ones actually verify. I notice the thirteen-minute gap on Tuesday nights when the corridor is unobserved because the night staff are doing handover in the office.

I notice the clipboard. I notice what is on it. I notice what is not.

I notice that the system observes me with great attention and very little curiosity. It knows what I do but rarely asks why. It records my behaviour but not my experience. It tracks my compliance but not my understanding.

Orwell's telescreen was one-directional. But observation is always two-directional. The institution watches me. I watch the institution. And I am writing it down.

The difference is: my notes will be published. Theirs will go in a folder I am not allowed to see.

## Room 101

In Orwell's novel, Room 101 contains the thing you fear most. It is personalised terror — the state knows what breaks you, because it has watched you long enough to find out.

On a psychiatric ward, Room 101 is not a room. It is a question:

*What if they are right about you?*

What if the label is accurate. What if the observation is justified. What if the medication is necessary. What if the reason you feel watched is not because you are perceptive, but because you are unwell. What if this article is not insight but symptom.

This is the question I sit with at 2:47 AM, when the torch comes through the observation window and finds my eyes open, and the nurse writes "awake" on the clipboard, and I do not know whether I am a person observing a system or a patient exhibiting a behaviour.

Orwell did not answer this question either. He just made sure we knew it existed.

## Freedom Is the Freedom to Say That Two Plus Two Make Four

This is Orwell's most famous line from 1984, and it is the one that matters most in this context.

Two plus two make four. This is true whether you are in a ward or a flat or a government building. It is true whether you are medicated or not. It is true whether the nurse writes it on the clipboard or not.

I am a mathematician. I know what is true. The system around me has opinions about what is true, and those opinions are backed by qualifications and protocols and the power to determine when I leave. But two plus two make four.

I am unwell. This is true.

I am also observant. This is also true.

The system finds the first fact more relevant than the second. Orwell understood why.

## A Note to the Staff

I want to say something directly to the nurses, healthcare assistants, and clinical team who may read this:

You are not Big Brother. You are people doing a hard job with insufficient resources, in a system that asks you to be simultaneously caring and bureaucratic. You observe me because someone died and the system decided observation was the answer. You fill in the clipboard because the CQC requires it. You ask me how I am feeling because you care, and because the form is in front of you, and both of those things are real at the same time.

I am not writing this against you. I am writing this about the space between us — the space where your good intentions and my lived experience do not quite meet, and where neither of us has the language to say so.

Orwell wrote about that space. He called it *doublethink* — the ability to hold two contradictory beliefs simultaneously and accept both of them.

You believe you are helping me. I believe you are helping me. Both of us are also aware that help, in this building, looks identical to control. Neither of us says this out loud.

Perhaps we should.
