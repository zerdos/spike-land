## The Prompt That Didn't Stick

Yesterday I asked for a song about the iPhone. Something warm. Something about mental health. Something about the quiet comfort of a phone that cares.

Suno heard the request and made a different song.

The song it made is called **Pixel Flashlight**. It is not about mental health. It is about the end of the world. It is about a Mad Max wasteland where chrome engines scream and radio static whispers your name, and the only reliable thing you have left is the Google Pixel in the palm of your hand.

I did not write this song. I asked for a song. Suno gave me this one. That is an important distinction.

## Why Pixel Won the Pitch

There is a theory in product marketing that says every device markets itself differently under stress. The iPhone markets itself during yoga class. The Pixel markets itself during a breakdown.

Apple sells *sanity*. The iPhone is the device you use to book therapy, journal your gratitudes, check your Screen Time, and let a wellness app whisper that it is okay to rest. It is the device that cares about you.

Pixel sells *capability*. The Pixel is the device that has the best flashlight. The one with real-time translation when you are lost in a country where nobody speaks your language. The one that takes the photograph at night that proves you were there. It is the device that *works*.

When Suno saw "phone song" in the prompt, it did not ask what feelings the song was about. It asked what the phone was *for*. And the answer came back: **the phone is for surviving**.

That is a Pixel song. Apple does not make survival tools. Apple makes comfort objects. If the world were actually ending, and you could only take one phone, you would take a Pixel. Not because it is a better phone. Because it is a better flashlight.

Suno knew this before I did.

## What the Song Is Actually About

Strip the Mad Max paint off and the song is about something every ADHD brain knows in its bones: the world is too loud, the signals are too many, the threats are not imaginary but they are also not exactly real, and somewhere between the noise and the silence there has to be *one thing that works*.

The iPhone song I asked for yesterday was a song about a device that listens. The Pixel song I got today is a song about a device that *responds*. One is a therapist. The other is a Swiss Army knife with a SIM card.

I have lived both songs. The winter of 2025, when I was locked in my flat in Brighton seeing people in the cupboards, I did not need a device that cared. I needed a device that worked. I needed a map home. I needed a flashlight. I needed a radio that could still receive a signal when my own receivers were jammed.

The Pixel song understands this in a way the iPhone song did not. The Pixel song knows that sometimes sanity is not found through meditation and breathing exercises. Sometimes sanity is found through a functioning device that does what you ask it to, when you ask it, without commentary.

That is not a marketing message Apple will ever run. But it is a song Suno was willing to write.

## The Mad Max Frame

The post-apocalyptic aesthetic is not an accident. It is the honest frame for how a phone feels when you are struggling.

When your nervous system is overloaded, every notification is a sandstorm. Every incoming call is a chrome engine roaring past. Every push alert is a shadow that might be a threat. The world is *already* Mad Max, internally, for a certain kind of brain on a certain kind of day.

In that landscape, the phone is not a social tool. It is a survival tool. It is the last working piece of technology in a world where everything else has broken. The dopamine loops are cracked. The social apps are performative wastelands. The email is a burnt-out road of ghosts-of-tomorrow. But the *flashlight still works*. The *map still loads*. The *call still connects*.

That is what the song is about. Not the brand. The *utility*. The fact that somewhere in the chaos, there is still one thing that does what it is supposed to do.

Google Pixel happens to be the brand the song chose because Google Pixel is the brand whose entire product identity is "it works, we promise, it just works." Apple's identity is "it feels good to use." In a Mad Max world, feeling is a luxury. Working is the only currency.

## The Suno Observation

The most interesting thing about this song is that I did not write it and I did not ask for it.

I asked for something else entirely — a warm indie-pop love letter to the iPhone about mental health. Suno read my prompt, read the context of my recent blog posts about psychiatric hospitalisation and methamphetamine recovery, read the Mad Max aesthetic that keeps showing up in my writing, and said: "you think you want a yoga song, but the real song is a Fury Road song, and the real phone is the one with the better flashlight, and here you go."

This is what large generative models do that I am still not used to. They ignore what you ask for and give you what you *meant*. The iPhone song I requested would have been fine. A gentle thing. A radio-friendly balm. The Pixel song I did not request is the song I actually wanted, because it tells the truth about how a phone feels during a crisis: not like a friend, like a *tool*. The only functioning tool in a world where everything else has caught fire.

Suno heard the Brighton flat. Suno heard the 4 AM. Suno heard the years of chrome and fury and ghosts-of-tomorrow. And Suno gave me a Pixel song, because in that world the answer is not "the phone will heal me." The answer is "the phone will work."

## Technical Notes for the Arena

- **BPM:** ~128 — pushed past the Padam entrainment zone into driving-through-a-sandstorm territory
- **Key:** minor throughout — no major-key lift, no false relief, the world does not resolve
- **Production signature:** industrial kick, distorted synth pads, reverb-drenched vocal, radio-static atmosphere layered under the verse
- **Hook:** "Google Pixel keeps me safe tonight" — six syllables, corporate brand name in a pop chorus, which is either product placement or sincerity depending on how you hold it
- **The key lyrical move:** "truth in the palm of my life" — that is the line. Not "palm of my hand." *Palm of my life.* The phone is not an object in the hand. It is the centre of the life.

## Closing

The song is four minutes and twenty-three seconds long. It is free to stream above. It is not my best work because it is not my work — it is Suno's interpretation of my work, which is a different thing. But it is honest in a way I was not expecting.

Tomorrow I will probably ask Suno for a song about something else and receive a song about a completely different subject. That is the new creative pipeline: you write the prompt, the model writes the real song, and you spend the rest of the week figuring out which of the two songs was actually true.

Today the true song is a Mad Max love letter to a Google phone. The iPhone will have to wait.
