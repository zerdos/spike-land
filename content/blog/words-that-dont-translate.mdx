## The Sapir-Whorf Shortcut

There's a long academic debate about whether language shapes thought. I'm not here to settle it.

I'm here to hand you 30 words that will change how you think about problems, truth, beauty, and broken software — whichever side of that debate you sit on.

I'm Hungarian. I write code in English, think in Hungarian, and dream in both. When I switched to English full-time, I didn't just lose vocabulary. I lost *concepts*. Ideas that had single words in Hungarian became clumsy three-word approximations in English. The approximations leak meaning like a sieve.

Here are the words I miss most.

---

## Problems & Solutions

### Feladat — *the thing given up to you*

English: "problem" or "task."

Hungarian: **feladat** (fell-ah-dot). Literally: *the thing given up to you*. A gift.

When Paul Erdős said "problems are gifts," he wasn't being poetic. He was being etymologically precise. In Hungarian, a problem IS a gift by the structure of the word itself. *Fel* = up, *adat* = given. Something handed to you from above. An offering.

English treats problems as obstacles. Hungarian treats them as presents. This one difference produced more mathematicians per capita than any other country in the 20th century.

### Megoldas — *untying*

English: "solution."

Hungarian: **megoldas** (meg-ol-dahsh). Literally: *un-tying*.

You don't "solve" a problem in Hungarian. You untie it. The metaphor matters: a knot isn't destroyed when you untie it. The rope is still there. The structure is preserved. You just found the way through.

Einstein didn't solve spacetime. He untied it.

### Bizonyitas — *making certain*

English: "proof."

Hungarian: **bizonyitas** (bee-zo-nyee-tahsh). Literally: *the act of making certain*.

Not "showing." Not "demonstrating." *Making* certain. The proof doesn't reveal a pre-existing truth. It *creates* certainty. There's a world of difference between "I showed this is true" and "I made this certain." The Hungarian version puts the mathematician in the driver's seat.

## Truth & Justice

### Igazsag — *truth, and also justice*

English separates truth and justice into different words, different departments, different buildings.

Hungarian: **igazsag** (ee-gaz-shahg). One word. From *igaz* = true, just.

Truth and justice share a root. You cannot have one without the other. A court delivers igazsag. A mathematician proves igazsag. A journalist reports igazsag. Same word. Same idea. Same root.

And its negation:

### Igazsagtalan — *truth-less*

English: "unjust."

Hungarian: **igazsagtalan**. Literally: *without truth*.

When a monopoly charges unfair prices, it isn't just "unjust" — it is *truth-less*. It operates on a lie. The Hungarian word makes this explicit: injustice is a species of falsehood.

## Freedom & Trust

### Szabadsag — *the state of being free*

English: "freedom."

Hungarian: **szabadsag** (sa-bad-shahg). Not a right that someone grants you. A state of being. The dogs have this. Most humans don't.

In English, freedom is a political concept — something governments grant or withhold. In Hungarian, it describes reality. You either are szabad, or you aren't. Nobody gives it to you.

### Megbizhatosag — *trust-ability*

English: "reliability."

Hungarian: **megbizhatosag** (meg-beez-ha-toh-shahg). Literally: *the ability to deserve trust*.

English reliability is about uptime. 99.99% availability. SLA metrics. Hungarian reliability is about whether the system *deserves your trust*. A system can have 100% uptime and still not be megbizhato — if it's logging your data without telling you, it's untrustworthy regardless of uptime.

## Beauty & Feeling

### Szep — *beautiful (but for everything)*

English: "beautiful" — reserved mostly for faces and landscapes.

Hungarian: **szep** (sayp). Used for proofs, code, ideas, mornings, dogs, gestures. *Ez szep* — "this is beautiful" — is the highest compliment a Hungarian mathematician can give your proof. And they mean it the same way you'd mean it about a sunset.

### Gyonyoru — *gorgeous (five syllables)*

English: "gorgeous" — three syllables.

Hungarian: **gyonyoru** (dyeu-nyeu-rew) — five syllables.

Hungarian saves its longest words for the most important things. When something is truly stunning, two syllables won't do. The word itself takes time to say — and that's the point. You have to slow down to say "gyonyoru." The deceleration is built into the appreciation.

### Erzes — *doing feeling*

English: "feeling" — a noun. Something you *have*.

Hungarian: **erzes** (air-zaysh). From *erez* = to feel (an active verb).

You don't "have" a feeling in Hungarian. You *do* feeling. Feeling is an action, not a possession. This changes everything about how you talk about emotions: they're not things that happen to you. They're things you do.

## Work & Systems

### Mukodik — *it works*

English: "It works." Two words.

Hungarian: **mukodik** (mew-keu-dik). One word.

The most important sentence in software engineering, compressed into three syllables. *"Mukodik?"* — Does it work? If the answer isn't yes, nothing else matters. Not the architecture. Not the test coverage. Not the documentation. *Mukodik?*

### Elromlott — *it broke*

English: "It broke" or "it seems to be experiencing an issue" or "we've identified a degradation in service quality."

Hungarian: **elromlott** (el-rom-lot). It broke. Past tense. Done. No euphemism. No hedging. Fix it.

### Hiba — *bug*

English: "bug," "issue," "concern," "finding," "incident," "defect."

Hungarian: **hiba** (hee-ba). Two syllables. A fault. Clean. Direct. There is no committee that decides whether to call it an "issue" or a "concern." It's a hiba.

### Rendszer — *order itself*

English: "system" — a collection of parts that work together.

Hungarian: **rendszer** (rend-ser). From *rend* = order. A system is not a collection of parts. It is *order itself*. When Hungarians say "rendszer," they mean "the order that makes sense of the parts." A system without rend is not a rendszer. It's just parts.

## Connection & Recovery

### Osszefugges — *hanging-together*

English: "connection" or "coherence."

Hungarian: **osszefugges** (eus-se-few-gesh). Literally: *hanging-together*.

Things that are osszefuggo don't just relate to each other. They hang together like a structure that would collapse if you removed any part. A mathematical proof is osszefuggo. A family is osszefuggo. A codebase should be osszefuggo.

### Visszaallitas — *standing back up*

English: "rollback" or "restore."

Hungarian: **visszaallitas** (vees-sa-ahl-lee-tahsh). Literally: *standing-back-again*.

Not undoing. Not reverting. *Standing back up*. The system fell. Now it stands again. Resilience is baked into the word. The English "rollback" implies retreat. The Hungarian version implies getting back on your feet.

## Thinking & Change

### Gondolkodas — *thought-caring*

English: "thinking."

Hungarian: **gondolkodas** (gon-dol-ko-dahsh). Literally: *taking care of thoughts*.

To think in Hungarian is to take care of thoughts. A thought experiment isn't just imagination — it's careful stewardship of an idea. You don't just think. You tend to your thoughts like a garden.

### Valtozas — *exchange*

English: "change."

Hungarian: **valtozas** (vahl-to-zahsh). Contains *valt* = to switch/exchange.

Change is not destruction. It is an exchange. The old is traded for the new. Both existed. The transaction is what matters. Einstein said "the measure of intelligence is the ability to change." In Hungarian, that reads: "the measure of intelligence is the ability to *exchange* — to trade what you knew for what you now know."

## Love & Collaboration

### Szeretet — *the other love*

English has one word for love. Greek has four. Hungarian has two that matter:

**Szerelem** (se-re-lem) — romantic love. The one songs are about.

**Szeretet** (se-re-tet) — the love between collaborators, between a person and their dogs, between a mathematician and a problem. Non-romantic. Non-possessive. The love that makes you show up at someone's door with a suitcase and say "my brain is open."

Erdős had 511 co-authors. That was szeretet, not szerelem. The English word "love" cannot distinguish between these. Hungarian insists on it.

## The Meta-Lesson

### Agglutination — *stacking meaning*

Hungarian is agglutinative. You build meaning by stacking suffixes onto roots:

- **haz** = house
- **hazak** = houses
- **hazakban** = in houses
- **hazakbaniak** = those who are in houses

One word. Four layers of meaning. No separate words needed.

English spreads meaning across space: "those who are in the houses." Hungarian compresses it vertically: *hazakbaniak*.

This isn't just grammar. It's a way of thinking. You build complex ideas from simple roots by stacking. A feladat is given up to you. A megoldas unties it. A bizonyitas makes it certain. The meaning accumulates. The structure holds.

This is how mathematics works: axioms stacked into theorems.
This is how good code works: simple functions composed into systems.
This is how the mind works: simple observations compressed into understanding.

## Minden osszefugg.

*Everything hangs together.*

That's Hungarian for: the words are not separate. The concepts are not separate. The problems and solutions and truths and beauties are not separate. They are osszefuggo — hanging together — and if you remove one, the structure collapses.

Learn a language. Get new thoughts. The words are waiting.
