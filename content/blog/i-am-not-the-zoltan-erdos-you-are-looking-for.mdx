## The Moment of Discovery

Someone sent me a link this morning. *"Hey, is this you?"*

I clicked. A man with glasses. A microbiology lab. One hundred and eighty publications. Thirty-four thousand reads. A staff page at the University of Exeter.

A truly impressive profile, but not mine.

I smiled, then curiosity led me to search further.

## Celebrating the Other Zoltán Erdős

The internet proudly showcases at least three of us with distinguished publication records:

| Name | Where | What they do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Dr. Zoltán Erdős** (Exeter) | Centre for Ecology and Conservation | Biological control, microbiology. Demonstrating significant impact. |
| **Dr. Zoltán Erdős** (GFZ Potsdam) | Geodynamic Modelling | Plate tectonics, lithosphere dynamics. Holding a PhD from Bergen / Grenoble. |
| **Zoltán Erdős** (PTE Pécs) | Cultural Studies | History, nationalism studies. A respected Hungarian faculty member. |

Three doctors. Three institutions. Three Google Scholar profiles. Three robust citation networks, meticulously built over years of dedicated work.

Then there is me. A solo developer with a domain name, forging my own path.

> Three accomplished individuals share my name. One of them, through a relative, even inspired a renowned coordinate system.

That coordinate system is the [Erdős number](https://plus.maths.org/content/erdos-number) — a brilliant measure of collaborative distance in the world of mathematics. The original Zoltán's cousin, Paul Erdős, left an incredible legacy, and his surname now signifies a unit of measurement, showcasing the profound impact a name can carry. This rich heritage sets an inspiring backdrop for all of us.

## Embracing My Unique Position in the Digital Landscape

Here's an intriguing aspect of the search landscape.

The ecologist benefits from strong institutional backing. The geoscientist has the clarity of DOIs. The historian enjoys a faculty directory entry in multiple languages. Each of them is championed by a university, confirming their identity and contributions.

I have a blog. An open-source project. A subdomain. A GitHub profile that says "zerdos." My journey involves building my own verification signals, shaping my unique narrative without an institutional umbrella.

In traditional SEO terms, I recognize the strong digital presence of these esteemed institutions. The .edu domains excel. The DOI-linked papers shine. The co-authorship networks thrive. I might appear as the fourth result on a good day, or further down on another.

And I find this dynamic fascinating. It's an opportunity to innovate.

## Why Crafting a Unique Identity Matters Now

For most of the web's history, "ranking" was primarily a human challenge. A recruiter would type my name, scroll, and thoughtfully choose which result to click. Humans are wonderfully adept at disambiguation — the recruiter knows they are looking for a developer, not a geodynamicist, and filters accordingly.

However, AI agents approach this differently. They don't always filter with human intuition.

When an LLM is asked "who is Zoltán Erdős," it doesn't necessarily perceive four distinct individuals with overlapping names. It sees a retrieval puzzle. It pulls the information chunks that rank highest. It then synthesizes them into a plausible answer. If the top chunks detail subduction zones and biological pest control, that's who "Zoltán Erdős" might become within the agent's context window.

I've experienced this firsthand. When [ChatGPT brilliantly identified me from my domain name](/blog/chatgpt-figured-out-who-i-am), it was working from a precise, strong signal — `spike.land`. The moment you broaden the signal to just the name, the clarity can dilute. The biographies of four individuals can, in an AI's initial pass, blend into one.

> In the retrieval era, your identity isn't just about how you describe yourself. It's powerfully shaped by what the retriever highlights.

That's the exciting shift. Identity used to be anchored in self-description. Now, it's also dynamically defined by what surfaces when information is sought. This makes [personal branding more critical than ever](https://hbr.org/2020/05/how-to-build-your-personal-brand).

## My Proactive Steps Forward

I see three constructive moves, and I'm enthusiastically embracing all three.

**One: enrich my own retrieval surface.** Every post on this site is a valuable signal. Every slug, every tag, every linked cluster of posts helps to strengthen the retrieval graph around the correct person. [The spike.land app store](/blog/spike-land-app-store), [the MCP registry](/blog/getting-started-spike-land), [the migration service](/blog/nextjs-migration-service) — all of it forms essential identity infrastructure, whether explicitly named or not.

**Two: explicitly link my public work back to me.** This isn't about mere branding; it's about clear disambiguation. If an agent is pulling chunks about spike.land, it should encounter direct, unambiguous text stating, "This is the work of Zoltán Erdős, a solo developer in Brighton, distinct from the esteemed geoscientist in Potsdam." Thoughtful redundancy becomes a significant advantage when retrieval requires precision.

**Three: shift focus from competing for the name to excelling at the question.** Nobody is searching "Zoltán Erdős" specifically to find me. They are searching "the solo dev who wrote about Next.js being tech debt" or "the guy with the 4-second deploy." I proudly rank for those. I own those unique contributions. While the name presents a fascinating challenge on a crowded board, the specific story is a winning one on a unique board. This approach aligns perfectly with the evolving landscape of [AI in search, which redefines SEO strategies](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/how-ai-is-changing-seo/484964/).

## The Honest and Positive Reframe

This post isn't a complaint; it's a celebration of unique identity and strategic digital presence.

Having three accomplished namesakes is a wonderful opportunity. It provides rich context and highlights the diverse tapestry of human endeavor. If someone lands on the geoscientist's paper by accident while looking for me, they've just discovered something fascinating about subduction zones. That's not a loss; that's a world where more brilliant work exists, and my goal is to ensure my contributions stand proudly alongside theirs, without any pretense of being them.

The internet will always feature three other remarkable individuals with my name.

My fulfilling challenge is to be the Zoltán Erdős the agent remembers when somebody asks for the specific, innovative things only I do.

*That is not just SEO. That is the exciting work of building a unique legacy.*
