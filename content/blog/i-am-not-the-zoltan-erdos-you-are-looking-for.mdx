*Search for me. You will not find me. You will find three other men wearing my name.*

<Tldr>

Someone sent me a ResearchGate link thinking it was me. It was not. There are at least three published researchers named Zoltán Erdős — a geodynamics postdoc in Potsdam, an ecologist at Exeter with 34,057 reads, a cultural historian in Pécs — and all of them rank above me for my own name. This is a post about what that means now that AI agents do the ranking on your behalf.

</Tldr>

## The Moment

Someone sent me a link this morning. *"Hey, is this you?"*

I clicked. A man with glasses. A microbiology lab. One hundred and eighty publications. Thirty-four thousand reads. A staff page at the University of Exeter.

Not me.

I laughed. Then I searched.

## Meet the Other Zoltán Erdős

The internet has at least three of us with publication records:

| Name | Where | What they do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Dr. Zoltán Erdős** (Exeter) | Centre for Ecology and Conservation | Biological control, microbiology. Big numbers. |
| **Dr. Zoltán Erdős** (GFZ Potsdam) | Geodynamic Modelling | Plate tectonics, lithosphere dynamics. PhD Bergen / Grenoble. |
| **Zoltán Erdős** (PTE Pécs) | Cultural Studies | History, nationalism studies. Hungarian faculty post. |

Three doctors. Three institutions. Three Google Scholar profiles. Three real citation networks that took years to build.

Then there is me. A solo developer with a domain name.

> Three men share my name. Exactly one of them made it a coordinate system.

That coordinate system is the [Erdős number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erd%C5%91s_number) — the distance between you and Paul Erdős in the math-collaboration graph. The original Zoltán's cousin, essentially. He is not in the ranking either, but his surname outlived him into a unit of measurement. That is the weight the name carries before any of us show up.

## Ranking Last for My Own Name

Here is the uncomfortable part of the search result page.

The ecologist has institutional inertia. The geoscientist has DOIs. The historian has a faculty directory entry written in two languages. Every one of them has a university pointing back at them with "this person is ours."

I have a blog. An open source project. A subdomain. A GitHub profile that says "zerdos." No institution behind me saying *"confirmed, this is the one."*

In old-school SEO terms, I am outgunned. The .edu domains win. The DOI-linked papers win. The co-authorship networks win. I am the fourth result on a good day, the tenth on a bad one.

And I used to think that was fine. It is not fine anymore.

## Why It Matters Now

For most of the web's life, "ranking" was a human problem. A recruiter typed my name, scrolled, and decided which result to click. Humans are good at disambiguation — the recruiter knows they are hiring a developer, not a geodynamicist, and filters accordingly.

Agents do not filter that way. At least not reliably.

When an LLM is asked "who is Zoltán Erdős," it does not see four distinct humans with overlapping names. It sees a retrieval problem. It pulls the chunks that rank highest. It stitches them into a plausible answer. If the top chunks are about subduction zones and biological pest control, that is who "Zoltán Erdős" becomes inside the agent's context window.

I know this directly. When [ChatGPT figured out who I am from my domain name](/blog/chatgpt-figured-out-who-i-am), it was working from a narrow, specific signal — `spike.land`. The moment you widen the signal to just the name, the signal dilutes. Four people's biographies bleed into one.

> In the retrieval era, you are not who you say you are. You are who the retriever pulls up.

That is the shift. Identity used to live in how you described yourself. Now it lives in what ranks when someone asks.

## What I Can Actually Do About It

I see three realistic moves, and I am taking all three.

**One: make my own retrieval surface rich.** Every post on this site is a signal. Every slug, every tag, every linked cluster of posts tightens the retrieval graph around the right person. [The spike.land app store](/blog/spike-land-app-store), [the MCP registry](/blog/getting-started-spike-land), [the migration service](/blog/nextjs-migration-service) — all of it is identity infrastructure whether I call it that or not.

**Two: link the public things back to me explicitly.** Not as branding theatre. As disambiguation. If an agent is pulling chunks about spike.land, it should have direct, unambiguous text saying "this is the work of Zoltán Erdős, a solo developer in Brighton, not the geoscientist in Potsdam." Redundancy is a feature when retrieval is imprecise.

**Three: stop competing for the name and start competing for the question.** Nobody is searching "Zoltán Erdős" to find me. They are searching "the solo dev who wrote about Next.js being tech debt" or "the guy with the 4-second deploy." I rank for those. I own those. The name is a losing battle on a crowded board; the specific story is a winning one on an empty board.

## The Honest Reframe

There is a version of this post that is a complaint. It is not this version.

Having three accomplished namesakes is not a problem. It is free context. If someone lands on the geoscientist's paper by accident while looking for me, they have just read something interesting about subduction zones. That is not a loss. That is a universe where more good work exists, and my job is to make sure mine sits next to theirs without pretending to be theirs.

The internet will always have three other men with my name.

My job is to be the one the agent remembers when somebody asks for the specific thing only I did.

*That is not SEO. That is the work.*
