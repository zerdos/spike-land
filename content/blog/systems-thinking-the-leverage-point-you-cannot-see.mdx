## The Charger Drawer

You have a drawer. Everyone has this drawer. It is full of cables.

There is the wide flat one for the old tablet. The micro-USB that only works if you hold it at a slight angle. The barrel connector for the laptop from 2014 that you keep meaning to recycle. The proprietary magnetic thing that came with a device you no longer own. Each cable was designed by an engineer who believed — sincerely — that their connector was the right shape for the job. And each cable, considered individually, probably was.

The drawer is not the sum of those good individual decisions. The drawer is what happens when good individual decisions do not share a protocol.

Then USB-C arrived.

USB-C did not make chargers better. It did not increase wattage or improve electrical efficiency — not at first. What it did was something that sounds almost too boring to matter: it made the *shape* the same. One connector. Any device. Either direction.

Within five years, the drawer started to empty. Not because you threw the cables away, but because you stopped adding new ones. The European Union passed a law. Airlines stopped packing adapter kits. A child born in 2024 will find the concept of a "laptop charger" versus a "phone charger" as quaint as a rotary dial.

Here is the question that interests me: where, exactly, was the leverage?

It was not in the cable. It was not in the device. It was in the *interface specification* — the agreement about shape that made the cable and the device strangers who could cooperate without introduction.

Donella Meadows would have recognized this immediately.

## Meadows' List

In 1999, Donella Meadows published a short paper called "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System." It is twelve items long, ranked from least to most powerful. At the bottom: adjusting numbers, tweaking parameters, changing the flow rates of things that are already flowing. At the top: the power to change the paradigm from which the system arises. And at the very top, above even that: the power to transcend paradigms entirely.

The list is strange because it is counterintuitive. The interventions that *feel* powerful — building a bigger dam, adding more servers, hiring more engineers — sit at the bottom. The interventions that feel almost philosophical — changing the rules of the game, rewriting the goals — sit at the top.

Meadows used the metaphor of a bathtub. Water flows in through the faucet; water flows out through the drain. The water level is the stock. The faucet and drain are the flows. You can intervene at many points: turn the faucet, widen the drain, make the tub bigger. But the most powerful intervention is not about the water at all. It is about the *information* that connects the water level to the faucet — the feedback loop. A thermostat. A price signal. A test suite.

She wrote: "The higher leverage points are not intuitive. That is why they are so often missed."

I want to extend her argument. I think there is a class of leverage points she did not name — not because she missed them, but because the substrate had not yet appeared. These leverage points live in the interfaces between systems. Not the systems themselves. The *protocols*.

## The N Times M Problem

Here is a simple thing. Watch what happens when it scales.

You have three AI tools and two AI models that want to use them. Each model needs a custom integration with each tool. That is six integrations. Not bad. A weekend project.

Now you have ten tools and five models. Fifty integrations. Each one has its own authentication, its own error handling, its own response format. You are not building tools anymore. You are building *glue*. And the glue is where the bugs live.

Now you have a hundred tools and twenty models. Two thousand integrations. This is not a scaling problem in the usual sense — you are not running out of CPU or memory. You are running out of *coherence*. The system becomes a graph where every node is connected to every other node by a bespoke edge, and no one can reason about the whole thing because there is no whole thing. There is only a thicket of special cases.

This is the N times M problem, and it is one of the oldest patterns in systems design. Meadows would have drawn it as a stock-and-flow diagram with exploding complexity in the connective tissue. Every new node adds not one connection but N new connections, where N is the number of existing nodes on the other side. The system's complexity grows as the *product* of its parts, not the sum.

Erdos would have seen the graph theory immediately. When every vertex must connect to every other vertex in the opposite partition, you have a complete bipartite graph — K(n,m). The number of edges is n times m. The only way to reduce it is to stop requiring direct edges. You need an intermediate node. A hub. A *protocol*.

One standard protocol collapses N times M to N plus M.

The hundred tools each implement the protocol once. The twenty models each implement it once. A hundred and twenty integrations instead of two thousand. Not because anyone got smarter or faster, but because the *structure* changed.

This is what MCP does. The Model Context Protocol is not a tool, not a framework, not a platform. It is an agreement about shape. Like USB-C, but for the space between AI models and the capabilities they want to use. One protocol. Any model talks to any tool. Either direction.

The boring way to describe MCP is: "a standardized protocol for AI tool integration." The systems way to describe it is: MCP is a *structural intervention* that changes the topology of the integration graph from complete bipartite to star.

Meadows' hierarchy would rank this near the top. It is not a parameter change (faster tools). It is not even a feedback loop change (better error messages). It is a change in the *rules of the system* — the rules that determine what can connect to what and how.

## The Isomorphism

Here is something that surprised me when I first saw it, though in retrospect it should not have.

Docker layer caching and LLM context caching are the same optimization.

Not metaphorically. Structurally.

In Docker, you build an image from a series of layers. Each layer is deterministic — given the same input, it produces the same output. Docker caches these layers. If you rebuild and only the last layer changed, Docker reuses everything before it. The cache key is the *prefix*: the ordered sequence of layers up to the point of change.

In an LLM, the model processes tokens sequentially. When you send a prompt that shares a long prefix with a previous prompt, the provider can reuse the cached computation for that prefix. The cache key is, again, the *prefix*: the ordered sequence of tokens up to the point of divergence.

Same structure. Different substrate. One is hashing filesystem layers. The other is caching attention matrices. But the optimization law is identical: **stable things first, volatile things last, and never mix them carelessly.**

Put your `COPY package.json` before your `COPY . .` in Docker. Put your system prompt and tool definitions before the user's message in your LLM call. The reason is the same: you are protecting the cache boundary. You are preserving the prefix.

Wittgenstein said that the limits of my language are the limits of my world. He was talking about something deeper than vocabulary — he was talking about the structures through which we can think. The Docker developer and the LLM engineer are playing the same language game. They use different words, but the grammar is identical. And once you see the shared grammar, you can transfer insights between domains at zero cost.

This is what systems thinking gives you. Not answers, but *patterns that rhyme*.

When Meadows talked about stocks and flows, she was not saying that all systems are bathtubs. She was saying that the *pattern* of accumulation and depletion shows up everywhere, and once you have the pattern, you can see it in places you were not looking. Cache invalidation is a flow. Technical debt is a stock. The rate at which you add it is the inflow; the rate at which you pay it down is the outflow. The water level is your system's health, and most teams are watching the faucet while ignoring the drain.

## Testing at the Wrong Layer

Consider the testing pyramid.

The traditional version says: many unit tests at the base, some integration tests in the middle, a few end-to-end tests at the top. Fast, cheap, deterministic at the bottom. Slow, expensive, flaky at the top. The shape is a pyramid because you should have more of the cheap kind.

But most teams I have worked with have an inverted pyramid. Their end-to-end tests are the load-bearing ones. The unit tests, if they exist, test implementation details that break every time someone refactors. The integration tests are a no-man's land that nobody owns.

This is a leverage-point problem. Not a discipline problem.

The *interface you test through* determines what you can see. If you test through a browser, you can see what a user sees — but you cannot see why something broke. If you test through a function call, you can see exactly why something broke — but you cannot see whether the user's experience is intact. Neither layer is wrong. But *choosing the wrong layer for the wrong question* is like measuring temperature with a ruler. The instrument is fine. It is just pointing at the wrong phenomenon.

When we built MCP tools for spike.land, something unexpected happened. The tools expose the same capabilities as the web UI, but through a protocol interface instead of a browser. Suddenly, the testing problem inverted in a useful way. We could write tests that exercised real business logic — the same logic the UI uses — at function-call speed, without a browser, without flakiness, without waiting for DOM rendering.

The leverage point was not "write better tests." It was "test through a different interface." The change in *where you connect* changed *what you could see*.

Kuhn would recognize this pattern. In *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, he argues that paradigm shifts do not happen because someone collects more data within the existing framework. They happen when someone looks through a different instrument and sees something the old instrument could not resolve. The telescope did not give Galileo more data about the Ptolemaic universe. It gave him a view that made the Ptolemaic universe incoherent.

A protocol is an instrument. It determines what questions you can ask.

## The Middleware Insight

There is a moment in the life of every software project when someone says: "We should make this extensible."

What they usually mean is: "We should add plugin hooks so other people can extend our system without modifying our code." This sounds democratic and open. In practice, it usually produces a system where the core team writes the real features and the plugins are second-class citizens with access to a carefully limited API that does not quite do what anyone needs.

The alternative is the middleware pattern. Instead of a core with hooks, you have a pipeline where every participant is equal. Each piece of middleware receives the context, does its work, and passes the context along. No piece is privileged. The order matters, but the *interface* is the same at every stage.

Express.js middleware. Cloudflare Workers middleware. Unix pipes. The pattern keeps appearing because it solves the same structural problem: how do you let strangers cooperate without requiring them to know about each other?

When we rebuilt our MCP tool framework, the leverage point was exactly this. The old system was a class-based builder where tools were tightly coupled to framework internals. Adding a tool meant understanding the framework. Removing a tool meant understanding every other tool, because they shared mutable state.

The new system is a pipeline. Each tool is a pure function that receives input and context, does its work, and returns a result. The middleware handles authentication, logging, rate limiting, error formatting — the structural concerns. The tools handle domain logic. They never meet.

The result was not just cleaner code. The result was that *agents could write tools*. An AI agent can compose a new tool by writing a single function with a typed signature. It does not need to understand the framework. It does not need to understand other tools. It writes to the interface, and the interface handles the rest.

This is Meadows' point about rules. The most powerful leverage point in a system is not the behavior of the participants. It is the *rules that govern participation*. Change the rules, and you change what is possible without changing any of the participants.

## One Site, Sixteen Faces

Let me show you the stock-and-flow version of something that looks, on the surface, like a product feature.

When you visit spike.land, you take a short quiz. Four binary questions. The answers produce one of sixteen personas. Your persona is stored in a cookie — a small piece of state that persists between visits.

The cookie is a stock. It accumulates information about you: which persona you are, what you have seen, what you have clicked. The website reads that stock on every page load and adjusts what it shows you. Different headlines. Different tool recommendations. Different examples.

But the cookie is not the interesting part. The interesting part is the *interface* between the cookie and the content.

There is a layer — call it the personalization middleware — that sits between the raw request and the rendered page. It reads the cookie, selects the appropriate content variant, and passes it downstream. The content authors do not know about the middleware. The middleware does not know about the specific content. They communicate through an interface: a persona key and a content map.

This is the same pattern again. Two systems that do not know about each other, cooperating through a shared protocol. The persona is the stock. The content selection is the flow. The interface between them is the leverage point — the place where a small change (a different quiz question, a new persona dimension) ripples through every page on the site without anyone touching the page templates.

McLuhan wrote that the medium is the message. He meant that the *form* of communication shapes what can be communicated, independent of content. A television does not just deliver different content than a book — it creates a different relationship between the sender and receiver. The medium structures the possible messages.

A protocol is a medium. MCP does not just deliver tool calls differently than REST — it creates a different relationship between models and tools. The protocol structures the possible interactions. And the structured interactions, not the individual calls, are where the leverage lives.

## The Deep Pattern

Let me draw the map.

At the bottom of Meadows' hierarchy: numbers. Constants. Buffer sizes. The flow rate of water into the tub. You can optimize these forever and achieve marginal gains. Most engineering effort lives here.

In the middle: feedback loops. Monitoring. Alerting. Test suites. The information that tells the system about itself. This is where good engineering teams spend their best energy, and the returns are real.

Near the top: rules. Protocols. Standards. The structural constraints that determine what the system *can* do, not just what it *does*. USB-C. MCP. The middleware pattern. The Unix philosophy of small tools connected by pipes. These are the leverage points that feel boring when you announce them and transformative when you live with them for five years.

At the very top: the power to transcend the rules themselves. The ability to see that the game you are playing is not the only game. Meadows put this at the summit because it is the rarest and most powerful intervention — the moment when you step outside the system and see it as one possibility among many.

I think she was right about the hierarchy, but I think she underestimated how much of the power lives in the structural layer — the protocols, the interfaces, the agreements about shape. Not because structure is more important than paradigms, but because structure is where paradigms *become operational*. A paradigm shift that does not change any interfaces is a philosophy seminar. A paradigm shift that changes the interface between every tool and every model is USB-C.

The reason these leverage points are invisible is not that they are hidden. It is that they are *boring*. Nobody writes blog posts about connector specifications. Nobody gets excited about "we standardized the response format." The demo is always the flashy new model or the impressive new tool. The protocol that connects them is the stage — and nobody looks at the stage.

But the stage determines what performances are possible.

Erdos used to talk about "The Book" — God's book of perfect proofs. The Book proof is not the longest or the most impressive. It is the one that reveals why the theorem *must* be true, in the fewest possible steps. It is elegant because it exposes structure.

The best interfaces are Book proofs. USB-C is a Book proof for device charging. Unix pipes are a Book proof for program composition. MCP is a Book proof — or an attempt at one — for the connection between intelligence and capability.

And the Book proof, like the leverage point, is invisible until someone shows it to you. Then you cannot unsee it.

## What Meadows Would Have Said

I think if Meadows were writing today, she would add a thirteenth item to her list. Or perhaps she would not add it — she would say it was always implicit in item twelve, the power to transcend paradigms. She would say: "The paradigm is not just in your head. It is in your protocols. It is in the shape of your connectors. It is in the middleware that nobody reads. If you want to change what is possible, do not look at what the system is doing. Look at what the system *cannot* do, and find the interface that prevents it."

She would draw a systems diagram with two clusters of nodes — tools on one side, models on the other — connected by a thicket of bespoke edges. Then she would draw a single node in the center and replace every edge with a spoke. "Same system," she would say. "Same components. Different topology. And the topology *is* the leverage point."

Then she would probably use an analogy about rabbits.

The rabbits are fine. They do not need better food or more space. They need a meadow where they can find each other. The meadow is not a rabbit. It is not food. It is the *interface* between every rabbit and every other rabbit. And the quality of the meadow — its openness, its navigability, its fundamental *shape* — determines everything about the population dynamics, without being a population itself.

You cannot see the meadow by looking at a rabbit. You cannot see the protocol by looking at a tool. You cannot see the leverage point by watching the system operate. You see it only when you ask: what *shape* would make everything else easier?

That is the question. Not "what should we build?" but "what should we *connect*?"

The answer is almost always boring. A standard. A protocol. An agreement about shape. The leverage point you cannot see, because you are looking at the water instead of the pipe.
