Last Tuesday, I typed a single request into Claude Code. Eleven minutes later, a fully tested feature was live on our staging environment.

That same afternoon, a junior colleague sat down with the same model to do a similar task. Forty-five minutes and twelve chat messages later, he was still wrestling with broken code.

The difference was not talent. It was not the AI.

**The difference was context.**

He typed a question. I delivered a complete briefing. He was interrogating a stranger, hoping the AI would guess what he wanted. I was handing the AI a map of our codebase, our conventions, and clear boundaries around the task.

The gap between eleven minutes and forty-five minutes was not built during the conversation. It was built before either of us typed a single word.

## The Death of Magic Words

Let me be direct: trying to find the "magic words" to trick an AI into doing what you want is an outdated strategy. It was necessary in 2023, when models needed careful coaxing. Today, models are smart enough to understand your intent. The bottleneck has moved.

The bottleneck is now **information about your specific project**.

Think of it like giving directions. If someone asks you how to get to the grocery store, "turn left at the light" works fine in your town. But if they are in a city they have never visited, you need to give them the whole map — or better yet, hand them one with the route already highlighted.

Your prompt is just the final instruction: "go to the store." Context engineering is everything that makes that instruction unambiguous.

If your AI gives you a bad result, the old instinct was to rewrite the prompt. The new instinct should be: *What context was missing?* That is the leverage point. Almost always, the problem is upstream of the question.

## The 5-Layer Context Stack

To get perfect results on the first try — what we call a "zero-shot prompt" — you need to provide five layers of context. Think of them as the layers of a building. Skip the foundation and the penthouse wobbles.

{/* Diagram: 5 layers of context — Identity, Knowledge, Examples, Constraints, and Tools */}

### Layer 1: Identity
**Who is the AI in this moment?**

Give the AI a role. This narrows its vast knowledge to exactly the slice you need — like adjusting the aperture on a camera.

*Example:* "You are a senior TypeScript developer working on a Next.js app. You write strict, fully-tested code."

### Layer 2: Knowledge
**What does the AI need to know about your specific world?**

The AI knows general programming. It does not know your database schema, your folder layout, or why you chose Hono over Express. You have to provide the map.

*Example:* Point the AI to your database schema file or explain your authentication flow.

### Layer 3: Examples
**What does "good" look like here?**

Instead of describing your coding style in abstract terms, show it. One example is worth a hundred words of description.

*Example:* "Follow the exact data-fetching pattern used in `src/components/UserProfile.tsx`."

### Layer 4: Constraints
**What must the AI not do?**

AI models love to be "helpful" by rewriting code you did not ask them to touch. Without boundaries, helpfulness becomes chaos. Constraints are not restrictions — they are focus.

*Example:* "Do not modify any files outside of the `src/dashboard` folder. Do not add any new npm packages."

### Layer 5: Tools
**What can the AI actually do?**

If you give the AI access to read files, run tests, or search the codebase, it fundamentally changes how it solves the problem. A model with `yarn test` access will naturally verify its own work before presenting it to you. A model without it is flying blind.

*Example:* Give the AI access to run your test suite, and watch how much more careful its code becomes.

## CLAUDE.md: Your Secret Weapon

The easiest way to apply all five layers is a `CLAUDE.md` file in the root of your project.

Claude Code reads this file automatically every time it starts a session. It is your permanent context file — the onboarding document for a brilliant colleague who gets amnesia every single morning.

But here is the key: **write it for the AI, not for humans.**

Humans want to know *why* a decision was made. The AI just needs to know *what* to do.

**Good AI instructions:**
> "Testing framework: Vitest. Test files must end in `.test.ts`. Run tests with `yarn test`. Require 100% coverage."

**Bad AI instructions:**
> "We decided to move away from Jest in 2024 because Vitest was faster, so please try to write Vitest tests if you can."

The first is a rule. The second is a story. The AI needs rules.

Every time the AI makes a bad assumption, add a new rule to this file. Over time, `CLAUDE.md` becomes a living document that encodes your team's judgment. It is the institutional memory that the AI cannot build on its own.

## The Effort Inversion

Here is the biggest mindset shift.

{/* Graph: effort inversion — high upfront prep vs high backend iteration */}

**The old way:** Spend 2 minutes writing a prompt. Spend 45 minutes arguing with the AI to fix its mistakes. Each round of fixes introduces new confusion. The conversation degrades.

**The new way:** Spend 15 minutes gathering context, writing clear constraints, and pointing to examples. Spend 2 minutes writing the prompt. The AI gets it right on the first try.

The total time is less. But more importantly, the *shape* of the effort changes. You invest upfront instead of paying downstream. Like packing carefully for a trip versus buying everything you forgot at airport prices.

This is a classic systems pattern: small investments at leverage points produce disproportionate results. Context is the leverage point. The prompt is just the trigger.

My colleague's mistake was asking the AI a question. My move was handing it a briefing. The eleven-minute gap was not built during the conversation. It was built before it started.

Preparation is the work. The prompt is just pressing send.
