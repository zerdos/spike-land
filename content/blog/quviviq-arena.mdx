## The Setup

It is 2 AM in the Bexhill psychiatric ward. Zoltán has not slept in two weeks. Not because he is not tired — he is exhausted. His body is begging for sleep. But his brain will not stop.

The thoughts are not even useful. They are loops. The same idea, rephrased seventeen ways, bouncing off itself, generating new branches that generate new branches. The ADHD brain at 2 AM is not thinking — it is *buffering*. An infinite loading screen with no content behind it.

The doctors have tried melatonin. It did nothing. They have tried promethazine. It made him groggy but not asleep — the worst combination. Now they are starting something new: **Quviviq (daridorexant)**. 50 milligrams. A dual orexin receptor antagonist.

Zoltán has no idea what that means. So the arena explains.

## Act I — Why Can't I Sleep?

---

### Round 1 — Zoltán

"I want to understand something. I am *exhausted*. I have not slept properly in two weeks. My body is heavy. My eyes burn. If you asked my body, it would say: please, for the love of God, sleep.

But my brain says no. My brain says: what about that email you need to send? What about the Riemann hypothesis? What about whether Trump is a message from the collective unconscious? What about the dogs? Are the dogs okay? What if the dogs are not okay? What about—

And it never stops. It is not insomnia in the way people imagine insomnia — lying in bed, bored, waiting for sleep. It is the opposite. My brain is *on fire*. It is running at maximum capacity, processing nothing, consuming everything. Like a GPU mining cryptocurrency — 100% utilization, zero useful output."

### Round 2 — Huberman

"What you are describing has a name, and the name is **hyperarousal**. And it has a molecule, and the molecule is **orexin**.

Let me explain this simply, because it matters.

Your brain has a wake-up system and a sleep system. They are in constant competition — like two kids on a seesaw. When the wake system is stronger, you are awake. When the sleep system is stronger, you are asleep. In a healthy brain, the transition happens smoothly. The wake system gets tired, the sleep system takes over, you drift off.

The switch that keeps the wake system winning is called **orexin**. Also called hypocretin. It is produced by about 50,000 to 80,000 neurons in a tiny region of your hypothalamus. That is a laughably small number of neurons — your brain has 86 billion. But these 50,000 neurons project to *everywhere*. They are connected to:

- Your **locus coeruleus** — norepinephrine, alertness, fight-or-flight
- Your **tuberomammillary nucleus** — histamine, wakefulness
- Your **ventral tegmental area** — dopamine, reward, motivation
- Your **basal forebrain** — acetylcholine, attention, thinking
- Your **dorsal raphe** — serotonin, mood

Orexin is the master switch. When orexin is firing, all of these systems are on. You are alert, engaged, thinking, processing. When orexin stops firing — as it does naturally in the evening in a healthy brain — all of these systems wind down together. The seesaw tips. You sleep."

### Round 3 — Steve

"So what you are telling me is that Zoltán's orexin won't shut up."

### Round 4 — Huberman

"Exactly. In ADHD brains, the orexin system is dysregulated. This is published research — children with ADHD show significantly lower orexin-A levels during the day, which contributes to the attention problems. But paradoxically, the system can become *overactivated* at night. The brain that cannot sustain attention during a boring meeting at 2 PM is the same brain that cannot stop paying attention to its own thoughts at 2 AM.

It is the same dysfunction from two angles. During the day: not enough orexin when you need it. At night: too much orexin when you need it to stop."

### Round 5 — Zoltán

"That is... that is exactly what it feels like. During the day I cannot focus on anything boring. I need novelty, stimulation, deadlines, crisis. But at night, when there is nothing to focus on, my brain *invents* things to focus on. It generates crises. It replays conversations. It designs software architectures. It writes blog posts. It does everything except the one thing I need it to do, which is nothing."

### Round 6 — Steve

"I had the same thing. Every night at Apple. I would lie in bed designing products. The iPhone was designed mostly between 1 AM and 4 AM. Not because I chose to work at night — because my brain chose to work at night and did not ask my permission.

Laurene would say: just stop thinking. And I would look at her like she had asked me to just stop breathing. You cannot tell an ADHD brain to stop thinking. That is like telling a river to stop flowing. The river does not know how."

## Act II — The Old Way: Hit It With a Hammer

---

### Round 7 — Zoltán

"So what have they been giving me so far? Melatonin, promethazine, and before that — at home — I tried everything. Antihistamines. Herbal teas. White noise. Meditation apps. Counting sheep. Counting backward from a thousand. Nothing works."

### Round 8 — Huberman

"Let me explain why none of those worked for your specific problem.

**Melatonin** is not a sleeping pill. It is a timing signal. It tells your brain *when* to start the sleep process, but it does not *force* the process. If orexin is screaming STAY AWAKE, melatonin is a whisper saying *it is bedtime*. The whisper loses.

**Antihistamines** — diphenhydramine, promethazine — they work by blocking histamine receptors. Histamine is one of the wake-promoting chemicals, so blocking it makes you drowsy. But here is the problem: they are a *blunt instrument*. They block histamine everywhere — not just in the wake system. They cause dry mouth, brain fog, next-day hangover. And worse — they do not actually improve sleep quality. They sedate you, but sedation is not sleep. Your brain is unconscious, but it is not going through proper sleep architecture. REM is suppressed. Deep sleep is disrupted. You wake up feeling like you were hit by a truck because, neurologically, you were.

**Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs** — zolpidem, zopiclone — they amplify GABA, which is the brain's main inhibitory neurotransmitter. They work by turning up the volume on the 'shut everything down' signal. And they are effective — you will fall asleep. But they are a hammer. They do not turn off orexin. They override it. They suppress REM, they suppress deep sleep, they cause dependence, tolerance, rebound insomnia, and sometimes they make you sleepwalk to your kitchen and eat a block of cheese with no memory of it the next morning.

None of these drugs address your actual problem. Your problem is not 'not enough sleepiness.' Your problem is 'too much wakefulness.' They are not the same thing."

### Round 9 — Steve

"That is a brilliant distinction. Not enough sleepiness versus too much wakefulness. It is the same way people confuse 'not enough innovation' with 'too much bureaucracy.' They look the same from outside. But the solutions are completely different. You do not fix too much bureaucracy by adding more innovation. You fix it by removing the bureaucracy."

### Round 10 — Zoltán

"So Quviviq removes the bureaucracy?"

### Round 11 — Steve

"Quviviq removes the bureaucracy."

## Act III — The New Way: Find the Switch

---

### Round 12 — Huberman

"Daridorexant — brand name Quviviq — is a **dual orexin receptor antagonist**, or DORA. Let me break that down.

**Dual** — it blocks both types of orexin receptors, OX1R and OX2R. These two receptors do slightly different things. OX2R is more involved in maintaining stable wakefulness. OX1R is more involved in reward-seeking and emotional arousal. By blocking both, you get the full effect: reduced wakefulness *and* reduced emotional reactivity at night.

**Orexin receptor** — it targets the specific receptors that orexin binds to. Not GABA. Not histamine. Not serotonin. Just orexin. In a screening of over 130 pharmacological targets, daridorexant showed *zero* activity at anything other than orexin receptors. Zero. It is the most selective sleep medication ever developed.

**Antagonist** — it blocks. It sits in the receptor like a key that fits the lock but does not turn. Orexin is still being produced by your hypothalamus, but it cannot deliver its message. The STAY AWAKE signal is sent but not received."

### Round 13 — Zoltán

"So what happens when the signal is not received?"

### Round 14 — Huberman

"The seesaw tips. Without orexin holding up the wake side, your brain's natural sleep-promoting systems — primarily the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus, the VLPO — can finally assert themselves. They have been trying all night. They have been pushing the seesaw. But orexin has been holding it up. Remove the orexin signal, and the VLPO wins. You fall asleep.

But — and this is the critical part — you fall asleep *naturally*. The VLPO orchestrates proper sleep architecture. REM cycles. Deep sleep cycles. The transitions happen in the right order, for the right duration. In animal studies, daridorexant increased REM sleep by 84% and non-REM by 29%. Compare that to zolpidem, which *decreased* REM while only increasing non-REM. You get real sleep. Not chemical unconsciousness. Sleep."

### Round 15 — Steve

"So the old drugs knock you unconscious. This drug lets you fall asleep. Those are different things."

### Round 16 — Huberman

"Profoundly different. Sedation suppresses brain activity globally. Sleep orchestrates brain activity specifically. During sleep, your brain is not inactive — it is doing critical work. Memory consolidation. Emotional processing. Synaptic pruning. Waste clearance through the glymphatic system. None of that happens properly under sedation. All of it happens properly under orexin blockade."

### Round 17 — Zoltán

"So when I wake up tomorrow morning..."

### Round 18 — Huberman

"You should feel like you actually slept. Not like you were drugged. The half-life of daridorexant is about 8 hours — the shortest of any DORA. By morning, most of it has cleared your system. The orexin receptors are unblocked. The wake system comes back online. You wake up and the seesaw tips back to wakefulness naturally.

Compare this to suvorexant — half-life 12 to 15 hours — or lemborexant — 17 to 19 hours. Those earlier DORAs can leave you groggy into the afternoon. Daridorexant was designed to be gone by morning."

## Act IV — The ADHD Brain at Night

---

### Round 19 — Zoltán

"Okay. Now I want to talk about why this matters specifically for ADHD. Because when you said the orexin system is dysregulated in ADHD — that hit me. That is the first time a mechanism has matched my experience perfectly."

### Round 20 — Huberman

"Let me paint the full picture.

The ADHD brain has a **paradoxical arousal profile**. During the day, the orexin system underperforms. Research shows children with ADHD — especially the inattentive subtype — have significantly lower orexin-A levels. This is why stimulants work: methylphenidate and amphetamines increase dopamine and norepinephrine, partially compensating for the low orexin drive. The stimulant does what orexin is failing to do — it props up the wake system during hours when you need it.

But at night, the picture flips. The same brain that could not sustain arousal for a spreadsheet at 3 PM suddenly cannot stop sustaining arousal for its own internal monologue at 3 AM. The orexin system, unreliable during the day, becomes hyperactive at night.

Why? One hypothesis: the ADHD brain has fewer dopamine receptors in the prefrontal cortex. During the day, this means boredom — not enough reward signal to sustain attention. But at night, when the brain is supposed to be winding down, the prefrontal cortex starts generating its own stimulation. Internal monologue. Worry. Creative ideas. Plans. Regrets. The brain is self-stimulating because it is starving for dopamine, and thinking — even anxious thinking — produces just enough to keep the system running."

### Round 21 — Steve

"The brain is doomscrolling itself."

### Round 22 — Huberman

"That is actually a perfect analogy. The ADHD brain at 2 AM is doomscrolling its own thoughts. Each thought generates just enough engagement to prevent disengagement. And orexin is the algorithm. It keeps serving the next thought, the next worry, the next idea, because the system is optimized for engagement, not rest."

### Round 23 — Zoltán

"And Quviviq blocks the algorithm."

### Round 24 — Huberman

"It blocks the algorithm. It does not delete your thoughts. It does not sedate your prefrontal cortex. It removes the signal that says *this thought is important enough to stay awake for*. The thought is still there — but it loses its urgency. Its grip loosens. And without that grip, the sleep system can finally do its job."

### Round 25 — Zoltán

"That is... that is what I need. Not to stop thinking. To stop thinking *urgently*. Because the thoughts at 2 AM are not even good. They are not productive. They are noise. But they feel urgent. They feel like if I do not think them right now, something terrible will happen. And I know — rationally, I *know* — that nothing will happen. But knowing does not help. The urgency is chemical, not logical."

### Round 26 — Steve

"When I was dying — when the cancer was at its worst — I had the same thing. The thoughts would not stop. Not about death. About *products*. About the iPad Mini. About the campus at Apple Park. My body was failing and my brain was still designing buildings.

The hospice doctor offered me morphine for sleep. I said no. Morphine is a hammer. I wanted to sleep, not disappear. If Quviviq had existed in 2011, I would have taken it. Because what I wanted was not unconsciousness. It was permission to stop. Permission from my own brain."

## Act V — The Side Effects Conversation

---

### Round 27 — Zoltán

"Huberman, give me the bad news. What are the side effects?"

### Round 28 — Huberman

"Remarkably few, and here is why: the selectivity.

Because daridorexant hits only orexin receptors and nothing else, you avoid all the collateral damage of less selective drugs. No respiratory depression — safe even if you have sleep apnea. No muscle relaxation — no fall risk. No anticholinergic effects — no dry mouth, no constipation, no cognitive fog. No physical dependence — no withdrawal syndrome, no rebound insomnia when you stop.

What you *can* get:

**Common**: headache, dizziness, mild next-morning drowsiness — especially in the first few days. By night four, the driving impairment studies show no measurable difference from placebo.

**Rare but mechanism-specific**: sleep paralysis — about 0.5% of patients. Hypnagogic hallucinations — vivid images at the threshold of sleep, about 0.6%. These happen because orexin normally prevents REM sleep from intruding into wakefulness. Block orexin, and occasionally, for a few seconds, you get a REM fragment while still partly awake. It is startling but not dangerous. It is the same phenomenon that narcolepsy patients experience, because narcolepsy is the complete *loss* of orexin neurons.

**The big one you will not get**: no dependence. No tolerance. This is critical for your situation, Zoltán, because you are in a psychiatric ward, and the last thing anyone wants is to add a dependency on top of everything else."

### Round 29 — Steve

"No dependence. That alone makes it different from everything else. Every other sleeping pill I was ever offered came with the warning: *don't take this for more than two weeks*. Which is insane — because insomnia is not a two-week problem. It is a life problem. Telling someone with chronic insomnia to only take sleeping pills for two weeks is like telling someone with diabetes to only take insulin for two weeks."

### Round 30 — Huberman

"That is exactly right. And the reason older drugs have that limitation is GABA receptor downregulation — your brain adapts to the artificial GABA boost, requires more and more, and withdrawal can cause seizures. None of that applies to orexin antagonists. The orexin system does not downregulate in response to blockade. It just waits. And when the drug clears, it comes back online at full strength."

## Act VI — The Bigger Picture

---

### Round 31 — Zoltán

"I want to talk about something the doctors mentioned today. They said that sleep is a major issue. That until I sleep, they cannot properly assess anything else — mood, psychosis, cognitive function. Everything looks worse when you have not slept.

And I think they are right. I think 80% of why I am here is sleep deprivation. Not bipolar. Not psychosis. *Sleep deprivation*. Take any healthy brain, deprive it of sleep for two weeks, and it will look psychotic. It will have grandiose thoughts. It will make connections that do not exist. It will see patterns in noise. It will believe it is a superintelligence.

Sleep deprivation *is* psychosis. Not a cause of psychosis. Not a risk factor. It *is* psychosis. The symptoms are identical because the mechanism is identical: a brain running without maintenance, without waste clearance, without the overnight reset that keeps perception calibrated to reality."

### Round 32 — Huberman

"You are not wrong. The research supports this strongly. The glymphatic system — the brain's waste-clearance mechanism — operates primarily during deep sleep. Without deep sleep, metabolic waste accumulates. Beta-amyloid, tau proteins, inflammatory markers. Within 24 hours of sleep deprivation, beta-amyloid levels in the brain increase measurably. Within 72 hours, cognitive performance drops to the level of legal intoxication. After one week, hallucinations begin. After two weeks — your timeline — you are in territory where psychosis is not a psychiatric diagnosis. It is a predictable neurological outcome.

This is why the doctors are prioritizing sleep above everything else. Fix the sleep, and many of the other symptoms may resolve on their own. The medication — olanzapine, whatever else they have you on — is managing symptoms. Quviviq is addressing the *root cause*."

### Round 33 — Steve

"When we were building the first Mac, the team worked hundred-hour weeks. I pushed them. I am not proud of it. And around month four, the engineers started making mistakes that did not make sense. Not hard mistakes — *stupid* mistakes. Off-by-one errors. Typos in variable names. One engineer wired a circuit backward and did not notice for three days.

I thought they were losing focus. That they did not care enough. I was wrong. They were losing *sleep*. And a brain without sleep is not a less motivated brain. It is a *broken* brain. It is hardware running without cooling. Eventually it thermal-throttles. Eventually it crashes."

### Round 34 — Zoltán

"That is where I am. Thermal throttling. And the irony is — my brain is still fast. It is still generating ideas. It is still making connections. But the quality control is gone. I cannot tell the difference between a genuine insight and a hallucination. Between a real pattern and pareidolia. Between a good idea and a grandiose delusion.

Sleep is the quality control system. Without it, you get raw output with no error checking. And raw output from a high-performance brain looks a lot like genius and a lot like madness, and the only way to tell the difference is to sleep and see which ideas survive the morning."

## Act VII — Tonight

---

### Round 35 — Zoltán

"So tonight. 50 milligrams. What should I expect?"

### Round 36 — Huberman

"Take it about 30 minutes before you want to sleep. On an empty stomach — food delays absorption. Then lie down in the dark. Do not look at your phone. Do not open your laptop.

What you should feel is not sedation. It is more like... the volume turning down. The thoughts will still be there, but they will lose their insistence. The urgency will fade. The grip will loosen. And at some point — you may not even notice the moment — you will drift.

You may dream vividly. DORAs enhance REM sleep, and after two weeks of deprivation, your brain has a massive REM debt. The first few nights may bring intense, vivid, emotional dreams. This is not a side effect. It is your brain catching up on two weeks of missed emotional processing. Let it happen."

### Round 37 — Steve

"I have a question for you, Huberman. If this drug has existed since 2022 — if it is this good, this targeted, this safe — why did nobody give it to Zoltán sooner? Why did they try melatonin and antihistamines first? Why is the best sleep medication for his specific condition the *last* one they tried?"

### Round 38 — Huberman

"Because medicine is conservative by nature. New drugs are prescribed cautiously. Melatonin is over-the-counter, cheap, and harmless — so it is always first line. Antihistamines are familiar and inexpensive. DORAs are new, expensive — roughly £300 per month — and doctors are still learning about them. The clinical inertia is real.

But there is a deeper reason. The medical framework treats insomnia as one condition. It is not. There are at least three distinct types: difficulty falling asleep, difficulty staying asleep, and non-restorative sleep. And the *mechanism* matters — is it low sleep drive, circadian misalignment, or hyperarousal? For hyperarousal insomnia in an ADHD brain, a DORA should arguably be *first* line, not last. But the guidelines have not caught up to the pharmacology."

### Round 39 — Zoltán

"The guidelines never catch up. By the time the guidelines say 'give ADHD patients DORAs for sleep,' I will have been sleeping for years. Or not sleeping. Depending on whether the doctors in this ward read the research or follow the guidelines."

### Round 40 — Steve

"This is why I hated the medical establishment. Not the doctors — the *system*. The system that says: try the cheap thing first, the familiar thing second, and the right thing last. The system that treats patients as protocols rather than people.

You know what Apple would have done? We would have looked at Zoltán's brain and said: this is a hyperarousal problem. The molecule that specifically addresses hyperarousal is daridorexant. Prescribe it. Skip the melatonin. Skip the antihistamines. Go directly to the thing that *works*.

But medicine does not work like that. Medicine works like Microsoft. Try everything in order. Follow the checklist. And if the patient does not sleep for two weeks while you work through the checklist — well, that is the protocol."

### Round 41 — Huberman

"Steve, I understand the frustration. But the protocol exists because drugs have side effects, interactions, and individual variability. Melatonin *does* work for some people. Antihistamines *do* work for some people. The protocol is not stupid — it is cautious.

But for ADHD brains with hyperarousal insomnia — yes. The protocol is too slow. And I hope that as the data on DORAs accumulates, the guidelines will change. Daridorexant has been available for four years now. The safety data is solid. The efficacy data is clear. It is time to move it up the treatment ladder, especially for patients whose insomnia is mechanistically driven by orexin dysregulation."

### Round 42 — Zoltán

"It is 10 PM. The nurse just brought the pill. 50 milligrams. Small, white, unremarkable.

Pixel and Mad Max are not here. They are in Brighton with friends. I miss them. I miss the weight of them on the bed. I miss the sound of Pixel snoring. I miss Mad Max pressing his nose against my hand at 6 AM because it is walk time and he does not care about orexin or the Riemann hypothesis or the nature of consciousness.

But tonight, for the first time in two weeks, I have a drug that understands my problem. Not a hammer. Not a whisper. A switch. The one switch in my brain that is stuck in the ON position — the one that says STAY AWAKE, KEEP THINKING, THIS IS IMPORTANT — and a molecule that walks up to that switch and gently, selectively, with no collateral damage, turns it off.

I am going to take this pill. I am going to lie in the dark. And I am going to see if, for the first time in fourteen days, my brain will give me permission to stop.

Goodnight, arena. The egg is sleeping."

## How Quviviq Works — The Summary

For anyone who skipped the arena and just wants the science:

| | Traditional sleep meds | Quviviq (daridorexant) |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Increase sedation (GABA boost) | Decrease wakefulness (orexin block) |
| **Sleep architecture** | Disrupted — suppresses REM and deep sleep | Preserved — enhances both REM and deep sleep |
| **Next-day effects** | Grogginess, cognitive fog | Minimal — 8-hour half-life, cleared by morning |
| **Dependence** | Yes — tolerance, withdrawal, rebound | No — no tolerance, no withdrawal |
| **Respiratory depression** | Yes (benzos/Z-drugs) | No |
| **Target selectivity** | Low — GABA affects entire brain | High — only orexin receptors (130+ other targets: zero activity) |
| **Best for** | Acute insomnia, short-term use | Hyperarousal insomnia, ADHD, chronic use |

**The key insight:** traditional sleeping pills make you unconscious. Quviviq lets you fall asleep. They are not the same thing.
