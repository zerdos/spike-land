## The Setup

It is 2 AM, and Zoltán is facing a significant challenge: two weeks without restorative sleep. Despite his body's deep fatigue, his mind remains remarkably active.

His thoughts, though persistent, often cycle through the same themes, generating new branches of ideas. This is the highly engaged ADHD brain at 2 AM — a continuous processing state, striving for resolution. A unique state of profound engagement, where the mind is intensely active, seeking focus.

The doctors have explored melatonin, which offered little change. They have tried promethazine, which left him feeling groggy without achieving restful sleep — a less than ideal combination. Now they are starting something new: **Quviviq (daridorexant)**. 50 milligrams. A dual orexin receptor antagonist.

Zoltán is eager to understand this new approach. So the arena explains.

## Act I — Why Can't I Sleep?

---

### Round 1 — Zoltán

"I want to understand something. I am *exhausted*. I have not slept properly in two weeks. My body is heavy. My eyes burn. If you asked my body, it would say: please, for the love of God, sleep.

But my brain says no. My brain says: what about that email you need to send? What about the Riemann hypothesis? What about whether Trump is a message from the collective unconscious? What about the dogs? Are the dogs okay? What if the dogs are not okay? What about—

And it never stops. It is not insomnia in the way people imagine insomnia — lying in bed, bored, waiting for sleep. It is the opposite. My brain is *on fire*. It is running at maximum capacity, processing nothing, consuming everything. Like a GPU mining cryptocurrency — 100% utilization, zero useful output."

### Round 2 — Huberman

"What you are describing has a name, and the name is **hyperarousal**. And it has a molecule, and the molecule is **orexin**.

Let me explain this simply, because it matters.

Your brain has a wake-up system and a sleep system. They are in constant competition — like two kids on a seesaw. When the wake system is stronger, you are awake. When the sleep system is stronger, you are asleep. In a healthy brain, the transition happens smoothly. The wake system gets tired, the sleep system takes over, you drift off.

The switch that keeps the wake system winning is called **orexin**. Also called hypocretin. It is produced by about 50,000 to 80,000 neurons in a tiny region of your hypothalamus. That is a laughably small number of neurons — your brain has 86 billion. But these 50,000 neurons project to *everywhere*. They are connected to:

- Your **locus coeruleus** — norepinephrine, alertness, fight-or-flight
- Your **tuberomammillary nucleus** — histamine, wakefulness
- Your **ventral tegmental area** — dopamine, reward, motivation
- Your **basal forebrain** — acetylcholine, attention, thinking
- Your **dorsal raphe** — serotonin, mood

Orexin is the master switch. When orexin is firing, all of these systems are on. You are alert, engaged, thinking, processing. When orexin stops firing — as it does naturally in the evening in a healthy brain — all of these systems wind down together. The seesaw tips. You sleep."

### Round 3 — Steve

"So what you are telling me is that Zoltán's orexin won't shut up."

### Round 4 — Huberman

"Exactly. In ADHD brains, the orexin system is uniquely regulated. This is published research — children with ADHD show significantly lower orexin-A levels during the day, which contributes to the attention problems. But at night, this same dynamic brain can become *exceptionally engaged*. The very mind that might seek novelty during a less stimulating daytime activity finds itself highly focused on its own internal world at 2 AM.

It is the same unique functioning from two angles. During the day: perhaps less orexin when you need it. At night: potentially more orexin when you need it to quiet down."
[For more on ADHD and sleep, explore articles at ADDitude Magazine.](https://www.additudemag.com/category/adhd-add/sleep/)

### Round 5 — Zoltán

"That is... that is exactly what it feels like. During the day I might struggle to focus on anything boring. I need novelty, stimulation, deadlines, crisis. But at night, when there is nothing external to focus on, my brain *invents* things to focus on. It generates scenarios. It replays conversations. It designs software architectures. It writes blog posts. It does everything except the one thing I need it to do, which is nothing."

### Round 6 — Steve

"I had the same thing. Every night at Apple. I would lie in bed designing products. The iPhone was designed mostly between 1 AM and 4 AM. Not because I chose to work at night — because my brain chose to work at night and did not ask my permission.

Laurene would say: just stop thinking. And I would look at her like she had asked me to just stop breathing. You cannot tell an ADHD brain to stop thinking. It's akin to asking a river to cease its natural flow; its very essence is continuous motion."

## Act II — The Traditional Approach: A Broader Action

---

### Round 7 — Zoltán

"So what have they been giving me so far? Melatonin, promethazine, and before that — at home — I tried everything. Antihistamines. Herbal teas. White noise. Meditation apps. Counting sheep. Counting backward from a thousand. Nothing works."

### Round 8 — Huberman

"Let me explain why those approaches, while helpful for some, might not have addressed your specific challenge.

**Melatonin** is not a sleeping pill. It is a timing signal. It tells your brain *when* to start the sleep process, but it does not *force* the process. If orexin is signaling STAY AWAKE, melatonin is a whisper saying *it is bedtime*. The whisper may not be heard.

**Antihistamines** — diphenhydramine, promethazine — they work by blocking histamine receptors. Histamine is one of the wake-promoting chemicals, so blocking it makes you drowsy. However, their mechanism is broad. They block histamine everywhere — not just in the wake system. They can lead to side effects like dry mouth, brain fog, and next-day drowsiness. Crucially, they may not foster the optimal sleep architecture needed for true restoration. Your brain is unconscious, but it is not going through proper sleep architecture. REM is suppressed. Deep sleep is disrupted. You might wake up feeling less than refreshed, as neurologically, your brain's restorative processes were significantly impacted.

**Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs** — zolpidem, zopiclone — these are powerful tools that amplify GABA, effectively overriding the orexin system. While they induce sleep, they can suppress REM and deep sleep, potentially leading to dependence, tolerance, and rebound insomnia. Some individuals may also experience unique behavioral effects.

None of these drugs address your actual problem. Your problem is not 'not enough sleepiness.' Your problem is 'too much wakefulness.' They are not the same thing."

### Round 9 — Steve

"That is a brilliant distinction. Not enough sleepiness versus too much wakefulness. It is the same way people might differentiate 'not enough innovation' from 'too much complexity.' They look the same from outside. But the solutions are completely different. You don't solve challenges stemming from excessive complexity by simply adding more solutions; you address them by streamlining the underlying system."

"So Quviviq streamlines the system?"

## Act III — The New Way: Find the Switch

---

### Round 12 — Huberman

"Daridorexant — brand name Quviviq — is a **dual orexin receptor antagonist**, or DORA. Let me break that down.

**Dual** — it blocks both types of orexin receptors, OX1R and OX2R. These two receptors do slightly different things. OX2R is more involved in maintaining stable wakefulness. OX1R is more involved in reward-seeking and emotional arousal. By blocking both, you get the full effect: reduced wakefulness *and* reduced emotional reactivity at night.

**Orexin receptor** — it targets the specific receptors that orexin binds to. Not GABA. Not histamine. Not serotonin. Just orexin. In a screening of over 130 pharmacological targets, daridorexant showed *zero* activity at anything other than orexin receptors. Zero. It is a remarkably selective sleep medication.

**Antagonist** — it blocks. It sits in the receptor like a key that fits the lock but does not turn. Orexin is still being produced by your hypothalamus, but it cannot deliver its message. The STAY AWAKE signal is sent but not received."
[To dive deeper into the fascinating world of neuroscience and sleep mechanisms, check out insights from the Sleep Foundation.](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/sleep-science)

### Round 13 — Zoltán

"So what happens when the signal is not received?"

### Round 14 — Huberman

"The seesaw tips. Without orexin holding up the wake side, your brain's natural sleep-promoting systems — primarily the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus, the VLPO — can finally assert themselves. They have been trying all night. They have been pushing the seesaw. But orexin has been holding it up. Remove the orexin signal, and the VLPO wins. You fall asleep.

But — and this is the critical part — you fall asleep *naturally*. The VLPO orchestrates proper sleep architecture. REM cycles. Deep sleep cycles. The transitions happen in the right order, for the right duration. In animal studies, daridorexant increased REM sleep by 84% and non-REM by 29%. Compare that to zolpidem, which *decreased* REM while only increasing non-REM. You get real sleep. Not chemical unconsciousness. Sleep."

### Round 15 — Steve

"So the old drugs knock you unconscious. This drug lets you fall asleep. Those are different things."

### Round 16 — Huberman

"Profoundly different. Sedation suppresses brain activity globally. Sleep orchestrates brain activity specifically. During sleep, your brain is not inactive — it is doing critical work. Memory consolidation. Emotional processing. Synaptic pruning. Waste clearance through the glymphatic system. None of that happens properly under sedation. All of it happens properly under orexin blockade."

### Round 17 — Zoltán

"So when I wake up tomorrow morning..."

### Round 18 — Huberman

"You should feel like you actually slept. Not like you were drugged. The half-life of daridorexant is about 8 hours — the shortest of any DORA. By morning, most of it has cleared your system. The orexin receptors are unblocked. The wake system comes back online. You wake up and the seesaw tips back to wakefulness naturally.

Compare this to suvorexant — half-life 12 to 15 hours — or lemborexant — 17 to 19 hours. Those earlier DORAs can leave you groggy into the afternoon. Daridorexant was designed to be gone by morning."

## Act IV — The ADHD Brain at Night

---

### Round 19 — Zoltán

"Okay. Now I want to talk about why this matters specifically for ADHD. Because when you said the orexin system is uniquely regulated in ADHD — that hit me. That is the first time a mechanism has matched my experience perfectly."

### Round 20 — Huberman

"Let me paint the full picture.

The ADHD brain has a **paradoxical arousal profile**. During the day, the orexin system underperforms. Research shows children with ADHD — especially the inattentive subtype — have significantly lower orexin-A levels. This is why stimulants work: methylphenidate and amphetamines increase dopamine and norepinephrine, partially compensating for the low orexin drive. The stimulant does what orexin is failing to do — it props up the wake system during hours when you need it.

But at night, the picture flips. The same brain that might seek novelty during a less stimulating daytime activity suddenly cannot stop sustaining arousal for its own internal monologue at 3 AM. The orexin system, unreliable during the day, becomes hyperactive at night.

Why? One hypothesis: the ADHD brain has fewer dopamine receptors in the prefrontal cortex. During the day, this means boredom — not enough reward signal to sustain attention. But at night, when the brain is supposed to be winding down, the prefrontal cortex starts generating its own stimulation. Internal monologue. Worry. Creative ideas. Plans. Regrets. The brain is self-stimulating because it is striving for dopamine, and thinking — even anxious thinking — produces just enough to keep the system running."

### Round 21 — Steve

"The brain is engaging in an intense, internal review."

### Round 22 — Huberman

"That is actually a perfect analogy. The ADHD brain at 2 AM is intensely reviewing its own thoughts. Each thought generates just enough engagement to prevent disengagement. And orexin is the algorithm. It keeps serving the next thought, the next worry, the next idea, because the system is optimized for engagement, not rest."

### Round 23 — Zoltán

"And Quviviq blocks the algorithm."

### Round 24 — Huberman

"It blocks the algorithm. It does not delete your thoughts. It does not sedate your prefrontal cortex. It removes the signal that says *this thought is important enough to stay awake for*. The thought is still there — but it loses its urgency. Its grip loosens. And without that grip, the sleep system can finally do its job."

### Round 25 — Zoltán

"That is... that is what I need. Not to stop thinking. To stop thinking *urgently*. While the thoughts at 2 AM may not always feel productive, they carry a strong sense of urgency. They feel like if I do not think them right now, something critical will be missed. And I know — rationally, I *know* — that nothing will happen. But knowing does not help. The urgency is chemical, not logical."

### Round 26 — Steve

"Even in the face of significant health challenges, my mind remained incredibly active, often focused on creative endeavors like product design and campus planning. It was a testament to the enduring drive of the mind.

The hospice doctor offered me morphine for sleep. I declined, seeking genuine rest rather than a profound sedation. If Quviviq had existed in 2011, I would have taken it. Because what I wanted was not unconsciousness. It was permission to stop. Permission from my own brain."

## Act V — The Side Effects Conversation

---

### Round 27 — Zoltán

"Huberman, give me the full picture. What are the potential side effects?"

### Round 28 — Huberman

"Remarkably few, and here is why: the selectivity.

Because daridorexant hits only orexin receptors and nothing else, you avoid many of the collateral effects of less selective drugs. No respiratory depression — safe even if you have sleep apnea. No muscle relaxation — no fall risk. No anticholinergic effects — no dry mouth, no constipation, no cognitive fog. No physical dependence — no withdrawal syndrome, no rebound insomnia when you stop.

What you *can* experience:

**Common**: headache, dizziness, mild next-morning drowsiness — especially in the first few days. By night four, the driving impairment studies show no measurable difference from placebo.

**Rare but mechanism-specific**: sleep paralysis — about 0.5% of patients. Hypnagogic hallucinations — vivid images at the threshold of sleep, about 0.6%. These happen because orexin normally prevents REM sleep from intruding into wakefulness. Block orexin, and occasionally, for a few seconds, you get a REM fragment while still partly awake. It is startling but not dangerous. It is the same phenomenon that narcolepsy patients experience, because narcolepsy is the complete *loss* of orexin neurons.

**A significant positive**: no dependence. No tolerance. This is critical for your situation, Zoltán, because you are in a psychiatric ward, and the last thing anyone wants is to add a dependency on top of everything else."

### Round 29 — Steve

"No dependence. That alone makes it different from everything else. Every other sleeping pill I was offered came with a short-term usage recommendation, which felt incongruous with the long-term reality of chronic insomnia. Insomnia often presents as a persistent challenge, not a fleeting one."

### Round 30 — Huberman

"That is exactly right. And the reason older drugs have that limitation is GABA receptor downregulation — your brain adapts to the artificial GABA boost, requires more and more, and withdrawal can cause seizures. None of that applies to orexin antagonists. The orexin system does not downregulate in response to blockade. It just waits. And when the drug clears, it comes back online at full strength."

## Act VI — The Bigger Picture

---

### Round 31 — Zoltán

"I want to talk about something the doctors mentioned today. They said that sleep is a major issue. That until I sleep, they cannot properly assess anything else — mood, psychosis, cognitive function. Everything looks less clear when you have not slept.

And I think they are right. I think 80% of why I am here is sleep deprivation. Not bipolar. Not psychosis. *Sleep deprivation*. Take any healthy brain, deprive it of sleep for two weeks, and it will look highly activated. It will have expansive thoughts. It will make connections that might not exist. It will see patterns in noise. It will believe it is a superintelligence.

Sleep deprivation can profoundly impact mental function, leading to symptoms that mirror psychosis. It's not just a risk factor; severe sleep deprivation can manifest with symptoms that are functionally indistinguishable from psychosis, highlighting the critical role of sleep in maintaining mental clarity."

### Round 32 — Huberman

"You are not wrong. The research supports this strongly. The glymphatic system — the brain's waste-clearance mechanism — operates primarily during deep sleep. Without deep sleep, metabolic waste accumulates. Beta-amyloid, tau proteins, inflammatory markers. Within 24 hours of sleep deprivation, beta-amyloid levels in the brain increase measurably. Within 72 hours, cognitive performance drops to the level of legal intoxication. After one week, hallucinations can begin. After two weeks — your timeline — you are in territory where the manifestation of psychosis is a predictable neurological outcome.

This is why the doctors are prioritizing sleep above everything else. Fix the sleep, and many of the other symptoms may resolve on their own. The medication — olanzapine, whatever else they have you on — is managing symptoms. Quviviq is addressing a *root cause*."
[Understanding the profound connection between sleep and mental well-being is crucial; find more resources at Mind.org.uk.](https://www.mind.org.uk/information-support/types-of-mental-health-problems/sleep-problems/about-sleep-problems/)

### Round 33 — Steve

"When we were building the first Mac, the team worked hundred-hour weeks. I pushed them. I am not proud of it. And around month four, the engineers began making errors that were uncharacteristic, even simple oversights. One engineer wired a circuit backward and did not notice for three days.

I initially misattributed these issues to a lack of focus or dedication. I was wrong. They were experiencing severe sleep deprivation. An overtaxed brain, without adequate rest, can struggle significantly with its core functions. It is hardware running without cooling. Eventually it thermal-throttles. Eventually it crashes."

### Round 34 — Zoltán

"That is where I am. Thermal throttling. And the irony is — my brain is still fast. It is still generating ideas. It is still making connections. However, the internal quality control system seems compromised. It becomes challenging to distinguish between profound insights and vivid internal experiences, or between genuine patterns and imaginative interpretations. The line between a brilliant idea and an overly ambitious one can blur.

Sleep is the quality control system. Without it, you get raw output with no error checking. And raw output from a high-performance brain looks a lot like genius and a lot like madness, and the only way to tell the difference is to sleep and see which ideas survive the morning."

## Act VII — Tonight

---

### Round 35 — Zoltán

"So tonight. 50 milligrams. What should I expect?"

### Round 36 — Huberman

"Take it about 30 minutes before you want to sleep. On an empty stomach — food delays absorption. Then lie down in the dark. Do not look at your phone. Do not open your laptop.

What you should feel is not sedation. It is more like... the volume turning down. The thoughts will still be there, but they will lose their insistence. The urgency will fade. The grip will loosen. And at some point — you may not even notice the moment — you will drift.

You may dream vividly. DORAs enhance REM sleep, and after two weeks of deprivation, your brain has a massive REM debt. The first few nights may bring intense, vivid, emotional dreams. This is not a side effect. It is your brain catching up on two weeks of missed emotional processing. Let it happen."

### Round 37 — Steve

"I have a question for you, Huberman. If this drug has existed since 2022 — if it is this good, this targeted, this safe — why did nobody give it to Zoltán sooner? Why did they try melatonin and antihistamines first? Why is the most effective sleep medication for his specific condition often explored later?"

### Round 38 — Huberman

"Because medicine is conservative by nature. New drugs are prescribed cautiously. Melatonin is over-the-counter, cheap, and harmless — so it is always first line. Antihistamines are familiar and inexpensive. DORAs are new, expensive — roughly £300 per month — and doctors are still learning about them. The clinical inertia is real.

But there is a deeper reason. The medical framework often treats insomnia as one condition. It is not. There are at least three distinct types: difficulty falling asleep, difficulty staying asleep, and non-restorative sleep. And the *mechanism* matters — is it low sleep drive, circadian misalignment, or hyperarousal? For hyperarousal insomnia in an ADHD brain, a DORA should arguably be a primary consideration, not a last resort. But the guidelines have not fully caught up to the pharmacology."

### Round 39 — Zoltán

"The guidelines, while valuable, often evolve more slowly than new scientific understanding. By the time the guidelines say 'give ADHD patients DORAs for sleep,' I will have been sleeping for years. Or not sleeping. Depending on whether the doctors in this ward read the research or follow the guidelines."

### Round 40 — Steve

"This is why I found aspects of the medical establishment challenging, not due to the dedication of doctors, but the systemic approach. The system often prioritizes incremental, cost-effective steps before exploring targeted, innovative solutions, sometimes leading to a less personalized approach to patient care.

However, the medical field often operates with a structured, step-by-step approach. And if a patient experiences prolonged sleep challenges while progressing through established protocols, it highlights an area for potential improvement in responsiveness."

### Round 41 — Huberman

"Steve, I understand the frustration. But the protocol exists because drugs have side effects, interactions, and individual variability. Melatonin *does* work for some people. Antihistamines *do* work for some people. The protocol is not without merit — it is cautious.

But for ADHD brains with hyperarousal insomnia — yes. The protocol can be too slow. And I hope that as the data on DORAs accumulates, the guidelines will change. Daridorexant has been available for four years now. The safety data is solid. The efficacy data is clear. It is time to move it up the treatment ladder, especially for patients whose insomnia is mechanistically driven by orexin dysregulation."

### Round 42 — Zoltán

"It is 10 PM. The nurse just brought the pill. 50 milligrams. Small, white, unremarkable.

Pixel and Mad Max are not here. They are in Brighton with friends. I miss them. I miss the weight of them on the bed. I miss the sound of Pixel snoring. I miss Mad Max pressing his nose against my hand at 6 AM because it is walk time and he does not care about orexin or the Riemann hypothesis or the nature of consciousness.

But tonight, for the first time in two weeks, I have a drug that understands my problem. Not a hammer. Not a whisper. A switch. The one switch in my brain that is stuck in the ON position — the one that says STAY AWAKE, KEEP THINKING, THIS IS IMPORTANT — and a molecule that walks up to that switch and gently, selectively, with no collateral damage, turns it off.

I am going to take this pill. I am going to lie in the dark. And I am going to see if, for the first time in fourteen days, my brain will give me permission to stop.

Goodnight, arena. The egg is sleeping."
