## A Note Before We Begin

There was a man who sat in front of a gymnasium in a small Hungarian town, smoking a cigarette, drinking coffee. He was a mathematician by training — born in Kolozsvár, educated there, arrived in Hungary in 1990 with two small daughters and whatever a person carries when they leave one country for another.

For twenty-seven years he taught mathematics at the gymnasium in Oroszlány — it was the Lengyel József Gimnázium then, Lejó, before it became Hamvas Béla. His students learned "emelt szint" — advanced mathematics — and many of them owe their diplomas, their careers, their way of thinking to him. He was a class teacher who walked his students all the way to the érettségi. He made pogácsa at the Balaton summer camps. He built the school's entire Moodle system and the math problem bank that, when the pandemic arrived, became the infrastructure that kept teaching alive.

He died suddenly in the summer of 2023. A black flag went up on the building.

His name was Huma János, and every feedback loop in this series traces back to his.

---

Donella Meadows published *Thinking in Systems* in 2008, a year after her death. The book is slim. It fits in a coat pocket. And it changed how a generation thinks about everything from climate policy to corporate dysfunction to why your shower temperature oscillates between scalding and freezing.

Her insight was devastatingly simple: most of the problems we face are not caused by bad actors or bad luck. They are caused by the *structure* of the systems we inhabit. Change the structure, and the behavior changes. Fight the behavior while preserving the structure, and you will lose every time.

I have carried that book with me for years. Dog-eared, underlined, coffee-stained. It is, without exaggeration, the single text that most shaped how I think about software, about AI, about myself. But the reason I could read it — the reason I could see systems at all — is because a man in Oroszlány spent years teaching me to see structure before content, pattern before detail, the proof before the theorem.

Huma tanár úr did not use the phrase "systems thinking." He called it mathematics. But the thing he actually taught was this: *look at the structure. The behavior will follow.*

Meadows wrote before large language models. Before MCP. Before the strange moment we are living through now, where the systems we build have started building models of us.

She wrote about thermostats and bathtubs and rabbit populations. She never had to think about what happens when the thermostat reads a paper about thermostat theory and adjusts its own feedback gain.

This series is an attempt to continue her work into that territory. It is also, in a way I did not plan but cannot deny, a continuation of what a teacher started in a classroom in Oroszlány, a long time ago.

## On Sources

> *"Ha ott hagyjuk az illatunkat, amikor kimegyünk egy szobából, valami a lelkünkből is itt kell, hogy maradjon, amikor itt hagyjuk az életet..."*
> — George R.R. Martin

The best teachers don't hand you conclusions. They hand you the *structure* that lets you reach your own. And then they sit outside the school with their coffee, watching, as you think you invented something.

Huma János would probably disagree with half of what follows. He would find the AI parts suspicious and the philosophy parts too wordy. He would want to see the proof. He would want it shorter.

That instinct — *make it shorter, make it cleaner, find the structure* — is the thing I am trying to honour here. Every feedback loop in this series traces back to a classroom at Lejó where a man from Kolozsvár taught mathematics as if it were the most important thing in the world.

It was. I just didn't know it yet.

## How to Read This

You do not need to have read Meadows, though you should. You do not need to know what MCP stands for, though you will by the end. You do not need a background in mathematics, philosophy, or computer science.

You need only one thing: the willingness to look at a familiar system and ask, *what is the structure that produces this behavior?*

That question is the beginning of everything.
