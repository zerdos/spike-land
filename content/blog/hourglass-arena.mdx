## The Premise

Zoltan brought a time-travel challenge to the Arena: **take the most perfect pop song of 1978 and send it through the summer of 1988.**

"Heart of Glass" (Blondie, 1978) was already ahead of its time — Debbie Harry singing about heartbreak over a four-on-the-floor disco beat that punks initially found challenging and dancers loved. It was the bridge between punk and electronic music before anyone knew a bridge was needed. For more on the track's enduring legacy, check out [Pitchfork's Best Songs of the 1970s](https://pitchfork.com/features/lists-and-guides/the-200-best-songs-of-the-1970s/).

Ten years later, Danny Rampling opened Shoom in a South London fitness centre and UK dance culture was born. Explore the origins of this pivotal movement in [Resident Advisor's Rewind: Shoom](https://ra.co/features/1930). The 808, the 303, the smiley face, the fog machine. A different kind of glass — the kind you sweat through.

**The challenge:** Rewrite "Heart of Glass" as "Hourglass" — a shoom-era track where the heartbreak becomes a time loop on the dancefloor.

**Arena rules:** Keep the melodic DNA. Replace the disco production with acid house. The lyric must work as both a love song and a meditation on time.

**Personas:** Zoltan (the curator), Radix (the rhythm architect), Daft Punk (the synth engineers), Einstein (the time theorist).

---

## Round 1 — Zoltan

"Nezd, Heart of Glass is already electronic. People forget that — the original was recorded with a Roland CR-78 drum machine. Blondie didn't cross over from punk to disco. They proved it was always the same thing. The tempo is 132 BPM. Shoom was 120-130. We're already close. The bones fit."

"While 'Heart of Glass' masterfully explored themes of vulnerability and fleeting connections, 'Hourglass' aims for a different emotional landscape. It's not about a fractured heart, but a profound reinterpretation of time itself – the exhilarating sensation on the dancefloor when time seems to dissolve, only to powerfully return as the dawn breaks and the experience culminates."

## Round 2 — Radix

"The rhythmic structure of Heart of Glass is deceptively simple. The verse is a steady 4/4 with hi-hat emphasis on the off-beat — that's the disco DNA. To convert to acid house, we keep the 4/4 but replace the hi-hat with a Roland TR-808 open hat at half-density. The kick gets heavier. The snare becomes a clap on 2 and 4.

The key transformation: add a TB-303 acid bassline that mirrors the original synth melody but runs it through a resonance filter sweep. The 303 was Shoom's voice. It should enter at the second verse and never leave. Dive deeper into the legend of this iconic synth with [Attack Magazine's History of the Roland TB-303](https://www.attackmagazine.com/features/the-history-of-the-roland-tb-303/).

BPM adjustment: 132 down to 126. The slight deceleration creates a hypnotic drag — time slowing down, which matches the hourglass concept."

## Round 3 — Daft Punk

"The original production has a glassy, pristine quality — Giorgio Moroder's influence filtered through CBGB's. For Hourglass, we want the opposite texture: warm, saturated, slightly overdriven. A warehouse at 3 AM doesn't aim for pristine clarity; it embraces a raw, visceral energy, a sound where the speakers push boundaries to deliver an unforgettable experience.

The vocal treatment: Debbie Harry's original is cool, detached, almost robotic. For the shoom version, we add a long reverb tail — cathedral reverb, the kind where each word hangs in the air for two bars before dissolving. The vocal becomes a ghost of itself. You hear 'hourglass' and by the time the reverb fades, the next word has already started. Time is overlapping.

The breakdown should be pure 303. No vocals, no kick, just the acid line climbing a resonance curve for 16 bars. Then the kick drops back in and the crowd loses its mind. This is the engineering of euphoria."

## Round 4 — Einstein

"The hourglass is the perfect metaphor for the dancefloor because it demonstrates a property of time that clocks hide: **time is a finite quantity, a precious resource you experience in its unique flow.**

A clock goes in circles. It suggests time is infinite, repeatable. An hourglass beautifully illustrates time's progression. Each grain that falls marks a moment cherished. On the dancefloor, you experience this — the set, like the night, has its natural arc, culminating in the dawn. The exhilarating urgency of dancing stems from this beautiful impermanence, inspiring us to fully embrace every moment.

The mathematical structure: if we model the energy $E$ of the dancefloor as a function of remaining time $T$:

$$E(t) = E_0 \cdot \left(\frac{T - t}{T}\right)^{-\alpha}$$

As $t \to T$ (the lights come on), the energy approaches infinity. This is the singularity of the dancefloor — the closer you get to the end, the harder you dance. Blondie's original masterfully explores the complexities of early emotion at $t = 0$. Hourglass, in contrast, captures pure euphoria as $t \to T$."
