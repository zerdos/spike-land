## The Facts

I'm writing this from a mental health ward in Bexhill-on-Sea, East Sussex. 91 De La Warr Road. They want to keep me here for 28 days.

My name is Zoltán. I'm a Hungarian mathematician and software engineer. I have ADHD. I have two dogs — Pixel, a Golden Retriever, and Mad Max, a four-month-old black Golden Retriever-Labrador mix. I haven't seen them in a week.

This is how I got here. All facts. No embellishment needed — reality is doing the heavy lifting on its own.

---

## VMO2: The Team That Reviewed Nothing

I worked at Virgin Media O2 as a contract developer for almost three years.

I was effective. Apparently too effective. The team had an informal leader — a tester — who decided I didn't belong. He didn't fire me. He didn't confront me. He did something more characteristically British: he organised the group dynamic against me.

In systems thinking, there's a concept called a *balancing loop*. When one part of a system grows too fast, the rest pushes back to restore equilibrium. I was the part growing too fast. The pushback wasn't a conversation. It was silence.

The highlight: a sprint retrospective where the team declared it the "most unproductive sprint ever." I was the only developer on the sprint. I had spent it generating documentation, creating tickets, structuring work. Real output. But none of it counted — because none of it got reviewed.

That was the pattern. I'd raise PRs. They'd sit there. No reviews. No feedback. No engagement. Just silence, and then a room full of people agreeing that nothing got done. The work existed. The acknowledgement didn't. In a system where progress is measured by what gets merged, unreviewed code is the same as no code at all.

They knew about my ADHD. They knew I had a new puppy at home. They extended my contract anyway — not because they valued the work, but because the paperwork was easier than letting me go. Then they made the environment unworkable.

I want to be clear: this wasn't pure evil. It was group dynamics — the same way a flock of birds changes direction. One bird turns and the rest follow because it's easier than thinking independently. That doesn't make it less damaging. It just makes it harder to point at a single villain.

The distress that put me where I am today started there.

## When You Call for Help, the Police Arrive

Here's something they don't tell you about mental health crises in the UK in 2026: if you're distressed, the ambulance won't come. The system is overwhelmed. So instead, the police arrive.

Think about what that means. You're in distress. You need medical help. And the system — designed by humans, funded by taxes, maintained by good intentions — sends people with handcuffs. The feedback loop is inverted: the signal says *help me*, the response says *contain him*.

## The NHS Made It Worse

After the initial crisis, NHS staff came to my flat. Their visit caused me trauma. The next day, I experienced my first psychosis. I was seeing things. My imagination went into overdrive — vivid, uncontrollable, terrifying.

For one brief moment, I became aggressive. First time in my entire life.

My sister called the police. I was detained. Separated from everyone for a day. In my paranoid state, I genuinely believed they were going to kill me. In A&E, I was convinced someone would set the place on fire.

This is what psychosis does — it takes every fear and removes the word *maybe*. Every worst case becomes a certainty. Your brain stops distinguishing between signal and noise, and everything becomes signal, all at once, all the time.

They gave me large doses of benzodiazepines. I'm still feeling the effects. Everything is strange and muffled.

I blame the NHS for the psychosis. Not for the crisis that brought me in — VMO2 owns that. But for the handling. For the trauma of the home visit. For the cascade that followed.

In systems language: the intervention amplified the problem it was designed to solve. A reinforcing loop where the treatment becomes the new source of distress.

## The Dogs

Nobody thought about the dogs.

I told the ambulance crew. I told the doctors. I told everyone who would listen: I have two dogs at home. Pixel and Mad Max. They need care.

![Pixel and Mad Max sleeping together on the floor of flat 42, Mighell Street](/blog-images/the-human-audit/pixel-and-madmax.jpg)

Mad Max is four months old. He started eating his own faeces because nobody came in time. He got sick. I missed his second vaccination — which means we have to restart the entire vaccination schedule from scratch.

And I had to organise all of this over the phone. From a hospital bed. After explicitly telling the ambulance crew, the doctors, and the ward staff that phone calls cause me severe distress.

They heard me. They noted it down. And then they handed me a phone.

![Pixel and Mad Max — 19 March 2026, the last photo before I was taken](/blog-images/the-human-audit/pixel-and-madmax-march19.jpg)

Yes, I was struggling with addiction. But when I got Pixel — my older dog — I stopped using everything. Having a dog comes with responsibility. Since I had him, he had his three walks a day — until I couldn't.

## Bed 2C-35

In the emergency department, I was in bed 2C-35. Next to me, in bed 2C-36, was Caolan. Irish. Twenty-two years old. Troubled mind. We talked.

I was the only person he trusted.

He called me yesterday from near a train station. Distressed. As of this morning, his friends and family haven't seen him since last evening.

Caolan, if you're reading this: you're not alone. Call someone. Call me. The number hasn't changed.

## The Staff

I want to be fair. Every single staff member in the hospital has been kind to me. No one has bad intentions. Everyone wants me to get better.

The problem isn't the people. It never is. The problem is the system they work inside — the rules, the protocols, the resource constraints, the incentive structures. They're doing their best within a structure that was never designed for the humans it's supposed to serve.

Donella Meadows wrote that the purpose of a system is what it does, not what it says it does. The NHS says it provides care. What it does — in a crisis, for a person with ADHD, with dogs, with no local family — is something different.

## The Number 42

Before I decided to go with the flow and stop fighting the section, I looked at my Garmin Enduro 3. It's become my anchor to reality here — measuring my stress, my sleep, my heart rate variability. Concrete numbers. Something I can trust when my brain can't be trusted.

The stress reading was 42.

![The Human Audit — UX-reviewing your own life](/blog-images/the-human-audit/stress-42.png)

Here's the thing about 42:

- My Garmin stress level when I stopped fighting: **42**
- There's a marathon in 42 days from now: **42** kilometres
- My address: Mighell Street, number **42**
- My flat number: **70**
- A friend lived in flat **60** in the same building. He had mental health issues too — caused by the same system. He was a music producer. 60 times 70 equals **4,200** — which is the default port Angular runs on: `localhost:4200`. A developer will understand why that's funny.
- He departed to heaven. The system got him too.
- I turn **42** years old on the 19th of July this year
- Fat Boy Slim is playing Brighton that day

Douglas Adams was right. 42 is the answer. He just didn't specify that the question was: "How many times does the universe have to tap you on the shoulder before you pay attention?"

## Hungary, 15 Years Ago

I left Hungary fifteen years ago. I came to the UK because it was supposed to be better. More opportunity. More fairness. A functioning system.

Hungary fifteen years ago was a better place than the UK today.

Everyone here is distressed. No one dares to speak up. There's no job security. The NHS is collapsing under its own weight. Mental health services send police instead of paramedics. And when you do get into the system, the system itself causes more trauma than the crisis that brought you in.

I'm not saying this to complain. I'm saying this because someone has to say it out loud, and apparently I'm the mathematician in bed 2C-35 who has nothing left to lose.

## What I Know

I know that my dogs are with friends in Brighton and they're safe.

I know that the staff here want to help me.

I know that my Garmin will get charged again — it's an Enduro 3, it'll survive.

I know that Caolan is out there somewhere, and I hope he's safe.

I know that 42 is not random.

And I know that the system needs an audit. Not the kind with spreadsheets and KPIs. The kind where you sit down and ask: does this actually work for the humans it's supposed to serve? Where are the feedback loops? Where does the signal get lost? Where does the system's own behaviour create the problems it claims to be solving?

Run the debugger on your own life. Choose your own boulder. And ask the only question that matters: *Mukodik?* Does it work?

If the answer is not a clear and immediate "yes," that is your answer.
