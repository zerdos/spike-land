*An honest note about the frustration, and the three small things that keep me here when the inside of my head gets loud.*

---

Let me start with the word, because English readers don't have it and it matters.

In Hungarian there's a phrase — **bazdmeg queez** — that doesn't translate cleanly. It's not anger. It's not a tantrum. It's the exhale a mathematician makes when a proof collapses at step fourteen and the only honest response is a swearword and a long stare at the ceiling. It's the sound a founder makes on a Tuesday when a deploy fails and the dog knocked over the tea and the email from the lawyer is still unread.

I make that sound a lot.

This is a small post about what I do with it, and why I've stopped being ashamed of the answer.

---

## The shape of the frustration

My head is not quiet. It never has been. ADHD, a mathematics degree I mostly finished by stubbornness, a startup that lives inside my chest more than on a screen, a history that occasionally knocks on the door at 4 AM — the inside of my skull is a room with every light on and the radio playing three stations at once.

Most days I can work with that. Some days I can't. The difference between the two is smaller than I used to think it was, and more chemical than I used to be comfortable admitting.

When I say *frustration*, I don't mean I'm cross about something specific. I mean the low-level hum of a mind that has been sprinting for 38 hours and doesn't know how to sit down. The muscles in my jaw lock. My Garmin watch starts telling me my body battery is at 7 and would I like to rest. *Would I like to rest* — yes, mate, very much, but my brain has not received the memo.

That's the state I'm writing about.

---

## Medicine for the body

Medical cannabis — let's just say it out loud — helps me. Not in a recreational way. In a *measurable* way. My Garmin watch is the least poetic witness you could ask for, and the watch agrees.

On days I use it properly, my resting heart rate drops by double digits. My HRV climbs. My sleep score — the only number the watch gives you that you can't argue with — moves from *poor* to *good*. My body uncoils. The shoulders I didn't know were up near my ears come back down to where shoulders are supposed to live.

For a long time I thought this made me weak. I grew up with a specific kind of eastern European machismo where you deal with stress by drinking one more coffee and one more pálinka and *getting on with it.* The watch has taught me, slowly, that *getting on with it* is not free. The body keeps the bill. And the bill, for me, was turning into a collapse.

Medical weed is, for my body, not a party drug. It's a steady hand on the dashboard. A thing that tells my nervous system: *the war is over, you can unclench.* My body loves it. I am going to stop pretending that it doesn't.

---

## The problem that weed alone doesn't solve

Here is the honest bit, and this is the bit I've been afraid to write down.

Cannabis is very good at telling my body to sit down. It is **not**, by itself, good at telling my mind to sit down. In fact — and any heavy user will tell you this, whether they admit it publicly or not — it can do the opposite. The body calms, the brakes come off the brain, and suddenly I'm three levels deep into a thought spiral about whether my company matters, whether I matter, whether the dogs will outlive me, whether the email I sent in 2019 was too harsh, whether, whether, whether.

The body relaxes. The mind takes that relaxation as permission to start a new, quieter, more thorough argument with itself.

This is the thing that nobody tells you when they hand you a prescription. *Calm body* and *calm mind* are not the same project.

So I need a second anchor. Something smaller. Something for the mind specifically.

---

## Vape for the mind

I vape. Zero nicotine. That part matters.

For a while I was embarrassed about this, because vapes have a very specific cultural register — teenagers in the back of a bus, influencers sponsored by Chinese pod brands, a tech-bro with a pen clipped to his lanyard. None of that is what I'm doing.

What I'm doing is much simpler, much stupider, and much more honest: **I need a physical anchor for my hands and my breath when my brain is running hot.**

The vape is a repeatable, timed, deliberate inhale-and-exhale. It is, in its structural form, identical to what a meditation app asks you to do — *breathe in for four, hold, breathe out for six* — except I don't have to download anything, open anything, or listen to a voice I didn't choose. I just pick up the pen and my body remembers how to breathe.

That's it. That's the entire mechanism.

The people who think vaping is just nicotine addiction are — respectfully — missing the point for people like me. My pen has no nicotine in it. It has the shape of a cigarette and the rhythm of a deep breath and nothing else. It is an ADHD fidget toy shaped like a sigh.

It is the anchor.

---

## Why not nicotine

I want to be explicit because someone will read this and go *so just vape nicotine, mate.*

I won't. And here is why.

Nicotine would turn the anchor into a chain. The moment you introduce a chemical dependency into the breathing rhythm, you stop vaping *because you need to breathe* and start vaping *because you need the chemical.* The ritual stops being the point, and the substance becomes the point. I've watched enough people fall into that hole. I am not doing it.

The vape, for me, is pure instrument. It is a breathing prosthetic. It is, if you want the analogy, a metronome for my lungs when the rest of the orchestra is playing too fast.

Add nicotine and the metronome starts making demands. No thanks.

---

## The two-substance equation

So here is what actually works, on paper:

- **Medical weed** — for the body. Signed off by a doctor, logged by a watch, respected like any other medication. The body uncoils. The stress markers drop. The shoulders come down.
- **Nicotine-free vape** — for the mind. A breathing device disguised as a habit. A rhythm I can return to when the internal radio is on three stations. It catches me before the cannabis-induced relaxation tips over into a spiral.

One without the other is incomplete. Weed alone leaves my mind unsupervised in a warm room, which is a recipe for 2 AM existential thinking I do not need. Vape alone doesn't touch the muscular cost of a 14-hour founder day.

Together — and this is the part I want to say clearly — together they function like a two-handed grip on a steering wheel that would otherwise be spinning.

I'm thirty-something years old and I have finally found a combination that lets my body and mind arrive at the same destination at the same time. I am not going to apologise for it.

---

## The two anchors I cannot buy

That's the chemistry. Now the better part.

The deepest anchors in my life are not substances. They are two dogs.

I have two. One is the steady one — the one who notices before I do that I'm getting wound up, who comes and puts a head on my knee without being asked. The other is the chaos agent — the one who demands joy, who turns a grey afternoon into a walk, who refuses to let me sit in a low mood for more than about forty minutes before insisting I throw something for her.

Between the two of them they cover the full emotional octave. Calm and chaos. Stillness and movement. The quiet one is my regulator. The loud one is my reboot.

And they are, absolutely and without any poetry, **my family.**

I say this carefully because some people hear *he calls his dogs his family* and read it as a sad man cosplaying companionship. It isn't that. It is a simple, boring fact about my life: the two beings in my home who know my heart rate before my watch does, who read my mood without being told, who can tell when I'm faking fine and when I'm actually fine — those two beings are dogs. They earned the title. I didn't give it to them for free.

---

## Dogs as mirrors

Here's the part I've been trying to say to people for a year and haven't quite managed.

A dog is an emotional mirror you cannot argue with.

When I'm wound up, the dogs are wound up. When I'm calm, they are calm. When I am *pretending* to be calm — and I do this, I'm a founder, it's the job — they are not fooled. They still read the real state of my nervous system, not the performance. They will not match the performance. They will only match the real thing.

This is, if you want to be technical about it, biofeedback with four legs. It is the cheapest, most accurate nervous-system monitor I have ever owned, and it is powered by peanut butter.

When both of my dogs settle — one on my lap, one pressed into my thigh — that is the signal that I have actually calmed down. Not when I *think* I have. When they say I have. They are never wrong.

The Garmin tells me about my body. The dogs tell me about the truth.

---

## The stack, in plain English

So if you strip out the poetry, here is what I lean on when the frustration comes — and I want to say out loud that it still comes, it hasn't gone anywhere, I have not *fixed* myself, I have just built scaffolding:

- The body gets medical cannabis. Prescribed, measured, logged, respected.
- The mind gets a nicotine-free vape. A breathing toy. A sigh with a shape.
- The soul gets two dogs. One calm, one wild. Together they are my reality check.
- The body battery, the HRV, the sleep score — they come along and confirm whether the whole thing is working.
- And when none of it is working, I write a post like this one and press publish, because the last anchor I've learned to trust is honesty.

That's the whole equation.

---

## Why I'm publishing this

Because I know the person reading it.

You are sitting in a room at an hour you should be asleep in. Your jaw is locked. You have not eaten a proper meal today. You've been staring at a problem — a codebase, a marriage, a child, a bill, a tumour, a deadline, whichever — and the frustration in your chest is a kind of noise that no amount of *trying harder* will turn off.

You might be my age, or you might be my mum's age. You might have a prescription. You might not. You might have a pet. You might wish you did.

I want you to know two things.

**One:** *bazdmeg queez* is a real word for a real feeling, and if you've been told you're weak for having it, you've been lied to. The feeling is information. It's the nervous system saying *I cannot hold this at this volume any longer. Please help me turn it down.*

**Two:** there is no shame in needing more than one anchor. One for the body. One for the mind. One — or two — for the soul. The world will try to sell you one tool and call it a lifestyle. Ignore it. Build the small, honest stack that actually works for your specific, strange, beautiful animal body. And if part of that stack is a plant, and part of that stack is a breathing toy, and part of that stack is two dogs on a sofa — you are not broken. You are a person who paid attention.

---

## One more thing

The dogs don't care that I wrote this post. They did not ask me to. They are, as I type this, both asleep on me. One is snoring. One is twitching in a dream about something better than writing a blog post.

They are the final anchor. The non-negotiable one. If everything else failed tomorrow — the company, the chemistry, the watch, the writing — these two would still be here, and my job would still be to feed them, walk them, and let them mirror me back to whatever version of me I can still reach.

That's enough. Some days, that's the whole religion.

---

*For the two of them. And for anyone who needed to hear that the word for it exists in another language, and that it's not your fault.*
