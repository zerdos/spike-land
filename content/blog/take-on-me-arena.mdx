## The Premise

Zoltán brought a range-based challenge to the Arena: **The High Note**.

"Take On Me" (a-ha, 1985) is defined by its chorus. The shift from the low-energy verse to the soaring "In a day or two" is more than a melody; it’s a phase transition. The goal was to build a tool that visualizes this transition as a climb up a frequency ladder.

**Arena rules:** High-precision pitch tracking for the 0.95 range. Integrated synth-pop neon palette. 165 BPM synchronization.

**Personas:** Zoltán (the curator), Radix (the signal architect), Daft Punk (the synth-wave designers), Einstein (the researcher of frequency oscillation).

---

## Round 1 — Zoltán

"Nézd, when this song plays, the energy in the room shifts. I want the interface to capture that 'rotoscoped' feeling. It shouldn't feel like a modern flat app; it should feel like you're stepping into the 1985 music video. Neon pink, purple, and a hint of a sketch-like glow."

## Round 2 — Radix

"Morten Harket’s range is incredible. We’ve mapped the **Pitch Bar** to highlight the chorus. The verse sits comfortably at $0.4 - 0.55$, but the 'Take on me' line spikes to $0.95$. We need the interface to 'bloom' when those high notes are reached, using the `active-glow` variable to reward precise vocal placement."

## Round 3 — Daft Punk

"The synths are relentless. We added an **Ambient Glow** engine with two orbs: 'Electric Pink' and 'Cyber Purple'. They pulse with the 165 BPM master beat. This provides a tactile rhythm for the singer, essentially functioning as a visual metronome for the rapid-fire syllables."

## Round 4 — Einstein

"The physics of the high note involves high localized air pressure and vocal cord oscillation. By adding **Theta** and **Delta** binaural beats, we allow the user to focus on the technical execution of the melody while the brain stays in a 'sleep-friendly' alpha/theta transition state. It’s performance training for the subconscious."

---

## The Range Formula

The Arena modeled the vocal 'Lift' $L$ as a function of the pitch $P$ and the frequency of the beat $f_b$:

$$L(t) = \frac{\Delta P}{\Delta t} \cdot \sin(2\pi f_b t)$$

As the pitch derivative $\Delta P / \Delta t$ increases during the chorus, the interface's opacity and sweep animations accelerate, matching the exhilaration of the climb.
