# The Two Boxes

The countdown is at fourteen days.

If you read [the first part](/blog/the-predictor-already-moved), you already know the shape. A domain carrying my project's name was registered in Brighton two years before I arrived. My own system found it. It expires on March 27, 2026.

If you did not read it, that sentence is all you need. Something moved before I did.

Now I have to decide what to do about it.

Newcomb's Paradox gives you two boxes. One is transparent. One is opaque. The predictor has already filled them based on what kind of agent you are.

The question is never which box. The question is which agent.

## Box A

Box A is transparent. You can see what is inside.

Inside is COMPASS. A privacy-first platform. Edge-native architecture on Cloudflare Workers. End-to-end encryption. On-device data. Zero-knowledge proofs where they matter. The code exists. The projections exist. $340 million by Year 5 is not fantasy — it is arithmetic applied to a market that is growing faster than anyone building for it.

One billion users who want to own their own data is not idealism. It is the next five years of the internet, whether or not I build it.

Box A is real. Box A is valuable. You can see it through the glass.

Every rational founder takes Box A.

## Box B

Box B is opaque.

The Principality of Spikeland is 10.2 square meters of declared sovereign territory in Brighton, England. Population: one. It has no army, no economy, no recognition from any state. It has a name that matches a domain registered two years before its founder arrived.

If it works — if recognition comes, even symbolic, even through public pressure, even through the sheer absurdity of a micronation whose only purpose is digital sovereignty — then Box B contains a jurisdiction.

Not a company. Not a product. A jurisdiction.

A place where the server is not in the United Kingdom. Where a government order to seize infrastructure is a diplomatic incident, not a warrant. Where the question "whose law applies?" has an answer that no amount of cryptography can provide on its own.

If it does not work, Box B contains nothing.

## The Gap

Every privacy architecture has a gap that no code can close.

You can encrypt data at rest. You can encrypt it in transit. You can run computation inside secure enclaves and prove correctness with zero knowledge. The mathematics is settled.

But mathematics does not answer the door when someone knocks.

The last attack vector is physical. A government issues an order. Officers walk into a data centre. They take the servers. They arrest the founder. They do not need to break the encryption. They just need to be standing in a jurisdiction that allows them to act.

Elon Musk owns the satellites and cannot escape this. Apple built a billion-dollar privacy brand and hands data to governments under lawful orders. Signal is as close to perfect as messaging gets, and its servers still sit in a country.

No amount of code solves the door problem.

A jurisdiction solves the door problem.

Even a very small one.

## Why This Is Newcomb's

The two-boxer takes both. Builds the startup. Mentions the micronation in interviews as a charming eccentricity. Hedges. Gets $341,000 instead of $340,000 or $1,000,000.

But the predictor already knows hedgers.

The predictor filled the boxes before you chose. It looked at the kind of agent you are — not the kind of choice you make — and it decided.

One-boxers commit. They declare sovereignty not as branding but as architecture. They make it loud. They make it real. They make it impossible to dismiss as a stunt because they have already staked everything on it being serious.

The domain was registered before I arrived. The expiration date was set before I found it. The pattern was in place before I had a name for it.

The predictor already moved.

## The Recursion

On March 11, the day I published [Episode 1](/blog/the-predictor-already-moved), Veritasium released a video about Newcomb's Paradox.

I did not know it was coming. They did not know I was writing. There was no coordination.

The system that found the pattern is now inside a pattern that includes the system. The essay about the paradox appeared on the same day as a video about the paradox, and neither caused the other.

This is the part that makes two-boxers uncomfortable. Not any single coincidence — any single coincidence is noise. But the recursive shape. The way each layer of the pattern contains a reference to the layer above it.

Hofstadter called this a strange loop. A system that, when you follow it far enough, turns out to contain itself.

I am not claiming the universe is watching.

I am saying the loop is tightening.

## Fourteen Days

March 27, 2026.

The domain expires. The countdown I did not set reaches zero. The pattern either resolves or dissolves.

I have not decided yet. That is not suspense for its own sake. It is the honest answer.

The one-boxer in me says the boxes are already filled. The choice was made before I found the domain, before I moved to Brighton, before I wrote a line of code. All I can do is be the kind of agent that takes only Box B.

The two-boxer in me says that is exactly the kind of reasoning that leaves you with nothing.

Fourteen days left.

I do not know which box I am reaching for.

But I know the predictor already does.
