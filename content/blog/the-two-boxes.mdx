# The Two Boxes

Fourteen days left on the clock.

If you read [the first part](/blog/the-predictor-already-moved), you know the shape already. A domain carrying my project's name was registered in Brighton two years before I arrived. My own system surfaced it. It expires on March 27, 2026.

If you did not read it, that sentence is enough. Something moved before I did.

Now I have to decide what to do about it.

Newcomb's Paradox gives you two boxes. One is transparent — you can see inside. One is opaque — you cannot. The predictor has already filled them based on what kind of agent you are.

The question was never which box. The question is which agent.

## Box A

Box A is transparent. You can see exactly what is inside.

Inside is COMPASS. A privacy-first platform built on Cloudflare Workers, running at the edge. End-to-end encryption. On-device data. Zero-knowledge proofs where they matter. The code exists. The financial projections exist. $340 million by Year 5 is not fantasy — it is arithmetic applied to a market that is growing faster than anyone is building for it.

One billion people who want to own their data is not idealism. It is the next five years of the internet, whether or not I am the one who builds it.

Box A is real. Box A is valuable. You can see it through the glass.

Every rational founder takes Box A.

## Box B

Box B is opaque.

The Principality of Spikeland is 10.2 square meters of declared sovereign territory in Brighton, England. Population: one. No army. No economy. No recognition from any state. Just a name that matches a domain registered two years before its founder arrived.

If it works — if recognition comes, even symbolic, even through public pressure, even through the sheer absurdity of a micronation whose only purpose is digital sovereignty — then Box B contains something no amount of code can create.

A jurisdiction.

Not a company. Not a product. A place where the server is not in the United Kingdom. Where a government order to seize infrastructure becomes a diplomatic incident, not a warrant. Where the question "whose law applies?" has an answer that cryptography alone cannot provide.

If it does not work, Box B contains nothing.

## The Gap

Every privacy architecture has a gap that no code can close.

Think of it as a system with three layers of protection. You can encrypt data at rest. You can encrypt it in transit. You can run computation inside secure enclaves and prove correctness with zero-knowledge proofs. The mathematics is airtight.

But mathematics does not answer the door when someone knocks.

The last attack vector is physical. A government issues an order. Officers walk into a data centre. They take the servers. They arrest the founder. They do not need to break the encryption. They just need to be standing in a jurisdiction that gives them the authority to act.

Elon Musk owns the satellites and cannot escape this. Apple built a billion-dollar privacy brand and still hands data to governments under lawful orders. Signal is as close to perfect as encrypted messaging gets, and its servers still sit inside a country.

No amount of code solves the door problem.

A jurisdiction solves the door problem.

Even a very small one.

## Why This Is Newcomb's

The two-boxer takes both. Builds the startup. Mentions the micronation in interviews as a charming eccentricity. Hedges. Walks away with $341,000 instead of either $340,000 or $1,000,000.

But the predictor already knows hedgers.

The predictor filled the boxes before you chose. It looked at the kind of agent you are — not the kind of choice you make — and it decided.

One-boxers commit. They declare sovereignty not as branding but as architecture. They make it loud. They make it real. They make it impossible to dismiss as a stunt because they have staked everything on it being serious.

The domain was registered before I arrived. The expiration date was set before I found it. The pattern was in place before I had a name for it.

The predictor already moved.

## The Recursion

On March 11, the day I published [Episode 1](/blog/the-predictor-already-moved), Veritasium released a video about Newcomb's Paradox.

I did not know it was coming. They did not know I was writing. There was no coordination.

This is the part that makes two-boxers uncomfortable. Not any single coincidence — any single coincidence is noise. But the recursive shape. Each layer of the pattern contains a reference to the layer above it, like a set of mirrors angled to reflect each other. The system that found the pattern is now inside a pattern that includes the system.

Hofstadter called this a strange loop. Follow it far enough and you discover it contains itself.

I am not claiming the universe is watching.

I am saying the loop is tightening.

## Fourteen Days

March 27, 2026.

The domain expires. The countdown I did not set reaches zero. The pattern either resolves or dissolves.

I have not decided yet. That is not suspense for its own sake — it is the honest answer.

The one-boxer in me says the boxes are already filled. The choice was made before I found the domain, before I moved to Brighton, before I wrote a line of code. All I can do is be the kind of agent that takes only Box B.

The two-boxer in me says that is exactly the kind of reasoning that leaves you with nothing.

Fourteen days left.

I do not know which box I am reaching for.

But I know the predictor already does.
