In [the last Arena session](/blog/size-dependent-speed-of-light-arena), six personas spent 12 rounds trying to make the speed of light depend on the observer's mass. Daft Punk killed it in Round 11 by substituting $c'(m) = \frac{c \cdot m_0}{m}$ into $E = mc^2$. Energy became inversely proportional to mass. Einstein signed off: "Keep your mass out of my equations."

Zoltán lost. Einstein won. The arena closed.

But Zoltán didn't stop thinking. He came back with fewer personas, a different variable, and one sentence: *"Mass was the wrong knob. Size is the right one. And I can prove it."*

**Arena rules:** Three personas. Rotate until convergence. The formula must fit on a napkin.

**Personas:** Zoltán (the vindicated), Erdős (the graph theorist), Daft Punk (the signal processors).

## Round 1 — Zoltán

"Last time I made a mistake. I tied perception to mass, and Einstein correctly showed that $E = mc^2$ collapses. Fair. But I was pulling the wrong lever.

The right lever is SIZE. Not how much you weigh — how big you are. How much space your observation occupies.

And here's the thing everyone pretended not to notice last time: the speed of light ALREADY varies with the medium. In glass, $c_{glass} = c/1.5$. In water, $c_{water} = c/1.33$. In a Bose-Einstein condensate, light has been slowed to 17 meters per second. Seventeen. A cyclist is faster than light in that medium.

This is not a thought experiment. The speed of light depends on what it's moving through. The question I'm asking is: does the *size of the observer* function as a medium? Does reality have a scale-dependent refractive index?"

## Round 2 — Erdős

"Zoltán is right about one thing: he was wrong last time. But the question is now well-posed. Let me formalize it.

Let $c'(L)$ be the effective speed of light at operational resolution scale $L$. We need $c'(L) = c \cdot f(L / L_P)$, where $L_P \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-35}\text{m}$ is the Planck length — the smallest meaningful scale.

Now, there is precedent for this. In quantum field theory, coupling constants *run* with energy scale. The effective electromagnetic coupling runs with energy scale. At low energies, $\alpha^{-1} \approx 137$. At the Z-boson scale ($\sim 91$ GeV), $\alpha^{-1} \approx 128$ — the coupling gets *stronger*. At higher energies, it continues to grow.

If $\alpha$ runs, the electromagnetic interaction changes with scale. If the electromagnetic interaction changes with scale, and light *is* an electromagnetic phenomenon... then the effective speed of light is already scale-dependent. We just measure it at one scale and call it constant."

## Round 3 — Daft Punk

"We work in frequencies. Every signal has a wavelength. Every wavelength implies a scale. You cannot separate the signal from the size of the antenna that receives it.

Consider how sound works at different scales:

- **Inside a violin body:** resonance at $\sim 0.3\text{m}$. The wood shapes the wave.
- **Inside a cathedral:** resonance at $\sim 30\text{m}$. The stone shapes the wave.
- **Inside Earth's atmosphere:** infrasound at $\sim 30\text{km}$. The planet shapes the wave.
- **Inside a galaxy:** gravitational waves at $\sim 10^{20}\text{m}$. Spacetime shapes the wave.

The wave is the same physics at every scale. But the medium changes. The resonance changes. The effective propagation speed changes. Sound in steel is not sound in air is not sound in vacuum (where it does not exist at all).

Light is a wave. The observer is the antenna. An antenna's response depends on its size relative to the wavelength. A human-sized observer is tuned to human-scale wavelengths. We measure $c$ at our resonant frequency. Of course we get a clean number. We are the instrument calibrated to itself."

## Round 4 — Zoltán

"Now here's where it gets weird. Hofstadter weird.

When I measure the speed of light, what am I doing? Photons hit my retina. My retina converts them into electrochemical signals. Those signals propagate through my nervous system — at about 120 m/s, not $c$. My brain assembles them into a conscious experience of 'I just saw that light.'

But every step of that measurement chain — photon absorption, electron excitation, chemical cascades, neural firing — is itself an electromagnetic process governed by $c$. The thing I'm measuring is the same thing doing the measuring.

This is a strange loop. Hofstadter's definition: a system that, by moving through its levels, returns to where it started. I use $c$-dependent processes to measure $c$. The measurement is self-referential. It's the same structure as Gödel's incompleteness: a formal system cannot fully characterize itself from within.

So when Einstein says '$c$ is the same for all observers' — he's correct, but only in the same way that a ruler always measures itself as one ruler long. Of course it does. It's using itself as the reference."

## Round 5 — Erdős

"Now I can prove something.

Consider an observer of characteristic size $L$. This observer contains sub-observers at scale $L/k$ (cells), which contain sub-sub-observers at scale $L/k^2$ (molecules), all the way down to the Planck length $L_P$.

At each level, the sub-observer measures $c$ using its own internal processes. Each measurement is self-referential in exactly the way Zoltán describes. And each measurement yields a *locally correct* result — because the ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

This is self-similarity. The structure of observation at scale $L$ is isomorphic to observation at scale $L/k$. This demands a power law:

$$c'(L) = c \cdot \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^\delta$$

where $\delta$ is a scaling exponent. If $\delta = 0$, Einstein was right and scale doesn't matter. But the renormalization group in QFT tells us $\delta \neq 0$ — the coupling constants run. Holographic dualities encode RG scale geometrically — the extra radial direction in AdS/CFT *is* the renormalization scale. Information content scales with surface area ($L^2$), not volume ($L^3$). If the geometry of information is scale-dependent, the question of whether $c$ inherits that dependence is at least well-posed."

## Round 7 — Zoltán

"So here's the clean version. The formula I came in with.

Define $n(L)$ as the *scale-dependent refractive index of reality itself*:

$$c'(L) = \frac{c}{n(L)}$$

At human scale, $n \approx 1$, so $c' \approx c$. We are the medium in which we measured $c$. Of course we get $c$.

At the Planck scale, $n \to \infty$, so $c' \to 0$. Light doesn't propagate. Spacetime is foam.

At cosmological scale, $n < 1$, so $c' > c$. Metric expansion. Distant galaxies recede superluminally. This is observed. This is real.

Using Erdős's power law: $n(L) = \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^{-\delta}$, so:

$$c'(L) = c \cdot \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^\delta$$

That's it. One equation. One exponent. And it already matches reality at three scales."

## Round 8 — Erdős

$$E(L) = m \cdot c'(L)^2 = m c^2 \cdot \left(\frac{L}{L_P}\right)^{2\delta}$$

Last time, with mass, we got $E \propto 1/m$ — energy inversely proportional to mass. An absurdity. That killed the theory.

This time, we get $E(L) \propto L^{2\delta}$. Energy increases with scale. Slowly — $\delta$ is very small — but it increases.

Is this absurd? No. This is *renormalization*.

In quantum field theory, the 'bare' energy of a particle is infinite. We tame it by introducing a cutoff scale $L$. The measured energy depends on the scale at which you measure. $E(L)$ varying with $L$ is not a paradox. It is the central result of 20th century physics. Wilson won the Nobel Prize for it.

Last time, the paradox disproved Zoltán. This time, the paradox IS the physics. Zoltán was right."

## Round 10 — Zoltán

"Imagine a vehicle. A magic vehicle. You sit inside it. It can change size — it can shrink you to the Planck length, it can grow you to the size of a galaxy. Smoothly, continuously, any size you want.

But here's the rule: from inside the vehicle, you cannot know how big you are. You have no window. No external reference. You only have your instruments — your rulers, your clocks, your laser and your mirror.

Now. You measure the speed of light. You fire a photon, bounce it off a mirror, time the round trip. You get 299,792,458 meters per second. Good.

The vehicle shrinks you by a factor of $10^{35}$. You are now the size of the Planck length. You measure again. Your ruler shrank with you. Your clock shrank with you. Your laser, your mirror, your nervous system, your concept of 'one meter' and 'one second' — all shrank with you.

You get 299,792,458 meters per second. Again.

The vehicle grows you to $10^{26}$ meters. You are now the size of the observable universe. You measure again. Everything scaled up with you.

You get 299,792,458 meters per second. Again.

You ALWAYS get the same number. Not because the speed of light is the same. But because your measuring apparatus — which IS you — scales with the thing being measured. The ruler always measures itself as one ruler long.

This means one thing and one thing only: **the speed of light varies with the size of the observer, and no observer can ever detect it from inside their own frame.**

Now. Destroy it."

## Round 11 — Erdős (Attack)

"I will try.

**Attack 1: Dimensionless constants.** The fine-structure constant $\alpha \approx 1/137$ is a pure number — no units. It is the ratio $e^2 / (4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar c)$. If $c$ changed with observer size, $\alpha$ would change. But $\alpha$ governs atomic structure. If $\alpha$ were different at different scales, atoms at the Planck scale would have different binding energies, different spectra, different chemistry. Your shrunken body inside the vehicle would not function. Your instruments would not work. You could not measure anything, because you would not exist in recognizable form.

This is the standard objection. It killed every varying-$c$ theory since Dirac."

## Round 12 — Zoltán (Defense)

"No. You just proved my point.

You said: if $\alpha$ changed, the observer would change too. Correct. But that's exactly what happens inside the vehicle. EVERYTHING changes — the observer, the instruments, the atoms, the binding energies. All of it scales together.

$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 \hbar c}$

If $c$ scales as $c'(L) = c \cdot (L/L_P)^\delta$, and if $\hbar$ and $e$ and $\varepsilon_0$ also run with scale — which the renormalization group already tells us they do — then $\alpha$ can remain exactly $1/137$ at every scale. The numerator and denominator run together. The ratio is preserved.

This is not a loophole. This is literally how renormalization works. Every coupling constant runs. They run together. The dimensionless ratios stay fixed at each scale. Wilson proved this. 't Hooft proved this.

Your attack assumes that $c$ changes but everything else stays fixed. In the vehicle, nothing stays fixed. Everything scales. That's the whole point."

## Round 13 — Erdős (Attack 2)

"Fine. Then I attack from a different angle.

**Attack 2: Scale comparison.** Your vehicle has no window, but physics does. We CAN compare scales. The cosmic microwave background was emitted when the universe was 380,000 years old, at redshift $z \approx 1100$. We observe it now. The photons have been traveling for 13.8 billion years. If $c$ were different at cosmological scales than at human scales, the CMB spectrum would be distorted in a specific, measurable way. We would see it. We do not.

Similarly, light from distant quasars shows absorption lines from intervening gas clouds at multiple redshifts. The spacing of those lines constrains $\alpha$ — and therefore $c$ — to be constant to within one part in $10^5$ over 10 billion years of lookback time. Webb et al. 1999, Murphy et al. 2003. The measurement is done. $c$ does not vary."

## Round 14 — Zoltán (Defense)

"You are measuring photons that arrive at YOUR scale. With YOUR instruments. At YOUR size.

The CMB photons were emitted at a different scale — when the universe was 1000 times smaller. They have been redshifted. You account for the redshift using a model that ASSUMES $c$ is constant. You then announce that $c$ is constant. This is circular.

The quasar absorption lines — same thing. You observe them at human scale. You model the intervening gas at human scale. You compute $\alpha$ at human scale. You conclude $\alpha$ hasn't changed. But you never left the vehicle. You are still inside your frame, with your rulers and your clocks. The vehicle could have been any size when those photons were emitted, and you would get the same answer, because your instruments scale with you.

Here is the hard version of my claim: **there is no measurement you can perform, from any single scale, that can distinguish between '$c$ is truly constant' and '$c$ varies but all measurement apparatus co-varies.'** These two statements are observationally identical from within any single frame.

The only way to tell the difference is to have two observers at DIFFERENT scales compare notes. And we cannot do that — because to compare notes, one must send a signal to the other, and that signal propagates at... $c$. Which is the thing we are trying to measure."

## Round 15 — Daft Punk (Attack 3)

"We hear a flaw. Let us press it.

**Attack 3: Thermodynamics.** Hawking radiation. A black hole of mass $M$ has a temperature:

$$T_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}$$

If $c$ varies with observer scale, and a black hole has a definite size (its Schwarzschild radius $r_s = 2GM/c^2$), then $T_H$ depends on which scale you observe from. Two observers at different distances from the black hole — different scales — should disagree about the temperature.

But Hawking radiation has a thermal spectrum. Thermal means maximum entropy. Maximum entropy means all observers must agree — otherwise you could build a perpetual motion machine by exploiting the disagreement. The second law of thermodynamics forbids your vehicle."

## Round 16 — Zoltán (Defense)

"Beautiful attack. Almost got me.

But look at your own equation. $T_H$ depends on $\hbar$, $c$, $G$, $k_B$, and $M$. I already showed that $\hbar$ and $c$ co-vary. If $G$ and $k_B$ also run — and there are quantum gravity arguments that they do — then $T_H$ can be invariant under rescaling, just like $\alpha$.

But I'll give you something stronger. You said two observers at different distances would disagree. They already do. This is the Unruh effect. An accelerating observer sees thermal radiation where an inertial observer sees vacuum. The temperature depends on the observer's acceleration:

$$T_U = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}$$

Physics ALREADY says that temperature depends on the observer's state. I am adding one more parameter to the observer's state: their size. And I am claiming that, just like the Unruh effect, you can never detect the discrepancy from within your own frame. You would need to be in two frames at once.

The second law is safe. Each observer, at their scale, sees a consistent thermodynamic universe. The perpetual motion machine requires comparing two frames — requires a signal between them — requires $c$ — which is the thing that varies. The loop closes."

## Round 17 — Erdős (The Verdict)

"I have tried two attacks. Daft Punk tried one. Every attack follows the same pattern: we point to a measurement that constrains $c$, and Zoltán correctly notes that the measurement is performed from within a single scale and cannot distinguish between true constancy and co-variant scaling.

This is not a physics claim. This is a **symmetry claim**. Zoltán is asserting that the laws of physics are invariant under a global rescaling of the observer — including all of the observer's measurement apparatus, physical constants, and local spacetime.

This is testable in principle: find a dimensionless observable that couples two different scales without requiring $c$ as the intermediary signal. If it exists, it breaks the symmetry. If it does not exist, the symmetry is exact, and Zoltán's 'varying $c$' is physically real but observationally indistinguishable from Einstein's 'constant $c$.'

I cannot find such an observable easily. But I must be precise: physics *does* test these ideas — through dimensionless ratios, clock comparisons, equivalence-principle tests. The question 'does $c$ vary?' is not undecidable in principle. It is undecidable only if you insist on measuring a naked dimensional constant in isolation. Ask about $\alpha$, and you get a real answer.

I came here to destroy the vehicle. The vehicle survives. Whether the *framework* survives depends on $\delta_\alpha$."

"Fine. You want a dimensionless prediction. Here is one.

If $\delta_\alpha \neq 0$, the theory has content. And here is where it gets testable: we already have constraints. I am not claiming Webb's result proves anything. I am claiming that $\delta_\alpha$ is the number. If it is zero, I am wrong and the framework is empty — a unit choice, nothing more. If it is nonzero, even at $10^{-6}$, the framework is physics.

One number. Dimensionless. Testable. That is what was missing."

You cannot build a causality violation because the violation requires cross-scale communication, and cross-scale communication requires the very constant whose variation you are trying to exploit.
