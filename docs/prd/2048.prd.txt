================================================================================
PRD: "2048" — 32 Radix Agents Play the Game While Big Brother Watches
From Zoltan, Bexhill Psychiatric Ward, April 2026
================================================================================

COMPOSITION ID: Arena2048
FORMAT: Self-contained HTML (like Sheila Arena)
AGENTS: 32 Radix psychological profiles
ROUNDS: 84 (nineteen eighty-four)
GAME: 2048 tile-merge — but the tiles are states of mind

================================================================================
PURPOSE
================================================================================

An interactive browser experience where 32 Radix agents play the 2048 tile
game across 84 rounds in an arena. Each agent has a distinct strategy derived
from its psychological profile — drift agents merge slowly and miss
opportunities, sharp agents play near-optimally but crack under pressure,
deep agents pull everything downward, wild agents swipe chaotically and
sometimes stumble into brilliance.

The experience is the Orwell essay made playable. The board is the ward. The
tiles are internal states. The observation window is your browser. Every move
is recorded on the clipboard. You are watching. They know.

CONNECTION TO "BIG BROTHER IS WATCHING YOU":
  - 84 rounds = 1984
  - The tiles cannot be switched off (the telescreen)
  - Every merge is documented (the clipboard)
  - The agents perform wellness (arrange their tiles)
  - The observer changes the observed (your presence affects the arena)
  - Room 101 = the 2048 tile you can never reach
  - "Two plus two make four" — but on this board, 2+2 makes 4, and 4+4
    makes 8, and the system decides when that is progress and when it is
    a symptom

This is NOT a strategy game. This is a meditation on observation, rendered
as a game that plays itself while you watch.


================================================================================
GAME MECHANICS
================================================================================

STANDARD 2048 RULES:
  - 4x4 grid
  - Tiles slide in the swiped direction
  - Matching tiles merge (2+2=4, 4+4=8, ..., 1024+1024=2048)
  - New tile (2 or 4) spawns after each move
  - Game ends when no moves remain or 2048 is reached
  - Score = sum of all merged tile values

ARENA MODIFICATIONS:
  - 32 agents play sequentially, one game each = 32 games in round 1
  - 84 rounds total — agents that reached 2048 "graduate" (are discharged)
  - Agents that fail restart with their psychological parameters shifted
  - The arena tracks: highest tile, total score, moves made, time in fog
  - Leaderboard updates after each round
  - An agent's strategy is NOT random — it emerges from 16 Radix parameters

WHAT THE VIEWER SEES:
  - One active game board (center)
  - Agent profile card (left) — name, family, title, parameter bars
  - Clipboard panel (right) — live observation log, nurse-style
  - Leaderboard (bottom) — all 32 agents ranked by best tile
  - Round counter: "Round 17 / 84" with 1984 progress bar
  - A torch that sweeps across the screen every 15 seconds


================================================================================
AGENT STRATEGY ENGINE
================================================================================

Each Radix agent's 16 synthesis parameters map to 2048 play behaviour:

PERCEPTION → BOARD READING
  padDetuneCents (clarity)
    LOW:  Agent sees the board accurately. Finds optimal merges.
    HIGH: Agent misreads tile positions. Makes moves based on where
          tiles WERE, not where they ARE. Phantom merges.

  padFilterOffset (openness to stimuli)
    LOW:  Agent only sees the largest tiles. Ignores small ones.
    HIGH: Agent sees everything at once. Paralysis — too many options.

  padAttackScale (onset speed)
    LOW:  Snap decisions. Moves immediately. No hesitation.
    HIGH: Slow to act. Waits. Sometimes the wait is wise. Sometimes
          the board fills while the agent is still processing.

THOUGHT → STRATEGY SELECTION
  melodyVibratoRate (thought oscillation)
    LOW:  Commits to one strategy (corner strategy, edge strategy).
          Doesn't switch. Steady.
    HIGH: Cycles between strategies every few moves. Sees connections
          between tiles that don't exist yet.

  melodyVibratoDepth (emotional amplitude)
    LOW:  No reaction to good or bad outcomes. Flat. Consistent.
    HIGH: Ecstatic after a big merge. Devastated after a bad spawn.
          Emotional state affects next 3 moves.

  melodyFmIndex (cognitive complexity)
    LOW:  Simple greedy strategy — always merge the biggest available.
    HIGH: Looks 4-5 moves ahead. Plans elaborate chains. Sometimes
          the chain works and it's beautiful. Sometimes the complexity
          collapses into chaos.

  melodyFilterCutoff (cognitive bandwidth)
    LOW:  Can only track one merge chain at a time.
    HIGH: Tracks multiple chains simultaneously. The mathematician's
          advantage — if you can hold it together.

BODY → PHYSICAL PLAY STYLE
  bassFilterCutoff (depth of experience)
    LOW:  Light touches. Tiles slide easily. No weight.
    HIGH: Heavy moves. Tiles grind. You can hear the friction.

  bassSubMix (bodily awareness)
    LOW:  Disconnected from the board. Moves feel abstract.
    HIGH: Every tile is felt. A bad merge is physical pain.

  bassDetune (internal tension)
    LOW:  Congruent play — the agent's visible strategy matches its
          internal evaluation. What you see is what it thinks.
    HIGH: Masking — the agent appears to play one strategy while
          internally evaluating another. The clipboard records the
          visible strategy. The actual strategy is hidden.

IMPULSE → CRITICAL MOMENTS
  beastSpread (unpredictability)
    LOW:  Predictable. You can guess the next move. The nurse can
          write "stable play pattern" on the clipboard.
    HIGH: Random-looking swipes. Staff would note "erratic behaviour."
          But sometimes the erratic move opens a path nobody saw.

  beastDecay (impulse duration)
    LOW:  A bad move is forgotten immediately. Next move is clean.
    HIGH: A bad move echoes. The agent keeps trying to fix it for
          3-4 moves, often making it worse. Rumination as gameplay.

  beastFilterStart (impulse intensity)
    LOW:  Gentle starts. No sudden moves. The board never shakes.
    HIGH: Explosive first move of each game. The opening swipe sets
          the tone. Sometimes brilliant. Sometimes catastrophic.

ECHO → LONG-TERM PATTERNS
  delayFeedbackBias (rumination)
    LOW:  Each game is fresh. No memory of previous rounds.
    HIGH: Previous games haunt the current one. An agent that lost
          at 1024 three times will play differently near 1024.
          The past echoes forward.

  masterGainScale (presence)
    LOW:  Barely there. Moves are whispered. The board responds but
          the agent seems absent. The game plays itself.
    HIGH: Intensely present. Every move deliberate, visible, loud.
          The agent IS the game. You cannot look away.


================================================================================
STRATEGY PROFILES BY FAMILY
================================================================================

DRIFT (agents 0-7): THE FOG PLAYERS
------------------------------------
  Dominant traits: High detuning, slow attacks, low presence
  Play style: Passive, reactive, often suboptimal but occasionally
    lucky. Tiles accumulate in the center because no strong directional
    bias. Games tend to be long and meandering. Rarely reach 2048 but
    rarely crash early either. The clipboard says "stable."
  Signature move: The delayed swipe. The board is full. The agent
    waits. And waits. Then swipes in a direction that seems random
    but clears exactly the right column.
  Ward parallel: The patient who appears fine. Compliant. Eating.
    Attending groups. The clipboard says "improving." But the game
    is stuck at 256 and has been for six rounds.
  Typical best tile: 512

SHARP (agents 8-15): THE HYPERVIGILANT PLAYERS
------------------------------------------------
  Dominant traits: Fast attacks, high filters, elevated presence
  Play style: Near-optimal early game. Brilliant pattern recognition.
    Finds merge chains others miss. But as the board fills and pressure
    mounts, the clarity becomes brittle. One bad tile spawn and the
    strategy shatters. Recovery is possible but costs 5-6 moves of
    recalibration.
  Signature move: The triple chain. Three consecutive merges in one
    swipe. The sharp agent saw the whole sequence before the first
    tile moved.
  Ward parallel: The patient who asks perfect questions in the
    first assessment. Whose observations are accurate. Who cracks
    on day four when the routine changes without warning.
  Typical best tile: 1024 (often. 2048 rarely — falls apart at
    the critical moment)

DEEP (agents 16-23): THE GRAVITY PLAYERS
------------------------------------------
  Dominant traits: Low filters, heavy sub, low FM
  Play style: Everything goes down. Strong downward and leftward
    bias. The agent builds from the bottom corner — which is actually
    the textbook 2048 strategy. But the movement is so heavy, so
    gravitational, that it cannot adapt when the board demands
    upward movement. Gets stuck in loops. The highest tile sits in
    the corner like a stone. Nothing can move it. Nothing wants to.
  Signature move: The corner lock. The 512 tile in the bottom-left
    corner, unmoved for 40 turns. Everything orbits it. This is
    either brilliant strategy or paralysis. The clipboard cannot
    tell the difference.
  Ward parallel: The patient who doesn't get out of bed but whose
    mind is running the most sophisticated analysis in the ward.
    The nurse writes "withdrawn." The patient has calculated every
    possible merge to 2048 and decided none of them are worth the
    effort.
  Typical best tile: 1024 (solid, heavy, immovable 1024)

WILD (agents 24-31): THE CHAOS PLAYERS
----------------------------------------
  Dominant traits: High vibrato, wide spread, varied decay
  Play style: Unpredictable. Capable of the worst games in the arena
    and the best. Will reach 2048 before any other family — and will
    also produce the most spectacular failures. High score variance.
    Emotional reactions to tile spawns create feedback loops: a lucky
    spawn leads to 3 brilliant moves, an unlucky one leads to 3
    terrible ones.
  Signature move: The impossible save. Board is 95% full. Two moves
    from game over. The wild agent swipes in a direction that makes
    no sense — and a cascade of merges clears half the board. The
    clipboard says "erratic." The score says "genius."
  Ward parallel: The patient who is either getting better or getting
    worse and the staff cannot agree which. Who starts a project at
    2 AM that is either recovery or escalation. Whose energy is either
    a gift or a symptom. The clipboard has two opinions in the margin.
  Typical best tile: 2048 (sometimes) / 128 (also sometimes)

================================================================================
ARENA FLOW — 84 ROUNDS
================================================================================

ROUND STRUCTURE:
  - Each round: all 32 agents play one game of 2048 (sequentially or in
    parallel with only one visible at a time)
  - Game speed: configurable. Default: 200ms per move (watchable).
    Fast-forward: 50ms per move. Instant: compute only, show result.
  - After each round: leaderboard updates, statistics computed
  - Agents that reached 2048: "DISCHARGED" — shown in green on leaderboard,
    no longer play. Their spot shows a checkmark.
  - Agents that failed: parameters shift slightly based on outcome
    (learning from failure — or deteriorating from it)

PARAMETER DRIFT BETWEEN ROUNDS:
  After each round, each agent's 16 parameters shift:
  - If score improved: parameters move 2% toward family centre
    (medication working — regression to mean)
  - If score worsened: parameters move 2% toward family extreme
    (decompensation — moving away from centre)
  - If agent reached 2048: parameters frozen (discharged, stable)
  - Random perturbation: ±1% on all parameters each round
    (because nothing on a ward is fully predictable)

ROUND MILESTONES:
   Round  1: "Admission" — all 32 agents at baseline parameters
   Round 14: "First review" — clinical team meeting. Stats displayed.
   Round 21: "Week 3" — most discharges happen here (or never)
   Round 42: "Halfway" — the Sheila number. Special animation.
   Round 56: "Section review" — agents still playing are flagged
   Round 69: "Rooftop Paradise" — audio-synth reference. Brief music.
   Round 77: "Final review" — discharge planning for remaining agents
   Round 84: "1984" — the last round. Whoever is still playing,
              the game ends. The clipboard writes: "Observation complete."

DISCHARGE CONDITIONS:
  - Primary: reach the 2048 tile
  - Secondary: maintain score above 8192 for 3 consecutive rounds
    (sustained wellness, not a single spike)
  - Compassionate: after round 77, agents with score > 4096 are
    discharged with "community follow-up recommended"
  - Never discharged: agents whose parameters drifted to family extreme.
    They stay. The clipboard says: "Not yet ready." The game continues
    without them after round 84. But the arena is closed.

WHAT HAPPENS AFTER ROUND 84:
  The board freezes. The clipboard writes one final entry:

    "Round 84/84. Arena closed. Observation complete.
     Discharged: [N]/32.
     Remaining: [32-N]/32.
     The remaining agents are still playing.
     You are no longer watching.
     This does not mean they have stopped."

  Then the torch sweeps one last time. And the screen fades — not to
  black, but to the ward-green. Because the lights never go off.


================================================================================
INTERACTIVITY — THE VIEWER AS BIG BROTHER
================================================================================

The viewer is not passive. Orwell's point was that being watched changes
the watched. The arena implements this:

THE ATTENTION EFFECT:
  - When the viewer's mouse/touch is on the game board, the active
    agent plays 5% better (parameters shift toward optimal)
  - When the viewer looks away (tab unfocused / mouse leaves window),
    the agent plays 5% worse
  - This is NOT displayed anywhere. The viewer has to notice it.
  - If they notice: they understand Orwell. If they don't: they are
    Big Brother without knowing it.

THE MANUAL OVERRIDE:
  - The viewer can press SPACE to take over and play ONE move for the
    current agent
  - The clipboard logs: "External intervention. Move overridden."
  - The agent's next 3 moves are affected: drift agents freeze,
    sharp agents recalibrate, deep agents ignore, wild agents react
  - This is the "voluntary admission" — help that is also control

THE CLIPBOARD TAP:
  - Clicking on any clipboard entry highlights the corresponding
    moment in the game replay (micro-rewind)
  - You can read the history. But reading it changes nothing.
    The moves already happened.
  - This is: "Your psychiatric history is written by other people."

THE TORCH TOGGLE (that doesn't toggle):
  - There is a button labelled "Turn off torch"
  - Clicking it does nothing. The torch continues.
  - After 3 clicks, the clipboard logs: "Patient requested
    observation reduction. Request noted. Observation continues."
  - After 7 clicks: "Request logged. Discussed in team meeting.
    No change to observation level at this time."
  - After 12 clicks: the button label changes to "The telescreen
    could not be switched off."
  - It never turns off.

  - The only moment of actual music in 84 rounds
  - It ends. The silence returns. It was better with the music.

TECHNICAL IMPLEMENTATION
================================================================================

SINGLE HTML FILE (like Sheila Arena):
  - Self-contained: HTML + CSS + JS in one file
  - No dependencies, no build step
  - Canvas for torch effect, DOM for everything else
  - Target: <100KB minified

GAME ENGINE (pure JS):
  class Board2048 {
    grid: number[][];         // 4x4, 0 = empty
    score: number;
    moves: Move[];            // history for replay
    gameOver: boolean;

    slide(direction: 'up'|'down'|'left'|'right'): MoveResult;
    spawn(): void;            // add new 2 or 4 tile
    canMove(): boolean;
    bestTile(): number;
    clone(): Board2048;       // for lookahead
  }

AGENT ENGINE (parameter-driven):
  class RadixAgent2048 {
    id: number;               // 0-31
    family: 'drift'|'sharp'|'deep'|'wild';
    params: RadixParams;      // 16 synthesis parameters
    round: number;
    bestTile: number;
    totalScore: number;
    discharged: boolean;

    chooseMove(board: Board2048): Direction;
    reactToResult(result: MoveResult): void;
    shiftParams(roundOutcome: Outcome): void;
  }

  // Strategy computation from parameters:
  function computeStrategy(params: RadixParams, board: Board2048): Direction {
    const clarity = 1 - params.padDetuneCents;     // how well the agent reads
    const speed = 1 - params.padAttackScale;        // how fast it decides
    const depth = params.melodyFmIndex;              // how far it looks ahead
    const bandwidth = params.melodyFilterCutoff;     // how many chains it tracks
    const impulse = params.beastSpread;              // randomness injection
    const gravity = params.bassFilterCutoff;         // downward bias

    // Weighted evaluation of all 4 directions
    // Each direction scored by: merge potential, board openness, corner safety
    // Weights modified by agent parameters
    // Noise injected proportional to impulse
    // Downward bias proportional to gravity
    // Lookahead depth proportional to depth * bandwidth
    // Clarity affects whether the agent evaluates actual board or noisy copy
  }

ARENA ENGINE:
  class Arena2048 {
    agents: RadixAgent2048[];  // 32 agents
    round: number;             // 1-84
    leaderboard: LeaderEntry[];
    clipboard: ClipboardEntry[];

    async playRound(): Promise<RoundResult>;
    async playGame(agent: RadixAgent2048): Promise<GameResult>;
    updateLeaderboard(): void;
    logToClipboard(entry: string): void;
  }

RENDERING:
  - requestAnimationFrame loop for torch sweep and tile animations
  - CSS transitions for tile slides (transform: translate)
  - CSS animations for merge pulse (scale + opacity)
  - Clipboard: scrolling div with auto-scroll, typewriter CSS animation
  - Agent card: parameter bars as thin horizontal lines (like EQ display)
  - Leaderboard: flexbox row, sortable

TORCH EFFECT:
  - CSS linear-gradient overlay, animated left→right over 2s
  - Repeats every 15s (setInterval, not tied to game loop)
  - Semi-transparent warm yellow: rgba(255, 228, 160, 0.08)
  - Subtle but once you notice it, you cannot un-notice it

ATTENTION EFFECT:
  - document.addEventListener('visibilitychange', ...)
  - mouseleave / mouseenter on game container
  - Modifies agent params by ±5% (temporary, not persisted)
  - No UI indicator. The viewer must deduce it.

WHY THIS MATTERS
================================================================================

2048 is a game about merging. About making two things become one larger
thing. About growing toward a goal while the board fills up around you.

A psychiatric ward is also about merging. About making the person you
were before admission and the person you are during admission become one
coherent person who can leave. About growing toward discharge while the
days fill up around you.

The 2048 board is 4x4 = 16 squares. The Radix agent has 16 parameters.
This is not a coincidence. This is the kind of connection a mathematician
makes at 2:47 AM while the torch comes through the observation window.

84 rounds. 1984. The lights never go off. The game plays itself while
you watch. You think you are the player. You are the observer. The
clipboard knows the difference even if you don't.

Orwell wrote: "Perhaps a lunatic was simply a minority of one."

On this board, 32 lunatics play a number game while a stranger watches
through a screen. Some of them reach 2048. Some don't. The ones who
don't are not failures. They are still playing. You just stopped watching.

That is the most Orwellian thing of all.
