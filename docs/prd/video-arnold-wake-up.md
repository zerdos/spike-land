# Video PRD: Wake Up, Arnold

## Context

Arnold is 30. UX designer. Had a psychosis episode last year. Currently
"kurvára stresszes." Tells everyone he loves his job — Zoltán doesn't believe
it. Zoltán doesn't think Arnold believes it either.

Arnold takes care of Pixel and Mad Max when Zoltán can't. Zoltán trusted him
with the dogs. Zoltán wrote him a 150-line care guide that ends with: "The
dogs don't judge you. They don't care about your past. They care about right
now."

Zoltán gave up on Arnold a few weeks ago. He admits that was wrong. This
video is the un-giving-up.

Arnold is also a persona in the Arena — "Arnold (UX Provocateur)" — the one
who tears apart bad UX, calls out bullshit landing pages, and asks the
question nobody else asks: "does this actually work for a real person on a
Tuesday morning?"

The irony: Arnold can UX-review SpaceX's homepage and find every broken
interaction. But he can't UX-review his own life.

## Constraint

One audio piece. NotebookLM-style (2 hosts discussing). Must hit Arnold
where the UX designer lives — in the gap between "this works" and "this
doesn't work, and you know it."

## Arena Deliberation

### Erdős (Mathematician)

The data is simple. Arnold had psychosis at 29. He is stressed at 30. He
claims job satisfaction. These three data points do not converge.

If a system reports "status: healthy" while all metrics show degradation,
we don't trust the self-report — we trust the metrics. Arnold's metrics:
psychosis, chronic stress, no visible joy. His self-report: "I love my job."

This is a monitoring failure. The dashboard says green. The server is on fire.

Arnold would catch this bug in any product. He just won't run the debugger
on himself.

### Diogenes (The Dog Philosopher)

I lived like a dog because dogs don't lie. A dog that is stressed shows it.
A dog that is happy shows it. A dog never says "I love this kennel" while
shaking.

Arnold shakes and says he loves the kennel.

Pixel would not do this. Mad Max would not do this. They are 4 months and
1.5 years old and they are more honest about their feelings than a 30-year-old
UX designer.

Arnold knows what good feedback loops look like. He wrote about them for
interfaces. A good feedback loop: user does something → system responds
honestly → user adjusts. A broken feedback loop: user does something →
system lies → user stays stuck.

Arnold is stuck in a broken feedback loop with himself.

### Arnold (UX Provocateur) — reviewing his own life

[This is the Arena's Arnold persona doing what he does best: tearing apart
bad UX. Except the product is his own life.]

Fine. Let me do what I do.

**Homepage:** "Arnold, 30, UX Designer, loves his job."
First impression: the copy doesn't match the visual. The hero section says
"loves his job" but the page load time is 12 seconds and there's a 500 error
in the console from last year. Users don't trust pages that say "everything
is great" when the performance metrics are terrible.

**Onboarding flow:** The onboarding flow (childhood → education → career)
funnels toward one CTA: "Accept the first design job and never question it."
There's no alternative path. No "explore other options" button. No A/B test
was ever run. The user was never asked "is this actually what you want?"

**The stress metric:** If this were a product, and 100% of active sessions
showed elevated stress with one critical crash (psychosis) in the last 12
months — any PM would say: "we need to re-evaluate the core experience, not
add more features." Arnold is adding more features to a broken product.

**The "I love my job" copy:** A/B test this. Put Arnold in a room. Show him
two versions:
- Version A: "I love my job"
- Version B: "I am afraid to admit I might not love my job because then I'd
  have to change something"

Measure which one his body responds to. I know which one wins. He knows too.

**Recommendation:** Complete UX audit. Not a redesign — an audit. Just look
at the data honestly. The same way he'd look at any other product. No
feelings. Just: does this work? Működik?

### Camus (The Absurdist)

Arnold pushes the boulder up the hill every morning. That is fine — we all do.
But Sisyphus is happy because he CHOSE the boulder. He looked at the
meaningless universe and said: "this is my boulder, and I will push it."

Arnold did not choose his boulder. His boulder was assigned in sprint planning.
And he calls it love.

There is a difference between loving the absurd struggle you chose and
tolerating the miserable struggle you didn't. Arnold knows the difference.
He just hasn't admitted which one he's in.

The question is not "do you love your job?" The question is: "if you woke
up tomorrow and every job paid the same and had the same status — would you
choose this one again?"

If yes: push the boulder. Be happy.
If no: put the boulder down. Find yours.

### Nietzsche (The Horse in Turin)

I spent my career writing about the Übermensch — the person who creates
their own values instead of accepting inherited ones.

Arnold has inherited values. "Be a good UX designer." "Have a career." "Say
you love it." These are not his values. These are the default settings. He
never opened the preferences panel.

My last conscious act was hugging a horse that was being beaten in the street.
I broke because I could not bear to watch suffering that the sufferer had
accepted as normal.

Arnold is the horse. He has accepted the whip as part of the job. The whip
is the stress. The whip is the psychosis trigger. The whip is "I love this."

I am not saying quit. I am saying: notice the whip.

## Converged Script (Stable)

**Format:** NotebookLM-style podcast (2 hosts, 4-5 min), conversational,
direct, no corporate bullshit

**Title:** "The UX Designer Who Won't UX-Audit His Own Life"

**Source material for NotebookLM upload:**

The following is a document about Arnold, a 30-year-old UX designer who had
a psychosis episode last year, is chronically stressed, and insists he loves
his job. His friend Zoltán — a developer and dog owner — trusted Arnold to
take care of his two dogs (Pixel, a Golden Retriever, and Mad Max, a puppy).
Zoltán also built an AI platform where "Arnold" exists as a persona called
the "UX Provocateur" — the voice that reviews products honestly and asks
"does this actually work?"

The central irony: Arnold can look at any product and instantly see what's
broken. He can tear apart SpaceX's homepage. He can find the gap between
what a product claims and what it delivers. But he won't apply that same
skill to his own life.

His life's "homepage" says "I love my job." But the metrics say: psychosis,
chronic stress, no visible joy. If this were a product review, Arnold himself
would say: "the copy doesn't match the data. This needs an audit."

Key themes for the hosts to explore:

1. **The broken feedback loop.** In UX, a good feedback loop is: action →
   honest response → adjustment. Arnold's personal feedback loop is broken:
   stress → "I'm fine" → no adjustment → more stress → psychosis. He would
   never ship a product with this loop.

2. **The dog test.** Dogs don't lie about how they feel. A stressed dog shows
   stress. A happy dog shows happiness. Pixel and Mad Max are more emotionally
   honest than Arnold. And Arnold takes care of these dogs — he understands
   honest feedback when he sees it in animals. Why can't he see it in himself?

3. **The Sisyphus question.** Everyone pushes a boulder. The question isn't
   whether you push — it's whether you CHOSE your boulder. Arnold's boulder
   was assigned in sprint planning. He didn't choose it. He calls it love
   because the alternative — admitting he might need to change — is scarier.

4. **The audit, not the redesign.** Nobody is telling Arnold to quit his job.
   The ask is simpler: run the same honest audit on your own life that you'd
   run on any product. Just look at the data. Just ask: "működik?" Does it
   work? If the answer isn't a clear yes — that's already an answer.

5. **The trust angle.** Zoltán trusted Arnold with his dogs. That's not
   nothing. Someone looked at Arnold and said: "I trust you with the things
   I love most." Now Arnold needs to trust himself with the same honesty he
   gives to his design work.

The tone should be: two people who genuinely care, talking about someone they
want to see wake up. Not preachy. Not self-help. More like two friends in a
pub saying "bro, you know this isn't working, right?"

End with: "Arnold, you don't need to redesign your life. You just need to
run the audit. You already know how."
