# Brighton, Dogs, and ADHD: Building a Startup While Everything Burns

![A developer walking two dogs along the pebble beach in Brighton, England, early morning sunlight, with the old pier in the background, cinematic photography](https://placehold.co/800x400)

_By Zoltan Erdos | Brighton, UK | January 2026_

It is 6:15 in the morning. My alarm goes off. The house is quiet. The dogs are still asleep. I get dressed in the dark and head to the gym.

This is how every day starts. Not because I am a morning person—I am not. But because I have ADHD, and without a rigid routine, my life falls apart. If you're a junior developer trying to figure out how to balance work, life, and side projects while your brain feels chaotic, let me tell you what building a startup looks like when your mind works differently.

## The Routine That Keeps Me Alive

My day has a rhythm. It has to.

![A colorful, structured daily schedule planner with distinct blocks for gym, dog walks, and coding sessions, isometric 3d illustration](https://placehold.co/600x300)

**6:30am**: Gym. No thinking. Just movement. My brain wakes up slowly while my body works. By the time I finish, my mind is ready.  
**8:00am**: First dog walk. My two dogs need this, and I need this too. We walk the same route every day. There is immense comfort in sameness.  
Then I work. Emails. Code. Meetings. The normal stuff.  
**11:00am**: Second dog walk. A break from the screen. Fresh air.  
Back to work. More code. More problems to solve.  
**1:00pm**: Third walk. Lunch break.  
**5:00pm**: Final walk of the day. The sun is lower. The dogs are tired. I am tired too, but in a good way.

This schedule might look boring. To me, it is survival. Our brains do not make schedules for us; we have to build them from scratch every single day. The dogs help. They don't care about my deadlines or my startup. They just know it is time for a walk, forcing me to step away from the screen. Structure is my medicine.

## The ADHD Reality

I need to be honest about something that many neurodivergent developers struggle with: I have always been afraid of feedback.

My mind takes every piece of criticism and turns it personal. Someone says, "This code could be better," and my brain hears, "You are a failure." I know this is not logical, but knowing something and feeling something are different things.

This makes working on a team incredibly hard. Code reviews feel like being judged. Questions feel like attacks. I have to fight this every day, reminding myself that feedback is a gift and that my work is not my worth.

ADHD makes communication harder in other ways too. I interrupt people. I forget what I was saying mid-sentence. I hyperfocus on the wrong things. I cannot start tasks even when they are urgent. Building a startup with a brain like this is not easy, but it is possible if you understand your own operating system.

## The Side Project Grind

I work full-time. I have a job that pays my bills. It is good work with good people. But in the evenings and weekends, I build Spike.land. 

Spike.land is my dream. A live code editor, a platform where people can create and share. 

![A glowing brain with interconnected nodes lighting up, representing hyperfocus and creative energy during a late night coding session, digital art](https://placehold.co/600x300)

Some nights I code for hours. The hyperfocus kicks in and suddenly it is 2am and I have built something wonderful. Other nights I cannot start. I stare at the screen. My fingers refuse to move. My brain says "not tonight."

This is the reality of building while working. The key is to keep going and to forgive yourself on the bad days. Tomorrow is a new day, and the code will still be there.

## Why Brighton

I live in Brighton, a city on the south coast of England. It rains here. A lot. The seagulls scream at 4am. But I love it anyway.

Brighton is full of creative people—artists, developers, dreamers. There is a feeling here that anything is possible, that weird is welcome, and that different is good. My dream is simple: I want to build Spike.land into something real. I want a small office here in Brighton. I want to work with nice people who understand that brains work in different ways.

## The Hope

Some days are really hard. The code does not work, the feedback stings, and the brain will not cooperate. 

But I keep going because I am deeply passionate about this craft. I love programming, JavaScript, distributed systems, and solving puzzles. When I am deep in a problem, I forget about everything else. That feeling is worth fighting for.

The routine helps hold everything together. I am building something slowly, imperfectly, with a brain that does not always cooperate. 

But I am building. And that is enough.
