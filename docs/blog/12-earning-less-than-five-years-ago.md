# Earning Less Than Five Years Ago: The Developer's Economic Reality

![A frustrated software developer sitting at a messy desk with a glowing computer screen showing shrinking charts, bathed in cool blue light, cinematic style](https://placehold.co/800x400)

_By Zoltan Erdos | Brighton, UK | January 2026_

I need to say something out loud. Something that feels uncomfortable to admit, especially to those just starting their careers in tech. 

I earn less money now than I earned five years ago.

There. I said it. A software developer in 2026, with more experience, more skills, more knowledge. And my paycheck got smaller, not bigger. If you are a junior developer looking at the current landscape, you deserve to know how we got here, and what it means for your future.

Let me tell you what happened.

## The Paycheck That Shrunk

In 2021, tech salaries were climbing. Every month, a new headline about developer shortages. Companies fought for talent. Recruiters sent messages daily. The money was good. The future looked bright. I thought this was normal. I thought salaries would keep going up and my career had a clear path: more years, more experience, more money.

That is not what happened.

Somewhere around 2023, things changed. The big tech layoffs started. Then they kept going. Suddenly, the market was full of experienced developers looking for work. The power shifted. Companies did not need to fight for us anymore. My salary did not go up with inflation. It went down in real terms. The career progression kind of stopped. I am not alone in this; many developers I know feel the same, but we rarely talk about it openly.

## The Housing Trap

I live in Brighton, on the south coast of England. It is a nice city. Close to London. Near the sea. Expensive. I rent, and I have rented for years. I would like to buy a flat someday—a small place to call my own.

![A conceptual split screen showing a rising house price graph on one side and a stagnant salary graph on the other, stylized vector art](https://placehold.co/600x300)

Here is the problem: it is borderline impossible to buy a flat if you are on payroll. The banks look at my salary, they look at house prices, and they do the math. The math does not work. Every year, prices go up more than I can save.

People tell me to become a contractor to earn more and save faster. But being a contractor comes with extra uncertainty—no sick pay, no holiday pay, no job security. In an economy like this, taking on more uncertainty feels dangerous. So I stay employed. I stay safe. I stay stuck.

## The Career Freeze

When I started as a developer, the path seemed clear. Junior, then mid-level, then senior. Maybe lead, manager, or architect. Steady growth with regular promotions. That ladder broke somewhere along the way.

Companies got flatter. Middle management disappeared. The rungs got further apart. Many senior roles vanished in layoffs, and the people who kept their jobs held on tight. There was nowhere to go. I am good at what I do, I keep learning, I ship features, and I mentor others. But the promotions stopped. 

I am not bitter; I am just realistic. The world changed, and the rules of the game changed with it.

## The Bigger Picture

Sometimes I step back and look at everything happening around us. Private equity is behind so much of the industry now. They buy companies, cut costs, raise prices, and extract value. It feels like every business I interact with follows this pattern.

I am not an economist, but what I see makes me nervous. When I look at the housing market, the layoffs, and how companies treat workers, I cannot shake the feeling that something is fundamentally broken.

## The AI Question

There is another factor transforming our reality.

![A futuristic AI robot sitting next to a human developer, both coding together on a large curved monitor, cyberpunk aesthetic](https://placehold.co/600x300)

In 2026, companies simply aren't employing developers the way they used to. AI coding tools got incredibly powerful. One senior developer equipped with the right AI tools can do the work that used to require a whole team. Companies figured this out, and they need fewer of us.

I use these tools myself. I believe they make me better. But I also know they make some jobs disappear. For junior developers, this is the hardest truth: why pay someone to learn when AI can help a senior do it faster? This is a massive challenge for our industry. Where will the next generation of seniors come from?

## What Now

So what do I do? What can any of us do?

I keep working. I keep learning. I keep shipping code.

I try to stay valuable—not just as someone who writes code, but as someone who understands problems, talks to users, and thinks about the whole picture. I work on my own projects, like Spike.land. Not because I expect to get rich, but because building something of my own feels like the only path that makes sense anymore.

I try to spend less. I lower my expectations. I find joy in small things: a walk by the sea, a good coffee, a difficult problem solved in code. I talk to other developers, share stories, and realize we are not alone. That helps more than you might think.

## The Honest Truth

I used to believe that hard work guaranteed success. That skills led to money. That the future was bright if you just kept improving. I do not believe that anymore.

Hard work matters. Skills matter. But luck and timing matter too. Forces beyond our control matter a lot. 

I am earning less than five years ago. My career progression stopped. I cannot buy a home. This is my reality, and it might be the reality for many entering the field today. 

But I am not giving up. I still code every day. I still love what I do. I still think technology can make the world better. I am just done pretending everything is fine. Acknowledging reality is the first step to figuring out what comes next.
