# The Last Two Days of Every Sprint: A Horror Story

![A calendar showing a two-week sprint where the first 12 days are completely empty and calm, but the last 2 days are violently scribbled over with red ink, fire icons, and exclamation marks.](https://placehold.co/800x400)

_By Zoltan Erdos | Brighton, UK | January 2026_

Picture this. It is Thursday. The sprint ends on Friday.

My Slack is exploding. Every message is urgent. Every comment is red. Every conversation starts with "we need to talk about your PR."

I have three pull requests (PRs) open. I submitted them eight days ago. For eight days, there was nothing. Silence. No comments. No questions. No feedback.

Now, with 36 hours left in the sprint, everyone has opinions. Strong opinions. Everything is wrong. Everything must change. Everything is on fire.

If you're new to software engineering, you might think this is an anomaly. But right now, in the age of high-speed AI development, this is every sprint. This is my life.

## The Pattern

Let me walk you through a typical two-week sprint and show you where the system breaks down.

**Day one:** The sprint starts. We have planning meetings. We pick our tickets. I am excited. I start working.

**Day two:** With the help of my AI coding agents, I finish my first ticket. The code is clean. The tests pass. I open a PR. I tag my reviewers. I write a nice description. I move on to the next task.

**Day three:** No comments on my PR. That is fine. People are busy. I finish another ticket. Another PR.

**Day four:** Still nothing. I send a polite message: "Hey, when you have time, could you look at my PR?" Someone says "sure, will do." I believe them. I shouldn't.

**Day five:** Week one is over. I have three PRs open. Zero feedback. I start feeling nervous.

![A developer's desk covered in sticky notes and empty coffee cups, illuminated only by the harsh glow of a monitor displaying dozens of unresolved code review comments.](https://placehold.co/600x300)

**Day six:** Sprint midpoint. I ask again: "Any blockers on those PRs? I want to fix issues early." Someone replies, "Looks fine from what I can see." That is not a real code review.

**Day seven to nine:** Silence. My PRs sit there. Waiting. Growing stale. The rest of the team's code is merging, changing the foundation my code was built on. Conflicts are forming.

**Day ten:** Two days left. I ask one more time. I get no response.

And then it happens.

## The Explosion

Thursday morning. Eight hours until the sprint review meeting where we show our work to the stakeholders. Suddenly, everyone cares.

The comments start flowing. Not one or two. Dozens. On every single PR.

"This pattern does not match our standards."
"You should use this approach instead."
"This whole section needs to be rewritten."
"Why did you do it this way?"

I stare at my screen. Eight days. They had eight days to tell me this. Instead, I learn everything is "wrong" when it is almost too late to fix it.

I start responding, fixing, and rewriting. But every fix creates new questions. Every change needs another review. The reviewers are also busy with their own last-minute rush.

The clock is ticking. I am sweating. My coffee goes cold.

By midnight, I am still working. Fixing things that could have been fixed days ago. Answering questions that could have been asked last week. 

## The Feedback Vacuum

Here is what makes this so painful, especially for junior developers trying to learn: I ask for feedback early. I beg for it. I want to know if I am on the wrong path before I walk ten miles in the wrong direction.

But the feedback never comes early. Why? 

Maybe people are too busy. Maybe reviewing code is boring. But there is a modern twist to this: they know I use AI tools.

When you use AI, some teams look at your code differently. They check harder. They put off reviewing your work because it feels like more effort—they expect to find weird AI hallucinations. 

But that extra checking takes time. And if they don't start until the last two days, that time does not exist.

![An illustration of a bottleneck in a factory assembly line, where fast robotic arms (AI) are piling up products in front of a single overwhelmed human inspector.](https://placehold.co/600x300)

## The Human Cost

Let me tell you what this does to a developer.

First, there is the stress. Those last two days are brutal. Constant anxiety. Every notification makes my heart jump.

Second, there is the waste. I spend hours rewriting code that was already "done." If I had gotten feedback on day four, the fix would take twenty minutes. On day twelve, it takes four hours because my context has faded and the codebase has moved on.

Finally, there is the frustration. Sometimes my colleagues would rather redo my entire PR themselves than give me feedback in time. They trash my work and rewrite it, because that is faster than the back-and-forth on a Thursday night. 

That hurts. I put effort into that code. And instead of helping me make it better, they throw it away.

## What Should Change

If you're joining a development team, or if you're managing one, here is what needs to happen to fix this horror story:

**1. Review early:** Not at the end of the sprint. On day two. On day three. When there is still time to fix things properly.
**2. Give partial feedback:** You do not need to review everything at once. Leave one comment. Point out one architectural issue. Start the conversation early.
**3. Trust the process:** If you ask a developer to change something, trust that they will try to do it well. Do not rewrite their code without giving them a chance to learn and fix it.
**4. Make reviewing a priority:** Code review is not the thing you do after your "real work." It *is* real work. It is how teams build quality software.

I know changing team habits is hard. But those last two days should not be a horror story. They should be calm. They should be about polishing, not panicking.

Until teams change how they review—especially in an era where AI helps us write code faster than ever—those last two days will keep being a nightmare. 

And developers will keep asking for feedback that never comes.
